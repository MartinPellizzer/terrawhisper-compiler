Ginkgo's utilization as medicinal and spiritual aid dates back thousands of years ago to ancient China. The Chinese believed that ginkgo leaves could be used to treat a variety of illnesses, including respiratory problems, digestive issues, and cognitive deficits. Ginkgo was so renowned for its health benefits that it earned the nickname "the Silver Lady". Eventually, its usage in traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) became widely accepted due to the plant's ability to promote blood circulation, reduce inflammation, and provide relief from stress.

Aside from its medicinal properties, ginkgo is often credited with having a role in divination. In ancient times, people would use ginkgo seeds to tell their fortune or predict future events by studying their markings and patterns. These seeds were believed to have magical powers that could reveal hidden knowledge about one's life path.

Ginkgo also holds significant cultural and mythological importance across the world. In Chinese culture, it is considered a symbol of longevity and resilience as ginkgos have lived for millions of years. The tree has even been linked to legends surrounding immortality, such as in the story of Tin Hau, the goddess who had nine ginkgo trees planted around her temple. In Japan, a unique type of ginkgo known as the "living fossil" or Shizen Fossil is revered for its ability to survive even in highly polluted urban environments, highlighting the tree's remarkable adaptability and endurance.

Ginkgo biloba has played an essential role throughout history, serving as a cornerstone of traditional medicine systems, a tool for divination, and a symbol of cultural significance. Its longevity as both a living specimen and as a source of knowledge continues to fascinate people around the world, keeping the history of ginkgo alive and relevant in today's society.
