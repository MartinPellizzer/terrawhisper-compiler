 Celery is a word derived from Old French "selere," and even older Latin terms such as “celeri” or its Greek root, which can be traced back to the ancient language term used for parsnip - apium graveolens. In Ancient Rome times celestine was cultivated not just because of culinary reasons but also considered important due medical advantages in managing and treating various ailments linked with digestion or stomach-related problems, including bloating issues as mentioned by historical Roman physicians such us Aulus Cornelius Celsus.

 Celery holds numerous cultural significance globally - it has been tradition for many years to serve celestine on important family feasts and occasions in Europe due its symbolism of purity, luck & abundance – hence a part if the wedding day meals too; In India, this vegetable is revered as one among holy plants like Ashoka or Tulasi since it has been connected with healing properties for ages. Also known to be used by ancient Greeks and Romans in rituals which represented love between Goddess Demeter & her daughter Persephone - the latter being a goddess of spring, celery is tied culturally throughout various societies around globe reflecting diverse values systems they hold dear; more than its utility as food or medicine it has become an integral part celebrating significant moments togetherness too.

 Celestine holds significance in many mythologies and legends from across the world - one of which involves a tale where King Midas' daughter, Marigold used her touch to turn everything into gold; as legend goes when she touched celery it turned golden too. It also finds references relating directly or indirectly with gods suchus Pan who was said be fond eating leaves during his travels across the forest - associating this food stuff for divinity itself has played a major role shaping its cultural importance over time

 Celestine is mentioned in various literary works; Jane Austen's 'Sense & Sensibility', it plays an important part of Dashwood family meal during the novel. Shakespeare’s play ‘The Tempests’, Proserpina (goddess associated to spring) offers celery stalk while guiding young Ariel spirit and emphasizing its importance as a symbol for growth or change too, proving how much cultural influence this humble vegetable had upon many literatures throughout history

 Celestine has held mystic properties in diverse beliefs including being used by shamans from Africa who believed it enhanced healing effects when consumed; In some parts of Europe like Bulgaria, people would put fresh stalks beside graves as an offering while grieving their loved ones passing - proving that beyond culinary purposes celery holds deeper spiritual meaning making significant connections to religious practices too.
