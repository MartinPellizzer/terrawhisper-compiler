The inflorescence type of Citrus limon is a panicle, where multiple flowers are arranged in clusters at the ends of branches. These flowers are relatively simple in structure, lacking petals, which makes them apetalous. In terms of symmetry, they are actinomorphic, meaning they display radial symmetry.

Each lemon flower is hermaphroditic, containing both male and female reproductive organs in the same flower. These flowers are axillary, meaning they arise from the leaf axils, typically found at the junction between the leaves and stems.

The shape of the lemon flower is cup-shaped, with a white color and a smooth, glabrous texture. They have a diameter of about 2.5 centimeters (0.98 inches) and a width of 2.0 centimeters (0.79 inches). The flowers consist of five floral parts, with five sepals but no distinct petals. Instead, the tepals, which are fused at the base, give the appearance of petals.

The lemon flowers are highly abundant in stamens, with approximately 20 stamens, and they possess a single pistil. The ovary of the flower is superior, indicating its position above the attachment point of other flower parts. The style of the pistil is slender and terminates in a capitate stigma.

Unlike some other plant species, Citrus limon flowers lack stipules, and their bracts are small and inconspicuous. The peduncle, which is the stalk that supports the flower cluster, is short and slender, as is the pedicel, the stalk connecting individual flowers to the peduncle.
