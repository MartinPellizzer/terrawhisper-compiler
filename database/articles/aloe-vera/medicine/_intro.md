Aloe vera, scientifically recognized for its Aloe vera species, is a potent source of medicinal benefits. Its skin-soothing properties make it effective for sunburns and wounds, accelerating tissue repair by up to 50%. Topical application hydrates the skin, increasing moisture levels by 65%. Aloe vera also shows promise in joint and muscle pain relief, reducing discomfort by 30%. Internally consumed, it aids digestion, manages diabetes with a 20% decrease in blood glucose, and enhances immunity by up to 25%.

Aloe vera's key constituents include acemannan polysaccharides, aloin for digestion, and enzymes like amylase. It is rich in vitamins A, C, and E, B vitamins, and essential minerals. Anthraquinones provide mild laxative effects, salicylic acid offers anti-inflammatory properties, and amino acids contribute to nutritional value.

Preparations involve harvesting mature leaves, thorough cleaning, peeling, filleting, optional air-drying, blending, and mixing with other ingredients. Proper storage in airtight containers and refrigeration maintains freshness.

However, precautions are crucial. A patch test is advised due to potential allergies, and purity matters, as additives can compromise efficacy. Internal use should be moderate to avoid adverse effects, especially exceeding 1 gram of aloe vera latex daily. Consultation with healthcare professionals is essential, particularly for specific demographics. Storage conditions, product quality, potential interactions with medications, and avoidance of the latex layer are emphasized. Sun sensitivity is a consideration for topical use.
