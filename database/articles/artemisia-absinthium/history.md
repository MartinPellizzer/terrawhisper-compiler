
Wormwood (Artemisia absinthium) has been a staple in traditional folk medicine across various cultures for centuries. Its bitter leaves were historically brewed into teas or tonics, believed to aid digestion, alleviate fever, and treat various ailments. In Chinese medicine, it was used to expel parasites, while in European herbalism, it was employed to soothe stomach troubles and even as a vermifuge. Ancient Greeks and Egyptians valued Wormwood for its medicinal properties, attributing it with healing properties for malaria, jaundice, and menstrual pains.

Ancient civilizations utilized Wormwood in divination practices, often burning it as incense during rituals to enhance clairvoyance or induce visions. The herb was believed to facilitate heightened states of consciousness, aiding in accessing otherworldly realms. Diviners and seers from cultures such as the Babylonians, Celts, and Romans revered Wormwood for its purported ability to bring lucid dreams or assist in prophetic experiences, using it in ceremonies seeking insights into the future.

Legends and myths enshroud Wormwood, deepening its mystique. It's said to have been named after Artemis, the Greek goddess of the hunt and wilderness, who gifted the plant with its medicinal properties. Some folklore suggests Wormwood was used in ancient Greek religious ceremonies to enhance spiritual connections. Moreover, its mention in religious texts, such as the Bible's Book of Revelation, where Wormwood represents bitterness and calamity, has contributed to its mysticism and associations with doom and punishment. Legends about its use in potions for magic spells and protection against evil spirits further illustrate its enigmatic nature throughout history.
