Wormwood (Artemisia absinthium) is a bitter herb used sparingly in culinary practices. While not typically consumed in large quantities due to its intensely bitter taste, its leaves are occasionally used as a flavoring agent in some traditional dishes. Its bitter profile is an acquired taste, with its leaves occasionally used in small amounts to add a distinct bitterness to certain cuisines, such as in traditional European dishes like vermouth or some regional specialty liqueurs. Despite its limited culinary use, the bitter and herbaceous qualities of wormwood can complement certain foods when used judiciously.

The flavor profile of wormwood is dominated by an intensely bitter and herbaceous taste. Its bitterness arises from the presence of compounds like absinthin and artabsin, which provide a strong, almost medicinal bitterness. The herb also exudes a complex aroma, featuring notes of camphor and a slightly woody, earthy undertone. This potent flavor profile makes wormwood a challenging ingredient to incorporate into dishes, requiring careful balancing with other flavors to avoid overpowering the palate.

When using wormwood in culinary preparations, precise measurement and caution are crucial due to its overpowering bitterness. Typically, only small quantities, such as a few fresh or dried leaves, are utilized to infuse dishes or beverages with its distinctive flavor. Additionally, its bitterness can be mellowed or balanced by pairing it with sweet or acidic components. It's important to note that wormwood contains thujone, a compound that in high concentrations can be toxic, so moderate and controlled use is advised in culinary applications to avoid adverse effects.
