Allium sativum, as known as Garlic, has a fibrous root system with branching, tuberous, and non-woody roots that anchor the plant and absorb water and nutrients. These roots are relatively shallow, reaching 10-20 cm in depth, and are whitish, smooth, and lack root nodules. Garlic roots do not form mycorrhizal associations and are well-adapted to cold temperatures.

The herbaceous garlic stems grow in a basal arrangement, are cylindrical, and can reach heights of 30-90 cm. They have one node at the base and flexible internodes. The stems are green, smooth, and fleshy.

Garlic leaves are simple, linear, and lanceolate, forming a basal rosette with an upright orientation. They are 15-40 cm long, 1-3 cm wide, green, and have a smooth, slightly waxy texture. The apex tapers to a sharp point, and the base wraps around the stem with an entire margin. They exhibit parallel venation and lack a petiole, attaching directly to the stem.

Garlic flowers are arranged in an umbel and are simple, with six tepals. They have actinomorphic symmetry, funnel-shaped appearance, and are typically white to pale purple.

The fruit of garlic is a capsule found in an umbel arrangement, with ovate shape and dimensions of 1.5x1.0x0.5 cm. The capsules are initially pale green and become brown or gray as they mature, with a smooth and shiny surface and triangular segments. They detach from the pedicel at maturity, and the pedicel is about 2 cm long.

Garlic seeds are monocotyledonous, whorled, ellipsoid, and lack appendages. Each bulb contains 10-20 seeds, which are hard and impermeable to water. The seed coat varies in color from pale brown to dark brown, is smooth and glossy, and has a waxy texture. The embryo typically has one fleshy cotyledon, and the radicle is underdeveloped in mature seeds.
