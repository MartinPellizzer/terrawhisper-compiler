Garlic (Allium sativum) is a versatile and easy-to-grow plant that can thrive in gardens or landscapes. To cultivate garlic, plant individual cloves in well-drained soil during the fall, around 5 cm deep and 10-15 cm apart, with pointed ends facing up. Ensure the location receives ample sunlight and maintain a consistent watering schedule during the growing season.

Ideal growing conditions for garlic include a sunny location with at least six hours of sunlight daily. The plant prefers loose, well-draining soil with a pH range of 6.0 to 7.5. Incorporating organic matter, such as compost, into the soil enhances fertility. Adequate moisture is crucial, especially during the early growth stages, but garlic is susceptible to waterlogging, so avoid overwatering. Fertilize with a balanced fertilizer in early spring, and provide additional nutrients if the soil lacks essential elements.

Pruning and maintenance are essential to ensure a healthy garlic crop. Remove any scapes that emerge, as they divert energy away from bulb development. Weed regularly to minimize competition for nutrients and space. Mulching around the plants helps retain moisture, suppress weeds, and regulate soil temperature. Harvest when the lower leaves turn yellow and dry, typically in late spring or early summer. Allow the bulbs to cure in a dry, well-ventilated area for a few weeks before storing them in a cool, dark place. Proper care throughout the growing season ensures a bountiful harvest of flavorful garlic bulbs.
