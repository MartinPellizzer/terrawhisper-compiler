The historical aspect of Gotu Kola (Centella asiatica) can be traced back to its role in traditional medicine and health practices across different cultures and regions. It has been used for centuries due to its various medicinal properties, including aiding cognitive function, skin health, and promoting overall well-being. In many parts of Asia, Gotu Kola was considered an essential herb during childbirth for inducing labor and easing the pain associated with delivery.

Centella asiatica also played a significant role in divination practices among certain cultures, such as Hinduism, where it was believed to have spiritual powers that could help predict the future. It was often used in sacred rituals and ceremonies aimed at seeking guidance from the divine realm. In some cases, the plant was even incorporated into talismans and amulets, which were thought to protect their wearers from harm and bring good fortune.

Lastly, Gotu Kola has been woven into numerous legends across different societies, reflecting its deep-rooted significance in their respective cultures. One such legend originated in Sri Lanka, where it is said that a prince discovered the medicinal properties of this plant after witnessing an elephant eating it and recovering from various injuries. Impressed by its healing abilities, the prince introduced Gotu Kola into mainstream herbalism practices, thus solidifying its place as a valuable remedy within Ayurvedic medicine.
