Tincture: This is a concentrated liquid made by soaking the root or parts of the dong quai plant in alcohol or water for a while, which can be taken orally after it's strained and mixed with other ingredients if needed. The tincture contains essential oils and active compounds from dong quai that may provide various health benefits when consumed.

Tea: It's another common way to consume the herb, where dried leaves, root bark or stems are steeped in hot water for a few minutes before drinking. This process releases the plant's essential oils, flavors and nutrients that can be beneficial when consumed as an everyday drink.

Pills and Capsules: These are made by grinding dong quai root into powder form and then mixing it with other ingredients like gelatin to create a solid, easily swallowed pill. Both pills and capsules allow the active compounds of dong quai to be taken in precise doses for specific health conditions.

Powder: This preparation involves milling and grinding the plant parts (dried root or leaves) into a fine powder. It can be mixed with water, juice or other liquids before consumption. The powder form is often used as an ingredient in various recipes, like teas, soups, or smoothies.

Essential oil: This is obtained by extracting volatile plant compounds using a distillation process. Dong quai essential oil can be added to carrier oils for use in aromatherapy, massage therapy, or as an ingredient in homeopathic remedies.

Ointment: It's made by combining dong quai extract with a base like petroleum jelly, creating a smooth application suitable for topical use on skin and muscles. This preparation can provide localized relief from pain, muscle strain, or other issues.

Supplement: These are pills, capsules, powders, or liquids that contain concentrated amounts of dong quai's beneficial compounds, usually derived from the plant's roots. They are often used to support overall health and well-being.

Extract: This involves a more complex process where dong quai is soaked in alcohol for a long time, resulting in an even more concentrated form of the herb that retains its medicinal properties and may have additional benefits.

Liquid extract: It's similar to tincture but has a lower concentration of alcohol and includes both water and glycerin as part of the extraction process, making it more pleasant tasting and less potent than tinctures.
