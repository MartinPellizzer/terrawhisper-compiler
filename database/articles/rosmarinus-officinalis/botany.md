Rosemary is scientifically known by its botanical name Rosmarinus officinalis and falls under several classifications within various biological systems, including domain Eukarya (life forms possessing a cell nucleus), kingdom Plantae or Plants as well as phylum Magnoliophyta which represents flower-bearing plants. Further classification places it in the order Lamiales with its family Lamiaceae commonly referred to as mints and deadnettle - type plants, followed by being assigned into a genus Rosmarinus known for their fragrant flowers such as Rosemary that can be found within this grouping too due to close similarities. Within those mentioned taxonomical aspects or the structure of classification systems used in biology we arrive at its species term officinalis which differentiates it from other rose bushes like wild and common ones, a reflection more accurately indicating towards unique Rosemary characteristics such as needle-like leaves that resemble small pine needles. It is also worth mentioning some synonyms or alternative names for this plant includes Cryptophiale hiberica Briq., Rosmarinus alpinus Hochst.; they represent the same species but under different nomenclatures depending on how early botanists were identifying it due to various characteristics that may have differed slightly from others.

The morphology of Rosemary can be described as having evergreen perennial shrubs or small trees with long narrow green leaves which appear like needles, arranged oppositely and the blooming flowers are whorls composed primarily white-blue coloring often in spikes that usually appears late summer through fall depending on cultivation conditions. The plant can grow up to 10 feet tall but generally ranges from two or three foot height with a spread of four feet wide, making it an ideal herbal garden addition due both for aesthetic and culinary purposes as the fragrant needles have long been used in cooking spices and essential oils that give them distinct aroma.

 Two well-known variations among cultivars (plants developed via selection), often are variegated or white blooming Rosemary, this results from a natural mutation where leaves turn to yellowish green with creamy edged borders instead of the usual dark and pale variability in leaf color patterns. Variant growth forms can also be observed within certain climatic environments producing prostrate rosemaries growing more sprawling groundcover or even shrubby dwarf Rosemary plants which are lower maintenance than its larger counterparts but still offer a similar aroma to the regular kind, making it easier for indoor use with some limited light exposure.

 The geographic distribution and natural habitats of Rosmarinus officinalis spans far stretches across Europe including the Iberian Peninsula in Spain where wild rosemary is native as well through parts Mediterranean region like Italy to North Africa, Morocco along westward Asia countries such Iran or Turkey. It has also been introduced into different continents by early humans via its useful qualities leading it being cultivated for culinary and medicinal uses within a diverse range of environments beyond what originally natural conditions allowed. These plants can be spotted either thriving near rocks, dry sandy soils with full exposure to direct sunlight as they are well adapted drought-tolerant shrubs that require moderate irrigation but not excessive watering in most instances - this ability is also a factor why it has spread beyond its native range where gardeners appreciate the versatility of these resilient herbs.
