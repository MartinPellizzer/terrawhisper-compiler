
Yarrow can induce photosensitivity in individuals when used improperly. This condition refers to an increased sensitivity of the skin to sunlight or ultraviolet (UV) radiation, leading to skin irritation, redness, rashes, or even severe sunburns. The constituents responsible for this effect in Yarrow are primarily the phototoxic compounds called sesquiterpene lactones, such as achilleine and achillicin. These components, when exposed to sunlight, can cause a reaction on the skin, resulting in photosensitivity.

The side effect of inducing photosensitivity caused by Yarrow can aggravate certain health conditions such as eczema, psoriasis, or other dermatological issues, making the skin more susceptible to damage from sun exposure. People who are most sensitive to this side effect are those with fair or sensitive skin types. Individuals undergoing treatments that make their skin more photosensitive, such as certain medications or therapies like chemotherapy, should also be cautious when using Yarrow. Moreover, prolonged exposure to Yarrow preparations, particularly on the skin, can increase the risk of photosensitivity reactions.

To avoid the side effect of photosensitivity when using Yarrow, proper preparations and dosage are essential. It is recommended to apply Yarrow-containing products, such as creams or oils, in diluted forms to the skin. A safe dosage typically involves using Yarrow extracts with concentrations of no more than 10% in topical preparations. Additionally, when using Yarrow for tea or ingestion, moderate intake and avoiding prolonged exposure to sunlight after consumption can help prevent photosensitivity reactions. Practicing good sun protection measures like wearing protective clothing and using sunscreen with a high SPF can further mitigate the risk of skin reactions caused by Yarrow-induced photosensitivity.
