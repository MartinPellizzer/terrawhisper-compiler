
Yarrow's Tannins are complex polyphenolic compounds found in varying concentrations throughout the plant's aerial parts, particularly in its leaves and flowers. The concentration of tannins in yarrow can range from 3% to 10%, with some variations depending on the specific species, growing conditions, and harvesting time. On average, the leaves contain approximately 7% to 8% tannins, while the flowers tend to have a slightly lower concentration, typically around 5% to 6%. These percentages may fluctuate due to environmental factors, such as soil quality, sunlight exposure, and plant age, impacting the overall tannin content within yarrow.

Tannins in yarrow possess notable astringent, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory properties. Their astringent nature contributes to yarrow's traditional medicinal use in promoting wound healing and reducing bleeding. Furthermore, the antioxidant properties of tannins aid in combating oxidative stress, potentially protecting cells from damage caused by free radicals. Additionally, these compounds exhibit mild anti-inflammatory effects, which could contribute to yarrow's historical use in alleviating inflammatory conditions.

An overdose of Yarrow's tannins can lead to adverse effects. Excessive consumption may result in gastrointestinal irritation, leading to symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and stomach pain. Moreover, tannins can interfere with iron absorption, potentially causing iron-deficiency anemia over prolonged periods of excessive intake. It's essential to consume Yarrow in moderation to avoid these potential side effects associated with an overdose of its tannin constituents.
