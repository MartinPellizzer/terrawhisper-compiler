Lavender loves direct sunlight with at least six hours a day; however, if located beneath deciduous trees or shrubs can get adequate amount after partial sun is blocked for some time due to their structure making its growth possible even in shaded areas during the initial phase until it establishes properly. In terms of soil conditions they prefer well-drained slightly acidic ones that have a pH range between 6 and 7 - make sure not too alkaline as this may result into stunted or yellowish leaves with reduced flowering capacity, which is one key characteristic for identification purposes (the bluish purple flowers blooming all over the plant).

For Lavender to flourishing in gardens; good practice advises on selecting a suitable spot. They need enough room without obstructions that block access during pruning time – also they prefer well-drained soil with sufficient drainage as it prevents root rot which can be fatal for this species if neglected, so planting lavenders about 2 to3 feet apart allows space around them allowing water flow from excess irrigation or rainfall.

 ideally cut lavenders stalk when one third of the flowers have turned brown indicating they are ready. Dry stems should further mature properly away from direct sunlight before finally stored either bundles tied at top or dried separately for future usage which is an important part in culinary context but also preserves them to keep fresh appearance as long time interior décor items such vases decoration elements even extending their use beyond mere aesthetic purposes into medicinal field because of its antibacterial properties.
