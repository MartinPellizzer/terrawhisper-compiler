Ammi visnaga inflorescence type is a compound umbel, wherein numerous small flowers cluster together in an umbrella-like arrangement. These flowers are simple in structure and exhibit actinomorphic symmetry, meaning they have radial symmetry, like a star.

Each flower of Ammi visnaga is complete and perfect, composed of all essential floral parts. They attach below the ovary, following a hypogynous attachment pattern. In terms of shape, these flowers are ovate, with a length of approximately 2 centimeters (0.79 inches) and a width of around 1 centimeter (0.39 inches). Their color is predominantly white, giving them a pure and delicate appearance.

The texture of Ammi visnaga flowers is smooth, and their surface is glabrous, meaning they are hairless. They consist of four whorls in their floral arrangement, with a total of 5 sepals, 5 petals, and 10 tepals. The presence of 5 stamens and a single pistil characterizes the inner structure. The ovary is superior, while the style is relatively short, leading to a capitate stigma at the top.

Notably, these flowers lack stipules, which are small leaf-like structures often found at the base of a flower. However, they do have small bracts present, contributing to their overall appearance.

The peduncle, or the main stem that supports the inflorescence, is relatively long, allowing the compound umbel to rise above the plant's foliage. In contrast, the pedicels, which connect individual flowers to the main stem, are short in length.
