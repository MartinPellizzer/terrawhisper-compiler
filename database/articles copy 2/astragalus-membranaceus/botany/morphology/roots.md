The root system of Astragalus membranaceus is primarily taproot in nature, with a fibrous structure. It exhibits minimal lateral branching, which sets it apart from many other plant species. The root tips are pointed, aiding in soil penetration.

Root density is a key metric for cultivation. Astragalus membranaceus typically features around 1,000 roots per square meter. This dense network of roots is an advantage for nutrient uptake and anchorage.

Root depth is essential for assessing its adaptability to different soil conditions. These roots typically extend to a depth of 50-80 cm (20-31 inches) in the soil, allowing the plant to access nutrients and water resources effectively. The root diameter ranges from 0.3 to 0.5 cm (0.12-0.20 inches), indicating a moderate thickness.

In terms of length, Astragalus membranaceus roots can grow up to 100 cm (39 inches), making them suitable for harvesting. The color of these roots is pale yellow-brown, and their surface texture is rough and wrinkled, possibly aiding in nutrient absorption and providing some protection.

One distinctive feature of Astragalus membranaceus roots is the presence of nitrogen-fixing nodules. These nodules contribute to soil health by facilitating nitrogen fixation, benefiting both the plant and the surrounding ecosystem.

Additionally, these roots feature abundant fine root hairs. These root hairs play a crucial role in absorbing water and essential nutrients from the soil.
