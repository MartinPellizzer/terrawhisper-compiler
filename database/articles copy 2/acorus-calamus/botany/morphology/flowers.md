The flowers of Acorus calamus are hermaphrodite, meaning they possess both male and female reproductive structures. They exhibit simplicity in their structure, composed of three sepals and three petals. These six floral parts collectively form the perianth, the protective layer surrounding the reproductive organs.

The flowers are radially symmetrical, meaning they can be divided into equal halves in any direction, resembling a tube with a length ranging from 3 to 8 centimeters and a width of approximately 0.5 centimeters. The thickness of the flower measures about 0.2 centimeters.

In terms of color, Acorus calamus flowers are pale yellow-green, giving them a distinct appearance. These flowers are unisexual, with separate male and female individuals on the same plant. Male flowers have six stamens with yellow anthers, while female flowers feature a single pistil.

The ovary is positioned above the sepals and petals, which is referred to as a superior ovary. It consists of three carpels. The style, a slender structure about 1 centimeter long, leads to a capitate stigma in female flowers.

Notably, Acorus calamus flowers are enclosed by green bracts, a feature that distinguishes them. However, they lack stipules. Interestingly, these flowers do not have a peduncle and are borne directly on the stem.
