Angelica sinensis, commonly known as Dong Quai, is a herbaceous plant with distinctive characteristics. Its fibrous root system consists of a taproot and adventitious roots, with a moderate lateral branching pattern. These roots are brown to reddish-brown, extending 15-30 cm below the soil and reaching a maximum length of 60 cm. They lack root nodules and have sparse root hairs.

The herbaceous stem of Angelica sinensis is round in cross-section, can grow up to 150 cm in height, and has a small diameter of approximately 0.5 cm. The stem is green with a purplish tinge, smooth (glabrous), and marked with fine lines or ridges. Nodes are distinct, and internodes span 20-30 cm.

The pinnately compound leaves are large, with 3-7 triangular-ovate leaflets, arranged alternately along the stem. They are dark green, thin, and serrated at the margin. The leaves lack stipules and have petioles 5-20 cm long.

The compound umbel inflorescence of Angelica sinensis features small, actinomorphic flowers with white to pale pink petals. Each flower has five floral parts in each whorl, and the sepals and petals are indistinguishable. The flowers are borne on peduncles up to 10 cm long and have two styles and two capitate stigmas.

Its schizocarp fruits are elliptical, dark brown, and split into two mericarps upon maturity. The seeds are elliptical, hard, and impermeable, with a brown, smooth, and shiny seed coat. They lack a visible hilum and contain a straight embryo with two fleshy cotyledons and a small radicle.
