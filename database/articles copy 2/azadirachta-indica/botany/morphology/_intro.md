The Azadirachta indica, or neem tree, has a root system that is of type taproot, with a deep main root aiding in stability and water absorption. Its fibrous lateral roots form a web-like structure, anchoring the tree and maximizing nutrient uptake. The stems are woody, oppositely arranged, and can grow up to 15 meters, with rough, fissured bark. Neem leaves are lanceolate, dark green, and thin, featuring serrated margins and pinnate venation.

Neem flowers form panicles with small, pale yellow, unisexual blossoms that exhibit radial symmetry. They have five sepals and five petals, with ten stamens and a single ovary. The flowers lack stipules and have short peduncles and pedicels. The fruits are drupes, ranging from green to yellow, with a smooth, fleshy exterior marked by longitudinal ridges. These dehiscent fruits split open to disperse the seeds.

Neem seeds are dicotyledonous, hard, oval, and woody, with a thin, impermeable, brown seed coat. They contain a well-developed embryo with two fleshy cotyledons and a single rudimentary radicle. The neem tree's distinct characteristics in its various parts contribute to its unique appearance and reproductive strategies.
