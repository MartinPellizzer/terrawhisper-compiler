
            In summary, the study examines the impact of fruits and fruit products on gut microbiota, gut motility, and constipation. Various fruits have been shown to modify the microbiota, increase fecal weight, and reduce transit time in human studies. However, there is limited evidence on which specific fruit components are responsible for these effects, suggesting a potential synergy of multiple components. Therefore, further research is needed to better understand the mechanisms of action of fruits in regulating gut health and constipation prevention.

            In the study, the authors conducted an extensive review of in vitro, animal, and human studies investigating the effects of fruits on gut motility and microbiota. They found that prunes, raisins, apple fiber isolate, kiwifruit, fig paste, and orange extract have been shown to increase fecal weight, reduce transit time, and modify the microbiota. While there is some evidence to suggest that specific fruit components may play a role in these effects, it is unclear whether a synergy of multiple components is responsible for them. Overall, the study highlights the potential benefits of incorporating fruits into one's diet as a natural remedy for constipation prevention and gut health maintenance.
