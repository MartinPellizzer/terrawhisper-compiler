
            The study aimed to compare the effects of psyllium hydrophilic mucilloid and docusate sodium on chronic constipation treatment in terms of stool softening (stool water content), laxative efficacy, bowel movement frequency, stool water weight, total stool output and O’Brien rank-type score combining objective measures of constipation. The study involved 170 subjects with chronic idiopathic constipation at three clinical sites who followed a two-week baseline phase with placebo, followed by two weeks of treatment with either psyllium (5.1g b.d.) plus docusate placebo or docusate sodium (100mg b.d.) plus psyllium placebo. In week 2, the study found that psyllium significantly increased stool water content compared to docusate, and also increased stool weight and output while reducing the O’Brien rank-type score combining objective measures of constipation. Furthermore, psyllium significantly increased bowel movement frequency compared to docusate in week 2, but not in week 1. Overall, the study showed that psyllium was superior to docusate sodium for stool softening and had greater overall laxative efficacy in patients with chronic idiopathic constipation.
