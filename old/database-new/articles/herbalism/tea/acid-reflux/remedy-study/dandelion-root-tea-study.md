The study identifies GI-protective substances in Dandelion root including taraxasterol, taraxerol, caffeic acid, chicoric acid, chlorogenic acid, luteolin, polysaccharides, inulin, and β-sitosterol. These substances have been found to have pharmacological effects against GI disorders such as dyspepsia, gastroesophageal reflux disease, gastritis, small intestinal ulcer, ulcerative colitis, liver diseases, gallstones, acute pancreatitis, and GI malignancy.
The underlying molecular mechanisms for these effects may include immuno-inflammatory mechanisms, apoptosis mechanism, autophagy mechanism, and cholinergic mechanism. However, the study notes that interactions of Dandelion's constituents with GI health-related biological entities or other ingested bioactive compounds should not be ignored.
The study concludes that while there is some in vivo and in vitro evidence to support the use of Dandelion root as a complementary and alternative medicine/therapeutic for GI disorders, more research is needed to identify bioactive compounds/complexes in various Dandelion species and to assess their metabolism, bioavailability, and safety.
