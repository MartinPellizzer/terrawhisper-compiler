
The study summarizes other clinical studies conducted over the past twenty years on the effects of maté on human health. The physiological effects of maté have been attributed to phenolics, methylxanthines and saponins. Antilipemic activity was found to be more consistent than glycaemic control. Maté has been found to protect low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-c) from oxidation in whole plasma. The antioxidant enzymatic complex was positively modulated, indicating that maté might help maintain redox homeostasis. Most clinical trials did not find a significant positive effect of maté consumption on glycaemia. However, it seems that maté's hypoglycaemic effect is more evident in type-2 diabetes mellitus subjects. Maté has shown potential to increase energy expenditure and weight loss. Carcinogenicity is related to consumption temperature, not maté itself. Long-term randomized double-blind placebo-controlled studies are essential to provide more consistent data.
