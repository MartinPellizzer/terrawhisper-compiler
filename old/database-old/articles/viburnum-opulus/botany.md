Cramp Bark (Viburnum opulus) belongs to the domain Eukaryota; kingdom Plantae or also known as Plants, phylums are Embryophyta and Viridiplantae which includes vascular plants like trees & flowering ones; class Magnoliopsida for Dicotyledons (different from monocots), order Cornales - the family Viburnaceae to group similar species together with flowers having 4 or more parts, genus 'Viburnum' consisting of over two hundred species belonging mainly in Asia and Europe. Cramp Bark can be also called Highbush cranberry due its edible brightly red fruits resembling those small sweet cherries you might have eaten once but don’t confuse it with the actual fruit-bearing plant, Vaccinium oxycoccos known as lingonberries or boggies.

 'Viburnum opulus var compactus' is a dwarf version growing only up to 1m high but with the same flowering properties of normal variety; then there’s another one called ‘Rose-leaf Highbush', boasting distinctive foliage more resembling rose leaves rather than typical Viburnum ones. These variations highlight how nature can create such a wide range even within closely related groups!

Cramp Bark's geographic distribution and natural habitats are quite varied too, it is found across the European mainland & extends through East-European regions to Siberia; while some other native occurrences go back towards NW of North America via northern states into British Columbia. This plant thrives in different environments like moist woodlands or swamps but also grows on well drained soils under full sunlight exposing their dense and round leaf clusters, bright red fruits & beautiful white flowers during springtime that attract beetles for pollination activities; all these factors influence Cramp Bark’s adaptability to various surroundings it resides in.
