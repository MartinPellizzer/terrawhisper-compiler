The etymology of "Elecampane" originates from its name 'Inula', derived either by Latin or Greek and translated to English as meaning a yellow-flowering herb, while "helenium", is said to be named after Helen of Troy in ancient Greece because the flower was believed to represent hope amidst tragedy. Elecampane belongs biologically under the sunflower family called Asteraceae; however this peculiar plant species' scientific name 'Inula helenium', which it shares with other members, can lead some confusion due to their varied names like Elf Flour or Narrow-leaved Knapweed being equally referred for plants of a different genus.

Elecampane has been historically utilized as more than just medicine by cultures across the globe; its roots have played significant roles both on mundane and cultural levels in societies that range from China, Iran to Europe especially medieval France where it was widely known not only because they used this herb for healing purposes but also during weddings wherein elecampanes would adorn bridal bouquets alongside flower-garnished horses as well.

Myths and legends connected with Elecampane are mostly European; among them, there's a popular belief that the goddesses Persephone (or Proserpine) of Greek mythology would emerge from Hades each year in Spring accompanied by a bouquet containing this herb symbolizing hope. Another story comes to us about how Saint Helen discovered Jesus Christ’s cross while visiting Jerusalem and came across Elecampane plants - finding them so reminiscently close to their likeness with Crucifixion imagery that she used it subsequently during Holy Communions for centuries thereafter as a symbol of atonement.

In literature, writers such Alexander Pope in his "Elegy Written On His Own Misfortunes", and William Wordsworth mention this versatile herb referencing the belief Elecampane has medicinal healing powers like alleviating fever or even helping cure ailments including lethargy & insomnia while simultaneously symbolizing hope amidst difficulties faced by human beings.

Folkloristic uses of elecampanes go beyond just its medical and cultural significance; it was believed to have magical properties too, such as being able ward off evil spirits or bring good fortune in matters related love life according ancient customs found around many different cultures worldwide from medieval times until today - a testament not only for the power they hold but also our reliance on nature even before modern medicine came into existence.
