The etymology of "Rehmannia" originates from German botanist Wilhelm Rehm who first described it as a plant species named Glottidium glutinosum in honor and reference to his mentors, Drs Carl Friedrich von Ledebour (Ledébec) & Martin Gattinger. It is derived mainly due to the genus 'Rehmannia' which was initially referred to by botanists as Rehmian plants. Its epithet glutinosa indicates that it has a sticky surface on some parts of its leaves and stems, thus giving rise to this unique plant species known around China in traditional medicine for centuries under the name "DI huang" which literally means 'red king' referring primarily as a major ingredient in prescriptions used against kidney weakness.

Myths surrounding Rehmannia range from belief that it was found after prayers made by a young woman in distress over her sick parent while traveling across mountains. The plant also featured heavily into many folklore tales involving immortality where consumption of this unique and highly-valued medicinal species ensured healthier, longer lives for those who could find ways to incorporate its use regularly as part their daily routines or lifestyle decisions according various traditions preserved in oral histories handed down generations.

Various references about Rehmannia are widely featured through multiple writings within literary works like "Huangdi Neijing" (The Yellow Emperor's Classic of Internal Medicine), one among the oldest texts related to Traditional Chinese medicine that remains hugely influential even today across regions where traditional medicinal practices prevail. These include using Rehmannia in various recipes, as a part formulas along with other herbs for healing different bodily maladies or general maintenance against certain chronic illnesses associated specifically towards kidney health issues according to TCM principles which emphasize balancing energies within our bodies based on nature's patterns reflected across medicinal plants such Rehmannia.
