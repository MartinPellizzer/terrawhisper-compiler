 Cinchona (botanically known as Cinchonate or simply cinchonoa) is a tree that belongs to Rubiaceae family, native primarily in the Andes Mountains and some parts of South America. It got its name from 'Countess Chinchay', who supposedly tended by physicians during her illness with bark extracts derived this medicinal plant back then known as Jesuit's Bark or Peruvian Tree Bark (later replaced Cichona in Europe). This word, "Cincho", is believed to have been a corruption of 'Countess Chinchay', giving rise to the modern name - Cinchoma and its other spelling variants like Quina.

 For thousands years before being rediscovered for it medical benefits by European explorers, indigenous tribes in South America used cinchona bark as a medicine plant with healing properties particularly targeting fevers including malaria which was rampant during those days making Europeans vulnerable to this disease. It's been credited greatly throughout history that Cinchona saved millions of lives worldwide when introduced quinine derived from the tree helped in treating and preventing Malarial fever, a major cause for death globally until effective drugs came along later on twentieth century (chloroquine).

 One interesting legend surrounding Cinchona has to do with its origin being credited sometimes by European scientists of 160s AD when Spanish explorer Francisco del Moguer was exploring South America, he discovered the tree and reportedly found local inhabitants utilizing it for medicinal purposes. Another myth revolves around Jesuit missionaries who were said have accidentally ingested Cinchona bark extract while treating a disease on one of their trips to Peru in 1630s AD; not only was this accidental discovery life-changing but also brought attention towards the plant's healing properties leading Europeans back then, especially Spanish and French scientists keenly searching these plants within Latin America.

 Cinchona has been referenced in literature through its medicinal uses as well as symbolically representing hope against death by several prominent authors like Charles Dickens who mentioned it prominently across works including David Copperfield, Oliver Twist and A Tale of Two cities.

 Lastly but not least significant are the traditional ways that indigenous tribes in South America have been using Cinchona bark for centuries before Western civilisation started utilizing its therapeutic benefits extensively; these methods ranged from treating minor ailments such as stomach pains and cold to major illness like malaria, all without knowing about quinine or any pharmacological concepts which was further exploited by Europeans when they began cultivating the plant outside South America.
