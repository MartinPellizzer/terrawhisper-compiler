

The medicinal constituents of yarrow include volatile oils, flavonoids, and essential amino acids. It has strong anti-inflammatory properties, thanks to the presence of eucannabinol. The plant's high level of antioxidants also contributes to its healing abilities, particularly when it comes to treating skin conditions.

Yarrow can be used in various ways for medicinal purposes. The most commonly utilized parts are the leaves and flowers. People often make teas or tinctures using these parts of the plant. They may also apply yarrow topically in the form of poultices, ointments, or compresses to treat skin conditions.

While yarrow is generally considered safe, there are some possible side effects one should be aware of. The consumption of large amounts can cause stomach upset and allergic reactions may occur in those sensitive to plant pollen. Pregnant women should also exercise caution as the plant might stimulate menstruation or cause uterine contractions.

It's essential to take precautions when using yarrow, especially if you have any underlying health conditions. Consult a healthcare professional before incorporating it into your routine, particularly if you are taking medications that may interact with the plant's properties. As with many medicinal plants, start with small doses and gradually increase as needed to avoid unwanted effects.
