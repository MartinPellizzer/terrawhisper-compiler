Yarrow's Flavonoids are a group of polyphenolic compounds found abundantly in the aerial parts of the yarrow plant (Achillea millefolium). The concentration of flavonoids in yarrow varies based on factors such as species, growth conditions, and extraction methods. Typically, yarrow contains around 3% to 5% flavonoids by weight in its dried form. Apigenin and luteolin are among the predominant flavonoids present in yarrow, constituting approximately 0.2% to 0.6% and 0.1% to 0.4% of the plant's dry weight, respectively. These flavonoids contribute significantly to the plant's medicinal properties.

Flavonoids found in yarrow possess various health benefits and properties. They exhibit anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial activities. These compounds are known for their potential to aid in wound healing due to their anti-inflammatory properties. Yarrow's flavonoids have been associated with promoting digestion and reducing spasms in the gastrointestinal tract. Additionally, their antioxidant properties contribute to scavenging free radicals, potentially reducing oxidative stress-related damage. Moreover, flavonoids in yarrow have been researched for their potential in managing conditions such as gastrointestinal disorders, skin inflammations, and improving blood circulation.

However, excessive consumption or misuse of yarrow's flavonoids may lead to certain side effects. Some individuals may experience allergic reactions such as skin rashes, itching, or respiratory issues upon contact or ingestion of yarrow. High doses of flavonoids can potentially cause digestive disturbances like nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea. Furthermore, pregnant women are advised to avoid yarrow due to the possibility of stimulating uterine contractions, which could pose a risk of miscarriage. It's crucial to be cautious with the consumption of yarrow and its constituents to prevent adverse reactions.
