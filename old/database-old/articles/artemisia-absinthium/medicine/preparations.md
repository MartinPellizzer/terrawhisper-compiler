
Wormwood (Artemisia absinthium), renowned for its medicinal properties, offers diverse preparations beneficial for health purposes. One of the most popular forms is Wormwood tea, brewed by steeping 2 grams of dried leaves in 250 milliliters of hot water for 5-10 minutes, aiding digestion and stimulating appetite. Tinctures, made by macerating the herb in alcohol (1:5 ratio), are dosed at 0.5-1 milliliter thrice daily for addressing digestive issues. 

Capsules, containing finely powdered Wormwood (500 milligrams per capsule), are used to combat parasites; typical dosage ranges from 200-400 milligrams thrice daily for adults. The essential oil, derived through steam distillation, should be diluted (5 drops per 10 milliliters of carrier oil) for topical applications or used sparingly in aromatherapy due to its potency. Poultices, made by crushing fresh Wormwood leaves into a paste, are applied externally to alleviate joint pain and insect bites. Infused oil, prepared by steeping the herb in a carrier oil like olive or almond oil, is used for massages to improve circulation and relieve muscle tension. 

Extracts, highly concentrated formulations, are sparingly used to address digestive issues (dosage: 5-10 drops per day). Bath soaks, created by adding 50-100 grams of dried Wormwood leaves to a bath, are believed to alleviate skin conditions and soothe muscle aches. Compresses, made by soaking a cloth in a Wormwood infusion and applying it to the abdomen, offer relief from menstrual pain. Additionally, salves containing Wormwood are applied topically for minor cuts and bacterial issues, serving as a natural antiseptic. These diverse preparations demonstrate the versatility of Wormwood in addressing various health concerns across different cultures and traditions.
