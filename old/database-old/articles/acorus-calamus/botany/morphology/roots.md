The root system of Acorus calamus is fibrous, characterized by numerous thin roots that spread extensively in the soil. These roots are adventitious, originating from the plant's base rather than from a primary taproot. They predominantly exhibit dichotomous branching, where each root repeatedly divides into two equal branches, facilitating efficient nutrient and water uptake.

The root tips are acute, helping the plant penetrate the soil effectively. In terms of density, Acorus calamus typically has approximately 1,500 roots per square meter, indicating a substantial root presence in its habitat.

Root depth for Acorus calamus ranges from 30 to 60 centimeters (12 to 24 inches) below the soil surface. The roots themselves have a diameter of 2 to 4 millimeters (0.08 to 0.16 inches) and can reach lengths of up to 60 centimeters (24 inches). The color of these roots is typically creamy-white to pale brown, and they have a smooth texture.

Notably, Acorus calamus lacks root hairs and lenticels on its roots' surface. There are no root nodules present, and root modifications are non-specialized. In terms of adaptations, Acorus calamus exhibits hydrophytic traits, well-suited for its habitat in wetlands.

Roots of Acorus calamus undergo seasonal senescence, especially during colder months, to conserve energy and resources. These roots serve essential functions, primarily anchoring the plant in the soil, and efficiently absorbing nutrients and water.

Furthermore, the roots release allelopathic compounds, such as β-asarone, which can impact neighboring plants and microorganisms in their vicinity. In terms of symbiotic relationships, Acorus calamus has limited mycorrhizal associations, indicating a relatively independent nutrient acquisition strategy.

While these roots do not form nitrogen-fixing nodules, they may engage in mycorrhizal associations, enhancing their capacity to absorb nutrients from the soil.
