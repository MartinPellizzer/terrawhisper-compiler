Garlic is a species of Allium sativum with its origins tracing back thousands to millions years ago when it was first domesticated by ancient people along various regions in Central Asia and Eastern Europe. The word "garlick" has been used since the Old English times around 804 A.D, which eventually evolved into today's term 'Garlic'.

Garlic had deep significance with varying cultures throughout history where it played a role as food preservation agent to ward off illness and increase life expectancy in ancient civilizations like Egyptians who used garlic-filled bandages for healing purposes. It was also believed that the pungent odor could drive away evil spirits, thus acting not only therapeutically but symbolizing protection too; this belief extended even into supernatural legends found amongst Native American communities where it served as a crucial ritual herb and purifier in their religious practices while Greeks honored garlic by linking its use to healthier living due both internally for medicinal uses, externally with cosmetic improvements including shinning skin.

Garlic has been present within myths and legends that depict how it was a food gift from the gods; an emblem of strength as in Greek myth where Jason carried one while Heracles another legend stating his conquest over death itself by eating garlic before facing Hades, king of Underworld. In literature's great books - like Shakespeare’s Othello or Edgar Allan Poe’s The Cask Of Amontillado—they illustrate characters with heightened emotional reactions as if infused directly from the volatile substance themselves!

Garlic had diverse uses in folklore; it was used for its supposed healing properties and magical abilities. It could protect against witches or ill-wishers, deter malicious spirits away when placed on a grave of loved ones after they passed so these 'spirits' would move along without causing havoc elsewhere within the community – all this while being considered beneficial medicinally because many herbalists attributed their healing potions with garlic’s power.

Hence throughout human history, Garlic has been more than just a culinary ingredient; it signified protection against evil forces both seen & unseen as well serving varied utilitarian purposes such providing medicine for curing disease amongst societies across different cultures all around the world over time which makes garlic much deeper and significant than simple pungent food.
