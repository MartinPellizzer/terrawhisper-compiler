The root system of garlic is fibrous, consisting of adventitious roots. These roots are not the main source of support for the plant, and they emerge from the base of the bulb, forming a dense network. These roots are crucial for anchoring the plant and absorbing water and nutrients from the soil.

In terms of structure, garlic roots are tuberous, non-woody, and typically branched. The branching pattern is dichotomous, with frequent forks, creating an extensive root system. The tips of these roots are pointed and acute, allowing for easy penetration into the soil.

The density of garlic roots is relatively high, with approximately 14 cm³ of roots per cubic centimeter of soil. Despite their density, the roots tend to be shallow, typically extending to depths of 10-20 cm (4-8 inches) in the soil. The diameter of these roots is about 0.2 cm (0.08 inches), and their length can vary but may reach up to 50 cm (20 inches).

In terms of color and texture, garlic roots are usually whitish or cream-colored and have a smooth, fleshy texture. Unlike some plants, they lack pronounced surface features and root nodules, which are absent in this species.

Although garlic roots may have sparse, short root hairs, they do not form mycorrhizal associations. Instead, they play a critical role in absorbing water and nutrients, especially in the storage tissues of the bulb.

One remarkable feature of garlic roots is their adaptation to cold temperatures. These roots are known for their ability to endure harsh winter conditions, which is why garlic is often planted in the fall for spring harvest. However, once the growing season ends, garlic roots typically undergo senescence, withering as the plant's energy is diverted to bulb development and reproduction.
