Alpinia officinarum has a raceme inflorescence type, which is a spike-like arrangement of flowers along a central stem. These flowers possess a relatively simple structure with a tubular shape. Notably, they exhibit bilateral symmetry, meaning the flower can be divided into two equal halves in only one plane.

The flowers of Alpinia officinarum are typically epigynous, attached above the ovary. They are approximately 5 cm (1.97 inches) in length and 2 cm (0.79 inches) in width. These flowers are predominantly white with delicate pink hues, giving them an attractive aesthetic.

Upon touch, the flowers are smooth and glabrous, lacking hair or trichomes. Each flower comprises six floral parts, consisting of three sepals and three petals. The sepals are green and measure around 1 cm (0.39 inches) in length, while the petals are white with pink hues and are about 2 cm (0.79 inches) long. Together, the sepals and petals form the perianth, the protective outer covering of the flower.

The reproductive structures of these flowers include one fertile stamen and two sterile staminodes, along with a single pistil. The ovary is located beneath the other floral parts, making it an inferior ovary with three lobes. The style extends from the ovary and terminates with a lobed stigma at its tip.

Notably, Alpinia officinarum flowers do not have stipules, and their bracts are small and inconspicuous. The flowers are borne on a peduncle that varies in length, supporting the inflorescence, while each individual flower is attached to the inflorescence through a short pedicel.
