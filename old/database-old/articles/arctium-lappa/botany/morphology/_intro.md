Arctium lappa, commonly known as burdock, exhibits various distinctive morphological characteristics. Its roots form a taproot system with a dichotomous branching pattern, reaching up to 90 cm in length. The stems are herbaceous, not woody, and alternate in arrangement, with a rough, pubescent surface. They can vary in height from 50 to 200 cm. The leaves are broadly ovate, pubescent, and have coarsely serrate margins. They are attached to the stem via petioles and have pinnate venation.

Burdock's flowers are arranged in clusters at the tip of the stem, with tubular, bell-shaped, bisexual, and actinomorphic petals. The flowers are typically purple, pink, or white, and the plant has an inferior ovary. The fruits are small, dry achenes that are oval to oblong, attached within involucral bracts, and do not split open at maturity. These achenes are adapted for attachment to various surfaces for dispersal.

The seeds of Arctium lappa are hard, dicotyledonous, and ellipsoid in shape, measuring about 4-5 mm in length. They have a brown, impermeable seed coat, requiring scarification for germination. Each seed contains two cotyledons, a small embryo, and a distinct hilum marking the attachment point.
