Arctium lappa has small dry fruits, or achenes, that are typically oval to oblong in shape, measuring between 4 to 7 millimeters (approximately 0.16 to 0.28 inches) in length, 2 to 4 millimeters (roughly 0.08 to 0.16 inches) in width, and about 1 millimeter (approximately 0.04 inches) in thickness. When fully mature, they adopt a dark brown to black coloration.

One noteworthy feature of Arctium lappa is the arrangement of its fruits. They are found in spherical clusters, each containing multiple achenes. These achenes are attached within the receptacle of the plant, which is primarily made up of involucral bracts. The outer surface of these fruits is smooth and shiny, with no prominent hairs or spines. There are no distinct markings on the fruits, and they do not exhibit dehiscence, meaning they do not split open when they reach maturity.

The specific morphology of Arctium lappa's fruits plays a vital role in their dispersal and propagation. The achenes are well-suited for attachment to animal fur, clothing, or passing objects due to their small size and smooth surface. This facilitates their distribution and the establishment of new plants in different locations.
