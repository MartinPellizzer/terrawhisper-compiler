The etymology of "Sage" originates from Latin Salveo meaning 'to heal'. It represents its role as a traditional herb for healing purposes in various cultures across history and is also known by different names like garden sage, common sage or simply salvia officinalis based on regional differences. Today people still use the dried leaves of this plant to prepare aromatic infusions often called tea that has numerous uses extending beyond health benefits into areas including wellness rituals as a soothing aid during times when relaxation is desired after an eventful day's work or for enhancing concentration while studying complex subjects due its reputation as both cognitive stimulant and calming agent.

Cultural significance of Sage can be seen through different lenses over the years, such in ancient Greece where it was considered sacred to goddess Artemis - patroness of hunting, wild animals and nature; Romans revered sage for its medicinal powers but also associated with their own gods like Jupiter who is said to have bestowed special wisdom upon humans after chewing some fresh leaves. In medieval Europe, sages were often used in various religious rituals such as during baptisms while native North Americans recognized the plant's therapeutic potential when treating ailments ranging from respiratory issues through its smoke released by burning dried foliage - an ancient form of steam therapy they called 'smudge'.

As with any herb or botanical, Sages have also had their fair share in mythology and legends. One such story comes to us via the Greek Gods; Zeus was said he sent Pan across land as punishment for not participating when it came time divvying up tasks among deities - a role which included being appointed god of nature's beauty including herbs like Salvia officinalis where they became part sacred protectorate under his divine authority.

 For instance, Shakespeare mentioned it as an ingredient for one type "fair lady" to improve both health qualities; Samuel Taylor Coleridge used its imagery within some poems too - showing how deeply rooted this plant is culturally around words.

Historically in different parts of the world where herbs were believed useful tools beyond mere healing or aromatic purposes, Sage had also been incorporated into everyday folkloristic practices like enhancing fertility (through a potent infusion used during marriage rituals), repelling bad luck and even clearing air within houses by burning dried leaves at entryways as part of cleansing routines.
