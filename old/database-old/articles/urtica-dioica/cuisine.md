 Culinary uses of Nettle (Urtica dioica) are becoming popular due to its nutritional properties and versatile culinary applications, including soups, pesto sauce or blended into smoothies as part nutrition supplementation in diets rich with vitamins A & K.

 The unique flavor profile of Nettle is a subtle combination between earthy notes (almost reminiscent to spinach) and mild sweetness from its high Vitamin C content, resulting an appealing taste for both traditional savory dishes as well as dessert items when blended with fruits.

 Edible parts include the green soft young stems of nettles which can be eaten raw or cooked in various recipes like salads and stir-fries; while older leaves may require a cooking process to reduce their natural prickly hairs, making them easier for consumption (though these should not go untouched when handled).

 Culinary tips emphasize that it's better pick young nettles in early spring as they have milder flavors and are less likely to be bitter; use gloves or tongues while gathering your stems/leaves for handling protection, blanching before cooking can help reduce the plant’s naturally present acids if required taste preferences demand.

 Although Nettles offer numerous health benefits such as promoting joint pain relief and skin improvements when consumed internally they should be treated with caution due to potential side effects or toxicity issues linked primarily from external contact which may cause irritation; however, culinary usage has minimal risks if handled properly by following safe practices.
