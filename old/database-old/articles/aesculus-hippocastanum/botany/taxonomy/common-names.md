Aesculus hippocastanum has many common names, but "Horse Chestnut" is perhaps the most recognizable. Its name is derived from the large seeds it produces, encased in spiky hulls, which are referred to as "conkers" and have been used in traditional children's games.

In North America, it is also known as the "Buckeye," a term that can be applied to various trees within the Aesculus genus. While "Spanish Chestnut" is sometimes used, it's important to note that Aesculus hippocastanum is distinct from the true sweet chestnut (Castanea sativa), even though the names may be somewhat similar.

Its primary common name, "European Horse Chestnut," highlights its origin on the European continent, distinguishing it from other horse chestnut species. As a tree, it is frequently referred to as the "Common Horse Chestnut" due to its widespread presence in gardens and parks.

Some variants of Aesculus hippocastanum have variegated leaves, leading to the name "Variegated Horse Chestnut." The "White Chestnut" name pertains to the light-colored seeds revealed when they are peeled from their prickly covering.

In the United States, particularly in Ohio, a similar-looking tree in the Aesculus genus goes by the name "Ohio Buckeye." These diverse common names reflect the tree's recognition and adaptability to various regions across the world, adding to its botanical significance and appeal.
