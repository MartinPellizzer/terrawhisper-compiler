The horse chestnut tree, scientifically known as Aesculus hippocastanum, has a well-defined taproot with a conical shape that narrows as it extends downward. The young tree's taproot can reach a depth of up to 2.5 meters, while mature trees can extend it to depths of 4.5 meters. This taproot has lateral roots that spread outward in search of water and nutrients, covering an area up to 8 meters from the trunk.

The stems of the tree are herbaceous and become cylindrical, reaching up to 30 meters in height with a diameter ranging from 50 to 120 centimeters. They have a brownish-gray color, initially smooth but becoming rougher with age.

The leaves are palmately compound, consisting of 5 to 7 leaflets arranged in pairs opposite to each other. The leaflets are obovate to elliptical, with serrated margins and a deep glossy green upper surface. In autumn, they turn vibrant yellow or bronze.

Horse chestnut flowers are bisexual, with conical to bell-shaped structures, white or pale pink in color, and emit a sweet, slightly musky fragrance. The capsules that house the seeds are woody, dark brown, and inedible.

The seeds are ovoid or spherical, measuring about 2.5 centimeters in length and are dark brown in color. They are not consumed due to their bitter taste.
