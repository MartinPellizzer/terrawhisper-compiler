Bilberry (Vaccinium myrtillus), a small shrub native to Europe, thrives best under partial shade with slightly acidic soil that drains well and pH levels between 4-5 in humid environments where temperatures average around 60°F–80° F during summer.

To plant Bilberry plants, first prepare a nursery bed by tilling the area to break up hard clumps of earth while providing drainage; make rows and mark even spaces that should have adequate room for growth between each bush (around 1 foot distance); remove any weeds or other competing vegetation then water well. Once plant establishment occurs, thin out weak plants if necessary so strong ones can flourish furthermore in the same area without competition issues.

To care properly for Bilberry bushes regularly perform tasks such as proper fertilization to encourage robust growth and abundant flowering; mulching helps retain moisture around roots while preventing weeds that would compete with your plants - organic material like wood chips or compost work best in this case (about 1.5 inches thick); prune mature bilberry bushes annually, especially during winter dormancy period when the plant is less active thus easier to maintain structure and promote healthy growth; water these acid-loving shrubs regularly but avoid overwatering as it can lead to root rot issues which weaken or potentially kill your plants.

Harvest Bilberries in late summer once they are fully ripe with their signature bright blue color, usually around August - this delicate timing will allow for a high yield and prevent any damage during picking due to the fragile nature of these berries; it's essential not only pick them one by one carefully but also don’t touch other fruits while doing so which helps keep pest infested areas at bay as they would usually concentrate on unharvested, rotting ones.

Like most plants and trees, Bilberries can face specific diseases or insect attacks that may harm their growth such cases include leaf spot (another fungal infection), root rot caused by excess water/humidity build-up around the roots leading to plant decay - prevention measures like proper drainage of soils combined with adequate air ventilation throughout your garden would greatly help reduce these issues; in case an infestation already starts developing, prompt intervention such as pruning affected branches or applying suitable treatments immediately aids swift recovery and healthy growth.
