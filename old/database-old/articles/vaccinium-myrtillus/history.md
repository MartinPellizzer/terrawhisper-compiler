The etymology of "Bilberry" originates from combining two different terms - 'bil', which refers to a small berry or fruit resembling that produced by Bilberries and another term possibly related with Latin words such as ‘billus’ meaning little ball, perhaps hinting at the round shape commonly associated among these fruits. This unique name was eventually coined together creating "Bilberry". The botanical classification of this plant goes under genus 'Vaccinium' which is within Ericaceae family and species myrtillis or Vincinum Myrtitis in Latin, referring to the common European Bilberries that are widely found across Europe.

Culturally significant since ancient times for their rich nutrition including a range of vitamins like A &C as well antioxidants and other nutrients essentials required by human body; they have been used extensively in traditional medicine around world regions where these plants are prevalent such Europe, Asia Minor to America.

Bilberry mythology has various tales from different cultures highlighting the uniqueness or magical properties of this plant-related fruits – one popular European legend says that it was planted on earth by God himself when he saw a beautiful garden with bilberries trees growing there; another tale talks about how these berries were created out love between two gods, and so they became known as 'love fruit'.

Bilberry has been referenced in numerous literary works throughout history - from William Shakespeare’s plays to more contemporary authors like Beatrix Potter who featured them prominently into her children's books; poets used it metaphorically often alluding its symbolic power with love and romance while writers integrated their unique health benefits when discussing dieting or healthy living.

Folkloristic use goes far beyond just consuming as food, Bilberries have been historically valued for various purposes such being considered natural medicines - helping treat digestive issues like indigestion & constipation; used in traditional herbal remedies to cure ailments related heart problems and even eye health was attributed with their richness of nutrients which helped improve vision.
