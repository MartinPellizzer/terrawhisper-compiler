The etymology of "Milk Thistle" originates from its unique look which resembles cow thorns (Silybum marianum). It has been suggested that these features gave rise to a name related to lactation and animals, leading people in ancient times to associate it with dairy cows. Therefore 'milky' part of the term is due to its milky-colored bract or leaves while "thistle" refers back again towards prickly nature resembling thorns - a common quality often present throughout certain types within this plant family known as Asteraceae (Compositae).

Milk Thistles have had important roles in several cultural contexts over time, serving both medicinal and symbolic purposes for different societies across the globe. In ancient Greece specifically its use was thought to provide protection against poisonous substances or even cure ailments such as gout caused by an excess of urine accumulation (also known as Podagra). Romans also used it extensively with numerous treatments revolving around medical problems involving liver and digestion - including diseases like jaundice, indigestions due to overconsuming rich foods or even general fatigue symptoms were seen positively impacted by the usage of Milk Thistle.

Many myths surround this plant's origins which date back all forms life stages from its sprouting seeds up until it blooms into a flower with purple petals at maturity – these stories are often tied to legends that involve gods and goddesses associated closely related figures who possessed magical powers over nature, including agriculture where crops like Milk Thistle flourish wildly on. It was thought the presence or lack of thorns in leaves might have signified different messages depending upon their meaning according within context each tale carried across cultures through time immemorial

The literary references to this plant are quite diverse too - Shakespeare’s Hamlet refers metaphorically using phrases which describe Milk Thistle being 'prick'd by the spur of some discontented man', showing how it could act like a symbol for pain endured because unrequired desires had pricked people similarly during conflicts between lovers or family members. Additionally in British children book "The Water Babies" written around 1863, characters face challenges while navigating their way through water filled obstacle course; they use Milk Thistle as a natural remedy for healing wounds sustained from underwater dangers encountered along this treacherous journey - thus it’s been noted both medicinally and symbolically important role.

Folkloristic uses range widely too – folk medicine practitioners worldwide applied various parts of the plant including seeds, roots, or its edible flowers as treatments for ailments ranging from gastrointestinal problems to skin conditions such eczema due largely in part because it contains silymarin which possesses antioxidant properties helpful at reducing oxides that can cause irritation on human body surfaces. It was also believed by some cultures who utilized this plant could provide spiritual protection against negative energies or even ward off evil spirits – hence making Milk Thistle not just an important part of history but a symbolic one too, illustrating the depth and complexity in which plants have been intertwined into humanity’s understanding throughout time.
