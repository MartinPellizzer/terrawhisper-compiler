Flaxseed (Linum usitatissimum) has many health benefits, such as improving digestion by reducing symptoms of bloating and constipation; providing essential nutrients like omega-3 fatty acids that help maintain a healthy heart while lowering blood pressure levels; offering anti-inflammatory properties to alleviate pain from rheumatism or arthritis, eases nerve disorders such as multiple sclerosis and diabetic neuropathy. Flaxseed contains substances like lignans (phytochemicals), mucilage gums with water-retaining properties that make them excellent dietary fiber sources; these components help prevent cardiovascular diseases, promote better skin health by moisturizing it from within and may also protect against breast cancer. The plant's seeds are the most commonly used part for extracting flaxseed oil to use in cooking recipes due primarily owing its high content of omega-3 fats but whole ground seed can be consumed directly or added into foods such as muesli, smoothies and granolas along with healthy yogurts. Flour made from the seeds is another edible version that's often used for baking bread in countries where this ingredient has been a traditional food item since ancient times (as far back as 30th century BC). Medicinal preparations of flaxseed include infusions, decoctions and ointments.

The medicinally active constituents found within Fluxseeds are largely related to their rich content in phytochemicals such; Alpha-linolenic acid (an essential fatty acid), Lignans like enterodiol & seciosolariciresino, flavonoids and other antioxidants. These compounds offer various health benefits while playing critical roles as anti-inflammatory agents that can reduce swelling or pain associated with diseases mentioned above (arthritis et al.).

As a precautionary measure it should always be kept at the back of your mind that flaxseeds could interfere with certain medications such anticoagulants or anti-diabetic drugs, so proper medical consultation prior consuming any substantial amount is important specially if you're suffering from related health issues. Pregnant women and nursing mothers should also exercise caution when including it in their diet due to limited scientific research available on its effects during such critical life stages but generally speaking flaxseed usage hasn’t been found unsafe under normal circumstances
