Astragalus, with botanical name Astragalus membranaceus, belongs to the domain Eukarya, kingdom Plantae, phylum Tracheophyta, class Magnoliopsida, order Fabales, family Fabaceae, genus Astragalus, species membranaceus. Common names include Milk Vetch, Locoweed, and Goat's-thorn.

Variants of Astragalus membranaceus include A. membranaceus var. mongholicus and A. membranaceus var. sinicus, both with subtle differences in leaf and stem morphology. The former has narrower leaves while the latter has broader ones.

Geographically distributed across Asia, North America, and Europe, Astragalus can be found in various types of habitats such as grasslands, meadows, and rocky areas. It thrives in well-drained soils with full sun exposure.

Astragalus has a biennial life cycle, meaning it completes its life within two years. In the first year, plants form rosettes of leaves; during the second year, they produce flowers followed by seeds before dying off. The seeds germinate and start the process again, continuing the plant's lifecycle.

Astragalus is a deciduous perennial herb with a woody root and angular stems that can grow up to one meter tall. It features trifoliate leaves composed of three leaflets each measuring about 1-2 cm long. The flowers appear on racemes or spikes, typically purple or white in color, followed by pods containing several seeds.
