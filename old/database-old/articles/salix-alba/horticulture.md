White Willow (Salix alba), a species of willows native to Europe, has various growth requirements such that it needs partial sunlight for best performance; avoid placing this plant where there is too much direct sun exposure or complete shade as both can hamper its development and health. It prefers well-drained soils enriched with organic matter like compost but also tolerates dry areas in need of improvement, though irrigation may be necessary during periods without rainfall to ensure optimum growth conditions for this tree species that normally attains a height between 15 -30m when left unpruned.

White Willow is propagated through seeds or cuttings but preferably from the latter because they grow faster and have stronger roots due lower genomic variation compared to seedlings, which may require some additional time for adaptation before growing optimally in their new location with ideal spacing of approximately 8m apart between adjacent trees ensuring proper air circulation.

Harvesting White Willow can be done for various purposes such as timber, poles or fuelwood depending on age and availability. For instance young shoots are commonly harvested before the full springtime flush but they need to consider growth cycle stages so that it doesn't interfere with future development; also remember not removing too many branches at once otherwise can lead weakening effects upon tree structure which may affect its health long-term hence should only be done occasionally if needed.

White Willow is susceptible towards certain pests and diseases like aphids (tiny insects that feed on sap), leaf hoppers or mites infestations causing defoliation impacting growth efficiency along with increased risks of fungal infections such as Cercospora Leaf Blight, which can cause severe damage if not promptly treated using appropriate pesticides and/or chemical control measures.
