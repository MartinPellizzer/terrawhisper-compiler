The term 'clove,' originated from a Middle English word that referred to an aromatic flower bud commonly used for spice and medicinal purposes by many civilizations since ancient times in Europe and Asia (and eventually throughout Africa, as well). "Eugenia caryophyllata," the botanical name of cloves is derived directly through its genus Eugenius which means 'well-born' or ‘noble.’ As it pertains to Clove itself coming from Greek work khliṓphon (κλίωφον) that roughly translates into "nail shaped" because the buds look like small nails.

The historical importance of cloves can be traced back thousands of years, and its cultural significance is immense in many parts around the world where it has been used for various purposes such as spice or medicine but also to symbolize fertility due to their shape resembling an embryo wrapped up within a flower. The popularity behind Clove rose exponentially when ancient Chinese people started using them during funeral rites, which marked cloves with spiritual connotations and made it even more precious across various cultures worldwide who wanted these mysterious 'floating nails' for themselves too!

Legends about the origins of Cloves exist amongst a lot of different societies that revolve around divine beings like gods or nature spirits. In Indian folklore, as described by Jaya Singh in his book "Folk-Literature," there are many stories where clove flowers come to earth from heaven through cracks made when the thunderbolt missiles aimed at a demonic figure fell short of their mark but still managed to produce blossoms that eventually became known for having an exquisite aroma.

Literary references about Cloves can be found in several classics including works by Shakespeare, Jane Austen and even Mark Twain's Adventures Of Huckleberry Finn showcases the character using clove as a means to disguise himself during one part of his journey; also Edgar Allen Poe’s “The Purloined Letter” mentions how criminals often smear things they steal in small bottles containing various spices such like Cloves.

Lastly, folkloristic uses span everything from being used as a remedy for headaches and toothache to warding off evil spirits through fumigation or burning them at the entrances of houses – indicating clove’s versatility over time has been immense making it an integral part not just in culinary arts but also plays a key role in culture's belief systems.
