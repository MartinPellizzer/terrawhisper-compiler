Sage (Salvia officinalis) has numerous applications for humans – both in terms of health benefits as well as culinary delights. This versatile herbaceous perennial belongs to a family called Lamiaceae - closely associated with other aromatic plants such as rosemary and thyme. In its natural habitats, it grows wildly on dry hillsides or rocky areas where the soil is generally poor in nutrients but well-drained; this adaptability allows cultivating even under challenging conditions.

Medicinally speaking, sage has been used for centuries to treat various ailments like indigestion, bronchitis and even as an antidote against snake bites due its anti-inflammatory properties - though more scientific studies are required before suggesting precise usage guidelines today based on modern medicine standards. In terms of cuisine preferences it can bring added fragrance to various dishes including stuffings for meats, sauces, eggs or pastries; the leaves also make excellent tea which is known not only due its delicious flavor but said promote mental clarity and memory retention too!

Horticulturally speaking Sage plants are both highly valued as an attractive garden feature. They have a charming natural beauty with their silvery green foliages that give them distinctiveness in flowerbeds or borders, while they can easily flourish among various companion plant species which share similar growing conditions such as Geranium sylvaticum (wild geranium) and Lavandula angustifolia - creating a harmonious blend of textures.

Historically speaking the cultivation traces back to ancient Greece where it is used extensively in traditional medicines, religious rituals or even culinary preparations dating as far back at least 20 centuries ago! Sage was highly esteemed amongst herbalists during Middle Ages for its various therapeutic properties and also played a significant role within spiritual ceremonies particularly related to wisdom & prophecy.
