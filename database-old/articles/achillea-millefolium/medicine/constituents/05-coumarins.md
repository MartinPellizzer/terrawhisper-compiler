
Yarrow's coumarins are natural organic compounds found in the Yarrow plant (Achillea millefolium), typically in concentrations ranging from 0.01% to 1.5% of the plant's dry weight. Coumarins are a class of phytochemicals known for their aromatic properties and diverse biological activities. In Yarrow, the concentration of coumarins can vary based on factors such as the plant's geographic location, growing conditions, and the specific species or variety of Yarrow being examined. Studies have identified various coumarin derivatives in Yarrow, including scopoletin, umbelliferone, and others, contributing to its therapeutic potential.

The coumarins present in Yarrow exhibit several health benefits and properties. They possess anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, which contribute to their traditional use in promoting wound healing, reducing inflammation, and managing various skin conditions like eczema and psoriasis. Additionally, these compounds have shown potential in aiding digestive issues by stimulating bile secretion and reducing spasms in the gastrointestinal tract. Furthermore, coumarins in Yarrow have been explored for their potential in managing pain and supporting cardiovascular health due to their blood-thinning properties, which may assist in improving circulation and reducing the risk of blood clots.

However, excessive consumption or misuse of Yarrow's coumarins can lead to certain side effects. High doses of coumarins may have anticoagulant effects, potentially leading to increased bleeding tendencies, especially when combined with blood-thinning medications. Furthermore, some individuals may experience allergic reactions or skin sensitivities upon contact with Yarrow, particularly if they have existing sensitivities to plants in the Asteraceae family. Moreover, prolonged or excessive use of Yarrow containing coumarins may result in digestive disturbances like nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea in some individuals.
