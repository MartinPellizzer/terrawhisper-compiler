The origin of "Sweet Annie" comes from combining two words - 'sugar' and 'annie'. It is believed that sugar refers to its sweet smell while annie was a reference or nickname for Artemisia absinthium, another species within the genus named after Goddess Diana (Artemis in Greek mythology). The specific naming of Sweet Annie comes from combining these two components together creating an alternative name and identity.

Throughout history, various cultures have used this plant as a source for medicinal purposes or incorporated it into rituals based on its mystical associations related to death goddesses like Hecate in ancient Greek mythology - known sometimes by the title "Queen of Witches". The association with such powerful deities imbued Sweet Annie symbolism that has transcended time. For instance, Native American traditions incorporated this herbal remedy as an all-purpose medication for various illnesses while other cultures regarded it to have protective properties against evil spirits and witchcraft during their rituals of divination

The myths surrounding the plant are rich in history including being associated with magical healers who were said to possess supernatural abilities, which often included herbalism. In an Old Scottish tale involving Robert Kirk or Sticksman (a man known for his ability as a healer), Sweet Annie was supposedly responsible when people transformed into birds at night - its presence allowed them this metamorphosis while warding off evil spirits lurking during their flights, and the herb helped maintain these mystical transformations.

Literary works mention Artemisia annua in various forms like an allusion to 'herbs' or even a specific reference; most notably by William Shakespeare who uses it figuratively as one of Ophelias favorite flowers symbolising despair and madness that grow along the brookside. Additionally, many novels incorporate herbal themes featuring medicinal plants such as Sweet Annie in stories with witchcraft elements - showcasing their mystical power both practically within potions or rituals but also metaphorically reflecting a deeper connection to ancient magic still relevant today

Folklore of different cultures have long been using the plant for various purposes. For instance, it was traditionally used as an insect repellent by placing dried plants near doorways and windows in households to keep away unwanted pests like mosquitoes without having a stifling effect on airflow or scent - making Sweet Annie essential home item providing relief from biting annoyances during warm months.
