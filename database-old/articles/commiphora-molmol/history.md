The etymology of "Myrrh" originates from a Greek term myrra or Myron derived through Latin and English adaptations as 'myrrhe'. The common Middle Eastern shrub Commiphora molmol is the botanical name for this resin-yielding tree. It has been widely known in various religious scriptures, literature writings that talk about healing remedies or even medicinal substances mentioned over years to have a profound impact on society's psyche and wellbeing historically.

The cultural significance of Myrrh is vastly spread throughout several parts for civilizations across the globe as it was highly valued in ancient times, particularly during religious events which still continue today – primarily Christian burial ceremonies where myrrhis used to anoint dead bodies with its strong and complex scented oil. This aromatic tree also holds a prominent place within rituals like funerals or even coronations that have long-lasting cultural influences across many societies, especially in the Middle East due to their close proximity of this plant’s origins from where it derives therapeutic value dating back thousands years ago when medical systems were not as advanced yet.

Mythological legends surrounding myrrh are equally rich and vivid like they play key characters into them involving epic journeys, quest for immortality or healing powers associated with deities found mainly throughout ancient Greek & Roman writings including works such The Golden Ass by Apuleius featuring tales on Isis using resin of this plant as part her magical potions to restore life back onto dead bodies. Another notable example is from Homer's epic poems 'The Iliad', which includes Myrrh playing significant roles throughout different narratives involving gods who could control nature itself through its powers embedded within each individual tree bark that were believed powerful enough influence change events during those ancient times where human knowledge about medicinal herbs weren’t widespread as it is today.

Literary references in works like The Bible and Greek literature highlight prominently the use of Myrrh due to various symbolic meanings attached such a profound balsam with potent healing properties, often used not just religiously or culturally but also medically for treating wounds that needed immediate attention during warfare circumstances given lack proper medical technology was around at those times. For example it's mentioned several occasions in The Old Testament as an ingredient when creating Holy Anointing Oil which would be utilized by priests performing rituals related to the priesthood’ religious life whilst Greek literary characters such Heracles and Odysseus used Myrrh essential oils either therapeutically or for spiritual purposes, revealing its widespread use in literature across different cultures over centuries.

Finally folkloristic usage of myrrg continues today through various applications ranging from alternative medicines as a remedy against coughs & colds to natural healing concoctions mixed with other herbs and spices making it versatile component within many home-remedies popular in the Eastern part due largely their deep historical roots rooted not only religion but also deeply woven into social structures reflecting humanities age old belief system based on spirituality, health & wellbeing.
