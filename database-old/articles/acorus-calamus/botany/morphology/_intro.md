Acorus calamus, commonly known as sweet flag, possesses a fibrous root system with numerous thin adventitious roots that exhibit dichotomous branching. These roots are creamy-white to pale brown, lack root hairs and lenticels, and have no specialized modifications. They adapt well to wetland habitats and undergo seasonal senescence.

The herbaceous stems of Acorus calamus are cylindrical and can grow up to 1.5 meters in height, with whorled arrangement and nodes along their length. The rhizomes, which grow underground, are horizontally arranged, tuberous to cylindrical, and have varying internode lengths.

The leaves are linear and lanceolate, green with a waxy sheen, and evergreen. They lack petioles, are sessile, and have entire margins. The flowers are hermaphrodite, unisexual, and have pale yellow-green tubular structures with six tepals. Male flowers contain six stamens, while female flowers have a single pistil with a superior ovary. Green bracts enclose the flowers.

The fruit of Acorus calamus is a small, oblong berry with a smooth, shiny surface, transitioning from green to brownish-green as it matures. Fruits grow in clusters throughout the flowering stem and have short pedicels.

The seeds are solitary, endospermic, and possess a hard, brown outer coat that is impermeable to water. They have a linear hilum and a centrally located straight embryo within the endosperm, ensuring successful development and germination.
