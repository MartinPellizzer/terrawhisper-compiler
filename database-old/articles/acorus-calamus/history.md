The etymology of "Sweet flag" originates from its botanical name Acorus calamus which is derived partly based on Latin words – ‘acorus’ for plant itself and 'calumous', the old form or a misspelling that leads to 'Calamus'. In Greek, the latter was translated as "κάλαμος" (kálamos) meaning reed. It has been speculated further by botanists how this name might have evolved over time with its usage of two important languages used in ancient times for communication - Latin and Ancient Greece's vernacular, Greek language which is the root behind several common names given to it today such as "Calamus" or 'Sweet Flag'.

Cultural significance plays a major role from ages across countries where Acorus calamus have flourished. Its association with purity has seen extensive use in religious ceremonies, particularly during rituals like marriage rites and other important occasions that require ritual bathing for both personal as well community cleanliness standards maintained by ancient practices following customary beliefs or cultural traditions handed down generations which further elevates its importance to the society of then era.

There are several myths surrounding Acorus calamus, particularly in European lore where it has been connected with various deities and legendary beings including water spirits such as Naiads from Greek Mythology who were known for guarding all that lives near spring or river waters like these sweet-smelling plants. Another popular one tells the story of how this plant came to be named "Sweet Flag" - it was believed a goddess, Venus used Acori's rhizomes (roots) which provided shelter when she bathed; her love interest Adonis found and admired them so much that he bestowed flowers made out the aromatic roots onto these watery beauties.

The sweet flag has been referenced in many notable literary works, such as Homer's Odyssey (Epic poem), where it is mentioned while recounting a visit to King Alcinous’ island and Calypso on her 'Sweet Isle'. It was used also for the production of perfumes by ancient Egyptians who saw sweet fragrances emanating from these reeds. Shakespeare too, in his play “Pericles”, mentions it while discussing potential treatment plans which further proves its significance during those times and among famous figures alike as an integral herbal medicine known throughout different eras for treating various ailments like indigestion or even bad breaths because of their pleasant aroma.

Sweet Flag was commonly used in several homes back then, not just medicinally but also due to its pleasing fragrance and other folklores that associated it with protection from evil spirits hence were often placed around the house as well for warding off ill omens or even bringing good luck into lives. Its usage went beyond personal needs too; people believed in using these plants during times of mourning rituals so they could find solace by planting them along funeral paths – signifying hope and comfort amidst sadness - showing just how versatile, its presence has been both throughout history as well cultural beliefs & folklore.
