The etymology of "St John Wort" originates from its Latin name Hypericum perforatum and has a history dating back to ancient Greece where it was commonly known as 'Kinos', an alternative for the herb used in treating various medical conditions. Throughout medieval times, this plant gained popularity due to its numerous medicinal uses by European monks who associated it with St John because flowering occurred during their annual celebration of his birth on June 24th, thus earning a common nickname as "St-John's wort".

The cultural significance and prevalent use in Europe over centuries have led to countless traditions surrounding this herb. One such tradition involves hanging bundles containing its flowers at windows or doorways during the holiday season for protection against witchcraft (specifically, it was thought that St John Worts fragrance might ward off evil spirits). Another common practice included using these flowering plants as part of midsummer festivals held across many European countries.

Many legends exist associated with this plant and its healing properties; one such tale narrates how Hippocrates, the father of medicine in ancient Greece was believed to use a salve made from Hypericum perforatums extract as an effective treatment for various skin conditions including rashes or wounds. While stories also suggest that it played some role during Christ's crucifixion when he used its leaves alongside nails prior his death on Calvary hill, this has been deemed unreliable due lack historical evidence supporting these claims.

Hypericum perforatums prominent presence in literature dates back to the 15th century where it appears prominently as a medicinal herb frequently prescribed by renowned doctors across England such Ralph Colwell and William Bullet, both known for their extensive knowledge on traditional remedies based upon ancient medical practices. Moreover notable mention includes English author Charles Dickens who incorporated this plant into his literary work with references to its use in treating melancholy or depression-like symptoms - which today remains one of the most common modern uses attributed towards St Johns Wort herbal extract.

With extensive traditional applications including treatment for ailments such as anxiety, insomnia and digestive issues due largely based upon trial and error methods over centuries; it's easy to understand why many European cultures have integrated Hypericum perforatums into their daily lives either through homeopathy practices or direct consumption of its flowers & leaves. These folkloristic uses serve as an indisputable testament not only towards the plant’s resilient evolution, but also demonstrate our ancestors keen observation for alternative healing methods that still resonate with us today
