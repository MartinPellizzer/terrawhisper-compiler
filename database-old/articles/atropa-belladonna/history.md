The plant commonly known as Deadly Nightshade or Atropa belladonna has a long and rich history that involves cultural implications ranging from its name to medicinal use in various cultures throughout time. Its Latin roots are evident with the word "Atropos" which refers both a fate in Greek mythology who cuts off life's thread as well as an ancient goddess associated witchcraft – perhaps alluding toward how this plant has been viewed for centuries, carrying potential poison and deadly effects if not handled properly or used wisely.

Deadly Nightshade is mentioned often throughout history due to its cultural significance in societies like those of Europe where it was a crucial resource linked with various rituals such as beauty treatments (hence the term "belladonna," referring figuratively for black eyed beauties and literally meaning beautiful lady from Venetian dialect). The plant has been employed also among medical practices to treat eye issues including inflammation or irritations.

In Greek mythology, it was thought that Atropos herself utilized Deadly Nightshade alongside Hemlock when she ended the lives of unfortunate individuals according fate’s decree; this belief further reinforced its sinister association as 'bane'. Many tales have featured use (or misuse)ofthis particular plant in their narratives, suchas Shakespeare'sinvolvement through Lady MacBeth who used an infusion to wash her hands. It has also been found references throughout classic works of literature including Charles Dickens', Bleak House where it is mentioned as a poisonous ingredient which helped lead the murder plot; Edgar Allan Poe’s ‘The Mystery Unravelled, where Atropa belladonna poisons people’.

Folklore has also included several references of this plant in its use by both witches and herbalists alike. It'ssaid that witch hunts often involved the search for those using plants such as Deadly Nightshade within their rituals or potions- further solidifying a mystic association with it over time due to superstition regarding what was deemed 'dark magic'. Furthermore, throughout many cultures around Europe and parts of Asia this plant has been used in various remedies by healers for its medicinal benefits that include treating everything from insect stings (as an antidote)to stomach ailments.

Thus it can be seen how Deadly Nightshade holds historical significance with far-reaching cultural, mythological and literary implications which have transcended through centuries despite potential risks associated if not handled properly or used carefully according its natural properties as both poisonous yet medicinal plant at different levels within each context mentioned above.
