Thyme with botanical name 'Thymus vulgaris', belongs to domain Eukarya; kingdom Plantae (plants); phylum Spermatophyta or Magnoliophytanae, which includes seed plants and flowering ones respectively ; class Dicotyledonaes due its two embryonic leaves in each germinating cotyledon. It is part of the order Labiatae that belongs to a group Lamiaceae (mint family). Thyme fits within this taxonomy as it shares common features with species having similar flower, growth or fragrance attributes such as basil and lavender, mints like sage among others forming distinctive aromatic groups.

There are variants in thymes; these may have different appearances because of changes over generations - natural selection processes at play within their environment which can lead to morphological differences from one another but they all belong under the same genus Thimus and species vulgaris, with minor distinctions based on habit or foliage.

Morphology refers mainly to physical characteristics that give identity; in thyme these include tiny leaf blades arranged opposite each other forming flat leaves of various sizes depending upon cultivar type (some bigger sized like Zwiebelzwiebach due for kitchen use), flower heads formed from bracteoles or clusters grouping upwards and a sturdy, branchy wooded stem system which may root at nodes. Thymus vulgaris also has aromatic qualities - the herbal part comes loaded with oils producing pleasant scent when crushed that is used for cooking seasonings like marinades; these scented substances give thyme its 'buzz' on menues globally due their healthful properties too which may boost immune system, digestion and circulation.

Thymus vulgaris (thyme) has a wide geographic distribution ranging from Eurasia to parts of North Africa spanning over Europe into the Mediterranean regions with varying climate types that support its growth in different environments - it can range for instance across semi arid savanna-like terrain as well survive more temperate climates where temperatures vary through winter seasons, and thymes tend prefer soils richer ones; they are often used along garden pathways or landscaped areas.
