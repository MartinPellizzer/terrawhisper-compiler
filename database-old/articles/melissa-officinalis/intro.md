Lemon Balm (Melissa Officinallis), also known as Sweet Melissa is a species belonging to the mint family – Labiatae/Lamiaceae or commonly called Nepetoidae due its nepetifolious leaves which are very aromatic and possess pleasant, lemongrass scent. This plant has been used not just for cooking purposes but additionally medicinally in numerous countries throughout history with several different traditional practices applied worldwide whereby it is considered a valuable herb across multiple cultural domains despite the varying beliefs of its exact effectiveness as an allopathic remedy.

Botanically, Lemon Balm grows to around 60 centimeters tall and has small white flowers that bloom between July until October every year depending on growing conditions; it is commonly grown in gardens for ornamental purposes because they provide a sweet fragrance similar to lemon which can be utilized aesthetcally when used as an added accent.

Historically, the Lemon Balm has been traced back centuries ago where its use was documented by Greek physician Dioscorides around 70 CE followed shortly thereafter with Hippocrates mentioning it too for treating digestive issues among other ailments during his lifetime (460-385 BCE). Its uses extended to Roman Emporer Nero who would reportedly eat lemon balm leaves before performing on stage so he could sing better due its natural nervine calming effect. In the Middle Ages, it was used as an air purifier by hanging bunches of dried plants over doorways whilst people slept at night or sat close during daytime activities; this tradition carried into modern times when Europeans still incorporate Lemon Balm in scent blends designed to promote relaxation and enhance moods. Additionally within some religions such the Buddhist faith, it is believed that placing freshly cut lemon balm branches upon an altar can ward off negative energy whilst attracting positive vibrations which are then harnessed by believers during their prayers or meditations sessions held at sacred sites where this particular plant grows abundantly.
