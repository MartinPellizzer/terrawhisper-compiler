The origin of 'Hawthorn,' commonly known as Crataegus oxyacantha is traced back to various countries like Asia and Europe where it thrived abundantly in their native lands before settling on the British Isles through human intervention or natural spread. Etymology points at its Latin name being a blend of Greek 'kratos' (power) with either an unknown term used as preposition, giving us Crataegus signifying bush; while oxyacantha indicates both sharp and spiny - which are the characteristic features one can find in their flowers or fruits. The word Hawthorn is believed to have evolved from Old English hæg-þornegin of early Britain where it was also called whitethorne, referring its white blossoms during springtime; whereas Middle Low German had a similar term - haagdörne which means hedge thorn.

Hawthorn has played vital roles in various cultures and communities through the ages for being known not just as medicine but it is also believed to have magical properties, making people rely on its healing powers while seeking guidance during significant life events or important transitions that were marked by rituals using leaves, bark & berries. For instance - Druids used hawthorn branches due its natural ability of warding off evil spirits and for creating mystical protective barriers; Celts considered it as a symbolic tree linked with fairy realms where they would celebrate Beltane or the coming spring festival around this time, while in ancient Greece certain traditions existed link Hawhorn to Apollo because he was believed responsible its cultivation.

The myths and legends surrounding hawthorn often speak of it as a magical shrub enveloped with layers mystic meanings that are interwoven throughout European tradition whether linked by superstition, divinity or folk tales such stories like "Thomas the Rhymer" who plucked thorm fruit & fell into trance allowing him to prophesy future events.

Hawthorn is not just a part of myths and legends but also finds its way in literary works from Shakespeare's plays (A Midsummers Night Dream) where it symbolizes love, romance or enchantment; to English Romantic poets like Wordsworth who describes these flowering shrub as 'the wild apple-trees', highlighting their beauty and charm amidst natural landscapes.

Folkloristic uses of hawthorn included practices related not just for healing purposes but also believed it had certain powers which would influence one's destiny or relationship if brought to particular parts were said rituals involved using leaves, bark & berries from this shrub either hung on doorways as protection against evil spirits or thrown into well waters seeking blessings during pregnancy among various cultural customs and traditions.
