The etymology of "Pelargonium" originates from ancient Greek roots where 'pelasgus', meaning foreigner or outsider was combined with 'argeoion' for geranium flower, resulting in the name Pelagronioum and later evolving into its modern-day form - Pelargonium. The term initially appeared across early classical texts from as far back to ancient Greek philosophers like Aristotle who used it when referring to a type of wild plant native mostly found along shores, where waves crash against the land creating salty air which this species thrives in.

Through cultural history and evolution around many continents over centuries Pelargonium has become an integral part both physically - for its uses as medicinal plants- but also symbolically; it's said to have played a role during Roman times where brides often gifted geranum seeds with their dowry representing hope, love & fertility which became traditions later spread around Europe.

Legend has it that Pelargonium grew wild in the garden of Hera – wife and sister-goddess queen Olympia's who also bore other gods such as Zeus or Apollo (fathered by her husband). As legend goes, a serpentine sea god sent to destroy this sacred place failed miserably when he tried eating one leaf from each plant species found there. The leaves were so toxic for him that they killed the mythical beast which later resulted in an abundance of these plants throughout Greece making it into widespread cultivation over time because every successive generation believed such folklore and took care to preserve them accordingly out respect or fear towards this storyline’s origins.

Pelargonium is one major reference character, within numerous literature pieces that explore mythological landscapes where characters use these florid beauties as symbolic representations of love & fertility like in the Roman period when brides often gifted Geranum seeds along with their dowry indicating hope and happiness. Additionally its mentioned several times on William Shakespeare’s works such plays including “As You Like It,” "A Midsummer Night's Dream," among others where Pelargonium is portrayed as symbols embodying emotions associated closely to these productions hence enricher literacy value furthermore contributing significantly towards shaping how culture saw, related or felt about it throughout the years.

From a folkloric point of view - pelagronioum has been used for centuries in various aspects relating everything from culinary use including using parts boiled down into soups that were believed helpful curing common cold symptoms to applying certain stems leaves & roots topically on skin issues like acne scars, cuts etc., thereby making it an important aspect across history reflective both practical and symbolic purposes.
