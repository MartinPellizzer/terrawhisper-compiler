Inflorescence Type:
Achillea millefolium exhibits a characteristic type of inflorescence known as a compound corymb. A corymb is a flat-topped or slightly convex cluster of flowers in which the lower pedicels (flower stalks) are longer than the upper ones, resulting in a nearly even, umbrella-like arrangement.

Floret Arrangement:
The inflorescence of Achillea millefolium consists of numerous small individual florets, each arranged in densely packed clusters. These florets are organized in a spiral pattern along the flat-topped corymb, with an average of 10 to 20 florets in each cluster.

Floret Characteristics:

Corolla: Each floret possesses a tubular corolla with five petal-like lobes, typically measuring around 2-4 millimeters in length. The corolla color can vary, with shades of white, pink, or yellow being the most common.

Calyx: Surrounding the base of the corolla, there is a cup-shaped calyx composed of five greenish or brownish bracts known as phyllaries.

Androecium: Achillea millefolium florets are perfect, meaning they contain both male and female reproductive structures. They have five stamens (male organs) fused to the corolla tube, with anthers bearing pollen sacs.

Gynoecium: The female reproductive part, the gynoecium, consists of a single superior ovary with a single style and bifurcated stigma.

Size and Measurements:

The diameter of a mature Achillea millefolium inflorescence can range from 3 to 8 centimeters.
Individual florets measure approximately 3 to 4 millimeters in diameter.
Phyllaries are usually 2-3 millimeters long and are arranged in several overlapping rows.
Inflorescence Development:

Achillea millefolium typically starts flowering in late spring to early summer, and the inflorescence continues to develop over several weeks.
The formation of florets is acropetal, with the youngest florets located at the center of the inflorescence and the older ones toward the periphery.



General Inflorescence Structure:
The inflorescence of Achillea millefolium is classified as a compound corymb, a type of determinate inflorescence where the central or terminal flower blooms first, followed by the lateral flowers. This inflorescence structure is characterized by a flat-topped or slightly convex arrangement of flowers, with the central flower opening before the peripheral ones. The compound corymb inflorescence of A. millefolium is a striking example of this type, displaying an average diameter of approximately 5 to 10 centimeters.

Flower Number and Arrangement:
Achillea millefolium is known for its profusion of small, hermaphroditic flowers. Within a single inflorescence, one can observe an impressive cluster of anywhere from 20 to 200 individual flowers, depending on factors such as plant age, environmental conditions, and genetic variation. These flowers are densely packed together in a spiral arrangement, creating a visually captivating display. The flowers exhibit bilateral symmetry and measure approximately 3 to 4 millimeters in diameter.

Individual Flower Structure:
Each individual flower of A. millefolium is composed of five distinct floral organs:

Ray Florets: Towards the periphery of the inflorescence, you'll find numerous ray florets, which are typically white or pinkish in color. These ray florets possess a tongue-like shape and are sterile, serving primarily as attractants for pollinators.

Disc Florets: At the center of the inflorescence, you'll encounter the fertile disc florets, which are usually yellow. These disc florets are hermaphroditic, containing both male (stamens) and female (pistil) reproductive structures.

Corolla: The corolla of A. millefolium's flowers is tubular in shape, composed of five fused petals. In the case of disc florets, the corolla is actinomorphic (radially symmetric), while ray florets display zygomorphism (bilateral symmetry).

Stamens: Each disc floret contains five stamens fused to the corolla tube. These stamens produce pollen, which is an essential component of the plant's reproductive strategy.

Pistil: The central pistil of the disc florets is comprised of two fused carpels, each containing a single ovule. This structural arrangement facilitates successful fertilization and seed production.

Inflorescence Maturation and Fertilization:
Achillea millefolium's inflorescence undergoes sequential flowering, with the central disc florets blooming first and subsequently maturing, followed by the peripheral ray florets. This temporal separation of flower maturation helps optimize pollination and seed set.





Number of Florets: A single Achillea millefolium inflorescence can contain anywhere from 20 to 200 individual florets, with the number varying based on environmental factors, genetic traits, and the age of the plant. This wide range highlights the flexibility of the plant to adapt to different conditions.

Floret Arrangement: These florets are arranged in concentric rings around a central disk, creating a visually striking pattern. The outermost ring consists of ray florets, which are sterile and serve to attract pollinators with their showy white or pink petals. The inner disk florets are hermaphroditic, containing both male and female reproductive organs.

Floret Anatomy: The ray florets typically have five petal-like ligules, while the disk florets have five fused petals forming a tubular structure. Each floret possesses a pistil with a bifurcated stigma and five stamens fused to the corolla tube. The stamens release pollen to fertilize the stigma, facilitating the plant's reproductive process.

Inflorescence Size: The size of the Achillea millefolium inflorescence can vary considerably, with a typical diameter ranging from 2 to 6 centimeters. This size variation allows the plant to allocate resources efficiently based on environmental conditions and available nutrients.

Height of Inflorescence: The inflorescence is borne on long, slender stems, known as peduncles, which can reach heights of 30 to 90 centimeters. This height variation can influence the accessibility of the inflorescence to different pollinators, optimizing cross-pollination opportunities.
