Sweet flag: A perennial plant known for its fragrant, sword-shaped leaves and rhizomatous growth. It has a sweet and earthy aroma.

Calamus: This name is often used interchangeably with Acorus calamus. It refers to a tall, aquatic plant with aromatic rhizomes.

Flagroot: A reference to the root system of the Acorus calamus plant, which is a source of its medicinal and aromatic properties.

Beewort: Another name for Acorus calamus, it may suggest the attraction of bees to the plant's flowers.

Bitter pepper root: Acorus calamus has a slightly bitter taste, and the "pepper root" may refer to its pungent flavor.

Myrtle flag: The plant's fragrance and appearance are reminiscent of myrtle, and "flag" is a common term for such plants.

Rat root: This name may suggest the use of Acorus calamus as a rodent repellent, as the scent is disliked by some rodents.

Cinnamon sedge: Acorus calamus has a spicy aroma similar to cinnamon, and "sedge" denotes its classification as a sedge plant.

Sweet sedge: Refers to the plant's pleasant, sweet scent, and its membership in the sedge family.

Grassleaf sweet flag: Describes the plant's grass-like leaves and sweet fragrance, combining multiple characteristics of Acorus calamus.










Common yarrow: A widely recognized herb with clusters of small white or pink flowers and feathery leaves, often used in traditional medicine.

Milfoil: Another name for yarrow, describing its finely divided leaves.

Thousand-leaf: Referring to the plant's highly divided and numerous leaves.

Soldier's woundwort: Historically used to treat wounds on the battlefield, as its scientific name "millefolium" means "thousand leaves" in Latin.

Nosebleed plant: Called so due to its traditional use to help stop nosebleeds, as it can constrict blood vessels.

Old man's pepper: A nickname possibly derived from its spicy aroma, reminiscent of pepper.

Staunchweed: Named for its traditional use to staunch or stop bleeding.

Sanguinary: Derived from "sanguis," meaning blood, highlighting its historical use in treating bleeding wounds.

Western yarrow: A regional variation of the common name yarrow, indicating its occurrence in the western parts of its native range.

Knight's milfoil: Possibly named after knights who might have used it for treating wounds on the battlefield, in line with the name "soldier's woundwort."
