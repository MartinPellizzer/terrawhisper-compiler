10 best herbal teas for upset stomach

Peppermint tea is a classic choice, its cooling properties easing digestive discomfort and reducing nausea. Ginger tea is another popular option, its natural anti-inflammatory properties helping to reduce inflammation and alleviate cramps. Meanwhile, chamomile tea provides a gentle, calming effect on the digestive system, promoting relaxation and reducing anxiety.

Licorice root tea is also effective, its sweet flavor and anti-inflammatory properties working to soothe stomach irritation and promote healing.

1. Chamomile

Chamomile tea contains anti-inflammatory and antispasmodic properties, which soothe the stomach lining and reduce inflammation.

The calming effects of chamomile tea's volatile oils target the smooth muscle of the gastrointestinal tract, helping to relax stomach muscles and alleviate discomfort. Furthermore, chamomile's daisy-like flowers possess flavonoids that stimulate the production of gastric juices, promoting healthy digestion and aiding in the prevention of acid reflux. Chamomile tea's mild sedative properties can also help reduce stress and anxiety, indirectly contributing to overall digestive well-being by diminishing the negative impacts of these factors on the gastrointestinal system.

Lastly, chamomile tea's tannins act as astringents, helping to protect the mucous membrane of the stomach and intestines, and reducing the risk of irritation and inflammation.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in chamomile tea that help with upset stomach.

  • Flowers: Chamomile flowers are used to make medicinal tea for upset stomach due to their calming properties, which can help reduce inflammation and relax the muscles of the digestive tract.
  • Leaves: Chamomile leaves have anti-spasmodic and anti-inflammatory properties that can soothe the digestive system, providing relief for upset stomach.
  • Seeds: Chamomile seeds contain therapeutic compounds that can help alleviate symptoms of upset stomach, including gas, bloating, and indigestion.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in chamomile tea that aid with upset stomach.

  • Apigenin: A flavonoid that has anti-spasmodic properties, helping to reduce smooth muscle spasms in the stomach.
  • Bisabolol: A terpene that has anti-inflammatory properties, helping to decrease inflammation in the stomach lining.
  • Chamazulene: A chemical compound that has anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, helping to protect the stomach lining from damage.
  • Matricin: A precursor to chamazulene, matricin also has anti-inflammatory properties, aiding in the relief of stomach inflammation.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic chamomile tea for upset stomach.

  1. Boil 8 ounces of water in a teapot or kettle for 3-5 minutes.
  2. Add 1-2 teaspoons of dried chamomile flowers to a teapot or infuser.
  3. Pour the hot water over the chamomile flowers and steep for 5-10 minutes.
  4. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the spent flowers.
  5. Drink the tea while warm, up to three times a day, for stomach upset relief.

2. Ginger

Ginger, a key ingredient in ginger tea, contains gingerols and shogaols, compounds with proven anti-inflammatory and digestive properties.

These compounds work by blocking nerve signals that cause nausea, reducing inflammation in the digestive tract, and relaxing intestinal muscles to alleviate stomach discomfort. Additionally, ginger increases saliva and bile production, which aids in the digestion of food and movement of food through the gastrointestinal tract. Ginger tea, when consumed during or after meals, can effectively soothe upset stomachs by enhancing digestion and providing rapid relief from symptoms such as cramps, bloating, and indigestion.

Thus, ginger tea's ability to help with upset stomachs is a result of its active components' ability to influence various aspects of the digestive process.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in ginger tea that help with upset stomach.

  • Rhizomes: Ginger rhizomes are a popular choice for medicinal tea due to their anti-inflammatory properties that aid in soothing digestive discomfort.
  • Roots: Ginger roots contain bioactive compounds that alleviate nausea, thus making them ideal for upset stomach relief.
  • Leaves: Rich in antioxidants, ginger leaves help treat gastrointestinal irritations, making them useful in calming an upset stomach.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in ginger tea that aid with upset stomach.

  • Gingerols: Gingerols are the main medicinal constituents in ginger, known for their anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties, which help alleviate upset stomach and soothe digestion.
  • Shogaols: Shogaols, derived from gingerols, further aid in reducing inflammation and nausea, thereby providing relief from upset stomach.
  • Paradols: Paradols, a product of gingerol degradation, exhibit antiemetic properties, helping to relieve symptoms of upset stomach such as nausea and vomiting.
  • Gingerdiols: Gingerdiols contribute to ginger's overall effectiveness in treating upset stomach by providing additional antispasmodic and anti-inflammatory benefits.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic ginger tea for upset stomach.

  1. Peel and chop 1 inch of fresh ginger into small pieces, then place in a tea infuser or loose in a teapot.
  2. Boil 4 cups of water and pour over the ginger, letting it steep for 10-15 minutes.
  3. Remove the tea infuser or strain the ginger pieces from the teapot.
  4. Add honey and lemon juice to taste, stirring until fully dissolved.
  5. Serve the ginger tea while warm, sipping slowly to soothe the stomach.

What is upset stomach and how can it affect your life?

An upset stomach, also known as dyspepsia, refers to discomfort or pain in the upper abdomen.

It often includes symptoms such as bloating, nausea, and heartburn. This condition can significantly impact people's lives by causing distress, disrupting daily activities, and affecting appetite and nutrition.

In severe cases, it can even lead to missed work or school and reduced quality of life.
