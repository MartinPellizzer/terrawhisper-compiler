10 best herbal teas for tickling throat

Among the top herbal teas for soothing a ticklish throat are chamomile, slippery elm, and licorice root.

Chamomile's anti-inflammatory properties help reduce irritation, while slippery elm's mucilage forms a protective coating. Licorice root, known for its demulcent effects, eases throat discomfort by creating a soothing film.

These herbal teas bring relief, promoting comfort and healing in a natural, caffeine-free way.

1. Slippery Elm

Slippery elm tea provides relief to a tickling throat due to its demulcent properties, meaning it contains substances that form a soothing film over mucous membranes.

This film creates a protective barrier against irritants and reduces the sensitivity of the throat's lining. The tea's active components, primarily mucilage, are responsible for this effect, as they can absorb water and expand, forming a gel-like layer. By coating and soothing the throat, slippery elm tea alleviates the irritation that causes the tickling sensation and calms the cough reflex, providing immediate comfort.

Native Americans traditionally used slippery elm for throat and digestive issues, showcasing its time-tested healing abilities.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in slippery elm tea that help with tickling throat.

  • Bark: Slippery elm bark contains mucilage, a substance that becomes a slick gel when mixed with water, providing a soothing coating for a tickling throat.
  • Leaves: Slippery elm leaves also contain mucilage, which helps to alleviate irritation in the throat and reduce tickling sensations.
  • Inner Bark: The inner bark of the slippery elm tree is rich in mucilage, making it an effective ingredient for a calming tea to soothe a tickling throat.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in slippery elm tea that aid with tickling throat.

  • Tannins: Tannins possess astringent properties that help to soothe a tickling throat by reducing inflammation and creating a protective barrier.
  • Mucilage: Mucilage forms a slick coating that lubricates and soothes a tickling throat, providing temporary relief.
  • Flavonoids: Flavonoids have anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects that alleviate throat irritation and help promote healing.
  • Sterols: Sterols can help reduce inflammation and provide additional soothing effects on a tickling throat.
  • Saponins: Saponins can help to soothe throat irritation by forming a foamy layer in the mouth and throat, creating a barrier that protects the mucous membranes.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic slippery elm tea for tickling throat.

  1. Measure - Pour 2 cups of water into a pot and add 1 teaspoon of slippery elm bark.
  2. Heat - Place the pot on a stove and bring the water to a boil.
  3. Steep - Reduce heat to low, cover the pot and let it simmer for 10 minutes.
  4. Strain - Remove the pot from heat, strain the tea into a cup, and discard the bark.
  5. Sip - Let the tea cool down until it's warm and drink it slowly, allowing it to coat your throat.

  2. Place one chamomile tea bag or 1-2 teaspoons of loose chamomile flowers in a teapot or mug.

  4. Remove the tea bag or strain the loose flowers from the liquid.
  5. Add 1-2 teaspoons of honey to the tea for added soothing properties, stirring well to combine. Enjoy while warm.
