What are the main causes of canker sores?

The list below provides the primary causes of canker sores.

  • Nutritional deficiencies: Lack of essential nutrients, such as vitamin B12, iron, and folate, in the diet can lead to the development of canker sores. These nutrients play a crucial role in maintaining the health of the mucous membranes, and their deficiency can result in inflammation and sores in the mouth.
  • Physical trauma: Injuries to the mouth, such as those caused by dental work, braces, accidental bites, or rough brushing, can cause canker sores. The trauma disrupts the delicate tissues in the mouth, making them prone to inflammation and infection, which can result in the formation of canker sores.
  • Food sensitivities: Consumption of acidic or spicy foods can trigger canker sores in some individuals. These foods can irritate the mucous membranes and cause inflammation, leading to the development of canker sores. Additionally, certain ingredients, such as chocolate, coffee, and nuts, can also contribute to canker sore formation.
  • Hormonal changes: Fluctuations in hormone levels, particularly during menstruation, pregnancy, and puberty, can increase the likelihood of developing canker sores. Hormonal imbalances can affect the immune system and the health of the mucous membranes, making them more susceptible to inflammation and infection.
  • Stress: Emotional stress and anxiety can weaken the immune system, making individuals more prone to infections, including canker sores. Stress can also cause inflammation and irritation in the mouth, increasing the risk of canker sore development.
  • Genetic predisposition: Some individuals are genetically predisposed to developing canker sores. Familial history of canker sores can increase the likelihood of an individual experiencing recurrent canker sores, as specific genes can make an individual more susceptible to the condition.
  • Allergic reactions: Allergies to certain bacteria or foods can cause canker sores. The immune system's response to the allergen can result in inflammation and the formation of sores in the mouth.
  • Gastrointestinal disorders: Conditions such as Crohn's disease, celiac disease, and irritable bowel syndrome can contribute to canker sores. These disorders can affect nutrient absorption, leading to nutritional deficiencies, and can also impact the immune system, increasing the likelihood of canker sore development.
  • Autoimmune disorders: Certain autoimmune disorders, such as lupus and Behçet's disease, can cause canker sores. In these cases, the immune system mistakenly attacks healthy cells, including those in the mucous membranes, leading to inflammation and the formation of canker sores.
