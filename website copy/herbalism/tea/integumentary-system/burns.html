10 best herbal teas for burns

Certain herbal teas, like chamomile and calendula, bring soothing relief to burns.

Chamomile, known for its anti-inflammatory properties, reduces redness and swelling. Calendula, a flowering plant from the marigold family, boasts impressive skin-healing abilities, accelerating the burn's recovery process.

Both teas, when applied topically as a cool compress, create a protective barrier on the skin, alleviating pain and promoting faster healing.

1. Chamomile

Chamomile tea aids burns through its anti-inflammatory properties, which reduce swelling and redness.

Its cooling effect soothes the skin, providing immediate relief. Chamomile contains tannins that create a protective layer over the burn, reducing pain and preventing infection. The antioxidants in chamomile tea promote tissue regeneration, speeding up the healing process.

Furthermore, its calming effects on the nervous system can help alleviate stress and anxiety associated with burns.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in chamomile tea that help with burns.

  • Flowers: Contain antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties that help soothe burn-related inflammation and support skin regeneration.
  • Leaves: Rich in flavonoids, offering a cooling effect on burns while aiding in the reduction of redness and swelling.
  • Seeds: Packed with essential oils that possess analgesic and healing properties, helping alleviate pain and promoting faster burn recovery.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in chamomile tea that aid with burns.

  • Apigenin: A flavonoid that possesses anti-inflammatory properties, helping to reduce redness, pain, and swelling in burns.
  • Bisabolol: A sesquiterpene alcohol that has shown to accelerate wound healing, provide pain relief, and reduce inflammation in burns.
  • Chamazulene: A chemical compound that demonstrates strong anti-inflammatory effects, aiding in soothing and calming irritated skin from burns.
  • Matricin: A precursor to chamazulene, which exhibits antiphlogistic (anti-inflammatory) and antimicrobial properties that help prevent infection in burns.
  • Alpha-Bisabolol Oxide A & B: Two compounds that exhibit significant anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties, which can help speed up the healing process in burns.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic chamomile tea for burns.

  1. Boil 8 ounces of water, then pour over 2 teaspoons of dried chamomile flowers.
  2. Steep the chamomile in hot water for 5-10 minutes.
  3. Strain the chamomile tea, discarding the spent flowers.
  4. Allow the tea to cool down to a warm temperature.
  5. Gently apply the warm chamomile tea to the burn using a clean cloth.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in calendula tea that help with burns.

  • Stems: Contain beneficial compounds, including mucilage, which helps to protect and soothe burned skin.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in calendula tea that aid with burns.

  • Flavonoids: Possess anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, aiding in reducing inflammation and oxidative stress caused by burns.
  • Triterpenes: Accelerate wound healing by stimulating collagen production and have anti-inflammatory effects that help alleviate burn-induced discomfort.

  • Volatile oils: Contain anti-inflammatory, antiseptic, and analgesic properties, helping reduce pain, inflammation, and risk of infection in burns.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic calendula tea for burns.

  1. Gather 1-2 tablespoons of dried calendula flowers.
  2. Steep the flowers in 8 ounces of boiling water for 10-15 minutes.
  3. Strain the liquid to remove the flowers.
  4. Allow the tea to cool down until it's warm, but not hot.
  5. Apply the calendula tea directly to the burn using a clean cloth.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in peppermint tea that help with burns.

  • Leaves: Contain menthol and menthone, which have a cooling and soothing effect on burned skin.
  • Flowers: Rich in antioxidants and volatile oils, helping to reduce inflammation and promote healing in burns.
  • Buds: High in rosmarinic acid, an anti-inflammatory compound that can alleviate pain and swelling caused by burns.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in peppermint tea that aid with burns.

  • Menthol: Possesses local anesthetic and analgesic properties that help relieve pain and discomfort caused by burns.
  • Tannins: Have astringent effects that can help to dry out weeping burns and promote healing.
  • Flavonoids: Exhibit anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties that help reduce inflammation and protect against infection in burns.
  • Rosmarinic acid: Known for its anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects, helping to alleviate pain and promote tissue repair.
  • Limonene: Has antiseptic properties that help protect burns from infection and support the healing process.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic peppermint tea for burns.

  1. Boil 8 ounces of water, reaching a full boil at 212°F (100°C) for optimal extraction of peppermint oil.
  2. Add 1 tablespoon of dried peppermint leaves or 1 peppermint tea bag to the boiling water.
  3. Steep the peppermint in the water for 5-7 minutes to allow the active compounds, like menthol, to release into the water.
  4. Carefully strain or remove the tea bag to prevent any remaining hot liquid from causing further burn damage.
  5. Allow the tea to cool to a warm temperature, no hotter than 104°F (40°C), before applying it gently to the burned area using a clean cloth.

Basil tea brings relief to burns due to its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties derived from basil's essential oils, specifically eugenol and citronellol.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in basil tea that help with burns.

  • Seeds: Packed with essential oils and fatty acids that have anti-inflammatory and regenerative effects, aiding in burn recovery.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in basil tea that aid with burns.

  • Orientin: A type of flavonoid that has strong anti-inflammatory properties, helping to reduce redness and swelling caused by burns.
  • Vicenin: A type of flavonoid that has antioxidant properties, helping to protect the skin from further damage caused by burns.
  • Eugenol: A type of phenol that has analgesic properties, helping to reduce the pain caused by burns.
  • Rosmarinic acid: A type of phenolic acid that has anti-inflammatory properties, helping to reduce redness and swelling caused by burns.

  • Carnosic acid: A type of diterpene that has antimicrobial properties, helping to prevent infection caused by burns.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic basil tea for burns.

  1. Chop 1 cup of fresh basil leaves, letting them wilt for 10 minutes to release their oils.
  2. Bring 4 cups of water to a boil, then pour it over the chopped basil in a heatproof container.
  3. Steep the basil in the hot water for 10-15 minutes, creating a strong basil tea.
  4. Strain the tea, discarding the basil leaves, and let the tea cool to a comfortable temperature.
  5. Soak a clean cloth in the cooled basil tea, then apply the cloth to the burn for 15 minutes to ease pain and promote healing.

  1. Peel and chop 2 ripe plantains into small pieces, place them in a pot, and pour 4 cups of water.
  2. Bring the water to a boil, then reduce heat and let it simmer for 15 minutes.
  3. Strain the liquid to remove plantain pieces, leaving only the tea.
  4. Allow the tea to cool down to a warm temperature that is still comfortably hot.
  5. Soak a clean cloth in the tea and apply it directly to the burn for 15 minutes, repeating as needed.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic aloe vera tea for burns.

  1. Slice 2 aloe vera leaves to expose the gel, then scoop out 2 tablespoons of gel and set aside.
  2. Boil 2 cups of water in a pot, add 1 teaspoon of green tea leaves and let it steep for 5 minutes.
  3. Strain the tea to remove the green tea leaves, then add the aloe vera gel to the hot tea.
  4. Stir well to combine the gel and tea, then let the mixture cool down to a drinkable temperature.
  5. Consume the tea immediately, up to three times a day, to soothe burns and promote healing.

  4. Strain the liquid into a cup, discarding the fennel solids.
  5. Allow the tea to cool slightly before applying it to the burn, ensuring it's warm but not hot.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic yarrow tea for burns.

  1. Harvest 2-3 tablespoons of fresh yarrow flowers and leaves, ensuring they are fully grown and free of pesticides.
  2. Rinse the harvested yarrow under cold running water, then chop coarsely.
  3. Place the chopped yarrow in a teapot and add 2 cups of boiling water.
  4. Allow the mixture to steep for 10-15 minutes, then strain to remove plant material.
  5. Drink the tea or let it cool, then use it as a compress or directly apply it to the burn for relief.
