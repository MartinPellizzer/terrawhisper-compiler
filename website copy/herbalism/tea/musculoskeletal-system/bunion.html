1. Nettle

Nettle tea provides anti-inflammatory properties due to its richness in antioxidants and minerals, reducing inflammation in the body.

Its natural analgesic effects ease discomfort, while the tea's ability to improve circulation enhances nutrient delivery to inflamed tissues, promoting overall foot health and easing bunion symptoms.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in nettle tea that aid with bunion.

  • Histamine: Stimulates blood flow and alleviates inflammation, aiding in the reduction of bunion swelling and pain.
  • Serotonin: Improves mood and reduces pain sensitivity, contributing to overall foot comfort.
  • Kaempferol: Possesses anti-inflammatory properties that help minimize bunion-related swelling and discomfort.
  • Quercetin: Acts as an antioxidant and reduces inflammation, easing bunion pain and improving joint health.
  • Acetylcholine: Enhances muscle contractions, promoting healthy foot and toe function to potentially slow bunion progression.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic nettle tea for bunion.

  1. Gather 2 cups of fresh nettle leaves, ensuring they are free from pesticides and pollution, as this can take up to 30 minutes depending on location.
  2. Rinse the nettle leaves thoroughly under cold running water to remove any dirt or small insects, then let them dry for 10 minutes.
  3. Coarsely chop the nettle leaves and place them in a heatproof container or teapot, then pour 4 cups of boiling water over them, allowing them to steep for 10-15 minutes.
  4. Strain the tea to remove the leaves, then let it cool down to a drinkable temperature, which should take approximately 10 minutes.
  5. Drink 1 cup of nettle tea, up to three times a day, to help alleviate bunion discomfort and inflammation. Make a fresh batch of tea daily to maintain potency and effectiveness.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic birch tea for bunion.

  1. Collect fresh birch bark from mature trees, avoiding damage to the tree (use only what you need and only take from trees in abundance).
  2. Rinse the bark thoroughly to remove any dirt or debris, then cut it into small pieces and place them in a pot.
  3. Add 4 cups of water to the pot, then bring the mixture to a boil over high heat.
  4. Reduce the heat to low and let the tea simmer for 20 minutes, allowing the bark to infuse the water.
  5. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the bark, and let it cool to a comfortable temperature before drinking.
