    10 best herbal teas for anxiety

    Several herbal teas, including chamomile, lemon balm, and passionflower, are well-known for their calming effects on the body and mind.

    These teas contain natural compounds that promote relaxation and reduce anxiety. Chamomile, for instance, has antioxidants that bind to receptors in the brain to decrease anxiety and initiate sleep.

    Lemon balm, on the other hand, contains rosmarinic acid, which increases GABA levels in the brain, inducing a sense of calmness. Lastly, passionflower has flavonoids that aid in lowering anxiety by increasing the levels of a chemical called gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA), resulting in a relaxing and soothing sensation.

    Regular consumption of these herbal teas can help alleviate anxiety and foster a more relaxed state.

    1. Chamomile

    Chamomile tea contains antioxidants that bind to certain receptors in the brain, promoting relaxation and reducing anxiety.

    This herbal tea's calming effects stem from its ability to increase levels of the neurotransmitter glycine, which aids in reducing anxiety and fostering better sleep. Moreover, chamomile's gentle aroma has been found to decrease anxiety and improve mood. Clinical studies confirm that regular consumption of chamomile tea leads to a significant decrease in anxiety symptoms, indicating its effectiveness in managing anxiety disorders.

    Lastly, chamomile tea boasts anxiolytic properties that help alleviate symptoms of anxiety by promoting a soothing effect on both the mind and body.

    Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in chamomile tea that help with anxiety.

    • Flowers: Contain high levels of apigenin, a compound that binds to benzodiazepine receptors in the brain, helping to reduce anxiety.
    • Leaves: Rich in antioxidants and flavonoids, shown to have a calming effect on the nervous system, aiding in anxiety relief.
    • Seeds: Contain essential oils and antioxidants, contributing to the relaxing properties of chamomile tea, making it beneficial for anxiety reduction.

    The list below shows the primary active constituents in chamomile tea that aid with anxiety.

    • Apigenin: A flavonoid that binds to benzodiazepine receptors in the brain, helping to reduce anxiety.
    • Chamazulene: A terpene with anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties that helps alleviate anxiety-related symptoms.
    • Luteolin: A flavonoid acting as a nervine, promoting relaxation and alleviating anxiety.
    • N-Butylphthalide: A compound working as a muscle relaxant, helping to soothe anxiety-induced physical tension.
    • Matricin: A precursor to chamazulene, offering anti-inflammatory effects and supporting nervous system health.
    • Bisabolol: A sesquiterpene alcohol that acts as a mild sedative and anti-inflammatory, contributing to anxiolytic effects.

    The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic chamomile tea for anxiety.

    1. Boil 8 ounces of water, reaching a temperature of 212 degrees Fahrenheit, for 10 minutes.
    2. Place one teabag or 1-2 teaspoons of loose chamomile flowers in a teapot or cup.
    3. Pour the boiling water over the teabag or loose chamomile flowers.
    4. Steep the tea for 5 minutes, allowing the chamomile to release its calming properties.
    5. Remove the teabag or strain the loose chamomile flowers, then enjoy your soothing chamomile tea.

    2. Lavender

    Lavender tea contains an active compound called linalool, which directly affects the brain's GABA receptors to produce a calming effect.

    This herbal infusion reduces anxiety levels by decreasing heart rate and lowering blood pressure. Furthermore, lavender tea promotes better sleep quality, which is crucial for mental health. Additionally, its pleasant aroma, emanating from the released volatile oils during brewing, creates a soothing ambiance that alleviates tension and contributes to stress relief.

    Altogether, lavender tea's physiological and psychological effects make it an effective tool in managing anxiety.

    Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in lavender tea that help with anxiety.

    • Flowers: Contain fragrant oils and compounds believed to have calming effects on the nervous system.
    • Leaves: Rich in soothing antioxidants, known to reduce stress and promote relaxation.
    • Buds: High in calming essential oils, often used to alleviate anxiety and improve sleep quality.
    • Stems: May contain therapeutic compounds that contribute to the tea's soothing properties.

    The list below shows the primary active constituents in lavender tea that aid with anxiety.

    • Linalool: This terpene alcohol possesses anxiolytic and sedative properties, contributing to lavender tea's soothing effect on anxiety.
    • Eucalyptol: With anxiolytic and neuroprotective effects, this terpene helps reduce anxiety and safeguards neural health.
    • Camphor: This aromatic ketone exhibits anxiolytic and analgesic properties, thereby alleviating anxiety and associated physical discomfort.
    • Geraniol: Acting as a natural anxiolytic and antidepressant, this acyclic monoterpene alcohol provides relief from anxiety symptoms.
    • β-Caryophyllene: Demonstrating anxiolytic and anti-inflammatory effects, this sesquiterpene helps decrease anxiety and inflammation related to stress.
    • Rosmarinic acid: This polyphenolic compound exhibits anxiolytic and antioxidant benefits, addressing anxiety and oxidative stress caused by anxiety.

    The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic lavender tea for anxiety.

    1. Boil 1 cup of water, taking 3-5 minutes to reach a rolling boil.
    2. Add 1 teaspoon of dried organic lavender flowers to a tea infuser.
    3. Place the tea infuser in the boiling water and let it steep for 5-7 minutes.
    4. Remove the tea infuser and stir in 1 teaspoon of raw honey for added calming effects.
    5. Serve the lavender tea immediately, allowing it to cool slightly before sipping.

    3. Lemon Balm

    Lemon balm tea reduces anxiety by promoting relaxation.

    Its active compounds, including rosmarinic acid and eugenol, have calming effects on the nervous system. By increasing GABA activity in the brain, lemon balm tea helps alleviate anxiety symptoms. Additionally, it decreases the levels of stress hormones like cortisol and adrenaline.

    Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in lemon balm tea that help with anxiety.

    • Leaves: Contain rosmarinic acid, which has been shown to have anti-anxiety effects.
    • Flowers: Possess flavonoids with potential calming properties, making them a soothing addition to tea.
    • Stems: May have trace amounts of therapeutic compounds, contributing to the tea's overall benefits.

    The list below shows the primary active constituents in lemon balm tea that aid with anxiety.

    • Rosmarinic acid: A potent antioxidant that helps reduce cell damage and stress in the body, contributing to an overall calming effect.
    • Citral: An organic compound with anti-anxiety properties, shown to decrease anxiety-like behaviors.
    • Nerol: A fragrant compound that exhibits anxiety-reducing effects, promoting relaxation.
    • Geraniol: A natural compound that has been found to have anxiolytic effects, helping to alleviate anxiety symptoms.
    • Caryophyllene: A sesquiterpene with stress-relieving properties, known to reduce anxiety and promote well-being.

    The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic lemon balm tea for anxiety.

    1. Harvest 2 tablespoons of fresh lemon balm leaves, or use 1 teaspoon of dried leaves.
    2. Rinse the leaves under cold water to remove any dirt or impurities.
    3. Boil 1 cup of water and add the lemon balm leaves.
    4. Steep the tea for 5-7 minutes to allow the herb to release its beneficial compounds.
    5. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the used leaves, and enjoy it while warm.

    • Flowers: Rich in antioxidants and essential oils, which can help reduce anxiety symptoms.
    • Stems: Contain beneficial alkaloids and flavonoids, which contribute to the plant's calming effects.
    • Buds: Harvested for their potent phytochemicals, including passionflavonoids, that aid in stress relief.

    • Flavonoids: These antioxidants help reduce anxiety by inhibiting the breakdown of neurotransmitters like serotonin and dopamine, which can help regulate mood and anxiety levels.
    • Chrysin: A flavonoid that acts as a GABA-agonist, promoting relaxation and reducing anxiety by binding to GABA receptors in the brain.
    • Benzoflavone: This flavonoid can help alleviate anxiety by modulating the activity of neurotransmitters such as GABA, serotonin, and dopamine.
    • Harman: A naturally occurring alkaloid found in passionflower, which potentially contributes to the anxiolytic activity of the plant by affecting neurotransmission.
    • Indole alkaloids: These compounds, notably harmine and harmaline, can act as MAO-inhibitors, affecting neurotransmitter levels and potentially reducing anxiety.
    • Vitexin: A flavonoid that helps reduce anxiety by modulating GABA receptors, thus reducing neuronal excitability.
    • Orientin: A flavonoid with antioxidant properties that supports the nervous system, and can help reduce anxiety by promoting neurotransmitter balance.
    • Isoorientin: Similar to orientin, it acts as an antioxidant that supports the nervous system, and can help decrease anxiety by aiding neurotransmitter balance and promoting GABA activity.
    • Luteolin: A flavonoid found in passionflower that provides anti-anxiety effects via various mechanisms, including neurotransmitter modulation and antioxidant protection.
    • Apigenin: A flavonoid that exhibits a strong affinity for benzodiazepine receptors in the brain, contributing to the anxiolytic activity of passionflower.

    The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic passionflower tea for anxiety.

    1. Select 1 teaspoon of dried passionflower leaves or 2 teaspoons of fresh leaves.
    2. Place the passionflower leaves in a tea infuser or teapot.
    3. Pour 8 ounces of boiling water over the passionflower leaves.
    4. Steep the tea for 10 minutes to extract the medicinal compounds.
    5. Remove the passionflower leaves and enjoy the warm anxiety-relieving tea.

    • Flowers: Valerian tea flowers contain antioxidants and volatile oils that contribute to the tea's calming effects, which can help alleviate anxiety and improve sleep quality.

    • Valerenic acid: Interacts with GABA receptors in the brain to reduce anxiety.
    • Lignans: Act as natural sedatives to calm the nervous system.
    • Flavanoids: Contribute to the overall calming effect of valerian tea.

    • Essential oils: Contain sedative properties that help alleviate anxiety symptoms.

    The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic valerian tea for anxiety.

    1. In a teapot, combine 1 teaspoon of dried valerian root with 8 ounces of boiling water.
    2. Steep the mixture, covered, for 10-15 minutes to allow the active compounds to infuse.
    3. Strain the tea to remove the solid valerian root particles.
    4. Optionally, add honey or lemon to taste.
    5. Drink the tea while it's still warm, up to three times a day, as needed.

    The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic ashwagandha tea for anxiety.

    3. Strain the tea to remove the ashwagandha powder and discard it.
    4. Add honey or lemon to taste (optional).
    5. Serve the tea hot and enjoy the calming benefits of ashwagandha.

    3. Pour the hot water over the holy basil leaves and steep for 5-6 minutes.
    4. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the leaves.
    5. Drink the tea while warm, up to 3 times a day as needed.

    8. Kava Kava

    This binding effect reduces anxiety by decreasing the activity in neural pathways responsible for fear and stress responses. Kava kava also increases the release of GABA, a neurotransmitter with calming effects, further promoting relaxation and reducing anxiety.     Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in kava kava tea that help with anxiety.

    • Roots: Contain high levels of kavalactones, which are thought to have anxiety-reducing effects.
    • Rhizomes: Rich in starchy ingredients and kavalactones, providing calming and relaxing properties.
    • Leaves: Contain lower concentrations of kavalactones but still contribute to anxiety relief and taste.
    • Flowers: While less potent, they do contain kavalactones and add a subtle, pleasant aroma to the tea.

    The list below shows the primary active constituents in kava kava tea that aid with anxiety.

    • Kavalactones: These compounds are responsible for the anxiolytic effects of kava kava tea, binding to receptors in the amygdala and reducing anxiety symptoms.
    • Dihydrokavalactones: These components contribute to the calming effects of kava kava tea, acting as muscle relaxants and helping to alleviate anxiety-induced muscle tension.
    • Flavokavains: These antioxidant compounds found in kava kava tea help protect against oxidative stress and provide neuroprotective benefits, supporting overall brain health and reducing anxiety.
    • Desmethoxyyangonin: A kavalactone with mood-enhancing properties, this compound in kava kava tea can elevate mood and relieve anxiety-related symptoms.

    The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic kava kava tea for anxiety.

    1. Obtain 2 tablespoons of dried kava kava root and 4 cups of water.
    2. Simmer the kava kava root in water for 15 minutes.
    3. Strain the liquid to remove the root particles.
    4. Pour the strained liquid into a cup.
    5. Consume the tea, up to 3 times a day.
