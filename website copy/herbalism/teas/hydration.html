10 Best Herbal Teas For Hydration

Herbal teas, composed of various dried herbs, fruits, and spices, can contribute to hydration due to their high water content.

Unlike caffeinated teas and coffee, most herbal teas do not contain diuretic properties, which can lead to fluid loss. In fact, some herbal teas, such as chamomile and peppermint, may even help stimulate the stomach and promote fluid absorption in the gut. Furthermore, herbal teas provide a flavorful and enjoyable alternative to plain water, encouraging individuals to consume more fluids throughout the day.

However, it's important to note that individual responses to herbal teas may vary, and excessive consumption should be avoided in specific medical conditions or when taking particular medications.

1. Peppermint

Peppermint tea helps with hydration because it mostly contains water, which is the primary component in hydration.

Unlike caffeinated beverages, peppermint tea does not cause dehydration, but rather, it adds to the body's fluid volume. Moreover, it can stimulate the production of saliva and digestive juices, aiding in digestion and further enhancing the body's hydration. Additionally, peppermint tea's mild diuretic effect can promote fluid balance in the body, encouraging frequent yet healthy urination.

Therefore, drinking peppermint tea can contribute to overall hydration and support various bodily functions.

The following procedure explains how to make peppermint tea for hydration.

  1. In a small pot, add 1 cup of water and bring it to a boil (3-4 minutes).
  2. Add 1 heaping teaspoon of dried peppermint leaves (or 1 peppermint tea bag) to the pot, then turn off the heat.
  3. Allow the peppermint leaves to steep in the hot water for 5-7 minutes.
  4. Strain out the peppermint leaves (if using loose leaves) and pour the tea into a cup.
  5. Add honey or lemon to taste, if desired, and enjoy your hydrating peppermint tea.

2. Chamomile

Chamomile tea helps with hydration because it is primarily made of water, which is the main component of hydration.

Unlike caffeinated beverages that can have a dehydrating effect, chamomile tea does not contain caffeine and therefore aids in maintaining the body's fluid balance. Moreover, chamomile tea is often consumed without any additives like sugar or milk, further promoting hydration. The soothing and calming properties of chamomile tea may also encourage individuals to drink more fluids, thereby enhancing their hydration status.

However, it is important to note that while chamomile tea can contribute to hydration, it should not be the sole source of hydration and should be consumed as part of a balanced diet that includes other fluids and water-rich foods.

The following procedure explains how to make chamomile tea for hydration.

  1. Heat 1 cup of water to boiling (2-3 minutes in a microwave or 4-5 minutes in a kettle).
  2. Add 1-2 teaspoons of dried chamomile flowers to a tea infuser or teapot.
  3. Pour the hot water over the chamomile flowers and let steep for 5-7 minutes.
  4. Remove the tea infuser or strain the chamomile flowers from the tea.
  5. Add honey or lemon to taste, if desired, and enjoy your hydrating chamomile tea.

3. Rooibos

Rooibos tea helps with hydration because it is a caffeine-free beverage that does not lead to dehydration like drinks containing caffeine.

Its water content contributes to the daily fluid intake, thus aiding in hydration. Unlike other teas, rooibos does not contain oxalic acid, which means it aids in the absorption of calcium and other minerals, further promoting hydration and overall health. The antioxidants present in rooibos tea also help in maintaining the body's fluid balance, which is crucial for staying hydrated.

Lastly, rooibos tea is often enjoyed without any sweeteners or milk, reducing the risk of dehydration caused by high sugar or sodium content in such additives.

The following procedure explains how to make rooibos tea for hydration.

  1. Boil 1 cup of fresh water (8 fl oz) and let it cool down to 205-212°F (for optimal extraction of rooibos tea compounds while preserving its delicate taste).
  2. In a teapot or infuser, place 1-2 teaspoons (about 2-4 grams) of loose-leaf rooibos tea, adjusting the amount based on personal preference and tea strength desired.
  3. Pour the hot water over the rooibos tea leaves, ensuring they are fully submerged.
  4. Allow the tea to steep for 5-7 minutes, depending on the desired strength, as rooibos does not become bitter with longer steeping times.
  5. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the spent tea leaves, and enjoy it as is or with a sweetener of your choice for a naturally caffeine-free, hydrating beverage.

4. Sage

Sage tea helps with hydration because it contains not only a high water content but also small amounts of essential electrolytes like sodium and potassium, which are crucial for maintaining the body's fluid balance.

Furthermore, the herbal tea is known to have antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties, which can help the body recover from physical activities, thereby aiding in the overall hydration process. Unlike caffeinated beverages, sage tea's mildly diuretic effect is much weaker, and it can still promote urination without causing excessive fluid loss. Additionally, for individuals who find it difficult to drink sufficient water throughout the day, drinking flavored teas like sage can encourage them to consume more fluids, thus aiding in their hydration.

Lastly, while water is the most basic and essential fluid for hydration, consuming a variety of fluids, including herbal teas like sage, can contribute to meeting daily fluid needs and prevent the boredom that may come with drinking only water.

The following procedure explains how to make sage tea for hydration.

  1. Heat 1 cup of water to boiling point (approximately 2-3 minutes in a microwave or on a stove).
  2. Crush 1-2 fresh sage leaves and add to the boiling water.
  3. Allow the sage leaves to steep in the water for 5-10 minutes.
  4. Remove the sage leaves and optionally add honey or lemon to taste.
  5. Enjoy your sage tea, either hot or cooled, for a natural and hydrating beverage.

5. Cinnamon

Cinnamon tea helps with hydration because it not only satisfies your taste buds, but also contributes to your daily fluid intake.

The water used to brew the tea aids in keeping your body hydrated, while the cinnamon adds beneficial properties such as antioxidants and anti-inflammatory agents. Furthermore, cinnamon tea may help regulate body temperature and sweat production, which can lead to further hydration. Additionally, the soothing warmth of cinnamon tea can encourage individuals to drink more frequently, indirectly promoting better hydration habits.

Lastly, cinnamon tea can be a flavorful and comforting alternative to plain water, making it an enjoyable way to stay hydrated.

The following procedure explains how to make cinnamon tea for hydration.

  1. In a pot, boil 1 cup of water for 3-5 minutes.
  2. Add 1 cinnamon stick and let it simmer for 10 minutes.
  3. Optional: for enhanced benefits, add 1 teaspoon of honey and a squeeze of lemon juice.
  4. Strain the tea to remove cinnamon solids.
  5. Serve and enjoy your cinnamon tea for hydration.

6. Ginger

Ginger tea helps with hydration because it consists of water as its primary ingredient, which when consumed in sufficient quantities, can contribute to meeting daily fluid intake needs.

Moreover, ginger tea can be an appealing alternative to plain water for some people, encouraging them to drink more fluids and stay hydrated. Additionally, ginger has been shown to have diaphoretic properties, which can promote sweating and help the body release toxins, further enhancing the hydration process. Furthermore, ginger tea can be beneficial for individuals who experience nausea or vomiting, as it can help settle the stomach and restore fluids lost due to these symptoms.

However, it's important to note that while ginger tea can aid in hydration, it should not be relied upon solely for hydration purposes, and other fluids such as water, electrolyte-infused drinks, and fruits with high water content should also be consumed.

The following procedure explains how to make ginger tea for hydration.

  1. Peel and finely chop a 1-inch piece of fresh ginger, then steep in 4 cups of boiling water for 10 minutes.
  2. Strain the ginger pieces from the water, then stir in 2 teaspoons of honey and the juice of half a lemon.
  3. Pour the ginger tea into a cup, then optionally garnish with a lemon slice and a cinnamon stick.
  4. Enjoy your warm and soothing ginger tea, allowing it to hydrate and comfort you.
  5. Sip slowly and mindfully, noticing the flavors and sensations, for maximum relaxation and hydration.

7. Echinacea

Echinacea tea helps with hydration because, despite containing caffeine, it is typically consumed without any added sugars or artificial sweeteners that might increase fluid loss.

The water used to brew the tea contributes to hydration, and the herbal properties of echinacea may help soothe a sore throat or cough, indirectly encouraging fluid intake. Furthermore, some studies suggest that echinacea has anti-inflammatory effects, which could potentially aid in the body's water balance regulation. However, it is essential to note that echinacea tea should not be the sole source of hydration, and individuals should still consume water and other fluids regularly.

Lastly, people with certain health conditions or those who are pregnant or breastfeeding should consult a healthcare professional before consuming echinacea tea or any other herbal tea.

The following procedure explains how to make echinacea tea for hydration.

  1. Heat 2 cups of water in a pot until boiling, then add 1-2 teaspoons of dried echinacea flowers and leaves, reducing heat to a simmer for 15-20 minutes.
  2. Turn off heat and let the tea steep for an additional 10 minutes.
  3. Strain the tea to remove the echinacea pieces.
  4. Add honey or lemon to taste, if desired.
  5. Drink the tea while warm for hydration and immune support.

8. Rosehip

Rosehip tea helps with hydration because it is primarily made from the fruit of the rose plant, which contains a lot of water.

The tea-making process extracts this water and other compounds, creating a hydrating beverage. Additionally, rosehip tea is caffeine-free, so it doesn't have a diuretic effect like some other teas, allowing the body to retain the fluids it takes in. Furthermore, rosehip tea is rich in vitamin C and other antioxidants, which can support overall health and help the body absorb and use water more effectively.

Finally, drinking rosehip tea can be a flavorful and enjoyable way to stay hydrated, making it a good alternative to plain water for some people.

The following procedure explains how to make rosehip tea for hydration.

  1. Rinse and gently cleanse 2 cups of fresh rosehips (about 4 handfuls), let dry for 30 minutes.
  2. Coarsely chop the dried rosehips, place them in a pot, and add 4 cups of water, bring to a simmer for 15 minutes.
  3. Strain the tea to remove rosehip pieces, let it cool down for 10 minutes.
  4. Serve the rosehip tea hot or cold, sweeten with honey if desired.
  5. Store leftover tea in the refrigerator for up to 3 days.

9. Hibiscus

Hibiscus tea helps with hydration because it contains a high concentration of antioxidants and electrolytes, which are essential for maintaining the body's fluid balance.

Furthermore, hibiscus tea is caffeine-free and naturally sweet, making it a more palatable and enjoyable alternative to water for some people, thereby encouraging them to drink more fluids. The tea is also known for its diuretic properties, which can help increase urine production and promote fluid elimination, aiding in the body's detoxification process. Importantly, hibiscus tea is made by steeping the plant's sepals in hot water, which releases a high amount of flavonoids and other bioactive compounds that can help the body absorb and retain water more effectively.

Overall, hibiscus tea is a delicious and healthy way to stay hydrated and support overall health and well-being.

The following procedure explains how to make hibiscus tea for hydration.

  1. Rinse 1/2 cup of dried hibiscus flowers in water to remove any impurities, then add them to a 4-quart pot.
  2. Pour 8 cups of water into the pot, place it on the stove, and bring the water to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer for 10 minutes.
  3. Remove the pot from heat, add in 1-2 tablespoons of raw honey or to taste, and stir until the sweetener is fully dissolved.
  4. Strain the tea into a pitcher or individual cups, using a fine-mesh sieve to catch any solid pieces of hibiscus.
  5. Serve the tea immediately, or let it cool to room temperature and refrigerate for up to one week. Enjoy your hydrating hibiscus tea!

10. Lavender

Lavender tea helps with hydration because it is often made by steeping dried lavender flowers in hot water, which releases the essential oils and compounds from the plant.

The resulting infusion not only provides hydration through its water content but also offers additional hydrating benefits due to the presence of antioxidants and other nutrients found in lavender. Moreover, lavender is known for its calming properties, which can encourage individuals to drink more fluids throughout the day, further aiding in hydration. Additionally, lavender tea may help stimulate digestion and absorption of water in the gut, enhancing the body's overall hydration status.

However, it is important to note that while lavender tea can contribute to daily fluid intake, it should not be relied upon solely for hydration, and plain water remains the most essential source of hydration for the human body.

The following procedure explains how to make lavender tea for hydration.

  1. Heat 1 cup of water to boiling (2-3 minutes in a microwave or 4-5 minutes in a kettle), then pour over 1-2 teaspoons of dried culinary lavender in a tea infuser or teapot with a strainer.
  2. Allow the lavender to steep in the hot water for 5-10 minutes, depending on how strong you prefer the flavor.
  3. While the lavender steeps, warm a mug by rinsing it with hot water and then dry it.
  4. After steeping, remove the lavender from the water by lifting the infuser or pouring the tea through a strainer into the warmed mug.
  5. Add honey or lemon to taste, if desired, and enjoy your hydrating lavender tea.
