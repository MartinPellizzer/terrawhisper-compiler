What to know about mouth ulcers before using medicinal herbs

Mouth ulcers, also known as canker sores, are small, shallow lesions that develop on the soft tissues in your mouth or tongue. They are usually round or oval with a red border and a yellow or white center. Mouth ulcers can be painful, making it difficult to eat, drink, or brush your teeth. The exact cause of mouth ulcers is unknown, but they may be triggered by stress, hormonal changes, or a lack of certain vitamins. Most mouth ulcers are harmless and clear up on their own within one to two weeks. However, if you have a mouth ulcer that lasts longer than three weeks, you should see a healthcare professional as it may be a sign of an underlying medical condition.

What is mouth ulcers and how it affects your life?

Mouth ulcers, also known as aphthous ulcers, are small, painful lesions that develop on the soft tissues inside the mouth, including the gums, tongue, and lining of the cheeks. These ulcers typically appear as shallow, round or oval sores, with a red border and a yellow or white center. Mouth ulcers can cause significant discomfort, making it difficult to eat, drink, and speak. In severe cases, they can also interfere with sleep and daily activities. While mouth ulcers are usually harmless and resolve on their own within 1-2 weeks, they can have a significant impact on a person's quality of life. Chronic or recurring mouth ulcers may be a sign of an underlying medical condition, such as anemia, vitamin deficiency, or autoimmune disorder, and should be evaluated by a healthcare professional.

What are the primary causes of mouth ulcers?

Mouth ulcers, also known as canker sores, can be caused by a variety of factors. The most common cause is injury to the mouth, such as biting the inside of the cheek or accidentally brushing too hard. Additionally, mouth ulcers can be triggered by stress, hormonal changes, or a lack of certain vitamins and minerals, such as vitamin B12, iron, and folic acid. Certain foods, including acidic or spicy dishes, can also contribute to the development of mouth ulcers. Finally, in some cases, mouth ulcers can be a symptom of an underlying medical condition, such as an autoimmune disorder or gastrointestinal disease. However, it's important to note that the exact cause of a particular mouth ulcer may not always be clear.
