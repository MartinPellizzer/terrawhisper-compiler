What to know before using Bromus erectus (Meadow Brome)?

Bromus erectus, also known as sterile brome or dog's tongue, is a perennial grass native to Europe and Western Asia.

Its seeds and young leaves have been used in traditional medicine to treat a variety of ailments, including digestive issues and skin conditions. Bromus erectus prefers well-drained soil and full sun, and it can grow in a variety of horticultural conditions, making it a popular choice for meadows and wildflower gardens.

As a botanical specimen, Bromus erectus stands out for its distinctive inflorescence, which features long, drooping branches adorned with spikelets that contain the plants' tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

What are the caring tips of Bromus erectus?

Caring for Bromus erectus is relatively low maintenance.

It is a drought-tolerant grass but benefits from regular watering during dry spells. A deep watering once a week is sufficient.

Fertilization is not necessary, but if desired, it should be done in the spring. This grass does not require frequent mowing, as it can be left to naturalize and provide winter interest.

However, if mowing is desired, it should be done in the late spring or early summer, as it can be damaging to the grass if done during the active growing season.

What are the harvesting tips of Bromus erectus?

Harvesting Bromus erectus is typically not necessary, as it is not a crop grass.

However, if the grass is being used for ornamental purposes, it can be cut back in the late winter or early spring. This will promote new growth and maintain a neat appearance. When cutting back, it is best to leave a few inches of growth.

Any seed heads that have formed can be collected and used for propagation.
