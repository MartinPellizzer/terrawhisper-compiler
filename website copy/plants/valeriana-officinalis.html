What to know before using Valeriana officinalis (Valerian)?

Valeriana officinalis, also known as garden valerian, is a flowering plant native to Europe and parts of Asia, but has since been naturalized in North America.

It is a hardy perennial that thrives in full sun to partial shade and well-drained soil. The plant is known for its tall, branched stems and sweetly scented, pink or white flowers that bloom in the summer. Medicinally, Valeriana officinalis is best known for its root, which contains active compounds that have a sedative and anxiety-reducing effect.

It has been used traditionally to treat insomnia, anxiety, and restlessness, and its effectiveness has been supported by some scientific studies.

What are the medicinal uses of Valeriana officinalis?

Valeriana officinalis has several medicinal uses, such as reducing anxiety, improving sleep quality, and alleviating symptoms of menopause.

Its constituents include volatile oils, alkaloids, and iridoids, which are believed to contribute to its medicinal properties. Valerian can be prepared in various forms, such as dried root, capsules, tinctures, and tea.

Some possible side effects of valerian include headaches, dizziness, stomach problems, and daytime drowsiness. Therefore, it is important to start with a low dose and gradually increase it, and to avoid driving or operating heavy machinery after taking valerian.

Additionally, valerian should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding, and should not be taken with sedatives, alcohol, or other medications that cause drowsiness.

What are the active constituents of Valeriana officinalis?

Valeriana officinalis has many active constituents, such as valepotriates, sesquiterpenes, and volatile oils, that contribute to its medicinal properties.

Valepotriates, a group of compounds unique to valerian, have been found to have sedative and muscle relaxant effects. Sesquiterpenes, another class of compounds present in valerian, are believed to act on the same GABA receptors in the brain as benzodiazepines, a type of prescription medication used to treat anxiety and sleep disorders.

The volatile oils in valerian, which include bornyl acetate and camphene, are thought to contribute to the plant's calming and sleep-promoting effects. Additionally, the isovaleric acid present in valerian has been shown to have muscle relaxant properties.

Overall, the combination of these active constituents in valerian makes it a popular natural remedy for insomnia, anxiety, and muscle tension.

What are the medicinal preparations of Valeriana officinalis?

Valeriana officinalis has many medicinal preparations, such as dried root extracts, tinctures, and teas.

The roots and sometimes the rhizomes of the plant are used to make these preparations. One common preparation is a tincture, where the root material is soaked in a mixture of water and alcohol, creating a potent extract.

Dried root extracts are also commonly used, often in the form of capsules or powder. Additionally, valerian tea can be made by steeping dried or fresh valerian root in hot water.

These preparations have been used traditionally for various purposes, including as a sleep aid and to help reduce anxiety.

What are the possible side effects of Valeriana officinalis?

Valeriana officinalis can have some side effects if used improperly, such as causing stomach upset, including nausea and vomiting, in some individuals.

Additionally, it may lead to headaches or dizziness in certain cases. Some people may also experience restlessness, excitability, or even insomnia after using valerian, which might seem counterintuitive since it is often used for sleep disorders.

In some instances, valerian can cause dry mouth and draggy feelings the next day, similar to the side effects of some pharmaceutical sleep aids.

You should take some precautions when using Valeriana officinalis for medicinal purposes, such as properly identifying the plant to ensure you're using the correct species, as there are many plants with similar names.

What are the horticultural conditions of Valeriana officinalis?

Valeriana officinalis needs full sun to partial shade and well-drained soil to flourish.

When planting, ensure there is enough space, ideally 2 feet apart, as the plant can grow up to 5 feet tall and 2-3 feet wide. Valerian thrives in organic-rich soil, so consider adding compost or well-rotted manure to the planting area. When it comes to caring for valerian, keep the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged.

Harvest the roots in the fall of the second year, after the plant has flowered and the leaves have died back. To minimize damage to the root system, carefully dig around and beneath the plant, then gently lift it out of the ground. Valerian is susceptible to pests such as aphids, slugs, and nematodes, and diseases like verticillium wilt and powdery mildew.

Regularly inspect the plant and use organic or chemical controls as necessary to manage pests and diseases.

Valeriana officinalis is typically harvested in the second year, after the plant has established a strong root system.

Wash the roots thoroughly and cut off the tops. Dry the roots in a cool, dark place for 1-2 weeks, or until they are brittle.

Store the dried roots in an airtight container in a cool, dark place.
