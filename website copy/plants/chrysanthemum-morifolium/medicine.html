Chrysanthemum morifolium (Ju Hua) Medicinal Guide

Chrysanthemum morifolium, also known as Chrysanthemum tea, has a variety of medicinal benefits including calming the nerves, reducing inflammation, and treating respiratory disorders.

The constituents of Chrysanthemum morifolium include flavonoids, volatile oils, and polysaccharides, which are believed to be responsible for its medicinal properties. Preparations of Chrysanthemum morifolium typically involve steeping the flowers in hot water to make a tea, but it can also be consumed as a supplement or extract.

Potential side effects of Chrysanthemum morifolium may include allergic reactions, dizziness, and dry mouth. Precautions should be taken when consuming Chrysanthemum morifolium, particularly for those who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or have allergies to members of the Asteraceae family.

It is also recommended to consult with a healthcare provider before consuming Chrysanthemum morifolium as a treatment for any medical conditions.

What are the medicinal preparations of Chrysanthemum morifolium (Ju Hua)?

Chrysanthemum Morifolium has many medicinal preparations, such as infusions, decoctions, tinctures, fluid extracts, dry extracts, powders, capsules, compresses, poultices, ointments, salves, liniments, syrups, teas, and bath additives.

An infusion is made by steeping the flowers in hot water, creating a tea that is often used for treating sore throats and red, dry, or painful eyes. A decoction is a stronger concentration, created by simmering the flowers in water for a longer period of time, and is used for colds, fever, and headaches.

Tinctures and fluid extracts are concentrated liquid forms, while dry extracts are powdered forms, and both can be added to other substances for consumption. Powders and capsules are convenient forms for ingesting the flower, and are often used for respiratory and cardiovascular conditions. Compresses, poultices, ointments, salves, and liniments are topical forms, used for treating skin conditions, wounds, and inflammation.

Syrups are sweet, liquid forms often used for sore throats and coughs, teas are a popular form for treating colds and headaches, and bath additives can be used for relaxation and detoxification.

  • 7. Low blood pressure: Consumption of Chrysanthemum morifolium may cause a drop in blood pressure, especially in individuals with low blood pressure or those taking medication to lower their blood pressure.

What are the precautions to take when using Chrysanthemum morifolium (Ju Hua) medicinally?

It's important to take some precautions when using Chrysanthemum Morifolium for medicinal purposes, such as consulting a healthcare provider first, as they can provide advice on its safe use and potential interactions with other medications.

Pregnant and lactating women should avoid it, and individuals with allergies should be cautious due to the risk of allergic reactions. Those with diabetes should use it with caution, and blood pressure levels should be monitored, as it may lower blood pressure and should be avoided in cases of low blood pressure. Additionally, limiting sun exposure after use and being mindful of potential herb-drug interactions are crucial, as well as following dosage instructions carefully and discontinuing use if a skin rash occurs.

Lastly, it's important to avoid large quantities, and to stop use before surgery, consult a healthcare provider for prolonged use, and check for potential interactions with specific medications.
