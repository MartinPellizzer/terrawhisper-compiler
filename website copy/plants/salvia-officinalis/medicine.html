Salvia officinalis (Sage) Medicinal Guide

Salvia officinalis, commonly known as sage, has a long history of use in traditional medicine for its numerous health benefits.

The constituents of sage include volatile oils, flavonoids, and tannins, which contribute to its medicinal properties. Sage can be prepared in various ways, such as teas, essential oils, and tinctures, making it a versatile herb for different ailments.

However, it can cause side effects such as dry mouth, throat irritation, and seizures in large doses. Pregnant women and people with epilepsy should avoid using sage, as it may have adverse effects on their health.

Therefore, it is important to consult with a healthcare professional before using sage as a medicinal herb.

What are the active constituents of Salvia officinalis (Sage)?

Salvia officinalis has several active constituents, such as Thujone, which has been found to have antimicrobial and insecticidal properties.

Bornyl acetate, Camphene, and α-Pinene and β-Pinene are other constituents that contribute to sage's aroma and have been studied for their anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects. Myrcene and Limonene are terpenes that are also present in sage and have been researched for their potential cancer-fighting properties.

The phenolic acids, Rosmarinic acid, Carnosic acid, and Carnosol, have been found to have antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial effects. Additionally, the flavonoids Luteolin-7-O-glucoside, Apigenin-7-O-glucoside, and Cirsimaritin have been studied for their potential neuroprotective and anticancer effects.

Overall, these constituents contribute to the medicinal properties of sage, including its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial effects.

The following list shows the active constituents of Salvia officinalis.

  • 1. Thujone: a terpene known for its neurotoxic effects and potential psychoactive properties.
  • 2. Bornyl acetate: a compound with anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties, often used in aromatherapy.
  • 3. Camphene: a terpene with potential anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects, also used as a food flavoring.
  • 4. α-Pinene: a terpene with potential bronchodilator and anti-inflammatory effects, also known for its refreshing scent.
  • 5. β-Pinene: a terpene with potential anti-inflammatory and antibacterial effects, also used in aromatherapy.
  • 6. Myrcene: a terpene with potential sedative and analgesic effects, also used in the production of fragrances.
  • 7. Limonene: a terpene with potential mood-enhancing and stress-relieving effects, also used in aromatherapy and as a flavoring agent.
  • 8. Cineole: a compound with potential anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antispasmodic effects, also used in the treatment of respiratory conditions.

What are the medicinal preparations of Salvia officinalis (Sage)?

Salvia officinalis has several medicinal preparations, such as infusions, tinctures, and dry powder.

An infusion is made by steeping sage leaves in hot water, creating a tea that can be consumed to soothe sore throats and alleviate symptoms of digestive disorders. A tincture is a concentrated extract of sage in alcohol, often used to treat digestive issues and menstrual cramps. Dry powder made from ground sage leaves can be encapsulated and taken orally for its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Sage oil, salve, cream, and ointment are topical applications used to heal wounds, soothe skin irritations, and reduce inflammation.

Liniment, a liquid preparation, is applied to the skin to relieve muscle pain and inflammation. Mouthwash, gargle, toothpaste, and mouth spray utilize the antibacterial properties of sage to maintain oral hygiene and treat throat infections. Steam inhalation of sage can alleviate respiratory conditions, while bath additives and foot soaks can provide relaxation and relief from tired muscles. Suppositories and pessaries are used for localized treatment of gynecological issues.

Extracts and elixirs are concentrated forms of sage, consumed for their various medicinal benefits.

The following list shows the medicinal preparations of Salvia officinalis.

  • 1. Infusion: An infusion of Salvia officinalis, often called sage tea, is made by steeping fresh or dried leaves in hot water to extract the medicinal compounds.
  • 2. Tincture: A tincture of Salvia officinalis is a concentrated alcohol extract of the leaves, which can be used in smaller doses for its medicinal properties.
  • 3. Dry powder: Dry powder of Salvia officinalis leaves can be encapsulated or added to food and drinks for internal use, or applied topically as a dry rub for skin conditions.
  • 4. Capsules: Capsules filled with dried, ground Salvia officinalis leaves provide a standardized dose of the herb for internal use.
  • 5. Oil: Oil of Salvia officinalis is typically made by infusing the leaves in a carrier oil, and can be used topically for skin conditions or taken internally for its medicinal benefits.
  • 6. Salve: A salve is an ointment made by combining oil of Salvia officinalis with beeswax, and can be applied topically for its soothing, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties.
  • 7. Cream: A cream made with Salvia officinalis extract can be used topically for its moisturizing, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties, and may help soothe skin irritations.
  • 8. Liniment: A liniment made with Salvia officinalis extract can be used topically as a rub or compress to relieve muscle and joint pain, and to increase local blood flow.
  • 9. Mouthwash: A mouthwash made with Salvia officinalis extract can be used as a natural remedy for gum inflammation, throat infections, and bad breath.
  • 10. Gargle: A gargle made with Salvia officinalis extract can be used as a natural remedy for sore throats, tonsillitis, and other throat infections.

What are the possible side effects of using Salvia officinalis (Sage) for medicinal purposes?

Salvia officinalis can have side effects if used improperly.

These side effects may include upset stomach, dry mouth, and throat irritation due to its drying properties. Additionally, it can cause dizziness, skin irritations such as rashes, and hormonal changes as it contains estrogen-like compounds.

In rare cases, it can lead to more serious issues like seizures, confusion, slowed heart rate, and low blood pressure. Moreover, it can result in nausea and vomiting, increased sun sensitivity, breathing problems, and allergy symptoms.

Lastly, long-term use or excessive intake can potentially damage the kidneys.

The following list shows the possible side effects of Salvia officinalis.

  • 1. Upset stomach: Consuming Salvia officinalis can lead to digestive discomfort, including symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and abdominal cramps.
  • 2. Dry mouth: The use of Salvia officinalis can result in reduced saliva production, causing a dry, parched sensation in the mouth.
  • 3. Throat irritation: Inhaling Salvia officinalis vapors or smoke can lead to throat discomfort, including dryness, soreness, or scratchiness.
  • 4. Dizziness: Salvia officinalis may cause lightheadedness or unsteadiness, potentially increasing the risk of falls and accidents.
  • 5. Rash or skin irritation: Topical application of Salvia officinalis extracts or oils can result in skin reactions, such as redness, itching, or hives.
  • 6. Hormonal changes: Salvia officinalis may interfere with hormonal balance, potentially affecting menstrual cycles and reproductive health.
  • 7. Seizures: In rare cases, excessive consumption of Salvia officinalis can trigger seizures or convulsions, posing a risk to individuals with a history of seizure disorders.
  • 8. Confusion: Salvia officinalis use can lead to disorientation, mental fogginess, or difficulty concentrating, impairing cognitive function.
  • 9. Slowed heart rate: Salvia officinalis may have a depressant effect on the cardiovascular system, leading to a decrease in heart rate and potentially causing complications for individuals with heart conditions.
  • 10. Low blood pressure: Salvia officinalis can cause a drop in blood pressure, which may result in symptoms such as lightheadedness, weakness, or fainting.

What are the precautions to take when using Salvia officinalis (Sage) medicinally?

Before using Salvia officinalis for medicinal purposes, it's important to take some precautions such as consulting a healthcare provider first to ensure its safety and effectiveness for your specific condition.

Determining the correct dosage is crucial, as excessive consumption can lead to unpleasant side effects. It's essential to check for any allergies to Salvia officinalis or related plants and to avoid its use during pregnancy or breastfeeding. Additionally, monitoring interactions with medications and discontinuing use in case of side effects are important precautions to take.

Furthermore, individuals should limit use with other sedatives or depressants, avoid using it before driving or operating machinery, and store it in a cool, dry place, keeping it out of reach of children. Those with pre-existing medical conditions should use caution, limit exposure to sunlight while using, and not use it in place of prescribed medications. It's also important to discuss its use with a healthcare provider before surgery, use caution with alcohol consumption, and discontinue use if it becomes habit-forming.

Following label instructions carefully, consulting a healthcare provider for prolonged use, and ensuring the package is not damaged before use are also necessary precautions.

The following list shows the precautions to take when using Salvia officinalis.

  • 1. Consult a healthcare provider first: Before using Salvia officinalis for medicinal purposes, it's essential to consult a healthcare professional to ensure it's safe and appropriate for your specific health condition and to determine the correct dosage.
  • 2. Determine correct dosage: Establish the appropriate dosage under the guidance of a healthcare provider, as consuming too much Salvia officinalis can lead to adverse effects.
  • 3. Avoid excessive consumption: Limit your intake of Salvia officinalis, as excessive consumption may cause hallucinations, dizziness, and other unpleasant symptoms.
  • 4. Check for allergies: Before using Salvia officinalis, make sure you are not allergic to it or any of its components to prevent an allergic reaction.
  • 5. Avoid during pregnancy or breastfeeding: Pregnant or breastfeeding women should avoid using Salvia officinalis, as its safety and potential effects on the fetus or infant are unknown.
  • 6. Monitor interactions with medications: Be aware of potential interactions between Salvia officinalis and any medications you are currently taking and consult a healthcare provider for guidance.
  • 7. Discontinue use in case of side effects: If you experience any side effects or negative reactions after using Salvia officinalis, stop using it immediately and consult a healthcare professional.
  • 8. Limit use with other sedatives or depressants: Using Salvia officinalis with other sedatives or depressants can increase the risk of adverse effects, so limit its use in these situations.
  • 9. Avoid use before driving or operating machinery: Salvia officinalis may cause dizziness or impair coordination, so avoid using it before driving or operating heavy machinery to ensure safety.
  • 10. Store in a cool, dry place: Keep Salvia officinalis in a cool, dry place, away from direct sunlight and excessive heat, to maintain its quality and potency.
