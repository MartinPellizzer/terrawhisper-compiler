10 Best Active Constituents of Salvia officinalis (Sage)

Salvia officinalis, commonly known as sage, contains several active constituents that have medicinal properties.

The most notable of these include volatile oils such as thujone, camphor, and cineole, which have been shown to have anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial effects. Additionally, sage contains phenolic acids, flavonoids, and diterpenes, which have been found to have various health benefits, including reducing inflammation, protecting against oxidative stress, and potentially improving cognitive function.

Rosmarinic acid, a specific phenolic acid found in sage, has been studied for its potential neuroprotective effects. Furthermore, sage is also known to contain tannins, which have astringent properties and can be used to treat minor wounds and skin irritations.

Overall, the active constituents of Salvia officinalis contribute to its wide range of medicinal uses, including as an anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, antioxidant, and neuroprotective agent.

1. Thujone

The thujone contained in Salvia Officinalis is a natural monoterpene ketone known for its potent biological activities.

Its main medicinal properties include antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory effects, making it a valuable constituent in traditional medicine. This Salvia officinalis compound has been found to help relieve conditions such as respiratory infections, skin inflammation, and poor digestion. The thujone is primarily found in the leaves and stems of the Salvia officinalis plant.

However, when used improperly or in large amounts, thujone can cause side effects such as seizures, nerve damage, and stomach irritation, so it should be used with caution.

2. Bornyl acetate

The bornyl acetate contained in Salvia Officinalis is a terpene ester that contributes to the plant's aroma and medicinal properties.

Its main medicinal properties include anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and local anesthetic effects. This Salvia officinalis constituent can help relieve conditions such as sore throats, bronchitis, and respiratory issues by reducing inflammation and spasms in the respiratory tract. The leaves and essential oil of the Salvia officinalis plant contain this constituent.

Improper use of Salvia officinalis bornyl acetate may cause side effects such as skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential harm if ingested in large amounts.

3. Camphene

The camphene contained in Salvia Officinalis is a bicyclic monoterpene with a distinctive aroma and several medicinal properties.

It has anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties, making it a valuable constituent in sage. Camphene helps relieve several health conditions, including skin conditions, respiratory issues, and inflammation. It is found mainly in the leaves of the Salvia Officinalis plant.

However, when used improperly or in excess, camphene can cause irritation to the skin and mucous membranes, and it may exacerbate respiratory conditions.

4. α-Pinene

The α-pinene contained in Salvia Officinalis is a major terpene component of its essential oil, known for its anti-inflammatory, bronchodilatory, and antimicrobial properties.

It has been shown to help alleviate symptoms of asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and other respiratory conditions. Additionally, α-pinene has been found to aid in the relief of pain, anxiety, and inflammation. This constituent is primarily found in the leaves of the Salvia officinalis plant, although it can also be extracted from its stems and flowers.

Improper use of α-pinene may result in skin and mucous membrane irritation, nausea, and dizziness.

5. β-Pinene

The β-pinene contained in Salvia Officinalis is a type of chemical compound called a terpene, known for its piney aroma.

Its main medicinal properties include anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and analgesic effects. This Salvia officinalis constituent can help relieve conditions such as respiratory infections, skin irritations, and digestive issues. The main parts of the Salvia officinalis plant that have this constituent are the leaves and flowers.

Some possible side effects of this Salvia officinalis constituent if used improperly include skin irritation, allergic reactions, and respiratory problems if ingested in large amounts.

6. Myrcene

The myrcene contained in Salvia Officinalis is a type of terpene known for its aromatic properties and potential medicinal benefits.

Its main medicinal properties include anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antibacterial effects, making it a valuable constituent in various herbal remedies. Myrcene has been shown to help relieve several health conditions, such as pain, inflammation, and infection. This constituent is found primarily in the leaves of the Salvia officinalis plant.

However, if used improperly or in excessive amounts, myrcene may cause adverse effects such as dizziness, dry mouth, and impaired coordination.

7. Limonene

The limonene contained in Salvia Officinalis is a terpene that contributes to the plant's citrusy aroma.

Its main medicinal properties include anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and anticancer effects. This Salvia officinalis constituent helps relieve conditions such as digestive disorders, skin irritations, and even certain types of cancer. Limonene is found mainly in the leaves of the Salvia officinalis plant.

Improper use of Salvia officinalis limonene can lead to side effects such as skin irritation, allergic reactions, and digestive issues.

8. Cineole

The cineole contained in Salvia Officinalis is a type of chemical compound called a terpene, which is known for its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties.

Its main medicinal properties include improving cognitive function, reducing inflammation, and providing relief from respiratory ailments. This Salvia officinalis constituent has been shown to help relieve conditions such as asthma, nasal congestion, and even certain types of bacterial infections. The cineole can be found in the leaves and flowers of the Salvia officinalis plant.

Some possible side effects of Salvia officinalis cineole, if used improperly, include skin irritation, mouth ulcers, and nausea.

9. Bornel

The bornel contained in Salvia Officinalis is a terpene alcohol that contributes to the plant's essential oil.

Its main medicinal properties include antibacterial, antifungal, and anti-inflammatory effects. This Salvia officinalis constituent has been found to help relieve conditions such as skin infections, dandruff, and throat infections. Bornel is primarily found in the leaves of the Salvia officinalis plant.

Improper use of this Salvia officinalis constituent can lead to potential side effects such as skin irritation, dry mouth, and dizziness.

10. Salvene

The salvene contained in Salvia Officinalis is a unique diterpene that has been found to possess significant medicinal properties.

Its main medicinal properties include anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial activities, making it a valuable constituent in various pharmaceutical and cosmeceutical applications. Salvia officinalis salvene has been shown to help relieve several health conditions, such as skin irritations, bacterial and fungal infections, and inflammatory disorders. This constituent is primarily found in the leaves of the Salvia officinalis plant, although it can also be extracted from the stems and flowers.

Improper use of salvia officinalis salvene may result in several side effects, including skin irritation, allergic reactions, and potential drug interactions, particularly with medications that suppress the immune system.
