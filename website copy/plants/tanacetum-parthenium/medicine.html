Tanacetum parthenium (Feverfew) Medicinal Guide

Tanacetum parthenium, also known as feverfew, has a long history of use in traditional medicine for treating a variety of ailments, including migraines, arthritis, and menstrual cramps.

The plant contains several active compounds, including parthenolide, which is believed to be responsible for its medicinal properties. Preparations of Tanacetum parthenium can be made from dried leaves and flowers, which can be taken in the form of capsules, teas, or tinctures.

However, it is important to note that Tanacetum parthenium can cause side effects such as mouth ulcers, digestive upset, and allergic reactions in some people. Additionally, pregnant women and individuals with allergies to other members of the Asteraceae family (such as chamomile and ragweed) should avoid using Tanacetum parthenium, as it may cause uterine contractions or allergic reactions.

As with any medication or herbal remedy, it is always best to consult with a healthcare provider before starting to use Tanacetum parthenium.

What are the active constituents of Tanacetum parthenium (Feverfew)?

Tanacetum parthenium has several active constituents, such as parthenolide, which is the most well-known and well-studied compound in feverfew.

Parthenolide is a sesquiterpene lactone that has been shown to have anti-inflammatory, anti-proliferative, and anti-migraine properties. Other sesquiterpene lactones found in feverfew include artabsin, costunolide, tanapartholides A, B, and C, 8β-hydroxycostunolide, 15-hydroxyparthenolide, 3-β,4-Δ,11-trihydroxyrepetalone, repetalone, tanacetin, parthenolide 16-acetate, 11,13-dihydrocostunolide, 11,13-dihydroparthenolide, 4-desacetylparthenolide, and several guaianolides, such as 3-β,4-Δ-dihydroxyguaian-7-one, 3-β,4-Δ-dihydroxyguaian-8-one, 3-β,4-Δ-dihydroxyguaian-11-one, 3-β,4-Δ-dihydroxyguaian-11,13-dione, 3-β,4-Δ-dihydroxyguaian-6,11,13-trione.

These compounds have been found to have various biological activities, including anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and cytotoxic effects, and are believed to contribute to the medicinal properties of feverfew, which are traditionally used to treat migraines, arthritis, and other inflammatory conditions.

What are the medicinal preparations of Tanacetum parthenium (Feverfew)?

Tanacetum parthenium has several medicinal preparations, such as infusion, tincture, dried leaves, capsules, tablets, and tea.

The preparation methods can vary, but they all aim to extract or preserve the medicinal properties of the feverfew plant. An infusion is made by steeping the dried or fresh leaves in hot water, while a tincture is created by soaking the leaves in alcohol. Dried leaves can be consumed directly or added to various dishes, while capsules and tablets offer a convenient and measured way of consuming feverfew. Tea can be made by steeping dried or fresh leaves in hot water.

Fresh juice can be extracted from the leaves and stems, and oil extract can be obtained through steam distillation. Topical preparations include ointment, salve, cream, balm, liniment, poultice, and compress, which are used to treat skin conditions and inflammation. Fluid extract is a concentrated form of the herb, prepared by soaking the plant in alcohol or water. Lyophilized powder is a dried form of the herb, prepared by freeze-drying, and glycerite is a preparation made by soaking the herb in glycerin.

Standardized extract is a concentrated form of the herb, with a consistent level of active compounds, while homeopathic remedies are highly diluted forms of the herb, used in homeopathic medicine.
