10 possible side effects of Tanacetum parthenium (Feverfew) if used improperly

Tanacetum parthenium, also known as feverfew, is a herbal supplement that can have various health benefits when used properly, but it can also cause several side effects if misused.

One potential side effect of improper use is mouth ulcers or stomach upset, as the herb contains compounds that can irritate the digestive tract. In addition, feverfew can increase the risk of bleeding, so it may interact with blood-thinning medications and lead to excessive bleeding.

Prolonged use or high doses of feverfew can also cause muscle cramps, joint stiffness, and skin rashes. Furthermore, pregnant women and children should avoid feverfew, as it can lead to complications such as miscarriage or allergic reactions.

Therefore, it is essential to consult with a healthcare professional before taking feverfew or any other herbal supplement.

1. Mouth Ulcers

Mouth ulcers caused by Tanacetum Parthenium refer to small, painful lesions that can develop on the mucous membranes of the mouth as a side effect of using this herbal remedy.

The main active constituents in Tanacetum parthenium that can cause mouth ulcers include parthenolide and other sesquiterpene lactones, which can have irritant effects on the mucous membranes. This side effect is most commonly associated with the use of feverfew in the form of dried leaves or capsules, particularly when used in high doses or for prolonged periods of time.

People who are more likely to experience mouth ulcers as a side effect of using Tanacetum parthenium include those with a sensitivity or allergy to plants in the Asteraceae family, as well as those with a history of digestive tract problems or mouth ulcers. To avoid developing mouth ulcers, it is recommended to use Tanacetum parthenium under the guidance of a healthcare professional, starting with a low dose and gradually increasing as tolerated. It is also important to avoid chewing or crushing the leaves, as this can release higher levels of the irritant compounds and increase the risk of mouth ulcers.

Additionally, regularly rinsing the mouth with water after taking feverfew and practicing good oral hygiene can help reduce the risk of developing mouth ulcers.

2. Digestive Problems

Digestive problems caused by Tanacetum Parthenium refers to gastrointestinal symptoms such as abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea.

These side effects are mainly attributed to the sesquiterpene lactones, particularly parthenolide, found in feverfew. The dried leaves, flowers, and stems are commonly used in medicinal preparations, and when misused or taken in large amounts, can lead to digestive issues.

Individuals who are sensitive to feverfew, have a history of allergic reactions to chamomile or ragweed, or are pregnant and breastfeeding are more likely to experience digestive problems. To avoid these side effects, it is recommended to start with small doses, under the guidance of a healthcare professional, and gradually increase the dosage.

Additionally, it is important to avoid consuming feverfew on an empty stomach and to drink plenty of water when taking it.

3. Joint Pain

Joint pain caused by Tanacetum Parthenium refers to discomfort or inflammation in the joints that may occur as a side effect of using this herbal supplement.

The main active constituents in Tanacetum Parthenium that can cause joint pain include parthenolide and other sesquiterpene lactones, which can have inflammatory effects in some individuals. This side effect is most commonly associated with misuse of feverfew in the form of dried leaves, which are often used to make teas or capsules, or as liquid extracts. People who are more likely to experience joint pain from Tanacetum Parthenium are those who are sensitive or allergic to its active constituents, as well as those who take excessive doses or use the herb for extended periods of time.

To avoid joint pain and other side effects, it is important to follow recommended dosages and use Tanacetum Parthenium under the guidance of a healthcare provider, who can help determine the appropriate preparation, dosage, and duration of use based on individual health needs and circumstances.

4. Skin Rash

Skin rash caused by Tanacetum Parthenium refers to a skin irritation that can occur as a side effect of using this herbal supplement.

The main active constituents in Tanacetum parthenium that can cause this side effect are sesquiterpene lactones, particularly parthenolide. This skin reaction can be triggered by misuse of feverfew in various medicinal preparations, such as dried leaves for tea, fresh leaves for chewing, or feverfew supplements in the form of tablets or capsules.

Individuals who are more likely to experience this side effect are those with a history of allergic reactions to daisies or other plants in the Asteraceae/Compositae family, which includes feverfew. To avoid this side effect, it is essential to start with a low dose and gradually increase it over time, and to avoid consuming large quantities of feverfew or using it for extended periods.

Additionally, monitoring for any signs of skin irritation and discontinuing use if a rash occurs is crucial.

Nausea caused by Tanacetum Parthenium refers to the feeling of discomfort or unease in the stomach that can lead to vomiting.

This side effect can be attributed to the main active constituents of feverfew, which include parthenolide and tanetin. These compounds can cause irritation in the digestive tract, leading to nausea.

The main medicinal preparations of feverfew that can cause this side effect if misused include dried leaves, tablets, and capsules. Individuals who are more likely to experience nausea as a side effect of feverfew are those who are sensitive to its active constituents or who consume large doses of the herb.

To avoid this side effect, it is important to follow the recommended dosage and consult with a healthcare provider before using feverfew, especially if taking other medications or have a history of stomach problems.

6. Vomiting

Vomiting caused by Tanacetum Parthenium refers to the forceful expulsion of stomach contents through the mouth as a result of using this herb.

This side effect is primarily attributed to the presence of active constituents such as parthenolide and tanetin. The main medicinal preparations that can cause vomiting if misused include feverfew supplements, teas, and tinctures.

Individuals who are more likely to experience vomiting as a side effect of feverfew use are those with a history of gastrointestinal sensitivities, those who consume excessive dosages, and those who combine feverfew with other drugs or herbs. To avoid this side effect, users should start with low dosages, gradually increase the dosage over time, and consult with a healthcare professional before combining feverfew with other substances.

8. Allergic Reactions

Allergic reactions caused by Tanacetum Parthenium refer to abnormal immune system responses that can occur after exposure to this plant.

These reactions are typically caused by the active constituents in feverfew, including parthenolide and tanetin. Medicinal preparations such as dried leaves, capsules, and tinctures can cause allergic reactions if misused or used in excessive amounts.

Individuals with a history of allergies to members of the Asteraceae family, which includes feverfew, as well as those with allergies to chamomile, ragweed, or daisies, are at a higher risk of experiencing allergic reactions to feverfew. To avoid these side effects, it is recommended to start with a low dose and gradually increase, while closely monitoring for any adverse reactions.

Consulting with a healthcare provider or herbalist before use is also advisable, especially for those with a history of allergies or those taking other medications.

Dizziness caused by Tanacetum Parthenium refers to a feeling of lightheadedness, unsteadiness, or vertigo that can occur as a side effect of using this herb.

The main active constituents in Tanacetum parthenium that can cause dizziness include parthenolide and tanetin. These compounds can affect the central nervous system and cause dizziness, particularly when taken in large doses or when combined with certain medications.

The main medicinal preparations of Tanacetum parthenium that can cause dizziness if misused include dried leaves, capsules, and tinctures. Individuals who are more likely to experience dizziness from Tanacetum parthenium include those with a history of motion sickness, sensitivity to feverfew, or those taking blood thinners or other medications that affect the central nervous system.

To avoid dizziness when using Tanacetum parthenium, it is important to follow dosage instructions carefully, avoid combining it with other medications without consulting a healthcare provider, and discontinue use if dizziness or other side effects occur.

To avoid experiencing tiredness when using Tanacetum Parthenium, it is recommended to follow the recommended dosage, avoid using it for extended periods of time, and consult with a healthcare provider before using feverfew in combination with other medications or supplements.
