Melissa officinalis (Lemon Balm) Medicinal Guide

Melissa officinalis, also known as lemon balm, has a long history of use in traditional medicine for its calming and soothing properties.

The main constituents of Melissa officinalis include essential oils, tannins, and phenolic acids, which are believed to contribute to its medicinal effects. Preparations of Melissa officinalis can be made using dried leaves to brew teas, or in capsule or tincture form.

Potential side effects of Melissa officinalis may include stomach upset, dizziness, and headaches, particularly when taken in large doses. It is important to note that Melissa officinalis may interact with certain medications, such as sedatives and thyroid medications, and should be used with caution in individuals with existing medical conditions, such as thyroid disorders.

As with any natural remedy, it is always best to consult with a healthcare provider before starting any new treatment.

What are the active constituents of Melissa officinalis (Lemon Balm)?

Melissa officinalis has several active constituents, such as Rosmarinic Acid, which has been found to have potential antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties.

Citral and Citronellal are other constituents that contribute to the plant's lemony scent and have been studied for their potential antimicrobial and insect-repellent properties. Geraniol and Limonene are terpenes that also contribute to the plant's aroma and have been researched for their potential anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and anticancer effects. Linalool and Nerol are terpene alcohols that have been found to have potential calming and sedative effects, and are also being studied for their antimicrobial properties.

Other constituents of lemon balm, such as Geraniol Acetate, Citral Acetate, Caryophyllene Oxide, β-Eudesmol, Germacrene D, β-Caryophyllene, Spathulenol, Ledene Oxide, Methyl Eugenol, trans-Ocimene, α-Pinene, Camphene, and β-Pinene, have also been identified, but their potential medicinal properties have not been as extensively researched.

The following list shows the active constituents of Melissa officinalis.

  • 1. Rosmarinic Acid: A phenolic compound with potent antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial properties.
  • 2. Citral: A lemon-scented compound with potential antimicrobial, antifungal, and insecticidal effects.
  • 3. Citronellal: A fragrant alcohol that contributes to the characteristic aroma of Melissa officinalis and has been studied for its potential insect-repellent properties.
  • 4. Geraniol: A rose-scented compound with potential antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective effects.
  • 5. Limonene: A cyclic monoterpene that contributes to the citrusy aroma of Melissa officinalis and has been studied for its potential antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and anticancer effects.
  • 6. Linalool: A terpene alcohol that gives Melissa officinalis its sweet, floral scent and has been studied for its potential anxiolytic, analgesic, and anti-inflammatory effects.
  • 7. Nerol: A hydroxylated derivative of geraniol, with a rosy scent and potential antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory effects.
  • 8. Geraniol Acetate: An ester of geraniol that has been studied for its potential antifungal and insecticidal properties.
  • 9. Citral Acetate: An ester of citral that has been studied for its potential antimicrobial and insecticidal properties.
  • 10. Caryophyllene Oxide: A sesquiterpene that has been identified as a major constituent of the essential oil of Melissa officinalis and has been studied for its potential anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and anticancer effects.

What are the medicinal preparations of Melissa officinalis (Lemon Balm)?

Melissa officinalis has several medicinal preparations, such as infusions, tinctures, and dry extracts.

An infusion is made by steeping the leaves in hot water to extract the medicinal compounds, and it is often used for its calming and sedative effects. Tinctures are made by soaking the leaves in alcohol or glycerin, and they can be taken orally or used topically.

Dry extracts are made by concentrating the medicinal compounds through various methods, and they can be found in capsule or tablet form. Fluid extracts are similar to tinctures but more concentrated, and they can be used in a similar manner. Essential oils, ointments, creams, salves, and balms are all topical preparations that contain lemon balm's medicinal compounds, and they are used for a variety of purposes such as wound healing, skin soothing, and pain relief.

Other preparations such as syrups, spirituous tinctures, glycerites, lip balms, tea bags, bath bags, and foot soak bags are also available and used depending on the desired administration route and the specific medicinal purpose.

The following list shows the medicinal preparations of Melissa officinalis.

  • 1. Infusion: A Melissa officinalis infusion is a hot water extract of the leaves and flowers, often used for its calming effects and to soothe digestive discomfort.
  • 2. Tincture: A Melissa officinalis tincture is an alcohol extract of the leaves and flowers, typically used for its antiviral, antispasmodic, and calming properties.
  • 3. Dry extract: A Melissa officinalis dry extract is a concentrated powdered extract, often standardized to contain specific amounts of active compounds, used for its anxiolytic, nootropic, and neuroprotective effects.
  • 4. Fluid extract: A Melissa officinalis fluid extract is a hydroalcoholic extract of the leaves and flowers, which can be used for its sedative, antidepressant, and antioxidant properties.
  • 5. Capsules: Melissa officinalis capsules contain dried leaf or standardized extract powder, providing a convenient and measured method for consuming the herb for stress relief, cognitive support, and potential antiviral effects.
  • 6. Compress: A Melissa officinalis compress is a cloth soaked in an infusion or diluted tincture, applied topically to soothe skin irritations, promote wound healing, and alleviate headaches or muscle tension.
  • 7. Poultice: A Melissa officinalis poultice is a mashed fresh plant or heated dried herb applied directly to the skin, used to reduce inflammation, relieve pain, and promote healing of minor wounds, insect bites, or skin infections.
  • 8. Essential oil: Melissa officinalis essential oil is a highly concentrated, aromatic liquid obtained through steam distillation of the leaves and flowers, used for its potent antimicrobial, antiviral, and nervine properties, often in aromatherapy or diluted for topical use.
  • 9. Ointment: A Melissa officinalis ointment is a semi-solid preparation, typically containing the essential oil or extract, used for its soothing and antimicrobial effects on the skin, treating minor wounds, rashes, and skin irritations.
  • 10. Cream: A Melissa officinalis cream is an emulsion of the herb's extract or essential oil in a water and oil base, providing a soothing, moisturizing, and potentially antimicrobial skincare product for various skin conditions, including acne, rosacea, and eczema.

What are the possible side effects of using Melissa officinalis (Lemon Balm) for medicinal purposes?

Melissa officinalis can have side effects if used improperly.

These potential side effects include gastrointestinal issues such as nausea, abdominal pain, diarrhea, and vomiting. Additionally, it can cause neurological symptoms like dizziness, headaches, and insomnia.

Respiratory problems may also occur, as shown by the possible side effect of wheezing. Furthermore, users may experience skin irritations, demonstrated by the side effect of rash, as well as muscle cramps and increased appetite.

Other possible side effects include dry mouth, fatigue, sweating, drowsiness, and an increased likelihood of experiencing allergic reactions, which can manifest as a rash.

The following list shows the possible side effects of Melissa officinalis.

  • 1. Nausea: Feeling of stomach discomfort or unease with a desire to vomit.
  • 2. Dizziness: Lightheadedness, vertigo, or feeling faint.
  • 3. Wheezing: High-pitched whistling sound while breathing, often due to a narrowed airway.
  • 4. Abdominal pain: Discomfort or cramping in the area between the chest and hips.
  • 5. Diarrhea: Frequent and loose bowel movements, often leading to dehydration.
  • 6. Drowsiness: Sleepy, lethargic, or fatigued feeling, possibly causing reduced mental alertness.
  • 7. Increased appetite: An enhanced desire to eat, potentially leading to weight gain.
  • 8. Vomiting: Forcible expulsion of stomach contents through the mouth.
  • 9. Headache: Pain or discomfort in the head, often accompanied by pressure in the forehead or temples.
  • 10. Rash: A noticeable change in the skin's color or texture, often itchy or painful.

What are the precautions to take when using Melissa officinalis (Lemon Balm) medicinally?

Before using Melissa officinalis for medicinal purposes, it's important to take some precautions such as consulting a healthcare provider first to ensure it's safe for your individual health situation.

It's also crucial to avoid excessive consumption, monitor medication interactions, and check for allergic reactions before use. Furthermore, it's recommended to limit sun exposure after application, avoid use during pregnancy and breastfeeding, and discontinue use if a rash occurs.

Additionally, individuals should not ingest concentrated tinctures or use for children without consulting a doctor, and should avoid use if suffering from glaucoma or gallbladder inflammation. It's also important to limit exposure to sunlight while taking, discontinue use before surgery, and avoid use if taking sedatives, antidepressants, or thyroid hormone medication.

Finally, individuals should discontinue use if experiencing dizziness, and limit intake if diagnosed with hypothyroidism, avoid use with alcohol, and not use for extended periods.

The following list shows the precautions to take when using Melissa officinalis.

  • 1. Consult a healthcare provider first: Before using Melissa officinalis for medicinal purposes, it's essential to consult a healthcare provider to ensure it's safe and appropriate for your specific health condition and to determine the correct dosage.
  • 2. Avoid excessive consumption: Limit your intake of Melissa officinalis to avoid potential adverse effects, such as stomach upset and headaches.
  • 3. Monitor medication interactions: Be aware of potential interactions between Melissa officinalis and any medications you're currently taking, as it may affect their effectiveness or cause unwanted side effects.
  • 4. Check for allergic reactions: Before using Melissa officinalis, perform a patch test to check for any allergic reactions, which can include skin irritation, itching, or swelling.
  • 5. Limit sun exposure after application: After applying Melissa officinalis topically, avoid sun exposure for at least 12 hours, as it can increase skin sensitivity and cause sunburn or skin irritation.
  • 6. Avoid use during pregnancy and breastfeeding: Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid using Melissa officinalis, as its safety and potential effects on the fetus or infant are not well-studied.
  • 7. Do not ingest concentrated tinctures: Avoid ingesting concentrated Melissa officinalis tinctures, as they can cause digestive issues, nausea, and vomiting.
  • 8. Dilute essential oils before use: When using Melissa officinalis essential oil, dilute it with a carrier oil before applying it to the skin, as undiluted essential oils can cause skin irritation and inflammation.
  • 9. Discontinue use if rash occurs: If you develop a rash or other skin irritation after using Melissa officinalis, discontinue use immediately and consult a healthcare provider.
  • 10. Do not use for children without consulting a doctor: Melissa officinalis should not be used for children without consulting a doctor, as its safety and effectiveness for pediatric use have not been established.
