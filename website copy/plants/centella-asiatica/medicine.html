Centella asiatica (Gotu Kola) Medicinal Guide

Centella asiatica, also known as gotu kola, is a medicinal plant that has been used for centuries in traditional medicine to treat a variety of health conditions.

The main constituents of Centella asiatica include triterpenoid saponins, triterpene acids, and flavonoids, which have been found to possess anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant properties. Preparations of Centella asiatica can be made into various forms, such as teas, capsules, tinctures, and topical creams, depending on the desired use and route of administration.

While Centella asiatica is generally considered safe, some potential side effects include stomach upset, nausea, dizziness, and headache, especially when taken in large doses. To minimize the risk of side effects, it is important to follow the recommended dosage and consult with a healthcare provider before starting any new supplement regimen.

Additionally, Centella asiatica may interact with certain medications, such as blood thinners and sedatives, so it is important to inform healthcare providers of any supplement use.

What are the active constituents of Centella asiatica (Gotu Kola)?

Centella Asiatica has several active constituents that contribute to its medicinal properties.

Asiaticoside and Madecassoside are two triterpenoid saponins that have been shown to possess anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and wound-healing properties. Madecassic Acid and Asiatic Acid are other triterpenoids found in Centella Asiatica that have been studied for their potential benefits in treating various skin conditions, such as acne, eczema, and psoriasis.

Centelloside and Centellose are two sugar components found in the plant that may contribute to its adaptogenic and nootropic effects. Isothankunic Acid, Brahmoside, and Thankuniside are other active constituents that have been found to have anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Additionally, Centella Asiatica contains β-Amyrin and α-Humulene, two compounds that have been shown to have potential therapeutic applications due to their anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and anticancer properties.

Cryptotanshinone and Tanshinone IIA are compounds that are not unique to Centella Asiatica but are sometimes found in extracts of the plant and have been studied for their potential cardiovascular and neuroprotective effects.

The following list shows the active constituents of Centella asiatica.

  • 1. Asiaticoside: A triterpenoid glycoside, shown to have potential in promoting wound healing and collagen synthesis.
  • 2. Madecassoside: A triterpene saponin, known for its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and skin-soothing properties.
  • 3. Madecassic Acid: A triterpene acid, demonstrated to have wound healing, anti-inflammatory, and potential anticancer effects.
  • 4. Asiatic Acid: A triterpene acid, exhibiting various bioactivities such as anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and skin-repair properties.
  • 5. Centelloside: A saponin found in Centella asiatica, with potential antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects.
  • 6. Centellose: A polysaccharide, acting as a mucilaginous dietary fiber in Centella asiatica, aiding in digestion and intestinal health.
  • 7. Isothankunic Acid: A prenylated flavone, displaying antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and potential cytotoxic activities.
  • 8. Brahmoside: A biflavone, exhibiting antioxidant and potential anticancer properties.
  • 9. Thankuniside: A phenolic compound, with antioxidant and potential anti-inflammatory effects.
  • 10. Centellic Acid: A triterpene acid, displaying various bioactivities such as anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and potential anticancer effects.

What are the medicinal preparations of Centella asiatica (Gotu Kola)?

Centella Asiatica has many medicinal preparations, such as infusions, tinctures, capsules, and tablets, which are taken orally for various health benefits.

The herb can also be consumed as a tea, and in syrup form for those who have difficulty swallowing pills. Topically, Centella Asiatica can be found in ointments, creams, gels, salves, oil, and extract, which are used to treat wounds, burns, and skin conditions like eczema and psoriasis. Additionally, it can be made into a lotion or balm for massaging affected areas of the body.

Each preparation has its own advantages and is used depending on the desired effect and route of administration.

The following list shows the medicinal preparations of Centella asiatica.

  • 1. Infusion: A tea made by steeping Centella asiatica leaves, often used for its potential diuretic and anti-anxiety properties.
  • 2. Tincture: A concentrated extract of Centella asiatica in alcohol, taken orally for its potential cognitive and skin benefits.
  • 3. Capsules: Dry powder of Centella asiatica encapsulated, typically used for its potential memory and anti-inflammatory effects.
  • 4. Tablets: Compressed form of Centella asiatica powder, often used for its potential to improve circulation and anxiety.
  • 5. Powder: Finely ground Centella asiatica leaves, commonly used in traditional medicine as a digestive aid and to improve memory.
  • 6. Ointment: A semi-solid preparation of Centella asiatica mixed with a base, used topically for its potential wound healing and anti-inflammatory properties.
  • 7. Cream: A water-based emulsion of Centella asiatica, used topically for its potential to soothe and hydrate skin.
  • 8. Gel: A semi-solid preparation of Centella asiatica in a water or alcohol base, used topically for its potential to reduce inflammation and promote wound healing.
  • 9. Salve: A thick, waxy preparation of Centella asiatica, used topically for its potential to protect and soothe skin.
  • 10. Oil: A liquid extract of Centella asiatica in a carrier oil, used topically for its potential to improve skin health and reduce inflammation.

What are the possible side effects of using Centella asiatica (Gotu Kola) for medicinal purposes?

Centella Asiatica can have side effects if used improperly, such as causing symptoms like headache, upset stomach, nausea, dizziness, and skin irritation.

In some cases, it may also lead to mouth ulcers, fatigue, and drowsiness, as well as changes in bowel movements, including increased frequency. Other potential side effects include dry mouth, changes in appetite, and vision changes. In rare instances, it may also cause more serious symptoms such as insomnia, confusion, hives, or rash.

Therefore, it is important to follow proper dosage instructions and consult with a healthcare provider before using Centella Asiatica.

The following list shows the possible side effects of Centella asiatica.

  • 1. Headache: Centella asiatica may cause headaches due to possible neurological effects.
  • 2. Upset stomach: Centella asiatica might cause digestive disturbances, leading to upset stomach.
  • 3. Nausea: Some individuals may experience nausea as a result of taking Centella asiatica.
  • 4. Dizziness: Centella asiatica could lead to dizziness, possibly due to its impact on circulation or neurological system.
  • 5. Skin irritation: Topical application of Centella asiatica might cause skin irritation in some individuals.
  • 6. Mouth ulcers: In rare cases, Centella asiatica may cause mouth ulcers or sores.
  • 7. Fatigue: Some people may experience fatigue as a side effect of taking Centella asiatica.
  • 8. Drowsiness: Centella asiatica might induce drowsiness, possibly due to its calming effects.
  • 9. Increased bowel movements: Centella asiatica could increase bowel movements, acting as a natural laxative.
  • 10. Dry mouth: Centella asiatica might lead to dry mouth, affecting saliva production.

What are the precautions to take when using Centella asiatica (Gotu Kola) medicinally?

It's important to take some precautions when using Centella Asiatica for medicinal purposes, such as consulting a healthcare provider first, as they can provide guidance based on your medical history and current medications.

Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid using it, and it's crucial to check for allergic reactions before use. Additionally, limiting sun exposure after use and avoiding the combination of Centella Asiatica with sedatives or caffeine is important. Monitoring blood sugar levels, checking for interactions with other medications, and limiting dosage to recommended amounts are also necessary precautions.

Discontinuing use if experiencing side effects, consulting a healthcare provider before surgery, avoiding use if suffering from certain medical conditions, checking for interactions with food, following storage instructions carefully, consulting a healthcare provider for children's use, and gradually reducing dosage when stopping use are all essential precautions to ensure safe and effective use of Centella Asiatica.

The following list shows the precautions to take when using Centella asiatica.

  • 1. Consult a healthcare provider first: It's important to get professional medical advice before starting to use Centella asiatica to ensure it's safe and appropriate for your specific health condition and to determine the correct dosage.
  • 2. Avoid in pregnancy and breastfeeding: Centella asiatica is not recommended during pregnancy and breastfeeding as it may have adverse effects on the fetus or infant.
  • 3. Check for allergic reactions: Discontinue use and seek medical attention if you experience symptoms such as hives, difficulty breathing, or swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat after taking Centella asiatica.
  • 4. Limit sun exposure after use: Centella asiatica may increase photosensitivity, so it's important to avoid excessive sun exposure and protect yourself with sunscreen, clothing, and hats.
  • 5. Do not use with sedatives or caffeine: Centella asiatica may interact with sedatives or caffeine, leading to increased drowsiness or other adverse effects.
  • 6. Monitor blood sugar levels: Centella asiatica may lower blood sugar levels, so it's important to monitor your blood sugar closely and adjust your medication as needed if you have diabetes or are taking medication that affects blood sugar.
  • 7. Check for interactions with other medications: Centella asiatica may interact with other medications, so it's important to consult a healthcare provider and check for potential interactions before starting to use it.
  • 8. Limit dosage to recommended amounts: Taking more Centella asiatica than recommended may increase the risk of side effects and interactions, so it's important to follow the recommended dosage.
  • 9. Discontinue use if experiencing side effects: If you experience any side effects such as nausea, dizziness, or skin irritation, discontinue use and consult a healthcare provider.
  • 10. Consult a healthcare provider before surgery: Centella asiatica may affect blood clotting and increase the risk of bleeding, so it's important to inform your healthcare provider if you are taking Centella asiatica before surgery.
