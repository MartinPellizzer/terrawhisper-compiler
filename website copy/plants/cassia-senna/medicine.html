Cassia senna (Senna) Medicinal Guide

Cassia senna, also known as senna, is a plant that has been used for medicinal purposes for centuries due to its laxative properties.

The active constituents of Cassia senna include anthraquinone glycosides, which are converted into aglycones in the intestines and stimulate colonic peristalsis. Preparations of Cassia senna include dried leaves and pods that can be used to make teas, as well as extracts and tinctures.

However, side effects of using Cassia senna can include abdominal cramps, diarrhea, and electrolyte imbalances. Prolonged use or overdose can lead to dependence and worsening constipation.

Therefore, it is important to use Cassia senna only under the guidance of a healthcare professional, as there are certain precautions to consider, such as avoiding its use during pregnancy and breastfeeding, and in individuals with certain medical conditions such as inflammatory bowel disease or intestinal obstruction.

However, long-term use of senna should be avoided due to potential side effects.

  • 7. Treats Hemorrhoids: Senna's laxative effects can help relieve the symptoms of hemorrhoids, including pain, itching, and swelling.
  • 8. Prevents Cancer Growth: Some studies suggest that senna may have anti-cancer properties, including the ability to inhibit the growth of cancer cells.

What are the active constituents of Cassia senna (Senna)?

Cassia senna has several active constituents that include sennosides A-D, anthraquinones, dianthrone glycosides, flavonoids, kaempferol, quercetin, rutin, tannins, resin, volatile oils, sterols, mucilage, chrysophanol, rhein, and aloe-emodin.

These compounds contribute to the herb's laxative effects, which are primarily due to the sennosides. These compounds work by stimulating the colon, increasing intestinal peristalsis, and increasing the amount of water in the stool. Anthraquinones, dianthrone glycosides, and dianthrone glycosides also have laxative effects.

Flavonoids, such as kaempferol, quercetin, and rutin, have antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Tannins have astringent properties, while resin and volatile oils provide therapeutic effects. Sterols and mucilage have a soothing effect on the gastrointestinal tract.

Chrysophanol, rhein, and aloe-emodin are anthraquinone derivatives that also contribute to the laxative effects of senna.

  • 1. Sennosides A-D: Stimulate bowel movements by increasing muscle contractions in the intestines.
  • 2. Anthraquinones: Primary active compounds in senna, have strong purgative effects.
  • 3. Dianthrone glycosides: Powerful laxatives that stimulate the large intestine and increase water content in stools.
  • 4. Flavonoids: Plant pigments with antioxidant properties, may help reduce inflammation and damage from oxidative stress.

  • 8. Tannins: Polyphenolic compounds with astringent properties, may help protect against oxidative stress and reduce inflammation.
  • 9. Resin: A sticky substance that coats the mucous membrane of the intestines, may help soothe irritation and inflammation.

What are the medicinal preparations of Cassia senna (Senna)?

Cassia senna has many medicinal preparations, such as infusion, which is made by steeping dried senna leaves or pods in hot water to extract their active components.

Decoction is a stronger preparation, made by simmering senna in water for a longer period. Tinctures and fluid extracts are alcohol-based preparations that extract both the non-water-soluble and water-soluble constituents of senna. Powder, capsules, and suppositories are solid forms of senna that can be taken orally or inserted rectally.

Ointments and liniments are topical preparations that contain senna and are used to treat skin conditions or muscle pain. Glycerites and syrups are sweetened, liquid preparations that contain a senna extract. Tea is an infusion of senna leaves, often consumed for its laxative effects. Liquid extract and dry extract are concentrated forms of senna extract, which can be added to various preparations.

Bath additive is a preparation of senna that can be added to bath water for a soothing soak.

The following list shows the medicinal preparations of Cassia senna.

  • 1. Infusion: A tea made by steeping senna leaves or pods in hot water, used as a stimulant laxative.
  • 2. Decoction: A strong senna tea made by boiling and simmering senna leaves or pods, used as a potent laxative.
  • 3. Tincture: A concentrated alcohol extract of senna leaves or pods, used in small amounts for its laxative effects.
  • 4. Fluid extract: A more potent and concentrated form of senna extract, used to treat constipation and cleanse the bowels.
  • 5. Powder: Finely ground senna leaves or pods, taken orally in capsules or added to food and drink for laxative effects.
  • 6. Capsules: Encapsulated senna powder, providing a measured dose for convenient and easy consumption as a laxative.
  • 7. Suppositories: Senna extract in a solid, bullet-shaped form intended for rectal administration, used for rapid laxative effects.
  • 8. Ointment: A topical preparation containing senna, used to soothe and protect irritated skin.
  • 9. Liniment: A liquid senna preparation for external use, often applied to the skin to stimulate circulation and relieve pain.
  • 10. Glycerite: A senna extract preserved in glycerin, a more palatable alternative to alcohol-based tinctures, used for laxative purposes.

What are the possible side effects of using Cassia senna (Senna) for medicinal purposes?

Cassia senna can have side effects if used improperly, such as diarrhea, abdominal cramps, and nausea.

In some cases, it can also cause vomiting, electrolyte imbalance, liver damage, and kidney damage. Additionally, senna may lead to allergic reactions, dizziness, headache, and rash.

It can also result in reduced potassium levels, interference with drug absorption, and stomach discomfort. Furthermore, senna use can change urine color.

Therefore, it is crucial to follow the recommended dosage and usage guidelines when taking senna-based products.

The following list shows the possible side effects of Cassia senna.

  • 1. Diarrhea: Can cause loose, watery stools or increased frequency of bowel movements.
  • 2. Abdominal cramps: May cause stomach discomfort or pain due to the irritant effect of senna on the intestinal lining.
  • 3. Nausea: Can cause an unpleasant sensation in the stomach, often accompanied by the urge to vomit.
  • 4. Vomiting: Can forcefully expel the contents of the stomach through the mouth, often as a result of irritation or infection.
  • 5. Electrolyte imbalance: May lead to an imbalance of essential minerals in the body, which can affect muscle and nerve function.
  • 6. Liver damage: Prolonged or excessive use can cause damage to the liver, leading to inflammation or impaired function.
  • 7. Kidney damage: Long-term or excessive use can cause damage to the kidneys, leading to impaired function or kidney failure.
  • 8. Allergic reactions: Can cause an allergic response, characterized by symptoms such as itching, swelling, or difficulty breathing.
  • 9. Dizziness: Can cause a feeling of lightheadedness, unsteadiness, or disorientation.
  • 10. Headache: Can cause pain or discomfort in the head, often accompanied by other symptoms such as nausea or sensitivity to light.

What are the precautions to take when using Cassia senna (Senna) medicinally?

It's important to take some precautions when using Cassia senna for medicinal purposes, such as consulting a healthcare provider first, since they can provide guidance based on your medical history and current medications.

Additionally, avoiding excessive use and limiting treatment duration is crucial to prevent potential laxative dependence and electrolyte imbalances. Furthermore, it's essential to monitor blood levels, avoid using it during pregnancy and breastfeeding, and check for allergies to prevent adverse reactions.

Lastly, being cautious of its use in certain populations like children, the elderly, and those with kidney, liver, or heart disease, as well as being aware of potential interactions with certain medications, staying hydrated, and discontinuing use if side effects occur, can help ensure its safe and effective use.

The following list shows the precautions to take when using Cassia senna.

  • 1. Consult a healthcare provider first: Before using Cassia senna for medicinal purposes, it is essential to consult a healthcare provider to ensure it is safe and appropriate for your specific health condition and to determine the correct dosage.
  • 2. Avoid excessive use: Using Cassia senna in excessive amounts can cause harmful side effects, including liver damage and severe diarrhea.
  • 3. Limit treatment duration: Prolonged use of Cassia senna can lead to dependence and potential damage to the digestive system.
  • 4. Monitor blood levels: Regularly monitoring blood levels is necessary when using Cassia senna to prevent potential adverse effects.
  • 5. Avoid in pregnancy: Pregnant women should avoid using Cassia senna as it may stimulate uterine contractions and lead to preterm labor.
  • 6. Avoid in breastfeeding: Nursing mothers should avoid using Cassia senna as it can pass through breast milk and cause harmful effects on the infant.
  • 7. Check for allergies: Individuals with known allergies to Cassia senna or other laxatives should avoid using it as it can cause allergic reactions.
  • 8. Avoid in children: Cassia senna should not be given to children under two years of age without consulting a healthcare provider.
  • 9. Avoid in elderly: Elderly individuals should use Cassia senna with caution as they may be more susceptible to adverse effects.
  • 10. Avoid in kidney disease: Individuals with kidney disease should avoid using Cassia senna as it can worsen kidney function and lead to further complications.
