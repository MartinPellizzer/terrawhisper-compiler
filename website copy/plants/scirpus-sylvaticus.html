What to know before using Scirpus sylvaticus (Wood Club-Rush)?

Scirpus sylvaticus, also known as wood club-rush, is a perennial plant native to Europe and Western Asia that has been used for medicinal purposes for centuries.

Its roots and rhizomes contain tannins, saponins, and essential oils, which have been used to treat a variety of ailments, including diarrhea, dysentery, and skin irritations. This plant thrives in moist and wet conditions, making it a popular choice for wetland restoration projects and bog gardens.

Scirpus sylvaticus can grow up to 3 feet tall, with long, slender, and triangular stems that are topped with brown or purple-black spikelets. Its leaves are typically narrow and linear, growing in a tufted arrangement around the base of the stem.

Despite its many uses and benefits, Scirpus sylvaticus can be invasive in certain areas, so it is important to plant it in controlled environments.

What are the medicinal uses of Scirpus sylvaticus?
