What to know before using Trifolium campestre (Large Hop Clover)?

Trifolium campestre, also known as low or field hop clover, is a plant with medicinal properties well-regarded in traditional medicine.

Its leaves and flowers contain isoflavones, which have been found to possess anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects, making it useful in treating skin irritations and promoting wound healing. Thriving in full sun and well-drained soil, Trifolium campestre is a hardy plant that grows low to the ground and displays small, white to pale-yellow flower heads.

This legume is also beneficial for horticultural purposes as it can improve soil fertility by fixing nitrogen, making it an excellent choice for crop rotation.

What are the medicinal uses of Trifolium campestre?

Trifolium campestre is used to treat a variety of ailments due to its medicinal properties.

Its active constituents, which include isoflavonoids, coumarins, and saponins, contribute to its health benefits such as anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant effects. Medicinal preparations often involve extracts from the aerial parts of the plant and are used to manage conditions like skin irritations, respiratory infections, and digestive issues. However, it is important to note that large hop clover can cause side effects like stomach upset and skin rash in some individuals, and it should be avoided by pregnant women and those with estrogen-sensitive conditions due to its isoflavonoid content.

What are the horticultural conditions of Trifolium campestre?

Trifolium campestre needs full sun to thrive, although it can tolerate partial shade.

It prefers well-drained soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5. When planting, sow seeds directly in the ground during the spring or fall, and cover lightly with soil. Keep the soil consistently moist until seedlings emerge, and then gradually reduce watering.

To care for Trifolium campestre, provide regular watering, but allow the soil to dry out between waterings. Fertilize sparingly, as too much nitrogen can harm the plant. When it comes to harvesting, trim the plant back regularly to encourage bushier growth and prevent seed production. Pests such as aphids and cutworms can affect Trifolium campestre, as well as diseases like powdery mildew and verticillium wilt.
