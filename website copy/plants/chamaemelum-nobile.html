What to know before using Chamaemelum nobile (Roman Chamomile)?

Chamaemelum nobile, also known as Roman chamomile, is a low-growing perennial herb native to western and southern Europe.

It is valued in horticulture for its delicate, feathery leaves and small, daisy-like white flowers with yellow centers. The plant prefers well-drained soil and partial shade, and it is often used as a groundcover due to its spreading habit.

In addition to its ornamental value, Chamaemelum nobile has a long history of use in herbal medicine. The flowers are harvested and dried to make teas and tinctures, which have been found to have anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic, and mildly sedative properties.

The herb is often used to treat digestive complaints, anxiety, and insomnia.

What are the medicinal uses of Chamaemelum nobile?

Chamaemelum nobile has several medicinal uses, such as reducing inflammation, promoting sleep, and soothing digestive issues.

Its constituents include volatile oils like bisabolol and chamazulene, flavonoids, and coumarins, which contribute to its calming and anti-inflammatory effects. Preparations of Chamaemelum nobile can be made through infusions, decoctions, tinctures, or essential oil extracts. Possible side effects of using Chamaemelum nobile may include allergic reactions, skin irritation, and eye inflammation, especially in large amounts or with prolonged use.

Precautions should be taken when using Chamaemelum nobile, as it may interact with certain medications, such as blood thinners, and should be avoided during pregnancy due to its potential to stimulate menstruation.

What are the possible side effects of Chamaemelum nobile?

Chamaemelum nobile can have some side effects if used improperly, such as allergic reactions in individuals sensitive to members of the Asteraceae/Compositae family, which may cause symptoms like skin rashes, hives, or swelling.

Ingesting roman chamomile may lead to digestive issues like nausea, vomiting, or stomach discomfort, especially when consumed in large amounts. Additionally, it might exacerbate hormone-sensitive conditions, such as breast cancer, due to its potential estrogenic effects. Moreover, chamaemelum nobile may interact with blood-thinning medications, such as warfarin or aspirin, potentially increasing the risk of bleeding and bruising.

Pregnant women should avoid using roman chamomile, as it might stimulate the uterus and lead to premature labor or miscarriage.
