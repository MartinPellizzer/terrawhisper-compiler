10 possible side effects of Urtica dioica (Nettle) if used improperly

Urtica dioica, also known as stinging nettle, has been associated with various health benefits when used properly, but it can also have adverse effects if used improperly.

One possible side effect of misuse is contact dermatitis, a skin irritation that can occur when touching the plant's stinging hairs. Ingesting large amounts of Urtica dioica can lead to gastrointestinal disturbances, including nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea.

Additionally, there have been reports of kidney damage associated with the long-term consumption of the plant. Pregnant women and individuals with chronic kidney disease should avoid using Urtica dioica altogether, as it may worsen their conditions.

It is important to consult with a healthcare provider before using Urtica dioica to ensure it is safe and appropriate for your individual health needs.

1. Upset Stomach

Upset stomach caused by Urtica dioica refers to a disturbance in the normal function of the stomach, resulting in symptoms such as discomfort, nausea, and bloating.

This side effect can be attributed to the presence of active constituents such as histamine, acetylcholine, and serotonin in Urtica dioica. The medicinal preparations of Urtica dioica that can cause this side effect if misused include teas, capsules, and tinctures.

Individuals who are more likely to experience this side effect are those who are sensitive to histamine or have a prior history of gastrointestinal issues. To avoid this side effect, it is recommended to start with a low dose and gradually increase the intake, while also staying hydrated and avoiding excessive use.

It is also important to consult with a healthcare provider before using Urtica dioica as a treatment, especially for those with pre-existing medical conditions.

2. Diarrhea

Diarrhea caused by Urtica dioica refers to an unpleasant side effect characterized by loose, watery stools occurring frequently.

This happens mainly due to the presence of irritant compounds such as histamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine in Urtica dioica. Infusions, decoctions, and tinctures are the primary medicinal preparations that can cause this side effect if misused or consumed in large quantities. People with a history of gastrointestinal sensitivity or irritable bowel syndrome are more likely to experience diarrhea after using Urtica dioica.

To avoid this side effect, it is crucial to follow recommended dosages and preparation guidelines strictly, and when in doubt, consult a healthcare professional for personalized advice.

3. Sweating

Sweating caused by Urtica Dioica refers to the production of excess perspiration triggered by this herb.

The main active constituents in Urtica dioica that can cause this side effect are histamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine. These compounds can stimulate the sweat glands, leading to increased perspiration.

The main medicinal preparations of Urtica dioica that can cause this side effect if misused include nettle tea, tinctures, and capsules. Individuals who are more likely to experience sweating as a side effect are those who are sensitive or allergic to nettle or other members of the Urticaceae family. To avoid this side effect, it is important to start with a low dose and gradually increase it over time, while also staying well-hydrated.

Additionally, it is recommended to consult with a healthcare provider before using nettle for medicinal purposes.

4. Flushing

Flushing caused by Urtica Dioica refers to a sudden reddening of the skin along with a sudden sensation of heat.

This side effect can be attributed to the main active constituents of nettle, which include histamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine. The medicinal preparations of nettle that can cause this side effect if misused include nettle tea, nettle capsules, and nettle tinctures.

Individuals with a known allergy to nettle or other plants in the same family, as well as those with sensitive skin, are more likely to experience flushing after using nettle. To avoid this side effect, it is important to start with a low dose of nettle and gradually increase the dosage, while also avoiding taking it on an empty stomach.

Additionally, it is recommended to consult with a healthcare provider before using nettle, especially for those with existing medical conditions or who are taking other medications.

5. Rash

Rash caused by Urtica Dioica refers to a skin reaction characterized by redness, itching, and small bumps.

This side effect is primarily caused by the plant's histamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine, which can irritate the skin upon contact. The main medicinal preparations of Urtica dioica that can cause this side effect if misused include fresh or dried leaves, root, and seeds. Individuals with a history of allergic reactions or sensitivity to nettle plants are more likely to experience this side effect.

To avoid this side effect, it is recommended to use nettle products that have been processed to remove or reduce irritants, follow package instructions carefully, and perform a patch test on a small area of skin before using nettle products widely.

6. Nausea

Nausea caused by Urtica Dioica refers to the feeling of discomfort or unease in the stomach that can sometimes lead to vomiting.

This side effect can be attributed to the main active constituents of Urtica dioica, which include histamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine. These chemicals can cause nausea when they are ingested in large quantities or when they are not properly metabolized by the body.

The main medicinal preparations of Urtica dioica that can cause nausea if misused include tea, capsules, and tinctures. People who are more likely to experience nausea after using Urtica dioica include those with sensitive stomachs, those who consume large amounts of the herb, and those who are not accustomed to taking it. To avoid nausea when using Urtica dioica, it is important to start with a low dose and gradually increase it over time.

It is also important to drink plenty of fluids and to avoid taking the herb on an empty stomach.

7. Vomiting

Vomiting caused by Urtica Dioica refers to the act of forcefully expelling stomach contents through the mouth as a result of using this herb.

The main active constituents in Urtica dioica that can cause vomiting include histamine, serotonin, acetylcholine, and other irritants found in the plant. Medicinal preparations of Urtica dioica that can cause vomiting when misused include decoctions, infusions, and tinctures made from the leaves, stems, or roots of the plant.

People who are more likely to experience vomiting from Urtica dioica are those who consume large doses of the herb, those who are sensitive or allergic to the plant, and those who use the herb for extended periods of time without proper medical supervision. To avoid vomiting as a side effect of using Urtica dioica, it is recommended to follow dosage instructions carefully, start with small doses and gradually increase, and discontinue use immediately if vomiting occurs.

It is important to consult with a healthcare provider before using Urtica dioica to ensure safe usage and avoid potential side effects.

8. Stinging Sensation

Stinging sensation caused by Urtica Dioica refers to the painful, tingling feeling experienced when handling the plant.

This sensation is primarily caused by the presence of formic acid, histamine, and other irritants in the plant's tiny hairs. The hairs break off when touched, releasing these chemicals which then penetrate the skin and cause the stinging sensation.

Medicinal preparations such as nettle tea, tinctures, and capsules, if not used correctly, can also cause this side effect. Those with sensitive skin or allergies are more likely to experience this side effect.

To avoid this side effect, it is recommended to handle the plant with care, wearing gloves and long sleeves, and to follow package instructions carefully when using nettle supplements.

9. Allergic Reactions

Allergic reactions caused by Urtica Dioica refer to the immune system's adverse response to the plant's allergens.

The main active constituents that can cause this side effect are formic acid, histamine, and several other chemicals. These allergic reactions can be triggered by the misuse of nettle in various medicinal preparations, such as teas, capsules, and topical creams.

Individuals with a known hypersensitivity to nettle, as well as those with allergies to other plants, are more likely to experience this side effect. To avoid allergic reactions, it is essential to start with a low dose when using nettle and closely monitor for any adverse reactions.

If any signs of an allergic reaction occur, it is crucial to discontinue use immediately and seek medical attention if necessary.

10. Decreased Blood Pressure

Decreased blood pressure caused by Urtica dioica refers to the effect of this plant's active constituents on lowering a person's blood pressure.

The main active constituents of Urtica dioica that can cause this side effect are histamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine, which can dilate blood vessels and reduce blood pressure. This side effect is most commonly associated with the use of nettle in medicinal preparations such as teas, tinctures, and capsules, especially when used in large doses or over extended periods of time. Individuals who are already taking medication for high blood pressure or who have a history of low blood pressure are more likely to experience this side effect.

To avoid this side effect, it is important to follow the recommended dosage and consult with a healthcare provider before using nettle, especially if you have a history of blood pressure issues.
