Cinnamomum verum (Cinnamon) Medicinal Guide

Cinnamomum verum, commonly known as true cinnamon, has a long history of use in traditional medicine for its numerous health benefits.

The bark and leaves of this tree contain active constituents such as cinnamaldehyde, eugenol, and linalool, which have been found to have anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties. Preparations of C. verum include dried bark powder, essential oil, and tea, which have been used to treat a variety of ailments including respiratory infections, digestive issues, and diabetes. However, it is important to note that C.

verum can cause side effects such as skin irritation, mouth sores, and liver damage in some individuals, especially when taken in large doses or over an extended period of time. Therefore, it is recommended to consult with a healthcare provider before using C. verum for medicinal purposes, especially for those who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or have pre-existing medical conditions. Additionally, it is important to use C.

verum in moderation, as excessive consumption can lead to toxicity and other adverse effects.

What are the active constituents of Cinnamomum verum (Cinnamon)?

Cinnamomum Verum has several active constituents that contribute to its aroma, flavor, and medicinal properties.

The most prominent of these is Cinnamaldehyde, which gives cinnamon its distinctive warm and sweet flavor. Another important component is Eugenol, a compound also found in cloves, which contributes to cinnamon's spicy and slightly bitter taste.

Linalool, a terpene alcohol, is responsible for the floral and lavender-like aroma of cinnamon. Coumarin, a fragrant organic compound, is present in small amounts in cinnamon and is known for its anti-inflammatory and anticoagulant properties. Epicatechin, a type of flavonoid, is a potent antioxidant found in cinnamon, contributing to its potential health benefits.

Other active constituents in cinnamon include Cinnamyl acetate, Geraniol, Carotol, Caryophyllene oxide, Beta-caryophyllene, Alpha-humulene, Alpha-terpineol, Bisabolene, Camphor, and Safrole, all of which contribute to the complex flavor, aroma, and medicinal properties of cinnamon.

The following list shows the active constituents of Cinnamomum verum.

  • 1. Cinnamaldehyde: A compound primarily responsible for the medicinal properties of cinnamon, known for its anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antioxidant effects.
  • 2. Eugenol: A phenolic compound contributing to cinnamon's pleasant aroma, exhibiting anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antiseptic properties.
  • 3. Linalool: A terpene alcohol with anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and antifungal properties, contributing to cinnamon's soothing and protective effects.
  • 4. Coumarin: A fragrant organic compound with anticoagulant and anti-inflammatory effects, found in lower concentrations in cinnamon compared to other plants.
  • 5. Epicatechin: A flavonoid compound that acts as an antioxidant, protecting cells from damage and supporting overall health.
  • 6. Cinnamyl acetate: A compound responsible for cinnamon's aroma, contributing to the spice's antimicrobial and antifungal properties.
  • 7. Gernijerol: A minor constituent in cinnamon with potential antioxidant and antimicrobial effects, requiring further research.
  • 8. Carotol: A sesquiterpene alcohol found in cinnamon oil, exhibiting antimicrobial and antifungal properties.
  • 9. Cinnamomum verum (C. verum) oil: Contains cinnamaldehyde, eugenol, and cinnamyl acetate, exhibiting antimicrobial, antifungal, and antiparasitic activities.
  • 10. Beta-caryophyllene: A sesquiterpene with anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antioxidant properties, found in cinnamon and other essential oils.

What are the medicinal preparations of Cinnamomum verum (Cinnamon)?

Cinnamomum Verum has many medicinal preparations, such as infusion, which is made by steeping cinnamon in hot water to extract its beneficial compounds.

Decoction is a stronger preparation, created by simmering cinnamon in water for a longer period. Tinctures and macerations are alcohol-based extractions of cinnamon, with tinctures often having a higher alcohol content.

Extracts are concentrated forms of cinnamon, while oil is obtained through steam distillation and is used topically. Powdered cinnamon can be encapsulated for easy consumption, and when steeped in hot water, it becomes tea.

Bath salts, balms, creams, lotions, ointments, and salves are topical preparations that contain cinnamon, which can help soothe and heal the skin.

The following list shows the medicinal preparations of Cinnamomum verum.

  • 1. Infusion: A medicinal preparation made by steeping Cinnamomum verum leaves or bark in hot water to extract its beneficial compounds.

  • 3. Tincture: A medicinal preparation of Cinnamomum verum, created by soaking its bark or leaves in alcohol, which extracts its active compounds.
  • 4. Maceration: A process of preparing Cinnamomum verum medicinally, where its bark or leaves are soaked in a liquid, usually water or alcohol, to release its beneficial constituents.
  • 5. Extract: A highly concentrated medicinal preparation of Cinnamomum verum, made by extracting its active compounds using a solvent.
  • 6. Oil: A medicinal preparation of Cinnamomum verum obtained by steam distillation of its leaves or bark, producing an essential oil used for various therapeutic purposes.
  • 7. Powder: A medicinal preparation of Cinnamomum verum, made by grinding its bark or leaves into a fine powder, used for direct consumption or as an ingredient in other preparations.
  • 8. Capsules: A medicinal preparation of Cinnamomum verum in the form of a capsule, containing its powder or extract, used for easy consumption and targeted delivery of its active compounds.
  • 9. Tea: A medicinal preparation of Cinnamomum verum, made by steeping its leaves or bark in hot water, releasing its beneficial compounds for consumption.

What are the possible side effects of using Cinnamomum verum (Cinnamon) for medicinal purposes?

Cinnamomum Verum can have side effects if used improperly, such as mouth sores and mouth irritation, which can result from consuming or handling large amounts of this spice.

Additionally, gastrointestinal issues like diarrhea, thirst, and decreased appetite may occur due to the compounds found in cinnamon. Moreover, some people may experience skin irritation after coming into contact with cinnamon, and allergic reactions can range from mild symptoms like headaches and dizziness to severe ones like breathing problems, confusion, unconsciousness, hallucinations, seizures, and even anaphylactic shock. Furthermore, cinnamon may lower blood sugar levels, which can be dangerous for people with diabetes or hypoglycemia, and long-term use or high doses of cinnamon may cause liver damage.

Therefore, it is crucial to use cinnamon in moderation and consult a healthcare professional before using it for medicinal purposes.

  • 1. Mouth sores: Cinnamomum verum may cause mouth sores due to its irritant effects on the mucous membranes.

  • 4. Dizziness: Cinnamomum verum may cause dizziness due to its potential to lower blood sugar levels, leading to symptoms such as lightheadedness.
  • 5. Headache: The use of Cinnamomum verum can cause headaches due to its ability to dilate blood vessels and increase blood flow.
  • 6. Skin irritation: Topical application of Cinnamomum verum may result in skin irritation due to its potent essential oils and compounds.
  • 7. Low blood sugar: Cinnamomum verum can lower blood sugar levels, which can be dangerous for people with diabetes or other conditions that affect blood sugar regulation.
  • 8. Liver damage: Excessive consumption of Cinnamomum verum may cause liver damage due to its high content of certain compounds that can be toxic to the liver in large amounts.
  • 9. Allergic reactions: Cinnamomum verum can cause allergic reactions in some people, resulting in symptoms such as hives, itching, or difficulty breathing.
  • 10. Mouth irritation: The use of Cinnamomum verum can cause mouth irritation due to its potent essential oils and compounds, which can irritate the mucous membranes of the mouth.

What are the precautions to take when using Cinnamomum verum (Cinnamon) medicinally?

It's important to take some precautions when using Cinnamomum Verum for medicinal purposes, such as consulting a healthcare provider before use, as cinnamon may interact with certain medications or medical conditions.

Additionally, individuals with allergies to cinnamon should avoid using it, and pregnant women should limit their cinnamon intake. Furthermore, consuming large amounts of cinnamon can be harmful, and people with upcoming surgeries should avoid using cinnamon due to the risk of bleeding.

Prolonged exposure to cinnamon can also cause skin irritation, and cinnamon oil should be diluted before topical use and kept away from eyes and mucous membranes. Children should not have access to cinnamon products, and users should discontinue use if they experience adverse reactions. It's also important to note that cinnamon should not be used as a substitute for prescribed medications and should be used under the guidance of a healthcare provider.

Lastly, users should limit their exposure to sunlight while using cinnamon and avoid consuming it if they have mouth sores.

The following list shows the precautions to take when using Cinnamomum verum.

  • 1. Consult healthcare provider before use: It's important to seek professional medical advice to ensure safety and effectiveness, especially if you have any pre-existing medical conditions or are currently taking medications.
  • 2. Avoid if allergic to cinnamon: Do not use Cinnamomum verum for medicinal purposes if you have known allergies to cinnamon or its components, as it may cause allergic reactions or worsen symptoms.
  • 3. Limit cinnamon intake for pregnant women: Pregnant women should limit their cinnamon intake, as high doses could potentially harm the fetus; consult a healthcare provider for appropriate usage.
  • 4. Do not consume large amounts of cinnamon: Excessive consumption of cinnamon can lead to toxicity, causing symptoms such as mouth sores, breathing trouble, and even liver damage.
  • 5. Monitor blood sugar closely with use: Cinnamon may lower blood sugar levels; people with diabetes should monitor their blood sugar closely and adjust medication dosages as needed, under the guidance of a healthcare provider.
  • 6. Avoid use before surgery due to risk of bleeding: To reduce the risk of increased bleeding during and after surgery, discontinue using cinnamon at least two weeks before the scheduled procedure.
  • 7. Limit exposure to skin to prevent irritation: Prolonged or concentrated contact with cinnamon can cause skin irritation, so it's essential to limit exposure and wash hands after handling.
  • 8. Do not use cinnamon oil near eyes or mucous membranes: Cinnamon oil can cause severe irritation and damage to the eyes and mucous membranes; keep it away from these sensitive areas.
  • 9. Dilute cinnamon oil before topical use: Cinnamon oil should be diluted with a carrier oil before applying it to the skin, as undiluted cinnamon oil can cause skin irritation, burning, or other adverse reactions.
  • 10. Keep cinnamon products out of reach of children: Cinnamon products should be stored in a secure location, out of the reach of children, to prevent accidental ingestion or misuse, which can lead to poisoning or other harmful effects.
