What to know before using Citrus bergamia (Bergamot)?

Citrus bergamia, also known as Bergamot orange, is a unique citrus tree that is primarily grown in Southern Italy.

It is known for its medicinal properties, which include the ability to reduce anxiety and depression, lower cholesterol levels, and improve skin health. The horticultural conditions for Citrus bergamia are specific, requiring a warm and subtropical climate with well-drained soil. The botanical characteristics of Citrus bergamia include its small size, with a height of around 12 feet, and its distinctive fruit that is green when unripe and turns yellow when ripe.

The fruit has a sour taste and is primarily used for the extraction of its essential oil, which has a wide range of applications in perfumes, food, and beverages.

What are the medicinal uses of Citrus bergamia?

Citrus bergamia has several medicinal uses, such as providing relief for anxiety and mild depression due to its calming and uplifting properties.

The constituents of bergamot include polyphenols, flavonoids, and volatile oil, which are known to have antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial effects. The preparations of bergamot can be in the form of essential oil, tea, or supplements, and it is often used in aromatherapy, topically, or internally. However, bergamot can cause photosensitivity and may increase the risk of skin cancer if used in excess, so it is important to avoid prolonged sun exposure after application.

Additionally, bergamot may interact with certain medications, such as antidepressants and antihypertensive drugs, so it is crucial to consult with a healthcare provider before use.

What are the medicinal preparations of Citrus bergamia?

Citrus bergamia has many medicinal preparations, such as essential oil, hydrosol, and dried peel extracts.

The essential oil is extracted from the rind of the fruit through steam distillation, while the hydrosol is a byproduct of this process. Dried peel extracts are made by soaking the fruit peel in a solvent, such as alcohol or glycerin. These preparations have been used traditionally for various purposes, such as in aromatherapy for relaxation and stress relief, as a flavoring agent in tea and confectionery, and in skincare for its purported benefits on oily and acne-prone skin.

The essential oil and hydrosol are also used in perfumery for their distinctive citrusy-floral scent.

What are the possible side effects of Citrus bergamia?

Citrus bergamia can have some side effects if used improperly, such as skin irritation and sensitivity to sunlight.

Photosensitivity is a common issue, where the skin becomes extra sensitive to sunlight after handling the fruit or its oil, leading to sunburns or pigmentation. Moreover, ingesting large amounts of bergamot oil can cause mouth irritation, muscle weakness, and even seizures due to the presence of certain compounds. Additionally, it can interact with certain medications, such as those for anxiety and high blood pressure, leading to potential health risks.

Lastly, overuse of bergamot, whether topically or internally, can result in digestive issues, including appetite changes, diarrhea, and nausea.

What are the precautions to take when using Citrus bergamia?

You should take some precautions when using Citrus bergamia for medicinal purposes, such as being mindful of its powerful phototoxic properties.

This means that when bergamot oil is applied to the skin and then exposed to sunlight, it can cause skin irritation, redness, and in some cases, severe burns. Therefore, it is crucial to avoid sun exposure or use of tanning beds for at least 12 hours after applying bergamot oil to the skin.

Moreover, pregnant and breastfeeding women should consult their healthcare provider before using bergamot oil, as its safety during pregnancy and lactation has not been established. Additionally, it is essential to dilute bergamot oil with a carrier oil before applying it to the skin, and to perform a patch test to ensure that you do not have an adverse reaction to it.

Lastly, always use high-quality bergamot oil from a reputable source to ensure its purity and effectiveness.

What are the horticultural conditions of Citrus bergamia?

Citrus bergamia needs full sun and well-drained soil with a pH between 5.0 and 6.5 for optimal growth.

When planting, ensure that you provide enough space for the tree to grow, ideally 12-15 feet apart in a row and 15-20 feet between rows. Regular watering is essential, especially during the first year of growth.

To care for Citrus bergamia, fertilize the tree with a citrus-specific fertilizer in early spring and late summer. Harvesting should be done when the fruit peel turns light green or orange, typically between November and March. Common pests and diseases include citrus greening, citrus canker, and citrus black spot, while pests include aphids, mealybugs, and scale insects.
