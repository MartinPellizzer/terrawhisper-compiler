Curcuma longa (Turmeric) Medicinal Guide

Curcuma longa, also known as turmeric, has been used for centuries in traditional medicine for its wide range of health benefits.

The root of the plant contains curcuminoids, such as curcumin, which have anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and neuroprotective properties. Preparations of Curcuma longa include dried powder, tinctures, and fluid extracts, and it is often used in cooking and supplements. However, high doses of Curcuma longa can cause digestive issues, such as nausea and diarrhea, and it may interfere with certain medications, such as blood thinners.

Therefore, it is important to consult with a healthcare provider before starting any new supplement regimen, especially for those who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or have underlying health conditions.

What are the active constituents of Curcuma longa (Turmeric)?

Curcuma longa has several active constituents, such as Curcumin, Demethoxycurcumin, and Bisdemethoxycurcumin, which are collectively known as Curcuminoids and have been found to have potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.

Cyclocurcumin has neuroprotective effects and is being studied for its potential role in treating neurodegenerative diseases. Curdione, Monodesmethoxycurcumin, Bisdesmethoxycurcuminaldehyde, Curcumol, and Curcumenol have demonstrated anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial activities. 1,2-dehydrocurdione, Diarylheptanoids, p-coumaric acid, and Ferulic acid are other bioactive constituents of turmeric with various health benefits, including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and chemopreventive properties.

Additionally, Bisabolene, α-Turmerone, β-Turmerone, and γ-Turmerone are sesquiterpenes found in turmeric that have been shown to have anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and anticancer activities.

The following list shows the active constituents of Curcuma longa.

  • 1. Curcumin: The most abundant constituent in Curcuma longa, known for its anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and anticancer properties.
  • 2. Demethoxycurcumin: A derivative of curcumin, with potential anticancer, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective effects.
  • 3. Bisdemethoxycurcumin: Another curcumin derivative, with reported anticancer, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory activities.
  • 4. Cyclocurcumin: A curcumin analog with potential chemopreventive and anti-inflammatory properties.
  • 5. Curdione: A sesquiterpene with reported anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and anticancer effects.
  • 6. Monodesmethoxycurcumin: A curcuminoid with antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and anticancer potential.
  • 7. Bisdesmethoxycurcuminaldehyde: A curcumin-derived compound with potential antiproliferative and antioxidant effects.
  • 8. Curcumenol: A sesquiterpene with reported anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and neuroprotective properties.
  • 9. Germacrone-13-coxide: A compound derived from Curcuma longa, with potential antimicrobial and antioxidant activities.
  • 10. Curcumene: A monoterpene in Curcuma longa, with reported antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and anticancer effects.

What are the possible side effects of using Curcuma longa (Turmeric) for medicinal purposes?

Curcuma longa can have side effects if used improperly.

Some possible side effects include gastrointestinal issues such as nausea, diarrhea, stomach upset, and yellow stool. Additionally, it can cause allergic reactions, skin rashes, and dizziness.

Turmeric may also interfere with iron absorption, increase liver function tests, menstrual flow, and bleeding risk. Furthermore, it can cause gallbladder contractions, decrease blood pressure, increase oxalate levels, and increase the risk of bleeding.

Therefore, it is important to consult with a healthcare provider before using turmeric, especially in large amounts or for extended periods.

  • 1. Nausea: Curcuma longa, also known as turmeric, may cause nausea due to possible irritation of the gastrointestinal tract.

  • 3. Dizziness: Dizziness may result from using Curcuma longa, possibly due to individual sensitivity or interaction with other medications.
  • 4. Rash: A rash can appear as a side effect of Curcuma longa, potentially caused by an allergic reaction or individual sensitivity.
  • 5. Headache: Headaches might be experienced as a side effect of Curcuma longa, possibly due to individual sensitivity or interaction with other substances.
  • 6. Yellow stool: The use of Curcuma longa can lead to yellow-colored stool, which is likely due to the pigments in the turmeric.
  • 7. Increased liver function tests: Curcuma longa may cause a temporary increase in liver function tests, reflecting the body's response to the herb.
  • 8. Increased menstrual flow: Curcuma longa can potentially increase menstrual flow, possibly due to its blood-thinning properties.
  • 9. Stomach upset: Stomach upset can occur as a side effect of Curcuma longa, potentially due to gastrointestinal irritation or individual sensitivity.

What are the precautions to take when using Curcuma longa (Turmeric) medicinally?

Before using Curcuma longa for medicinal purposes, it's important to take some precautions such as consulting a healthcare provider before use, as it can interact with certain medications and medical conditions.

Additionally, it should be avoided during pregnancy or breastfeeding and discontinued if an allergic reaction occurs. If you have a medical condition, it's important to limit intake and monitor blood sugar levels if diabetic.

Furthermore, it should be discontinued before surgery, and limited in conjunction with blood thinners, gallbladder issues, and medication for low blood pressure. It's also important to limit intake before anesthesia, discontinue use if experiencing stomach upset or skin rash, and limit intake if on medication for GERD, a bleeding disorder, iron deficiency, fertility issues, or autoimmune disorders.

Lastly, it should be discontinued if experiencing irregular menstruation.

The following list shows the precautions to take when using Curcuma longa.

  • 1. Consult a healthcare provider before use: It's important to seek professional medical advice to ensure curcuma longa is safe and suitable for your health condition and to determine the appropriate dosage.
  • 2. Avoid during pregnancy or breastfeeding: Curcuma longa is not recommended for pregnant or breastfeeding women as it can stimulate the uterus or affect milk production.
  • 3. Stop use if allergic reaction occurs: Discontinue use immediately if you experience symptoms such as hives, itching, or difficulty breathing, as these may be signs of an allergic reaction.
  • 4. Limit intake if have a medical condition: If you have a medical condition, such as gallbladder problems or gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), consult a healthcare provider before using curcuma longa.
  • 5. Monitor blood sugar levels if diabetic: Curcuma longa may lower blood sugar levels, so if you have diabetes, monitor your blood sugar levels closely and consult a healthcare provider before using curcuma longa.
  • 6. Discontinue use before surgery: Curcuma longa may slow blood clotting, so discontinue use at least 2 weeks before surgery to reduce the risk of excessive bleeding.
  • 7. Limit intake with blood thinners: Curcuma longa may increase the risk of bleeding when taken with blood thinners, so limit intake and consult a healthcare provider before use.
  • 8. Avoid alcohol consumption while using: Alcohol may interact with curcuma longa and increase the risk of liver damage.
  • 9. Limit intake with gallbladder issues: Curcuma longa may worsen gallbladder problems, so limit intake if you have gallstones or a bile duct obstruction.
  • 10. Do not use if prone to kidney stones: Curcuma longa may increase the risk of kidney stones in people who are prone to them, so avoid use if you have a history of kidney stones.
