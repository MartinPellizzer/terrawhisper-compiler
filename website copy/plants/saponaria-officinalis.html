What to know before using Saponaria officinalis (Soapwort)?

Saponaria officinalis, also known as soapwort, is a herbaceous perennial plant that is native to Europe and parts of Asia.

It is a popular ornamental plant in horticulture due to its star-shaped pinkish-white flowers and its ability to thrive in a variety of soil types, including sandy and clay soils, as long as they are well-drained. The plant contains saponins, which have medicinal properties and have been used in traditional medicine for their expectorant, diuretic, and skin-soothing effects. When the roots or leaves of the plant are crushed and mixed with water, they produce a lather that can be used as a gentle alternative to soap for washing delicate fabrics or as a skin cleanser for sensitive skin.

However, it is important to note that saponins can be toxic if ingested in large quantities, so soapwort should be used with caution.

Soapwort's roots and aerial parts can be harvested for medicinal and cosmetic uses.

Soapwort is relatively pest and disease-free, but it can be affected by aphids, spider mites, and powdery mildew.
