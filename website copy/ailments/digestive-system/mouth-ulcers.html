What is mouth ulcers and how it affects your life?

Mouth ulcers are small, painful lesions that develop on the soft tissues inside the mouth, such as the inside of the lips, cheeks, and gums.

These sores can make basic activities like eating, speaking, and drinking uncomfortable or even unbearable. The constant irritation can disrupt daily life, causing difficulty in enjoying meals or even brushing teeth. Moreover, mouth ulcers affect oral hygiene, leading to bad breath, and impact the overall quality of life, causing embarrassment in social situations due to visible lesions or avoidable speech difficulties.

In severe cases, mouth ulcers can lead to significant weight loss and even affect mental health, causing stress, anxiety, or depression.

What are the main causes of mouth ulcers?

The main causes of mouth ulcers are nutritional deficiencies, injury, and underlying medical conditions.

Nutritional deficiencies, particularly a lack of vitamin B12, iron, and folic acid, can lead to mouth ulcers. Injury to the mouth, such as biting the inside of the cheek or accidentally scraping the mouth with a toothbrush, can also result in mouth ulcers.

Furthermore, certain medical conditions, like celiac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, and autoimmune diseases, can increase the likelihood of developing mouth ulcers.

The most common causes of mouth ulcers are listed below.

  • Physical trauma: Mouth ulcers often result from accidental bites, cheek chewing, dental work, or rough brushing. These actions cause injury to the mouth's delicate tissues, leading to sores.
  • Nutritional deficiencies: Lack of essential nutrients, such as vitamins B-12, B-9 (folate), and iron, weakens oral tissues and triggers mouth ulcers.
  • Allergic reactions: Certain individuals develop mouth ulcers in response to allergies from foods, toothpastes, mouthwashes, or dental materials. Allergens can cause inflammation and tissue damage, leading to sores.
  • Hormonal changes: Fluctuations in hormonal levels, particularly in females, can result in mouth ulcers due to the impact of hormones on the immune system and oral tissues.
  • Emotional stress: High levels of stress and anxiety can weaken the immune system, making individuals more susceptible to developing mouth ulcers.
  • Genetic predisposition: Some people inherit a tendency to develop mouth ulcers due to genetic factors that influence their oral health and immune response.
  • Gastrointestinal diseases: Conditions like celiac disease, Crohn's disease, and ulcerative colitis can cause mouth ulcers as part of their symptomatology.
  • Immune system disorders: Certain immune system conditions, such as lupus or Behçet's disease, can produce mouth ulcers as a symptom of the underlying disease.
  • Certain medications: Specific drugs, such as nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), beta-blockers, or nicorandil, can cause mouth ulcers as a side effect.
  • Oral habits: Regularly consuming acidic or spicy foods and beverages, chewing tobacco, or holding objects like pens or pencils between the teeth can irritate oral tissues, leading to mouth ulcers.
