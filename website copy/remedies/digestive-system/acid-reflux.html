What is acid reflux and how it affects your life?

Acid reflux, also known as gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), is a condition where the stomach acid flows back up into the esophagus, causing irritation and inflammation.

This results in symptoms such as heartburn, chest pain, difficulty swallowing, regurgitation of sour liquid or food, and a sensation of a lump in the throat. These symptoms can significantly impact one's quality of life, causing discomfort, pain, and interfering with daily activities. For instance, experiencing heartburn during meals can make eating less enjoyable, while regurgitation can be an embarrassing situation in public.

Chronic acid reflux can also lead to more serious health complications such as esophageal ulcers, narrowing of the esophagus, and increased risk of esophageal cancer.

What are the main causes of acid reflux?

The main causes of acid reflux are a weak lower esophageal sphincter (LES) and hiatal hernia.

The LES is a muscle that acts as a valve, allowing food to enter the stomach while preventing digestive juices from flowing back into the esophagus. If the LES becomes weakened or relaxed, stomach acid can leak into the esophagus, causing acid reflux. A hiatal hernia occurs when a portion of the stomach protrudes through the diaphragm and into the chest cavity, which can also lead to acid reflux by disrupting the function of the LES.

Other contributing factors include obesity, pregnancy, smoking, and certain foods and drinks such as spicy, fatty, or fried foods, citrus fruits, chocolate, caffeine, carbonated drinks, and alcohol.

The most common causes of acid reflux are listed below.

  • Overeating: Consuming large amounts of food at once can lead to acid reflux as the stomach struggles to digest such a large quantity, increasing the likelihood of acid escaping into the esophagus.
  • Certain foods: Specific foods inherently relax the lower esophageal sphincter muscle, promoting acid reflux. These include fatty foods, spicy dishes, citrus fruits, tomato-based products, chocolate, garlic, onions, and mint.
  • Carbonated beverages: Drinks containing carbon dioxide, such as soda or sparkling water, can cause the stomach to expand with gas, placing pressure on the lower esophageal sphincter and leading to acid reflux.
  • Obesity: Excess body weight, particularly around the abdomen, exerts additional pressure on the stomach, causing acid to push up into the esophagus.
  • Lying down after eating: When lying down or sleeping immediately after a meal, gravity is unable to keep stomach acid from backing up into the esophagus, resulting in acid reflux.
  • Tight clothing: Wearing restrictive or tight-fitting clothes can put extra pressure on the abdomen, pushing acid into the esophagus and triggering acid reflux.
  • Smoking: Nicotine found in cigarettes weakens the lower esophageal sphincter muscle, allowing acid to flow back up into the esophagus.
  • Certain medications: Some medications, like nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), blood pressure drugs, sedatives, and muscle relaxants, can weaken or relax the lower esophageal sphincter muscle, contributing to acid reflux.
  • Pregnancy: During pregnancy, the growing uterus puts increased pressure on the stomach, causing acid to move into the esophagus and resulting in acid reflux.
  • Hiatal hernia: A hiatal hernia occurs when a portion of the stomach protrudes through the diaphragm into the chest cavity, leading to acid reflux as the lower esophageal sphincter muscle becomes displaced and weakened.

What precautions to take when using herbal remedies for acid reflux?

When considering herbal remedies for acid reflux, it is crucial to consult with a healthcare professional before use.

These remedies can interact with other medications and may cause unintended side effects. It is essential to research the quality and purity of herbal supplements, as they are not regulated by the FDA, and contamination or mislabeling can occur.

Additionally, pregnant or breastfeeding individuals should avoid herbal remedies without consulting a healthcare professional. Proper dosage is critical, and taking too much of an herbal supplement can lead to adverse effects.

Finally, monitoring symptoms and communicating with a healthcare provider throughout the process is necessary for safe and effective treatment.

The most important precautions to take when using herbal remedies for acid reflux are listed below.

  • Consult a healthcare professional before starting any herbal remedy.
  • Follow dosage instructions carefully, avoiding excessive intake.
  • Pregnant or breastfeeding women should avoid using herbal remedies without medical approval.
  • Be aware of potential allergic reactions and stop using the herb if they occur.
  • Check for interactions between herbal remedies and prescribed or over-the-counter medications.
  • Avoid using herbal remedies for an extended period without consulting a healthcare provider.
  • Purchase herbal remedies from reputable sources, ensuring their quality and safety.
  • Be cautious of claims that herbal remedies are a cure for acid reflux.
  • Maintain a food diary to identify potential dietary triggers of acid reflux.
  • Seek immediate medical attention for persistent or worsening symptoms while using herbal remedies.

What natural remedies to use with medicinal herbs for acid reflux?

Acid reflux, also known as gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), can be managed through natural remedies.

These remedies include consuming smaller, more frequent meals and avoiding trigger foods such as spicy, fatty, and fried foods, as well as citrus fruits and chocolate. Additionally, maintaining a healthy weight and avoiding lying down after meals can alleviate symptoms.

Elevating the head of your bed or using a wedge pillow can also help prevent acid from backing up into the esophagus while sleeping. Furthermore, chewing gum after meals can promote saliva production, which helps neutralize stomach acid.

Lastly, staying hydrated by drinking plenty of water throughout the day can dilute stomach acid and improve digestion.

The most effective natural remedies to use in conjunction with herbal medicine that help with acid reflux are listed below.

  • Elevate your head: When sleeping, raise your head by 6-8 inches by placing blocks under the legs at the head of the bed or using a wedge pillow. This helps keep your upper body elevated and prevents stomach acid from backing up into your esophagus.
  • Modify your diet: Eat smaller, more frequent meals and avoid trigger foods like fried, fatty or spicy foods, citrus fruits, chocolate, tomato-based products, carbonated drinks, and alcohol. Opt for lean proteins, whole grains, vegetables, and fruits.
  • Lose weight: If you are overweight or obese, losing even a small amount of weight can significantly reduce acid reflux symptoms. Excess weight puts pressure on the abdomen, pushing stomach acid into the esophagus.
  • Wear loose-fitting clothes: Tight clothing, especially around the waist, can put pressure on the abdomen and exacerbate acid reflux symptoms. Wear loose-fitting, comfortable clothing to reduce the risk of acid reflux.
  • Don't lie down after eating: Wait at least three hours after eating before lying down or going to bed. This allows time for your stomach to empty and reduces the likelihood of acid reflux.
  • Quit smoking: Nicotine relaxes the lower esophageal sphincter (LES), the muscle that controls the opening between the esophagus and stomach, allowing stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus.
  • Stay hydrated: Drink plenty of water throughout the day. Water helps maintain the right consistency of mucus in the esophagus, providing a protective barrier against stomach acid.
  • Exercise regularly: Regular physical activity can help maintain a healthy weight and strengthen the muscles in the abdomen and esophagus, reducing the risk of acid reflux.
  • Reduce stress: Stress can exacerbate acid reflux symptoms. Practice relaxation techniques like deep breathing, meditation, or progressive muscle relaxation to reduce stress and anxiety.
  • Chew gum: Chewing sugar-free gum after meals can increase saliva production, which helps neutralize stomach acid and wash it away from the esophagus.
