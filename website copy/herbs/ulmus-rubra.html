What to know about slippery elm before using it medicinally

Slippery elm, scientifically known as Ulmus rubra, is a herb that has been widely used for its numerous health benefits.

It is rich in mucilage, a substance that forms a soothing and protective film when in contact with moisture, making it an effective remedy for various digestive issues such as heartburn, acid reflux, and irritable bowel syndrome. In the culinary world, slippery elm has been used as a thickening agent in soups, stews, and even beverages due to its mucilaginous properties. As a horticultural plant, slippery elm is a deciduous tree that is native to North America and thrives in moist and well-drained soils.

It can grow up to 60 feet tall and has distinctive dark brown bark that is furrowed and scaly. Botanically, slippery elm belongs to the Ulmaceae family and has oval-shaped leaves that are serrated and pointed at the tip. Historically, slippery elm has been used by Native Americans for various medicinal purposes, including treating wounds, burns, and skin irritations.

It was also used as a food source during winter months due to its high nutritional value.
