What Are The Main Causes Of Peripheral Arterial Occlusive Disease?

Peripheral arterial occlusive disease (PAOD) is a condition where the blood vessels in the legs and arms become narrowed or blocked.

There are several main causes of PAOD. Atherosclerosis, which is the buildup of plaque in the blood vessels, is a major cause. This buildup is made of fat, cholesterol, and other substances that stick to the inner walls of the blood vessels, causing them to narrow. Diabetes is another significant cause of PAOD.

High blood sugar levels in people with diabetes can damage the blood vessels and nerves, leading to narrowed or blocked arteries. This damage can also cause blood to become thicker, making it harder for the body to circulate. Hypertension, or high blood pressure, is also a contributing factor to PAOD. When blood pressure is high, it can cause the blood vessels to stretch and become weak.

Over time, this can lead to the formation of plaque and the narrowing of the blood vessels.

Below are the main causes of peripheral arterial occlusive disease.

  • Atherosclerosis: Atherosclerosis is a cause of peripheral arterial occlusive disease because it leads to the buildup of plaque on the inner walls of arteries in the legs and feet, which can narrow or block the arteries, reducing blood flow and causing pain and damage.
  • Diabetes: Diabetes damages blood vessels and nerves, leading to poor circulation, which causes peripheral arterial occlusive disease by reducing or blocking blood flow to the legs and feet.
  • Hypertension: Hypertension causes peripheral arterial occlusive disease because high blood pressure makes the blood vessels in the legs and arms become narrower and stiffer, making it harder for blood to flow through them.
  • Smoking: Smoking damages the inner lining of blood vessels, making it harder for blood to flow to the legs and feet, which can lead to peripheral arterial occlusive disease.
  • Hyperlipidemia: Hyperlipidemia causes peripheral arterial occlusive disease by leading to the buildup of fatty deposits (plaque) inside the artery walls, which narrows the arteries and restricts blood flow to the limbs.
  • Age: As people age, the inner lining of blood vessels (arteries) becomes damaged and less flexible, leading to the buildup of plaque and narrowing of the arteries, which can cause peripheral arterial occlusive disease (PAOD).
  • Obesity: Obesity is a cause of peripheral arterial occlusive disease because excess body fat can lead to inflammation and hardening of the arteries, which narrows the blood vessels and restricts blood flow to the limbs.
