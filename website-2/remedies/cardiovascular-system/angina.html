What Are The Main Causes Of Angina?

Angina is a condition where the heart muscle does not get enough oxygen-rich blood.

There are several main causes of angina. One of the main causes is Coronary Atherosclerosis, which is a buildup of fatty deposits called plaque inside the coronary arteries. This buildup narrows the arteries, reducing blood flow to the heart muscle. As a result, the heart muscle does not get enough oxygen, leading to angina. Another main cause of angina is Hypertension, or high blood pressure.

When blood pressure is high, the heart has to work harder to pump blood through the arteries, including the narrowed coronary arteries caused by plaque buildup. This increased pressure can further reduce blood flow to the heart muscle, leading to angina. Hyperlipidemia, or high levels of lipids (fats) in the blood, is also a main cause of angina. High levels of lipids can contribute to the buildup of plaque in the coronary arteries, just like in Coronary Atherosclerosis. Additionally, high levels of lipids can also damage the inner lining of the arteries, making it easier for plaque to form.

This can lead to a reduction in blood flow to the heart muscle, causing angina.

Below are the main causes of angina.

  • Coronary Atherosclerosis: Coronary atherosclerosis is a cause of angina because the thick buildup of fatty plaque in the coronary arteries narrows them, reducing blood flow to the heart muscle, which then doesn't get enough oxygen and causes pain or discomfort in the chest.
  • Hypertension: Hypertension causes angina because it forces the heart to work harder to pump blood, which can lead to a decrease in blood flow to the heart muscle, causing chest pain and shortness of breath.
  • Hyperlipidemia: Hyperlipidemia, a condition where high levels of fat in the blood, can cause angina by clogging the arteries and reducing blood flow to the heart, making it harder for the heart to get the oxygen it needs.
  • Diabetes: High blood sugar levels in people with diabetes can damage the blood vessels and nerves, leading to hardening and narrowing of the arteries, which restricts blood flow to the heart, resulting in angina.
  • Smoking: Smoking damages the blood vessels and reduces blood flow to the heart, which can cause angina because the heart muscle does not get enough oxygen and nutrients.
  • Family History: Family history is a cause of angina because people with a close relative who had angina or heart disease are more likely to inherit genes that make their blood vessels narrow or weak, making it harder for their heart to get the blood it needs.
  • Age: As we age, the blood vessels in our heart become narrower and stiffer, making it harder for the heart to get the oxygen and nutrients it needs, leading to angina.
