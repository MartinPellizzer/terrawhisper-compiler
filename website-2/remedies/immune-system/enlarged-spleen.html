What Are The Main Causes Of Enlarged Spleen?

An enlarged spleen, also known as splenomegaly, can be caused by various factors.

One of the main causes is HIV infection, which weakens the immune system and can lead to the spleen becoming enlarged as it tries to fight off infections. Lymphoma, a type of cancer that affects the immune system, can also cause an enlarged spleen. In lymphoma, the abnormal immune cells can accumulate in the spleen, making it swell.

Leukemia, a type of blood cancer, is another cause of an enlarged spleen. Leukemia can lead to an overproduction of white blood cells, which can accumulate in the spleen and cause it to become enlarged. Other causes of an enlarged spleen include infections, such as malaria or mononucleosis, liver disease, and heart problems.

In addition, certain genetic disorders, like hereditary spherocytosis, can also cause the spleen to become enlarged.

Below are the main causes of enlarged spleen.

  • HIV: When HIV infects the body, it damages the immune system and makes the spleen work harder to fight off infections, leading to inflammation and enlargement of the spleen.
  • Lymphoma: Lymphoma causes an enlarged spleen because the abnormal white blood cells from the disease multiply and accumulate in the spleen, making it swell and become larger than normal.
  • Leukemia: Leukemia causes an enlarged spleen because cancer cells in the blood build up in the spleen, causing it to swell and become bigger than usual.
  • Sarcoidosis: Sarcoidosis can cause an enlarged spleen because the disease leads to the formation of abnormal immune cells that accumulate in the spleen, causing it to become swollen and enlarged.
  • Portal Hypertension: Portal hypertension is a cause of an enlarged spleen because the high blood pressure in the liver's blood vessels can cause the veins that drain blood from the spleen to become blocked, leading to an increase in blood stored in the spleen, causing it to enlarge.
  • Thalassemia: In people with Thalassemia, the spleen gets enlarged because it has to work too hard to remove damaged red blood cells from the blood, which leads to its enlargement over time.
