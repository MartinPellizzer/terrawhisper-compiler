What are the causes of hyperacidity?

Hyperacidity occurs when there's an excessive production of stomach acid, leading to discomfort, pain, and damage to the digestive system.

Several factors contribute to this condition, and understanding them can help prevent and manage hyperacidity. One major cause is Gastroesophageal reflux, where the stomach acid flows back up into the esophagus, causing heartburn and pain. This can happen due to a weak lower esophageal sphincter, which is a muscle that separates the esophagus and stomach. Another common cause is Peptic ulcer, a sore in the stomach lining or the first part of the small intestine. This sore can be caused by the overproduction of stomach acid, which wears away the protective lining of the digestive tract.

H. pylori infection, a bacterial infection, is also a major contributor to Peptic ulcers. This infection damages the stomach lining, allowing stomach acid to leak through and cause ulcers. Dyspepsia, or indigestion, is another cause of hyperacidity. This can be triggered by eating spicy, fatty, or heavy foods, which can irritate the stomach lining and cause acid production to increase. Lastly, caffeine intake is also a contributing factor to hyperacidity. Caffeine can stimulate the production of stomach acid, making it worse for people who already have hyperacidity.

Drinking coffee, tea, or energy drinks, especially on an empty stomach, can exacerbate the condition.

What herbs should you avoid if you have hyperacidity?

If you have hyperacidity, it's a good idea to limit or avoid certain herbs that can make the problem worse.

Glycyrrhiza glabra, also known as licorice root, contains a compound called glycyrrhizin that can increase acidity in the body. This can make stomach pain and heartburn symptoms even more severe. Capsicum annuum, or cayenne pepper, is known for its spicy flavor, but it can also irritate the stomach lining, leading to inflammation and more acid production. Cassia auriculata, a type of flowering plant, has been used in traditional medicine for various purposes, but it's also known to stimulate digestion and increase stomach acid.

This can be problematic for people who already experience hyperacidity. Tribulus terrestris has been used to treat various health issues, but it's also believed to stimulate the digestive system, which may worsen hyperacidity in some individuals. Sassafras albidum, or sassafras, contains a compound called safrole that can also stimulate digestion and increase stomach acid, potentially exacerbating hyperacidity symptoms. These herbs may not be suitable for everyone, especially if they have hyperacidity issues.

It's always a good idea to consult with a healthcare professional before using any new herbs or supplements, especially if you have a pre-existing condition.
