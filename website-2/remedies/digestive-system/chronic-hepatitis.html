What are the causes of chronic hepatitis?

Chronic hepatitis is a long-term inflammation of the liver that can be caused by several factors.

One of the main causes is hepatitis B virus (HBV), which is spread through bodily fluids like blood and semen. When someone with HBV has unprotected sex or shares needles, they can pass the virus to others. HBV can also be passed from mother to child during birth. Another common cause is hepatitis C virus (HCV), which is also spread through blood and bodily fluids. HCV can be passed through sharing needles or getting a tattoo with unsanitary equipment.

It's also possible to get HCV from a blood transfusion before strict screening procedures were in place. In people with HIV, the immune system is weakened, making them more susceptible to other infections, including hepatitis viruses. This is because HIV attacks and destroys CD4 cells, which are a type of immune cell that helps fight off infections. Autoimmune hepatitis is a condition where the immune system mistakenly attacks the liver, causing inflammation and damage. This can be triggered by genetics, hormones, or certain medications. Lastly, chronic hepatitis can also be caused by excessive alcohol consumption, which can lead to liver inflammation and damage over time.

When the liver is repeatedly exposed to high levels of toxins from alcohol, it can become scarred and damaged, leading to chronic hepatitis.
