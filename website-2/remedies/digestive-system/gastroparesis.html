Gastroparesis is a medical condition where the stomach muscles become weak and unable to move food through the digestive system.

This leads to symptoms like nausea, vomiting, and bloating. If left untreated, gastroparesis can cause severe dehydration, malnutrition, and even life-threatening complications like bowel obstruction or perforation. Dealing with gastroparesis is crucial to prevent these consequences and improve the quality of life. What Are The Main Causes Of Gastroparesis?

Gastroparesis is a condition where the stomach muscles are weak and cannot move food properly.

There are several main causes of gastroparesis. One of the main causes is diabetes. When you have diabetes, your body either doesn't produce enough insulin or can't use it properly. Over time, high blood sugar levels can damage your nerves, including the nerves that control the stomach muscles.

This damage can lead to gastroparesis. Another cause is diabetic neuropathy, which is damage to the nerves caused by diabetes. As nerves are damaged, they can't send the right signals to the stomach muscles, leading to gastroparesis. A third cause of gastroparesis is gastrectomy, which is the surgical removal of part of the stomach. When the stomach is partially removed, the remaining stomach muscles may become weak and unable to move food properly.

This can lead to gastroparesis.

Below are the main causes of gastroparesis.

  • Diabetes: Diabetes can cause gastroparesis because high levels of sugar in the blood damage the nerves that control the stomach muscles, slowing down their ability to move food into the small intestine for digestion.
  • Diabetic Neuropathy: Diabetic Neuropathy is a cause of gastroparesis because it damages the nerves that control the muscles in the stomach, leading to weakened stomach muscles that can't properly move food through the digestive system.
  • Gastrectomy: A gastrectomy, which is the surgical removal of part or all of the stomach, can cause gastroparesis because the remaining stomach muscles are left without their normal working stomach tissue to help them move food through the digestive system.
  • Idiopathic: Idiopathic gastroparesis is often referred to as a cause because it means that doctors cannot find a specific reason or cause for the delayed stomach emptying, despite running many tests and trying different treatments.
  • Cancer: Cancer can cause gastroparesis by damaging the nerves that control the stomach's muscles, leading to slowed or blocked digestion and making it hard for food to move through the digestive system.
  • Autoimmune Disease: In autoimmune disease, the body's immune system mistakenly attacks and damages the nerves that control the stomach muscles, leading to gastroparesis, a condition where the stomach takes too long to empty its contents.
  • Neurological Disorder: Neurological disorders, such as diabetes and Parkinson's disease, can cause gastroparesis by damaging the nerves that control the stomach muscles, which then leads to a loss of coordination and movement in the digestive system.
