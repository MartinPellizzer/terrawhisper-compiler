What Are The Main Causes Of Cirrhosis?

Cirrhosis is a serious liver disease that can be caused by several factors.

One of the main causes is alcoholism. When people drink too much alcohol, their liver works harder to break it down, leading to damage and scarring. This can cause the liver's normal tissue to be replaced by scar tissue, which can eventually lead to cirrhosis. Another cause of cirrhosis is Hepatitis B, a viral infection that affects the liver. Hepatitis B is usually spread through bodily fluids, such as blood or semen, when a person has unprotected sex with someone who has the virus.

It can also be spread from mother to child during childbirth. If left untreated, Hepatitis B can cause chronic liver inflammation, leading to scarring and cirrhosis. Hepatitis C is another viral infection that can cause cirrhosis. It is usually spread through blood-to-blood contact, such as through sharing needles or getting a tattoo with unsterilized equipment. Hepatitis C can also be spread through sexual contact with someone who has the virus.

If left untreated, Hepatitis C can cause long-term liver damage, leading to cirrhosis.

Below are the main causes of cirrhosis.

  • Alcoholism: When a person regularly drinks too much alcohol, it can damage the liver cells, leading to scarring and inflammation, and eventually causing cirrhosis, a serious disease where the liver becomes scarred and unable to function properly.
  • Hepatitis B: Hepatitis B is a cause of cirrhosis because the virus damages the liver's cells, leading to inflammation and scarring, which can cause the liver to become hard and non-functional.
  • Hepatitis C: Hepatitis C is a cause of cirrhosis because the virus causes inflammation and damage to the liver, leading to scarring and the growth of new liver cells, which can eventually replace healthy tissue and cause liver failure.
  • Nonalcoholic Steatohepatitis: Nonalcoholic Steatohepatitis (NASH) can cause cirrhosis because it leads to the scarring of liver tissue due to inflammation and the buildup of fat, which eventually replaces healthy liver cells and disrupts liver function.
  • Hemochromatosis: Hemochromatosis is a cause of cirrhosis because the body absorbs too much iron, which builds up in the liver and damages its cells, leading to scarring and cirrhosis.
  • Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency: Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency causes cirrhosis because the body can't stop a protein called neutrophil elastase from destroying liver cells, leading to scarring and damage to the liver over time.
  • Nonalcoholic Fatty Liver Disease: Nonalcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD) is a cause of cirrhosis because the excessive fat buildup in the liver can lead to scarring and inflammation, gradually damaging the liver's tissue and causing it to become stiff and hardened, ultimately resulting in cirrhosis.

What Herbs To Avoid If You Have Cirrhosis?

If you have cirrhosis, it's essential to be cautious with certain herbs that can harm your liver further.

One herb to avoid is Peppermint (Mentha piperita), as it can relax the muscles in the digestive tract, leading to a build-up of toxins in the bloodstream. Another herb to be careful with is Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla), which can cause stomach upset and worsen cirrhosis symptoms.

Licorice root (Glycyrrhiza uralensis) is also not recommended, as it can increase blood pressure and worsen liver damage. Dandelion root (Taraxacum officinale) may also not be suitable, as it can stimulate the liver and cause further inflammation. However, it's crucial to note that the effects of Dandelion on the liver are still being researched.

Other herbs to avoid include Milk Thistle's cousin, Holy Thistle (Cnicus benedictus), and Bupleurum (Bupleurum chinense), which can cause liver damage when taken in excess.
