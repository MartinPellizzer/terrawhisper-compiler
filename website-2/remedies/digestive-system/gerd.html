What Are The Main Causes Of Gerd?

The main causes of Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) can be broken down into several key factors.

Eating Habits are a significant contributor to GERD. This is because certain foods can relax the lower esophageal sphincter, allowing stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus. Foods high in fat, spicy foods, and citrus fruits are common culprits. When consumed in large quantities, these foods can put pressure on the lower esophageal sphincter, causing it to malfunction.

Obesity is another major cause of GERD. Excess weight, particularly around the abdomen, can put additional pressure on the stomach, causing it to push stomach acid upwards into the esophagus. This is due to the fact that excess fat tissue can compress the stomach, making it harder for the lower esophageal sphincter to function properly. A Hiatal Hernia, a condition in which the stomach bulges up into the chest through an opening in the diaphragm, is also a common cause of GERD. This occurs when the diaphragm's opening is weakened, allowing the stomach to move up into the chest and putting pressure on the lower esophageal sphincter.

When the stomach is in the chest, it can cause stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus, leading to heartburn and other symptoms of GERD.

Below are the main causes of gerd.

  • Eating Habits: Eating habits can cause GERD because consuming heavy, greasy, or fatty foods can put extra pressure on the stomach, causing stomach acid to leak back up into the esophagus.
  • Obesity: Obesity can cause GERD because excess body fat around the stomach pushes the stomach up, allowing stomach acid to flow more easily back up into the esophagus.
  • Hiatal Hernia: A hiatal hernia can cause GERD because the upper part of the stomach bulges up into the chest through an opening in the diaphragm, allowing stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus.
  • Sleep Disorders: Sleep disorders can cause Gerd by making the muscles in the esophagus relax too much, allowing stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus and cause discomfort.
  • Smoking: Smoking can cause Gerd (Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease) because it weakens the muscles in the esophagus, allowing stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus and cause heartburn and discomfort.
  • Hormonal Changes: Hormonal changes, such as those experienced during pregnancy or menopause, can cause stomach muscles to weaken, leading to a rise in stomach acid and increasing the risk of acid reflux and GERD.
  • Stress: When we are stressed, our body releases extra acid in the stomach, which can flow up into the esophagus and cause the symptoms of Gerd, such as heartburn and discomfort.
