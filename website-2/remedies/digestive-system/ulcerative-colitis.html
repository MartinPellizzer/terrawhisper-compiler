Ulcerative Colitis: Exploring Causes, Medicinal Herbs, and Herbal Preparations

Ulcerative colitis is a chronic condition where the inner lining of the colon and rectum gets inflamed and develops ulcers, leading to symptoms such as abdominal pain, diarrhea, and bloody stools.

If left untreated, ulcerative colitis can cause serious complications like colon cancer, narrowing of the colon, and an increased risk of colon rupture. It's essential to manage this condition to prevent these complications and maintain a good quality of life. Herbs and herbal preparations have been traditionally used to alleviate the symptoms of ulcerative colitis and prevent its complications. Turmeric (Curcuma longa) is a well-studied herb that has anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, which can help reduce inflammation and heal ulcers in the colon. Ginger (Zingiber officinale) is another herb that has been used to calm the digestive system and reduce nausea and abdominal pain. Licorice root (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is a soothing herb that can help protect the mucous membranes in the colon and reduce inflammation.

Herbal preparations can be used in various forms to treat ulcerative colitis. Decoction is a liquid preparation made by steeping herbs in hot water, which can be taken orally to help soothe the digestive system. Tincture is a concentrated liquid preparation made by steeping herbs in a solvent, which can be taken sublingually or added to food and drinks. Capsules are a convenient way to take herbal preparations, which can be filled with dried herbs or powders. To make the most of herbal preparations, it's essential to consult with a healthcare professional or a qualified herbalist to determine the best course of treatment. They can recommend the right herbs and preparations based on individual needs and health status.

Additionally, herbal preparations should be used in conjunction with conventional treatment and lifestyle changes to manage ulcerative colitis effectively.

What Are The Main Causes Of Ulcerative Colitis?

Ulcerative colitis is a chronic condition that affects the large intestine.

The main causes of this condition are not fully understood, but some factors are known to contribute to its development. One key cause is genetic predisposition. People with a family history of ulcerative colitis are more likely to develop the condition. This suggests that certain genetic traits can make a person more susceptible to the disease. Another cause is an autoimmune response.

In people with ulcerative colitis, the immune system mistakenly attacks the healthy cells in the large intestine, leading to inflammation and damage. This response is not fully understood, but it's thought to be triggered by a combination of genetic and environmental factors. A person's family history can also play a role in the development of ulcerative colitis. If a person has a close relative with the condition, they are more likely to develop it themselves. This is because family members often share similar genetic traits.

However, having a family history does not guarantee that a person will develop ulcerative colitis, and many people with a family history never develop the condition.

Below are the main causes of ulcerative colitis.

  • Genetic predisposition: Genetic predisposition is a cause of ulcerative colitis because a person is born with a mix of genes that can increase their risk of developing the condition, making them more likely to have an overactive immune system that attacks the healthy lining of their colon.
  • Autoimmune response: In ulcerative colitis, the body's immune system mistakenly attacks and damages the healthy cells lining the colon, causing inflammation and ulceration due to an overactive autoimmune response.
  • Family history: Research suggests that people with a family history of ulcerative colitis are more likely to develop the condition due to inherited genetic factors that affect the immune system and gut health.
  • Bacterial overgrowth: Bacterial overgrowth in the gut is a cause of ulcerative colitis because the excess bacteria produce toxins that irritate and inflame the lining of the colon, leading to symptoms of the disease.
  • Imbalanced gut microbiome: An imbalanced gut microbiome is a cause of ulcerative colitis because an overgrowth of bad bacteria in the gut leads to chronic inflammation, damage to the lining of the colon, and an impaired immune system, ultimately resulting in symptoms of ulcerative colitis.
  • Cytokine imbalance: Cytokine imbalance occurs when there is an overproduction of pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as TNF-alpha and IL-12, in the body, which leads to chronic inflammation in the colon and causes the symptoms of ulcerative colitis, including diarrhea, abdominal pain, and bloody stools.
  • Abnormal gut motility: Abnormal gut motility, or irregular movement of the digestive tract, can cause ulcerative colitis by allowing harmful bacteria and toxins to build up in the colon, leading to chronic inflammation and damage to the lining of the colon.

What Herbs To Avoid If You Have Ulcerative Colitis?

If you have ulcerative colitis, it's essential to be aware of certain herbs that may trigger symptoms or worsen the condition.

One herb to avoid is licorice root (Glycyrrhiza uralensis, not Glycyrrhiza glabra, which was excluded), which can cause stomach acid to increase, making symptoms worse. Another herb to be cautious with is peppermint (Mentha piperita), as it can cause stomach cramps and diarrhea. Also, avoid burdock root (Arctium lappa), as it may stimulate the digestive system and exacerbate inflammation.

Feverfew (Tanacetum parthenium) should also be avoided, as it can cause stomach upset and worsen symptoms. Additionally, some herbs like ginkgo biloba (Ginkgo biloba) and saw palmetto (Serenoa repens) may increase the risk of bleeding, which is a concern for people with ulcerative colitis. It's also best to avoid herbs like echinacea (Echinacea spp.). and yarrow (Achillea millefolium), as they can stimulate the immune system and cause inflammation.

Finally, avoid ashwagandha (Withania somnifera), which can cause stomach cramps and diarrhea in some individuals with ulcerative colitis.
