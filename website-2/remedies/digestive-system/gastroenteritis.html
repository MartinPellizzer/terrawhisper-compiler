What are the causes of gastroenteritis?

Gastroenteritis, also known as the stomach flu, is a common illness that affects people of all ages.

There are several main causes of gastroenteritis, and they can be broadly categorized into viral and bacterial infections. One of the main causes of gastroenteritis is Norovirus, a highly contagious virus that can spread through contaminated food, water, or contact with an infected person. Norovirus is known for its ability to cause severe vomiting and diarrhea, leading to dehydration and other complications. Another common cause of gastroenteritis is Rotavirus, a virus that primarily affects young children and can be spread through contaminated feces. Rotavirus can cause severe diarrhea, stomach cramps, and vomiting, and it's a leading cause of hospitalization in children under the age of five. Bacterial infections are also a common cause of gastroenteritis, with Salmonella being one of the main culprits.

Salmonella is a bacteria that can be found in contaminated food, such as undercooked eggs, raw meat, and unpasteurized dairy products. When ingested, Salmonella can cause severe diarrhea, stomach cramps, and fever. Escherichia (E. coli) is another type of bacteria that can cause gastroenteritis. E. coli can be found in contaminated food and water, and it can also be spread through contact with an infected person. E. coli can cause severe diarrhea, kidney failure, and other complications. Lastly, Campylobacter is a bacteria that can cause gastroenteritis, particularly in people who consume contaminated food or water. Campylobacter can cause severe diarrhea, stomach cramps, and fever, and it's a leading cause of food poisoning in many countries.

These viruses and bacteria can cause gastroenteritis by infecting the lining of the stomach and intestines, leading to inflammation, diarrhea, and vomiting.

What herbs should you avoid if you have gastroenteritis?

If you have gastroenteritis, it's best to steer clear of Cinchona officinalis.

This herb can cause stomach problems to worsen, and its bitter compounds might irritate your digestive system even more. Valeriana officinalis is another herb to avoid when you're not feeling well. While it's often used to calm the nerves, its sedative properties can actually slow down digestion, making it harder for your body to recover from gastroenteritis. Aconitum napellus is a highly potent herb that should be avoided when you're unwell.

It can cause nausea, vomiting, and stomach pain, which are symptoms you already have due to gastroenteritis. Adding this herb to your mix could make things even worse. Taxus baccata, or yew, is a toxic herb that can be particularly hazardous to someone with gastroenteritis. Its seeds and leaves contain a poison that can cause severe vomiting and diarrhea, which can dehydrate your body even further. Aristolochia clematitis is another herb you should avoid when you're dealing with gastroenteritis.

This plant's compounds can cause kidney damage and exacerbate dehydration, which can be a major concern when you're already experiencing stomach problems.

Is it safe to use herbal remedies for gastroenteritis during pregnancy?

Using herbal remedies for gastroenteritis during pregnancy is not recommended.

Some herbs can cause problems, like miscarriage or preterm labor. If you have gastroenteritis while pregnant, your healthcare provider may suggest antacids or anti-nausea medicines to help you feel better.
