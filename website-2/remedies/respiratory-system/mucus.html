What Are The Main Causes Of Mucus In Throat?

When you have mucus in your throat, it's usually because of an infection or allergic reaction.

The Common Cold is a main cause of mucus in the throat. When you catch a cold, your body's immune system fights off the virus by producing extra mucus. This mucus helps trap the virus and other germs, keeping them from spreading to other parts of your body. However, it can also make your throat feel congested and uncomfortable. Allergies are another common cause of mucus in the throat.

When you're allergic to something like pollen, dust, or pet dander, your body mistakes these particles for germs and produces mucus to fight them off. This can cause your throat to feel itchy, scratchy, and congested. A Sinus Infection is also a main cause of mucus in the throat. When your sinuses become infected, they produce extra mucus to fight off the infection. This mucus can then flow down the back of your throat, making it feel congested and uncomfortable.

Sinus infections often occur when bacteria or viruses infect the sinuses, and the mucus they produce can be thick and yellow or green in color.

Below are the main causes of mucus in throat.

  • Common Cold: When you have a common cold, your body's immune system produces excess mucus to trap the invading cold virus, which can then be expelled from the body, causing mucus to build up in the throat.
  • Allergies: When you have an allergy, your body's immune system reacts by producing extra mucus in your throat as a way to trap and remove the allergen, like pollen or dust, causing congestion and discomfort.
  • Sinus Infection: A sinus infection occurs when bacteria or viruses infect the sinuses, causing them to produce excess mucus that drains down the back of the throat, leading to mucus in the throat.
  • Bronchitis: Bronchitis causes mucus in the throat because the airways in the lungs become inflamed, leading to the production of extra mucus as a result of the body's attempt to fight off the infection and protect the lungs.
  • Pneumonia: When you have pneumonia, your body tries to fight off the infection by producing mucus, a thick and sticky liquid that traps the bad germs in your throat to prevent them from spreading further.
