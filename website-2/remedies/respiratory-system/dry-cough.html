Dry cough is a persistent and irritating cough that is often dry and hacking, and is not productive of mucus.

It is often caused by respiratory problems such as bronchitis or pneumonia, and can be triggered by dry air, smoking, or other irritants. If left untreated, dry cough can lead to complications such as bronchitis, pneumonia, or even heart problems due to the strain on the body. Therefore, it is essential to deal with dry cough promptly. Herbal remedies can be effective in soothing and calming the throat, reducing inflammation, and loosening mucus, making them a popular choice for treating dry cough. Licorice root (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is one of the herbs that can help to soothe the throat and calm irritation. It has anti-inflammatory properties that can reduce swelling and redness in the throat, making it a common ingredient in cough drops and lozenges. Thyme (Thymus vulgaris) is another herb that is known for its expectorant properties, which can help to loosen and clear mucus from the airways. It can be consumed as a tea, decoction, or tincture to help relieve dry cough. Echinacea (Echinacea purpurea) is a herb that is often used to boost the immune system and fight off infections.

It can be taken as a supplement or consumed as a tea to help prevent the development of dry cough. Herbal preparations can be made in various forms to suit individual needs. Decoctions are made by steeping herbs in boiling water for a longer period of time, which makes them more effective for treating dry cough. They can be made with a combination of herbs such as thyme and licorice root to provide a soothing and expectorant effect. Tinctures are concentrated liquids that are made by soaking herbs in a solvent such as ethanol or glycerin. Herbal remedies can be a safe and natural way to treat dry cough, especially when used in combination with other treatments such as rest and hydration.

What Are The Main Causes Of Dry Cough?

A dry cough is a cough that doesn't produce mucus.

There are several reasons why it happens. One cause is asthma, a condition where the airways in the lungs become narrow and swollen. This makes it hard for air to pass through, leading to a dry, hacking cough. Another cause is pneumonia, an infection in the lungs that makes them inflamed. The lungs become so irritated that they trigger a cough.

This cough can be dry because the body's natural response to infection is to clear out irritants, but in this case, there's no mucus to cough up. Bronchitis is another cause of dry cough. It's an inflammation of the airways, often caused by a viral or bacterial infection. The airways become inflamed, and this irritation leads to a cough. Since the airways are inflamed, there's no mucus to produce, resulting in a dry cough. These conditions can also be triggered by other factors, such as smoking, allergies, or exposure to pollutants.

However, asthma, pneumonia, and bronchitis are some of the main causes of dry cough.

Below are the main causes of dry cough.

  • Asthma: Asthma is a cause of dry cough because the airways in the lungs become inflamed and constricted, leading to a buildup of mucus and air trapped in the lungs, causing a dry, hacking cough.
  • Pneumonia: Pneumonia can cause a dry cough because the infection inflames the air sacs in the lungs, making them produce less mucus and less sputum, which usually helps to bring up the infection.
  • Bronchitis: Bronchitis is a cause of dry cough because the infection inflames the airways in the lungs, causing them to become irritated and inflamed, leading to a dry, hacking cough.
  • Allergies: When you have an allergy, your body releases chemicals to fight off the foreign substance, causing your airways to become inflamed and irritated, which can lead to a dry cough.
  • Lung Cancer: Lung cancer can cause a dry cough because the tumor or cancer cells may block or irritate the airways, leading to inflammation and irritation that triggers coughing.
  • Tuberculosis: Tuberculosis is a cause of dry cough because the bacteria that cause TB damage the lungs, making it hard for the body to produce mucus and leading to a persistent, dry cough.

What Herbs To Avoid If You Have Dry Cough?

If you have a dry cough, it's best to avoid herbs that can dry out your body even further.

Licorice root (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is already excluded, so let's move on. Angelica (Angelica sinensis) is another herb to be cautious with. It can have a drying effect on the body, which may worsen a dry cough. Sage (Salvia officinalis) is another herb to avoid. It has a natural decongestant property, but it can also dry out the mucus in your lungs, making your cough worse.

Mullein (Verbascum thapsus) is a soothing herb, but it's not suitable for dry coughs. It can make the mucus in your lungs thicker, which may make your cough worse. Eucalyptus (Eucalyptus globulus) is often used to relieve congestion, but it can also dry out the mucus in your lungs. And lastly, Coltsfoot (Tussilago farfara) is another herb to be cautious with. It can dry out the mucus in your lungs and make your cough worse. These herbs may not be suitable for dry coughs.
