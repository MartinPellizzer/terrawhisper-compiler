What Are The Main Causes Of Runny Nose?

A runny nose can be caused by three main things: Rhinitis, Allergies, and the Common Cold.

Rhinitis is an inflammation of the nasal passages. This can be caused by irritants like pollution, dust, and strong smells. When the nasal passages get inflamed, the body produces excess mucus to protect itself, leading to a runny nose. Allergies occur when the body's immune system reacts to something harmless, like pollen, dust, or pet dander.

When an allergen enters the body, it triggers the release of histamine, a chemical that causes blood vessels to swell and produce more mucus. This leads to a runny nose, as well as other symptoms like sneezing and congestion. The Common Cold is a viral infection that affects the upper respiratory system. When a cold virus enters the body, it attacks the cells lining the nasal passages and sinuses, causing inflammation and mucus production.

This leads to a runny nose, sore throat, and coughing.

Below are the main causes of runny nose.

  • Rhinitis: Rhinitis is a cause of runny nose because the sensitive lining inside the nostrils becomes inflamed, leading to the release of excess mucus and fluid from the nasal passages.
  • Allergies: When you have an allergy, your body thinks the thing you're allergic to, like pollen or dust, is bad and tries to get rid of it by releasing extra fluids from your nose, making it runny.
  • Common Cold: When you catch a common cold, the virus that entered your body releases chemicals that make the tiny blood vessels in your nose swell, causing them to leak and release excess fluid, resulting in a runny nose.
  • Sinusitis: Sinusitis is a cause of runny nose because the infection in the sinuses causes the nasal passages to produce excess mucus, which then flows out of the nose as a runny discharge.
  • Hay Fever: When people with hay fever breathe in tiny particles from pollen, their body's immune system thinks these particles are bad and releases a chemical called histamine, which causes blood vessels in the nose to swell and produce more mucus, resulting in a runny nose.
  • Pollen: When pollen from plants, trees, and flowers enters our nose, it irritates the inside lining, making the tiny blood vessels swell and produce more mucus, which then flows out as a runny nose.

If you have a runny nose, there are some herbs you should avoid.

Sage, also known as Salvia officinalis, can dry out your mucus membranes and make your runny nose worse. This is because it's a natural decongestant that can reduce mucus production. However, if your body is already producing less mucus, it can make your runny nose even more uncomfortable. Peppermint, or Mentha piperita, is another herb to steer clear of when you have a runny nose. It can irritate your nasal passages and make them produce more mucus, which can be frustrating if you're trying to recover from a cold or allergies.

Thyme, or Thymus vulgare, can also exacerbate a runny nose due to its expectorant properties. While it can help bring up mucus, it can also increase mucus production, making your runny nose worse in the short term. Additionally, you may want to avoid Lavender, or Lavandula angustifolia, as it can be drying to your nasal passages and exacerbate a runny nose. This is especially true if you're already using other drying products, such as nasal sprays or decongestants. Finally, Rosemary, or Rosmarinus officinalis, can be too stimulating for your nasal passages when you have a runny nose.

It can cause them to become inflamed and produce more mucus, making your symptoms worse.
