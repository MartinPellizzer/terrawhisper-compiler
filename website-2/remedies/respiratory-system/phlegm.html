However, if it becomes excessive, it can cause problems, such as coughing, chest congestion, and difficulty breathing. If left untreated, it can lead to more serious conditions like pneumonia and bronchitis. Therefore, it's essential to deal with phlegm effectively. However, decoctions can be beneficial for certain herbs, like licorice root, where boiling helps to release its active compounds.

It's essential to note that herbs and herbal preparations should be used under the guidance of a healthcare professional, especially if you have underlying health conditions or are taking medications.

What Are The Main Causes Of Phlegm?

Phlegm is a thick, sticky substance produced by the body to trap and remove germs and irritants from the airways.

The main causes of phlegm production are related to respiratory health issues. Asthma is a condition where the airways in the lungs become inflamed and narrow, making it difficult to breathe. This inflammation triggers the production of phlegm to help clear out the airways. Asthma attacks can cause phlegm to become thick and sticky, making it hard to cough up. Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a lung disease that makes it hard to breathe. It's often caused by smoking or exposure to pollutants.

COPD causes the airways to become narrowed and inflamed, leading to the production of phlegm. COPD patients often produce more phlegm than people with other respiratory conditions. Bronchitis is an infection of the airways that causes inflammation and irritation. This can lead to the production of excess phlegm, which is a thick, sticky substance that helps to trap germs and irritants. Bronchitis can be acute or chronic, and both types can cause significant phlegm production. In addition to these conditions, other factors can contribute to phlegm production, such as allergies, colds, and sinus infections.

However, asthma, COPD, and bronchitis are the most common causes of phlegm.

Below are the main causes of phlegm.

  • Asthma: Asthma is a cause of phlegm because the airways in the lungs become inflamed and irritated, producing excess mucus, which can be coughed up as phlegm.
  • COPD: COPD is a cause of phlegm because the damaged lungs are unable to properly remove mucus and other substances from the airways, causing the excess mucus to build up and be coughed up as phlegm.
  • Bronchitis: Bronchitis is a cause of phlegm because the inflamed airways in the lungs produce excess mucus, which then turns into thick, sticky phlegm to help trap and clear out the trapped bacteria, dust, and other irritants.
  • Pneumonia: Pneumonia occurs when a bacterial or viral infection causes inflammation in the lungs, leading to the production of excess mucus and phlegm as the body tries to trap and eliminate the invading germs.
  • Allergies: When the body reacts to an allergen, it produces extra mucus to trap the allergen, leading to an increase in phlegm production as the body tries to fight off the foreign substance.
  • Sinusitis: Sinusitis occurs when the sinuses become inflamed, causing them to produce excess mucus, which often turns into phlegm due to the presence of bacteria, dead cells, and other debris in the mucus.
  • Cold: When we get cold, our body tries to warm itself up by increasing mucus production in our airways, which turns into phlegm to trap cold air and prevent it from reaching our lungs.
