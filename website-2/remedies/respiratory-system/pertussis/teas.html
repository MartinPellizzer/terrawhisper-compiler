Pertussis relief for the immunocompromised: 9 herbal teas

People with weak immune systems, such as those with HIV/AIDS, cancer, or taking medicine that suppresses their immune system, are more likely to get severely ill with pertussis, also known as whooping cough.

This is because their bodies can't fight off the bacteria that cause the illness, which can lead to serious complications like pneumonia and even death. For those who are immunocompromised, pertussis can be a nightmare - it can cause constant coughing, difficulty breathing, and fatigue, making everyday activities like walking, eating, and even sleeping a challenge. They may also be more likely to experience other symptoms like fever, headache, and muscle pain.

In some cases, the cough can be so severe that it leads to inhaled food or liquids, which can be life-threatening. As a result, immunocompromised individuals often require hospitalization to receive supportive care, like oxygen therapy and fluids, to help manage their symptoms. Herbal teas have been used for centuries to help alleviate symptoms of various illnesses, including pertussis. For immunocompromised individuals, these teas can offer some relief from the harsh symptoms of whooping cough.

Some herbal teas contain antiviral and antibacterial properties that can help combat the bacteria that cause the illness, while others may help soothe the throat and calm the cough.

1. Echinacea purpurea

Echinacea purpurea teas have been used for centuries to help alleviate symptoms of pertussis, also known as whooping cough.

The teas contain properties and constituents like alkylamides, glycosides, and phenolic acids, which are thought to stimulate the immune system and reduce inflammation in the respiratory tract. The plant's ability to fight off bacterial and viral infections, particularly those that affect the lungs and airways, makes it a promising natural remedy for pertussis. If you or your child is experiencing the symptoms of pertussis, such as a persistent cough, fever, and difficulty breathing, you can try making a tea from dried Echinacea purpurea flowers and leaves. Steep 1-2 teaspoons of dried plant material in a cup of boiling water for 5-7 minutes, then strain and drink.

You can also add honey or lemon to make it more palatable. It's essential to consult with a healthcare professional before giving Echinacea tea to a child or using it as a replacement for conventional treatment. When taking Echinacea purpurea teas for pertussis, you can expect the following outcomes: reduced coughing, decreased fever, and improved breathing. Some people may also experience a faster recovery time and a reduction in the severity of symptoms.

However, it's crucial to note that Echinacea tea is not a substitute for medical treatment, and you should continue to follow your healthcare provider's advice and instructions.

2. Eucalyptus globulus

Eucalyptus globulus teas have been studied for their potential to help alleviate symptoms of pertussis, also known as whooping cough.

The tea's active compounds, including eucalyptol, limonene, and beta-caryophyllene, have anti-inflammatory and expectorant properties, which may help reduce the severity of coughing fits and other symptoms associated with pertussis. Eucalyptus globulus teas may also help loosen and clear mucus from the airways, making it easier to breathe.

To try using Eucalyptus globulus teas for pertussis, steep one teaspoon of dried leaves in a cup of boiling water for 5-7 minutes, then strain and drink 2-3 times a day. It's essential to consult with a healthcare professional before using Eucalyptus globulus teas, especially if you're already taking other medications or have underlying health conditions. If you're experiencing severe symptoms or difficulty breathing, seek medical attention immediately.

By drinking Eucalyptus globulus teas, you may notice a reduction in coughing fits, improved breathing, and a decrease in the overall severity of symptoms.

4. Thymus vulgaris

Thymus vulgaris teas, also known as thyme tea, have been used traditionally to help alleviate symptoms of pertussis, commonly known as whooping cough.

The active compounds in thyme, such as thymol and carvacrol, have antimicrobial properties that help combat the Bordetella pertussis bacteria responsible for the infection. Thymol, in particular, has been shown to exhibit antitussive properties, which can help reduce coughing fits. Carvacrol also has anti-inflammatory properties that can help soothe the throat and reduce irritation.

To use thyme tea for pertussis, you can steep 1 teaspoon of dried thyme leaves in a cup of boiling water for 5-7 minutes. Strain the tea and drink it 2-3 times a day. It's essential to consult with a healthcare professional before using thyme tea, especially if you're experiencing severe symptoms or are taking any medications. By taking thyme tea, you may experience a reduction in coughing fits, a decrease in fever, and an overall improvement in respiratory symptoms.

However, it's crucial to note that thyme tea should not be relied upon as the sole treatment for pertussis, and you should continue to follow the advice of your healthcare provider.

FAQ

Can drinking herbal tea prevent pertussis from forming?

Drinking herbal tea may not prevent pertussis, also known as whooping cough.

Pertussis is caused by a type of bacteria. Herbal teas often don't have the right ingredients to fight this bacteria.

They may help with colds and coughs, but it's not a guarantee against pertussis.

Is it safe to consume herbal teas for pertussis every day?

Herbal teas like thyme and eucalyptus are sometimes used to help with pertussis symptoms.

Some people drink them every day, but it's essential to remember that their effectiveness can vary. While thyme has antibacterial properties, eucalyptus may help ease coughs.

However, more research is needed to understand their impact on pertussis.

How long does it take for herbal teas to show results in pertussis?

Herbal teas like thyme and eucalyptus may help with pertussis symptoms.

They can start to show results in a few days to a week. Some people notice an improvement in their cough and breathing within 3-5 days, but it may take longer for others.

The effectiveness can vary depending on the individual and the severity of their condition.

What time of day is best to drink herbal tea for pertussis?

For pertussis, it's best to drink herbal tea at night, before bed.

This is because some herbal teas, like those with thymol, can help calm a cough and promote restful sleep.

Drinking them at night allows you to get a good night's sleep, which can help your body recover from the illness.
