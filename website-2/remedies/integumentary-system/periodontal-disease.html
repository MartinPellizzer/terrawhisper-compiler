What Are The Main Causes Of Periodontal Disease?

Periodontal disease is a serious infection of the gums and bones that support the teeth.

It's caused by a combination of factors. One main cause is poor oral hygiene, which means not brushing and flossing regularly. This allows plaque, a sticky film of bacteria, to build up on the teeth. Plaque can harden into tartar, which irritates the gums and makes them inflamed. If plaque and tartar are not removed, they can cause the gums to pull away from the teeth, creating pockets where bacteria can grow. Smoking is another major cause of periodontal disease.

Smoking weakens the immune system and reduces blood flow to the gums, making it harder for the body to fight off infections. This can lead to more severe gum disease and tooth loss. Smoking also slows down healing, which means that even if the disease is treated, the gums may not recover as quickly as they should. Genetics can also play a role in periodontal disease. Some people may be more prone to gum disease because of their genetic makeup. This means that they may be more likely to develop the disease, even if they take good care of their teeth.

However, genetics do not mean that someone will definitely get the disease - other factors, such as oral hygiene and smoking, can still contribute to its development.

Below are the main causes of periodontal disease.

  • Poor Oral Hygiene: Poor oral hygiene allows bacteria to build up on teeth and gums, leading to inflammation, infection, and damage to the soft tissue and bone that support the teeth.
  • Smoking: Smoking is a cause of periodontal disease because the chemicals in cigarette smoke damage the gum tissue and reduce blood flow to the gums, making it harder for them to heal and fight off infection.
  • Genetics: Genetics plays a role in causing periodontal disease because some people are naturally more prone to developing gum disease due to inherited traits that affect the way their immune system and teeth respond to bacteria.
  • Diabetes: Diabetes can cause periodontal disease because high blood sugar levels can damage blood vessels, reducing blood flow to the gums, making them more susceptible to infections that can lead to periodontal disease.
  • Hormonal Imbalance: Hormonal imbalance, particularly high levels of androgens (male hormones) such as testosterone, can cause periodontal disease by increasing inflammation and causing the bacteria in the mouth to grow and multiply more quickly, leading to gum infection and damage.
  • Medications: Medications can cause periodontal disease by reducing saliva flow, which helps to neutralize acids in the mouth and wash away bacteria, allowing plaque to form and cause infections.
  • Malocclusion: Malocclusion can cause periodontal disease because the uneven alignment of teeth can make it harder to clean between them properly, leading to the buildup of bacteria and plaque that can cause gum inflammation and infection.
