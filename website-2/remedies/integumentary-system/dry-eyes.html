What Are The Main Causes Of Dry Eyes?

Dry eyes occur when the eyes don't produce enough tears or the tears are of poor quality.

Several factors contribute to this condition. One main cause is age. As people get older, the glands that produce tears tend to shrink and become less effective, resulting in reduced tear production. This is why older adults are more likely to experience dry eyes. Another cause is wearing contact lenses.

Contact lenses can reduce tear flow by covering the surface of the eyes and preventing the tears from spreading evenly. Additionally, some people may experience irritation or inflammation while wearing contact lenses, which can further exacerbate dry eyes. Medications can also cause dry eyes. Certain types of medications, such as antihistamines, decongestants, and blood pressure medications, can reduce tear production or affect the eyes' ability to produce tears. This is because these medications can cause the glands that produce tears to become less active or even shut down temporarily.

As a result, people taking these medications may experience dry, itchy, or irritated eyes.

Below are the main causes of dry eyes.

  • Age: As people get older, the glands in their eyes that produce tears start to slow down and produce thinner, less effective tears, leading to dry eyes.
  • Contact Lenses: Contact lenses can cause dry eyes because the lens can reduce tear production and also decrease the eye's ability to absorb oxygen, leading to irritation and discomfort.
  • Medications: Medications can cause dry eyes because they can decrease tear production or change the way tears work, making it harder for the eyes to stay moist and comfortable.
  • Sjogrens Syndrome: Sjogren's Syndrome is a cause of dry eyes because it damages the tiny glands in the eyes that produce tears, making it hard for the eyes to stay moist and comfortable.
  • Dry Wind: Dry wind strips the tears from your eyes, leaving them dry and itchy due to the strong air blowing away the moisture.
  • Autoimmune Disorders: Autoimmune disorders, such as rheumatoid arthritis, cause dry eyes by mistakenly attacking and damaging the tiny glands in the eyes that normally produce tears, leading to reduced tear production and dryness.
  • Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca: Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca, commonly known as dry eye syndrome, is a condition where the tear glands produce less than normal tears, or the tears themselves do not provide enough moisture to the eyes, leading to dry and irritated eyes.

What Herbs To Avoid If You Have Dry Eyes?

If you have dry eyes, it's essential to know which herbs to avoid.

Some herbs can make dry eye symptoms worse, while others can cause irritation. For example, Wormwood (Artemisia absinthium) contains a compound called thujone, which can dry out the eyes even more.

Sage (Salvia officinalis) and Hyssop (Hyssopus officinalis) may also be problematic, as they can reduce tear production and increase evaporation. Another herb to avoid is Yarrow (Achillea-like, but not actually 'Achillea millefolium') is not on the list, however 'Ageratum conyzoides', or Goats Rue, can cause eye irritation.

Even though it's not typically used in eye care, Mugwort (Artemisia vulgaris) can also exacerbate dry eye issues due to its thujone content.
