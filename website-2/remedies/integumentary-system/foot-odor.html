Foot Odor: Causes, Herbal Remedies, and Effective Preparations

Foot odor is a type of body odor that occurs when sweat on the feet interacts with bacteria, causing an unpleasant smell.

It's essential to deal with foot odor because if left untreated, it can lead to discomfort, embarrassment, and even skin infections. If you don't address foot odor, the bacteria can multiply, causing the smell to get worse and potentially leading to conditions like athlete's foot or fungal infections. Its antiseptic properties help to reduce bacteria and prevent the growth of fungi, which in turn reduces the odor. It can be made into a decoction, a liquid preparation by steeping the herb in hot water, and used as a foot bath to help eliminate the bacteria causing the odor. Eucalyptus globulus, also known as eucalyptus, has deodorizing properties that can help to mask the smell of foot odor. It can be used as a tea by steeping the leaves in hot water, then soaking the feet in the solution. Alternatively, eucalyptus oil can be added to a foot bath or applied directly to the affected area. In addition to these herbs, herbal teas can also be a great way to help with foot odor. By drinking a tea made from herbs like Eucalyptus globulus, Cymbopogon citratus, or Melaleuca alternifolia, you can help to reduce the bacteria causing the odor from the inside out. Herbal preparations like tinctures, decoctions, or teas can be a safe and natural way to address foot odor.

What Are The Main Causes Of Foot Odor?

Foot odor occurs due to a combination of factors.

One main cause is bacterial overgrowth. Bacteria, especially those of the type Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus, thrive in warm and moist environments, such as between the toes and inside shoes. When sweat mixes with bacteria, it creates a strong smell. Another cause is sweat.

Our feet naturally produce sweat to regulate body temperature. However, when sweat accumulates in shoes and isn't allowed to dry, it creates an ideal environment for bacteria to multiply, leading to foot odor. Trimethylamine (TMA) is also a contributor to foot odor. TMA is a compound produced by the breakdown of certain amino acids in sweat, particularly when bacteria convert it into its smelly form.

This process is more likely to occur in warm, moist environments, which is why foot odor is often more noticeable during hot weather or when wearing shoes that don't breathe well.

Below are the main causes of foot odor.

  • Bacterial Overgrowth: Bacterial overgrowth in the feet occurs when sweat and bacteria mix together, creating a perfect environment for bacteria to multiply rapidly, leading to strong and unpleasant foot odor.
  • Sweat: Sweat is a cause of foot odor because when sweat mixes with bacteria on the skin of the feet, it breaks down into compounds that give off a strong and unpleasant smell.
  • Trimethylamine: Trimethylamine is a strong-smelling gas produced when bacteria in your body break down the proteins in sweat, especially when it gets trapped in your shoes or socks, causing foot odor.
  • E. coli: E. coli bacteria thrive in the warm, moist environment of the feet, breaking down sweat into compounds that give off strong, unpleasant smells.
  • K. pneumoniae: Klebsiella pneumoniae (K. pneumoniae) is a type of bacteria that can cause foot odor because it breaks down sweat and other substances on the skin, releasing strong-smelling compounds.
  • P. aeruginosa: Pseudomonas aeruginosa is a type of bacteria that lives on sweaty feet and breaks down sweat into compounds that cause strong, unpleasant odors.
  • B. subtilis: B. subtilis is a type of bacteria that lives on the skin and in shoes, breaking down sweat and other substances into compounds that give a strong, unpleasant smell.

What Herbs To Avoid If You Have Foot Odor?

If you have foot odor, there are some herbs you should avoid using.

Sage (Salvia officinalis) is one of them. It has a strong, pungent smell that can be overpowering and might make foot odor worse. Sage is often used to mask bad smells, but it can also bring out the underlying odor of foot sweat.

Another herb to avoid is Anise (Pimpinella anisum), which has a sweet, licorice-like scent. This can be appealing in cooking, but it's not helpful in reducing foot odor. Anise can actually make sweat smell worse. Also, avoid using Tansy (Tanacetum vulgare) and Lovage (Levisticum officinale), as they have strong, pungent smells that can be overwhelming.

These herbs can be too overpowering and might not address the underlying foot odor issue.
