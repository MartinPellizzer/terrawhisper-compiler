Fever Blister on Lip: Causes, Herbal Preparations, and Home Remedies for Relief

They are often triggered by stress, fatigue, certain foods, or even the sun. When the herpes simplex virus, which is usually dormant in the body, becomes active, it can cause a fever blister to form. What are the causes of fever blister on lip?

Fever blisters on the lip are usually caused by a viral infection called HSV-1 (Herpes Simplex Virus 1).

This virus is highly contagious and can be spread through skin-to-skin contact, sharing utensils, or even kissing someone who has the virus. When we come into contact with the virus, it enters our body and stays dormant until it's triggered by something. Stress is another major cause of fever blisters. When we're under a lot of stress, our immune system gets weaker, making it easier for the virus to become active. This is because our body's energy is focused on dealing with the stress, leaving less energy to fight off the virus. Hormonal changes can also cause fever blisters, especially during pregnancy or menstruation.

When hormone levels change, it can trigger the virus to become active. This is because hormonal fluctuations can weaken our immune system, making it harder for our body to fight off the virus. Fatigue is another contributing factor to fever blisters. When we're tired, our immune system gets weaker, making it easier for the virus to become active. This is because our body needs rest to function properly, and when we're not getting enough rest, our immune system suffers. Anxiety is also a trigger for fever blisters. When we're anxious, our body releases stress hormones like adrenaline, which can weaken our immune system.

This makes it easier for the virus to become active, leading to the formation of a fever blister.

What herbs should you avoid if you have fever blister on lip?

If you have a fever blister on your lip, there are certain herbs you should avoid.

Capsicum annuum, also known as cayenne pepper, is too spicy and can irritate the affected area, making it worse. Glycyrrhiza glabra, or licorice root, has a compound that can thin the skin, which might make your fever blister more painful and prone to infection.

Sanguinaria canadensis, or bloodroot, can be toxic if not used properly, and its irritating properties might exacerbate the blister. Ginkgo biloba, while often used for its anti-inflammatory properties, can also cause allergic reactions, which might be triggered by a fever blister. Cinchona officinalis, or Peruvian bark, contains quinine, a substance that can make your fever blister worse by causing inflammation and redness.

These herbs might seem harmless, but they can interact with your fever blister in unpredictable ways, making it harder for it to heal.
