What are the causes of stye?

A stye, also known as a hordeolum, is a painful bump on the eyelid caused by an infection or blockage.

The main causes of a stye are related to the eyelid's structure and its surroundings. One of the main causes is a bacterial infection, particularly Staphylococcus aureus, which is a common bacteria found on the skin. This bacteria can enter the eyelid through a small cut or scratch, causing an infection. Folliculitis, an inflammation of the eyelash follicle, is another cause of a stye.

When the follicle becomes infected, it can lead to a painful bump on the eyelid. Blepharitis, inflammation of the eyelid, can also cause a stye. This condition is often caused by an imbalance of the skin's natural oils, which can lead to bacterial growth. Staph bacteria are often responsible for blepharitis and can cause a stye. Meibomian gland dysfunction, a condition where the glands that produce the eye's tear film become blocked, can also cause a stye.

When the glands become blocked, they can become infected, leading to a painful bump on the eyelid.

What herbs should you avoid if you have stye?

Its essential oils can be irritating to the skin, and using rosemary oil on a stye could cause more inflammation and discomfort. This means you might want to avoid using rosemary oil or any products containing it until your stye has healed. Rhus toxicodendron, or poison ivy, is not an herb you'll usually find in a skincare routine, but its sap can cause a severe skin reaction.

If you have a stye, applying milk thistle oil to the affected area could make it worse, especially if you have sensitive skin. If you have a stye and accidentally get potato sap on it, it could make the stye worse and cause more redness and swelling.
