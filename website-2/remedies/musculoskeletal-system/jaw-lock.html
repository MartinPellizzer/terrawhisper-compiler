If left untreated, jaw lock can lead to chronic pain, limited jaw movement, and difficulties with eating, speaking, and even breathing. It's essential to address jaw lock promptly as prolonged suffering can result in further complications, such as temporomandibular joint (TMJ) disorder, teeth grinding, and even tooth loss. What Are The Main Causes Of Jaw Lock?

A jaw lock occurs when the jaw joint or the muscles surrounding it become stuck or frozen, making it difficult to move the jaw.

There are several reasons why this happens. One common cause is Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ) disorders. The TMJ is a complex joint that connects the jawbone to the skull, and it can become inflamed or damaged due to various reasons, such as a blow to the jaw, teeth grinding, or misalignment of the teeth.

This can lead to pain and stiffness in the jaw, making it hard to open or close the mouth. Temporomandibular disorders can also cause the jaw to lock in place, preventing it from moving freely. Another cause of jaw lock is Bruxism, which is the habit of grinding or clenching the teeth, usually while sleeping. This can put a lot of pressure on the jaw joint and muscles, leading to inflammation and stiffness, and eventually causing the jaw to lock.

Additionally, Bruxism can also lead to wear and tear on the teeth and jaw joint, making it more susceptible to damage and lock.

Below are the main causes of jaw lock.

  • TMJ: TMJ (Temporomandibular Joint) disorders can cause jaw lock because the joint's disc or cartilage can become misaligned, leading to muscle spasms and locking of the jaw in place.
  • Temporomandibular: Temporomandibular joint disorder (TMJ) can cause jaw lock because of the inflammation and irritation of the joint, which leads to a restriction in the movement of the jaw, causing it to lock in place.
  • Bruxism: Bruxism, or grinding and clenching the teeth, causes jaw lock by putting too much pressure on the jaw joint, leading to muscle strain and locking of the jaw in place.
  • Dislocation: Dislocation is a cause of jaw lock because when the jawbone moves out of its normal position, the jaw joint can become stuck, making it difficult to open or close the mouth.
  • Strain: The strain on the jaw muscles and joints from over-clenching or grinding of teeth can cause jaw lock due to the wear and tear on the ligaments that support the jaw joint.
  • Overbite: Overbite occurs when the upper teeth extend too far over the lower teeth, causing the lower jaw to lock into place due to an imbalance in the bite alignment.
  • Facial trauma: Facial trauma can cause jaw lock because it may damage or break the joints that connect the lower jawbone to the skull, leading to pain and stiffness that prevents the jaw from opening and closing normally.
