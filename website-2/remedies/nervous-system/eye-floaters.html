What Are The Main Causes Of Eye Floaters?

Eye floaters are tiny spots that appear in your vision, often looking like small dots or cobwebs.

There are several causes of eye floaters, including myopia (nearsightedness). Myopia occurs when the shape of the eyeball is too long, causing the light to focus in front of the retina, rather than directly on it. As a result, the eye's gel-like substance called vitreous can start to shrink and pull away from the retina, causing floaters to appear. Another cause of eye floaters is astigmatism, a condition where the cornea or lens inside the eye is not perfectly round.

This can cause light to focus at two different points, rather than one, leading to blurry vision and eye floaters. Astigmatism can cause the vitreous to become unevenly shaped, which can also lead to floaters. A cataract can also cause eye floaters. A cataract is a clouding of the lens inside the eye, which can cause vision to become blurry and distorted. As the cataract grows, it can cause the vitreous to become cloudy and develop floaters.

In some cases, eye surgery to remove a cataract can also cause floaters, as the surgery can cause the vitreous to become disturbed and release tiny particles into the eye.

Below are the main causes of eye floaters.

  • Myopia: As the clear gel-like substance in the eye, called vitreous, shrinks and separates from the retina, it can cast tiny shadows on the retina, causing the appearance of eye floaters in people with myopia, also known as nearsightedness.
  • Astigmatism: Astigmatism can cause eye floaters because it affects the shape of the cornea or lens inside the eye, causing the vitreous gel to pull and stretch, which can lead to tiny spots or debris that appear as floaters.
  • Cataract: A cataract can cause eye floaters because the cloudy lens inside the eye, which is normally clear, scatters light in a way that makes tiny spots or strings appear to float in front of the person's vision.
  • Glaucoma: Glaucoma can cause eye floaters because the increased pressure inside the eye damages the clear gel-like substance called the vitreous, which can break apart and cast shadows on the retina.
  • Age: As people get older, the clear gel-like substance inside their eyeballs, called vitreous, can break down and form tiny clumps that float across their vision, causing eye floaters.
  • Detachment: Detachment of the vitreous gel inside the eye is a cause of eye floaters because it can break down and form small clumps of debris that cast shadows on the retina, creating the sensation of floating objects in the line of sight.

What Herbs To Avoid If You Have Eye Floaters?

If you have eye floaters, it's essential to avoid certain herbs that can cause problems or make them worse.

One herb to avoid is Bilberry (Vaccinium myrtillus), as it may increase the risk of bleeding in the eyes, which can worsen floaters. Another herb to steer clear of is Red Clover (Trifolium pratense), which contains isoflavones that can cause eye problems, including increased blood flow to the eyes, potentially making floaters more noticeable.

Also, avoid St. John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum) as it can interact with other medications and worsen conditions that lead to eye floaters. Similarly, avoid Glaucium flavum (Glaucium) and Black Cohosh (Actaea racemosa), as they can cause eye problems and interact with other medications.

Finally, be cautious with Angelica (Angelica archangelica), as it can cause vision problems and interact with blood thinners, which may be used to treat conditions related to eye floaters.
