What are the causes of taste loss?

Taste loss, also known as ageusia, can be caused by several medical conditions.

One of the main causes is Sjogren's syndrome, an autoimmune disorder that affects the glands in the mouth, leading to dryness and inflammation. This results in a decreased ability to taste. Tongue cancer is another cause of taste loss. Cancer can damage the taste buds on the surface of the tongue, making it difficult to experience different flavors.

A medullary cyst, a rare condition where fluid-filled cysts form on the tongue, can also cause taste loss. The cysts can put pressure on the taste buds and make it hard for them to function properly. Glossopharyngeal neuropathy, a condition that affects the nerves controlling the tongue, can lead to taste loss. The nerves carry signals from the taste buds to the brain, so if they are damaged, it can disrupt the taste experience. Radiculopathy, a condition where the nerve roots in the spine are compressed or irritated, can also cause taste loss.

This is because the nerve roots can affect the nerves controlling the tongue, leading to a decrease in taste sensation.

What herbs should you avoid if you have taste loss?

If you have a problem with taste loss, you should be careful with certain herbs.

These herbs contain strong chemicals that can affect your sense of taste even more. For example, rosemary (Rosmarinus officinalis) and thyme (Thymus vulgaris) have compounds that can numb the tongue, making it harder to taste food.

Sage (Salvia officinalis) also has these kinds of chemicals, which can make your mouth feel numb and your taste buds less sensitive. Peppermint (Mentha x piperita) is another herb to watch out for, as its strong oils can also affect your sense of taste and make it harder to enjoy food. Even lavender (Lavandula angustifolia) can be a problem, as its fragrant oils can numb the tongue and reduce your sense of taste.

If you have trouble tasting food, it's a good idea to avoid these herbs or use them very carefully.
