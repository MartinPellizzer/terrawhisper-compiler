1. Eucalyptus globulus

Eucalyptus globulus essential oils contains cineole, a key bioactive constituent that helps relieve chills.

Cineole's decongestant properties break down mucus and reduce congestion, making it easier for the body to warm up. The oil's anti-inflammatory properties also help to reduce swelling and ease discomfort. By opening up airways and improving circulation, eucalyptus globulus essential oils helps the body to regulate its temperature more efficiently.

This results in a reduction in chills and an overall sense of relief.

  1. Gather 2 cups of Eucalyptus globulus leaves and 1 cup of water in a saucepan.
  2. Heat the water on low heat and add the Eucalyptus leaves. Let it simmer for 30 minutes.
  3. Strain the liquid and discard the leaves. You will get about 1 cup of eucalyptus infusion.
  4. Transfer the infusion to a still or a steam distillation apparatus. You can also use a DIY still.
  5. Steam distill the infusion for 1-2 hours to get the essential oil. Collect the oil in a separate container.

2. Zingiber officinale

Zingiber officinale essential oils contains boiactive constituents like gingerol and shogaol.

These compounds have warming properties that help to stimulate circulation and increase body temperature. Gingerol, in particular, has anti-inflammatory properties that can help to reduce muscle and joint pain, which can contribute to chills. Shogaol also has antimicrobial properties that can help to fight off infections, which can cause chills.

The warming and anti-inflammatory properties of these constituents make Zingiber officinale essential oils a natural remedy for chills.

  1. Gather 1 cup of fresh Zingiber officinale roots and 2 cups of water in a pot.
  2. Boil the water, then add the fresh roots and let them simmer for 30 minutes.
  3. Let the mixture cool down, then strain it to get the root infusion.
  4. Transfer the infusion to a still or a distillation apparatus and heat it gently to separate the oil.
  5. Collect the Zingiber officinale essential oil in a clean glass bottle and store it in a cool, dark place.

3. Echinacea purpurea

Echinacea purpurea essential oils contains active constituents like alkylamides, caffeic acid, and chicoric acid.

These compounds have anti-inflammatory properties that help reduce redness and swelling associated with chills. The essential oil also contains phenolic compounds like rosmarinic acid, which acts as an antioxidant and helps soothe the body. The immunomodulatory effects of the essential oil help regulate the body's immune response, which is often impaired when experiencing chills.

By reducing inflammation and regulating the immune system, Echinacea purpurea essential oils can provide relief from the symptoms of chills.

  1. Gather 2 cups of fresh Echinacea purpurea flowers, 1 cup of jojoba oil, and a clean glass jar.
  2. Combine the Echinacea flowers and jojoba oil in the glass jar, and stir well.
  3. Cover the jar with a lid and let it sit in a cool, dark place for 2 weeks, shaking the jar every day.
  4. After 2 weeks, strain the mixture through a cheesecloth into another clean glass jar, and discard the solids.
  5. Store the Echinacea essential oil in a dark glass bottle with a dropper lid in a cool, dry place.

4. Rosmarinus officinalis

Rosmarinus officinalis essential oils contains rosmarinic acid, borneol, and camphor as its active constituents.

These compounds provide anti-inflammatory and antispasmodic properties that help alleviate chills. Rosmarinic acid in particular, has been shown to reduce inflammation and relieve muscle spasms, making it effective in treating chills. Additionally, the antiseptic properties of camphor help to combat infections that may be causing the chills.

The stimulating properties of borneol also help to increase circulation, which can aid in reducing body temperature and alleviating chills.

  1. Gather 1 cup of fresh Rosmarinus officinalis leaves and 2 cups of water in a large bowl.
  2. Steep the leaves in the water for 2 hours in a warm place.
  3. Strain the mixture through a cheesecloth into a separate bowl, discarding the leaves.
  4. Transfer the liquid to a saucepan and simmer over low heat for 1 hour, reducing the liquid to 1 cup.
  5. Let the mixture cool, then store it in a dark glass bottle with a tight-fitting lid for up to 2 weeks.

5. Thymus vulgaris

Thymus vulgaris essential oils contains thymol and carvacrol as its active constituents.

These compounds have antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties that help fight off infections causing chills. Thymol, in particular, has been shown to have a direct effect on the body's temperature regulation, helping to warm the body and reduce shivering. Carvacrol's ability to reduce inflammation also contributes to the relief of chills by addressing underlying causes of the condition.

By applying Thymus vulgaris essential oils topically or inhaling its aroma, the body can benefit from these properties and help alleviate chills.

  1. Gather 1 cup of Thymus vulgaris leaves and 2 cups of water in a saucepan.
  2. Heat the water over medium heat and add the Thymus vulgaris leaves.
  3. Reduce heat to low and let it simmer for 20 minutes, then turn off the heat.
  4. Let the mixture steep for 4 hours, then strain it using a cheesecloth or a coffee filter.
  5. Bottle the essential oil and store it in a cool, dark place.

6. Lavandula angustifolia

Lavandula angustifolia essential oils contains linalool and linalyl acetate, two active constituents that contribute to its therapeutic properties.

These compounds have a calming effect on the body, which can help to alleviate chills by reducing inflammation and relaxing muscles. The antiseptic and antispasmodic properties of linalool and linalyl acetate also help to soothe and calm the nervous system, reducing symptoms of chills and promoting a sense of well-being. Furthermore, the oil's ability to reduce anxiety and promote a restful night's sleep can help to regulate body temperature and alleviate chills.

By applying Lavandula angustifolia essential oils topically or inhaling its vapors, you can harness its therapeutic properties to help manage chills and promote a sense of relaxation.

  1. Gather 2 cups of fresh Lavandula angustifolia flowers and 1 cup of carrier oil like jojoba or sweet almond oil.
  2. Dry the lavender flowers in a warm place for 1-2 weeks or use a food dehydrator.
  3. Combine 1 cup of dried lavender flowers with 1 cup of carrier oil in a clean glass jar.
  4. Steep the mixture for 2-3 weeks in a cool, dark place, shaking the jar every few days.
  5. Strain the liquid through a cheesecloth or coffee filter into a clean glass bottle, discard the solids.

7. Salvia officinalis

Salvia officinalis essential oils contains boiactive constituents like carnosic acid, carnosol, and ursolic acid.

These constituents have anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties that help reduce fever and chills. Carnosic acid, in particular, has been shown to have a cooling effect on the body, which can help alleviate chills. The antimicrobial properties of these constituents also help fight off infections that can cause chills.

By reducing inflammation and fighting off infections, Salvia officinalis essential oils can help the body recover from illnesses that cause chills.

  1. Gather 1 cup of fresh Salvia officinalis leaves and 1 cup of water. Clean the leaves with water.
  2. Place the leaves in a saucepan, add the water, and bring to a boil. Reduce heat to low.
  3. Steam the leaves for 30 minutes. Then, remove the saucepan from heat.
  4. Let the mixture cool for 10 minutes. Strain the liquid through a cheesecloth or a coffee filter.
  5. Transfer the liquid to a glass bottle with a tight-fitting lid. Store in a cool, dark place.

8. Melaleuca alternifolia

Melaleuca alternifolia essential oils contains cineole and terpinen-4-ol as its active constituents.

These compounds have antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties, which help to combat infections that can cause chills. Terpinen-4-ol, in particular, has been shown to have a strong effect against bacteria and viruses, helping to reduce the severity of symptoms. Cineole, on the other hand, has a warming effect on the body, which can help to counteract the cold feeling associated with chills.

By targeting the underlying causes of chills, Melaleuca alternifolia essential oils can help to provide relief from this uncomfortable symptom.

  1. Gather 1 cup of Melaleuca alternifolia leaves and 2 cups of distilled water in a large pot.
  2. Boil the water and then reduce heat to low. Add the Melaleuca leaves and let them steep for 2 hours.
  3. Strain the mixture into a separate container using a cheesecloth or a fine-mesh sieve. Discard the solids.
  4. Transfer the liquid to a distillation apparatus and heat it to separate the essential oil from the water.
  5. Collect the essential oil and store it in a dark glass bottle with a tight-fitting lid. Use 5-7 drops in a diffuser or apply topically diluted with a carrier oil.

FAQ

Can drinking herbal tea prevent chills from forming?

Drinking herbal tea may help prevent chills in some cases.

Ginger tea, for example, has natural warming properties that can raise your body temperature. Peppermint tea, on the other hand, can help ease congestion and promote blood flow, which can also help prevent chills.

These teas might be especially helpful during cold weather or when feeling under the weather.

Is it safe to consume herbal teas for chills every day?

While herbal teas can help with chills, consuming them daily might not be safe for everyone.

Some herbal teas contain caffeine or other stimulants that can interfere with sleep or cause jitters. Others may interact with medications or have side effects like stomach upset.

It's best to drink them in moderation and choose teas that are specifically labeled as safe for daily use.

How long does it take for herbal teas to show results in chills?

Herbal teas can help with chills, but it takes some time to notice results.

Some people feel better within 30 minutes to an hour after drinking a hot herbal tea.

The relief from chills can last a few hours, but it depends on the individual and the type of tea consumed.
