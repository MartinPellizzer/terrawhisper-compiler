What Are The Main Causes Of Lost Voice?

When you lose your voice, it's usually due to one of several main causes.

One of these causes is vocal strain, which happens when you use your voice too much or incorrectly. For example, shouting, singing loudly, or talking for a long time without resting your voice can put too much stress on your vocal cords, causing them to become irritated and swell. This makes it hard to speak and can lead to a hoarse or raspy voice.

Another cause of lost voice is laryngitis, which is an inflammation of the vocal cords. This can be caused by a viral or bacterial infection, such as a cold or flu, and can also be triggered by allergies or exposure to pollution and dust. When your vocal cords are inflamed, they become red and swollen, making it difficult to speak and producing a sore or hoarse voice. Lastly, hypothyroidism, a condition where the thyroid gland doesn't produce enough thyroid hormones, can also cause a lost voice.

Thyroid hormones play a crucial role in regulating the health and function of the vocal cords, so when they're lacking, the vocal cords can become swollen and inflamed, leading to a hoarse or raspy voice.

Below are the main causes of lost voice.

  • Vocal Strain: Vocal strain occurs when the vocal cords become irritated and inflamed, causing them to swell and vibrate improperly, resulting in a lost or weakened voice.
  • Laryngitis: Laryngitis occurs when the vocal cords, which are the two small bands of muscle tissue in the throat that help us speak, become inflamed or swollen, making it difficult for them to vibrate and produce sound, resulting in a lost or hoarse voice.
  • Hypothyroidism: Hypothyroidism can cause a lost voice because the thyroid gland, which regulates hormone production, is not working properly, leading to a decrease in thyroid hormones that help keep muscles, including the vocal cords, strong and functioning correctly.
  • Thyroid Nodule: A thyroid nodule can cause a lost voice because it can press on the nerves in the neck that control the vocal cords, leading to hoarseness or complete voice loss.
  • Tumor: When a tumor grows on the vocal cords or nearby nerves in the throat, it can damage or block the nerves that control the voice, leading to a lost or changed voice.
