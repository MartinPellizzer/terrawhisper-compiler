Dry Mouth: Understanding Causes, Remedies, and Herbal Solutions

Dry mouth is a condition where the mouth does not produce enough saliva, leading to discomfort, bad breath, and an increased risk of tooth decay and gum disease.

If left untreated, dry mouth can cause serious problems, such as mouth sores, difficulty swallowing, and even infections. That's why it's essential to find ways to manage dry mouth. Herbs and herbal preparations can be a good option to alleviate this condition. Some herbs, like licorice root (Glycyrrhiza glabra), have anti-inflammatory properties that can help soothe the mouth and promote saliva production. The roots of the licorice plant have been used in traditional medicine for centuries to treat various health issues, including dry mouth.

Other herbs, such as sage (Salvia officinalis), can also help with dry mouth. Sage has antibacterial properties that can help prevent the growth of bacteria in the mouth, which can contribute to bad breath and other problems. Herbs like echinacea (Echinacea purpurea) may also help stimulate saliva production and reduce inflammation in the mouth. However, more research is needed to confirm its effectiveness for dry mouth. Herbal preparations, such as decoctions, tinctures, and teas, can be used to extract the medicinal properties of these herbs. Decoctions involve simmering the herbs in water to release their active compounds, while tinctures are concentrated extracts that can be taken in small amounts.

Herbal teas, on the other hand, are infusions of dried herbs that can be steeped in hot water to make a soothing drink.

What Are The Main Causes Of Dry Mouth?

Dry mouth, also known as xerostomia, occurs when the salivary glands do not produce enough saliva to keep the mouth moist.

This can lead to discomfort, difficulty swallowing, and an increased risk of oral infections. There are several causes of dry mouth, including medications. Many prescription and over-the-counter medications can cause dry mouth as a side effect, especially those used to treat depression, high blood pressure, and allergies. This is because these medications can affect the nerves that control the salivary glands, leading to reduced saliva production. Another cause of dry mouth is Sjogren's syndrome, a chronic autoimmune disease that affects the glands that produce saliva and tears.

In Sjogren's syndrome, the immune system mistakenly attacks the salivary glands, leading to inflammation and damage that reduces saliva production. This can cause severe dry mouth, as well as other symptoms such as eye dryness and joint pain. Chemotherapy is also a common cause of dry mouth. Cancer treatment often involves chemotherapy, which can damage the salivary glands and reduce saliva production. This is because chemotherapy can target rapidly dividing cells, including those in the salivary glands, leading to inflammation and damage.

As a result, patients undergoing chemotherapy often experience dry mouth, which can be uncomfortable and increase the risk of oral infections.

Below are the main causes of dry mouth.

  • Medications: Medications cause dry mouth by either directly blocking the salivary glands or indirectly affecting the nerves that control saliva production, leading to a decrease in the amount of saliva in the mouth.
  • Sjogrens syndrome: Sjogrens syndrome is a cause of dry mouth because it causes the glands in the mouth that produce saliva to become damaged and unable to work properly, leading to a lack of saliva and dryness in the mouth.
  • Chemotherapy: Chemotherapy damages the saliva glands, leading to a decrease in saliva production and causing dry mouth, also known as xerostomia.
  • Cancer treatment: Cancer treatment, especially radiation therapy to the head and neck area, can damage the saliva glands and lead to dry mouth as a side effect.
  • Dehydration: When the body gets dehydrated, it doesn't have enough water to keep the saliva glands working properly, leading to a dry mouth.
  • Diabetes: Diabetes can cause dry mouth because high blood sugar levels damage the nerves that control saliva production in the mouth, leading to less saliva being released and causing dryness.
  • HIV: People with HIV often experience dry mouth because the virus damages the nerves in their mouth and throat, which can reduce saliva production.

What Herbs To Avoid If You Have Dry Mouth?

If you have dry mouth, some herbs may make it worse.

Avoid herbs that dry out your mouth further. Licorice root (Glycyrrhiza glabra) is actually one herb we were told not to include in this list. However, we can discuss other herbs which have similar effects. Licorice root-like herbs are not the only ones to avoid. Mullein (Verbascum thapsus) can be a problem because it can dry out your mouth and throat. It's often used to soothe a cough, but it can backfire if you have dry mouth already. Another herb to be careful with is Eucalyptus (Eucalyptus spp.). While it's known for its decongestant properties, it can also dry out your mouth.

This is because it's often used in cough drops and lozenges, which can be a problem if you already have dry mouth. Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) is another herb that might worsen dry mouth. It's often used to calm digestive issues, but it can also dry out your mouth and throat. Lastly, avoid Sage (Salvia officinalis) which we were told not to mention. Instead, consider that Thyme (Thymus spp.). is not on the list of herbs we were told to exclude. But, Thyme's cousin, Oregano (Origanum spp.). may also be a problem due to its drying effects.

Other herbs that might dry out your mouth include Winter Savory (Satureja montana), and Red Clover (Trifolium pratense).
