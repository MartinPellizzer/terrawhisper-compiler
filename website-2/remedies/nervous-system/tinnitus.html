What Are The Main Causes Of Tinnitus?

Tinnitus is a common condition where people hear sounds that aren't really there.

There are several reasons why it happens. One main cause is loud noises. When we're exposed to loud sounds, like music or construction work, it can damage the tiny hairs in our inner ear. These hairs help us hear, and when they're damaged, our brain can think it's still hearing the sound, even after the noise has stopped. This can lead to ringing, buzzing, or other sounds in the ear. Another cause of tinnitus is age.

As we get older, our ears naturally change. The tiny hairs in our inner ear can wear out or become less sensitive, leading to tinnitus. This is a common reason for tinnitus in older adults. Earwax is another cause of tinnitus. When earwax builds up in our ears, it can block the tiny hairs and cause them to vibrate abnormally. This can lead to tinnitus. However, earwax is a normal part of ear function, and it helps protect our ears from dirt and other small particles.

But if it builds up too much, it can cause problems.

Below are the main causes of tinnitus.

  • Loud Noises: Prolonged exposure to loud noises can cause tinnitus because it damages the tiny hair cells in our inner ears, leading to abnormal nerve signals that our brain interprets as ringing or other sounds.
  • Age: As we get older, the tiny hair cells in our ears that help us hear can wear out or die, leading to tinnitus, which is a ringing or buzzing sound in the ears.
  • Earwax: Earwax can cause tinnitus because it can block the ear canal and put pressure on the eardrum, causing unusual sounds in the ear when the body tries to clean out the blockage.
  • Ototoxicity: Ototoxicity is a cause of tinnitus because certain chemicals or medicines can damage the tiny hair cells in the inner ear, leading to loud ringing or other sounds in the ears.
  • Medications: Medications can cause tinnitus when they damage the delicate hairs in the inner ear or affect the nerves that send sound signals to the brain, leading to a ringing, buzzing, or other unusual sounds.

It raises blood pressure and can make tinnitus symptoms worse. Yohimbe (Pausinystalia johimbe) also increases blood pressure and can be too harsh for people with tinnitus.

Gymnema (Gymnema sylvestre) is another herb to be careful with. It can cause inflammation in the body, which may worsen tinnitus symptoms. Cayenne pepper (Capsicum annuum) and black pepper (Piper nigrum) contain compounds that can be too loud on the ears. While they may not cause tinnitus, they can make existing symptoms worse. St. John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum) can interact with medications for tinnitus and make them less effective.
