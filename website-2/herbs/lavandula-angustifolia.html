What is Lavandula angustifolia?

Lavandula angustifolia is a compact, perennial shrub native to the Mediterranean region, belonging to the family Lamiaceae.

Its botanical characteristics include narrow, linear leaves that are 2-4 cm long and 0.5-1 cm wide, and purple flowers in spikes, 5-8 cm long, appearing from June to September. The plant grows to a height of 30-50 cm and a width of 25-30 cm, with a spreading habit. Its flowers are highly fragrant and contain a mixture of linalool and linalyl acetate, making them a popular source of essential oil.

Lavandula angustifolia is also known as English lavender and is commonly cultivated for its ornamental and medicinal value.

What are the therapeutic properties this herb?

The main therapeutic properties of Lavandula angustifolia are its antimicrobial effects, which are attributed to the bioactive compounds, including volatile oils such as linalool and linalyl acetate, that inhibit the growth of bacteria and fungi.

The antioxidant properties of the plant are due to the presence of flavonoids and phenolic acids, which scavenge free radicals and protect against oxidative stress. This plant also exhibits anti-inflammatory effects, with saponins and terpenoids contributing to its ability to reduce inflammation and alleviate pain. Furthermore, Lavandula angustifolia possesses anxiolytic properties, which are partly due to the sedative effects of its volatile oils, that help to reduce anxiety and promote relaxation.

The sedative properties of the plant are also a result of its bioactive compounds, such as flavonoids and terpenoids, that interact with the GABA receptor and promote a calming effect.

What are the medicinal constituents this herb?

The most important healing constituents of Lavandula angustifolia are linalool, linalyl acetate, camphor, borneol, and caryophyllene oxide.

Linalool, a sesquiterpene alcohol, is known for its anxiolytic and sedative properties, reducing anxiety and promoting relaxation. Linalyl acetate, a monoterpenic ester, has antimicrobial and antiseptic properties, making it effective against bacteria, viruses, and fungi. Camphor, a monoterpenic ketone, is used for its analgesic and antiseptic properties, relieving pain and inflammation.

Caryophyllene oxide, a sesquiterpene epoxide, exhibits anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, reducing inflammation and oxidative stress in the body.

What are the possible side effects of using this herb improperly?

The possible side effects of using Lavandula angustifolia improperly include developing skin irritation due to its high concentration of linalool and linalyl acetate, which can cause contact dermatitis and allergic reactions in some individuals.

Additionally, Lavandula angustifolia may interact with medications such as blood thinners, diabetes medications, and sedatives, exacerbating their side effects and potentially leading to adverse reactions. Long-term use of Lavandula angustifolia essential oil has been linked to liver damage and toxicity in some cases, particularly when ingested or used in high concentrations. Furthermore, Lavandula angustifolia may induce seizures in individuals with a history of epilepsy or those taking anticonvulsant medications, as it can affect the nervous system and increase excitability.

The FDA has also warned about the potential for allergic reactions, including anaphylaxis, in individuals with known allergies to lavender.
