Citrus × latifolia

Citrus × latifolia, commonly known as the Tahiti lime, is a hybrid citrus plant that combines the characteristics of the key lime and the Persian lime.

The health benefits of the Tahiti lime include its high content of vitamin C, which supports immune function and antioxidant defenses, as well as its potential anti-inflammatory and antibacterial properties. The bioactive constituents of this plant include flavonoids, limonoids, and essential oils, such as limonene and beta-pinene, which contribute to its medicinal and culinary value.

classification

Citrus × latifolia is a hybrid of the pomelo (Citrus maxima) and the sweet orange (Citrus sinensis). It is classified as a species of the genus Citrus, in the family Rutaceae. The plant is commonly referred to as the Meyer lemon, although it is not a true lemon. The classification of Citrus × latifolia is further supported by its unique characteristics and genetic makeup. As a hybrid, it does not fit neatly into the traditional classification of citrus species.

Consuming large amounts of Tahiti lime juice can lead to an imbalance of electrolytes, resulting in symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and stomach pain. In rare cases, excessive consumption of Tahiti lime juice may also cause kidney damage due to its high oxalate content. Additionally, people with citrus allergies or sensitivities may experience skin irritation, itching, or hives after coming into contact with the fruit or its juice. It is essential to consume Tahiti lime in moderation and be aware of any adverse reactions.
