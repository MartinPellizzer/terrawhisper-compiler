Trifolium Pratense (Red Clover) Tea For Medicinal Use

Trifolium pratense, also known as red clover, is a flowering plant used to make a medicinal tea.

The tea is rich in isoflavones, specifically formononetin and genistein, which are also found in soy products. The constituents of red clover tea have been shown to have estrogenic activity, which may help alleviate menopausal symptoms such as hot flashes and night sweats. Red clover tea has also been used to support cardiovascular health by reducing inflammation and improving blood lipid profiles.

However, individuals with hormone-sensitive cancers, such as breast or uterine cancer, should avoid consuming red clover tea due to its potential to mimic estrogen in the body.

How to make Trifolium pratense tea

To make Trifolium pratense tea, also known as red clover tea, start by gathering 1-2 tablespoons of dried red clover flowers.

Red clover flowers can be harvested fresh, but they must be dried to preserve their medicinal properties and prevent spoilage. To dry the flowers, simply spread them out in a single layer on a paper bag or a clean cloth, and place them in a warm, dry, and well-ventilated area. Once the flowers are dry, you can steep 1-2 tablespoons of them in 1 cup of boiling water for 5-7 minutes to make a tea.

The resulting liquid should be strained and consumed while still warm, as the heat from the water helps to release the active compounds from the red clover flowers.

Possible side effects Trifolium pratense tea

If used improperly, Trifolium pratense tea, also known as red clover tea, may lead to an increased risk of bleeding due to its high content of isoflavones, which can interfere with blood clotting.

Consuming excessive amounts of this tea may also cause an imbalance in thyroid function, particularly for individuals with pre-existing thyroid conditions. Additionally, people with estrogen-sensitive cancers, such as breast or uterine cancer, should avoid drinking Trifolium pratense tea, as the isoflavones can stimulate the growth of estrogen-dependent tumors. Pregnant or breastfeeding women are also advised to limit or avoid consumption of this tea due to its potential to stimulate uterine contractions and affect fetal development.

Excessive consumption of Trifolium pratense tea may also lead to gastrointestinal symptoms, such as nausea, diarrhea, and stomach cramps, in some individuals.
