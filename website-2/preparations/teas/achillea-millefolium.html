Achillea Millefolium (Yarrow) Tea For Medicinal Use

Achillea millefolium tea, also known as yarrow tea, has been used for centuries for its medicinal properties.

The tea is made from the leaves and flowers of the yarrow plant, which contain a variety of constituents, including flavonoids, volatile oils, and sesquiterpenes. It has been traditionally used to treat menstrual cramps, fever, and digestive issues, as well as to stop bleeding and promote wound healing. The tea has anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties, which contribute to its ability to soothe digestive issues and reduce inflammation.

However, it's worth noting that yarrow tea can cause allergic reactions, interact with certain medications, and exacerbate bleeding disorders in some individuals.

How to make Achillea millefolium tea

To make Achillea millefolium tea, start by harvesting the leaves and flowers of the plant, as these are the parts used for medicinal purposes.

Dry the harvested parts thoroughly, either by tying them in small bundles and hanging them upside down or by using a food dehydrator at a temperature no higher than 104°F (40°C). Once the leaves and flowers are dry, you can store them in airtight containers for later use. To make the tea, combine one teaspoon of dried Achillea millefolium with eight ounces of boiling water, then let it steep for five to seven minutes.

After steeping, strain the tea and discard the solids, and you're left with a liquid solution that can be consumed for its medicinal properties.

Possible side effects Achillea millefolium tea

If used improperly, Achillea millefolium tea, also known as yarrow tea, can cause allergic reactions in some individuals, leading to symptoms such as hives, itching, and difficulty breathing.

Drinking large amounts of yarrow tea can cause stomach upset, including nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea due to the high concentration of sesquiterpene lactones, which can irritate the digestive tract. The tea's high content of thujone, a pyrrolizidine alkaloid, can cause liver damage if consumed excessively over a prolonged period. Interacting with certain medications, such as blood thinners, can lead to adverse effects like increased bleeding risk when yarrow tea is consumed in large quantities.

Additionally, yarrow tea should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding due to its potential to stimulate uterine contractions and affect fetal development.
