Hypericum Perforatum (St. John'S Wort) Tea For Medicinal Use

Hypericum perforatum, also known as St. John's Wort, is a flowering plant that has been used for centuries to make a medicinal tea.

The tea is rich in bioactive compounds such as hyperforin and hypericin, which have been shown to have anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. When used medicinally, Hypericum perforatum tea has been traditionally employed to treat mild to moderate depression, anxiety, and sleep disorders, due to its ability to affect neurotransmitter levels in the brain. The constituents of the tea also exhibit antimicrobial and antiviral properties, making it useful for wound healing and as an adjunctive treatment for viral infections.

However, consuming Hypericum perforatum tea in large quantities or for extended periods can lead to interactions with certain medications, such as blood thinners and immunosuppressants, and may cause side effects like photosensitivity and stomach upset.

How to make Hypericum perforatum tea

To make Hypericum perforatum tea for medicinal purposes, first harvest fresh Hypericum perforatum flowers, leaves, and stems in the morning, as the water content is typically lower at this time.

Dry the harvested plant material, either by air-drying it or using a low-temperature oven (less than 40°C). Grind the dried material into a fine powder. Combine 1-2 teaspoons of the powder with 250ml of boiling water, then let it steep for 5-10 minutes.

Strain the tea and discard the solids.

Possible side effects Hypericum perforatum tea

If used improperly, Hypericum perforatum tea, also known as St. John's Wort, can cause adverse interactions with certain medications, including blood thinners, transplant medications, and certain antidepressants.

Consuming excessive amounts of the tea can lead to serotonin syndrome, a potentially life-threatening condition caused by excessive levels of serotonin in the body. St. John's Wort can also increase the risk of bleeding in individuals taking anticoagulant medications, such as warfarin. Additionally, combining the tea with other medications that affect serotonin levels, like selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, can exacerbate serotonin syndrome.

Prolonged use of Hypericum perforatum tea can also cause photosensitivity, a condition characterized by increased sensitivity to sunlight, leading to burns and blisters upon exposure.
