Equisetum Arvense (Field Horsetail) Tea For Medicinal Use

Equisetum arvense tea, also known as horsetail tea, is a medicinal preparation made from the dried stems of the Equisetum arvense plant.

Its constituents, including silica, flavonoids, and alkaloids, provide several health benefits, including anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, which can be used to treat conditions such as arthritis and urinary tract infections. The tea's high silica content also makes it a popular remedy for promoting wound healing and strengthening bones. However, Equisetum arvense tea can cause kidney damage and interact with certain medications, including diuretics and blood thinners, due to its high silica and flavonoid content.

Additionally, individuals with kidney or liver disease should consult a healthcare professional before using this tea as a medicinal treatment.

Uses of Equisetum arvense tea

Equisetum arvense tea is commonly used to treat digestive issues such as irritable bowel syndrome and constipation.

It's also used to alleviate symptoms of urinary tract infections, including painful urination and frequent urination. Some people drink Equisetum arvense tea to help lower blood pressure and reduce the risk of cardiovascular disease. Additionally, it's used to treat arthritis and joint pain, providing relief for those suffering from inflammation and stiffness.

Equisetum arvense tea is also used to support the immune system and reduce fever.

Benefits of Equisetum arvense tea

Equisetum arvense tea helps to alleviate symptoms of arthritis and other inflammatory conditions by reducing joint pain and swelling.

This is beneficial for people who suffer from mobility issues and chronic pain, as it allows them to engage in physical activities and maintain independence. The tea also has antimicrobial properties, making it an effective treatment for urinary tract infections and other bacterial infections. Additionally, Equisetum arvense tea has been shown to lower blood pressure in individuals with hypertension, reducing the risk of heart disease and stroke.

By promoting digestive regularity and reducing symptoms of irritable bowel syndrome, the tea also supports overall gastrointestinal health.

Constituents of Equisetum arvense tea

Equisetum arvense tea contains a number of bioactive constituents that contribute to its medicinal properties.

The tea is rich in silicic acid, which is a key component that has been shown to help reduce inflammation and improve wound healing. Equally significant is the presence of flavonoids, including taxifolin and kaempferol, which have been found to possess antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Additionally, the tea contains saponins and caffeic acid, compounds that have been demonstrated to exhibit anti-inflammatory and antibacterial activities.

These bioactive constituents work together to provide a range of health benefits when consumed as a tea.

How to make Equisetum arvense tea

To make Equisetum arvense tea, start by gathering fresh or dried horsetail, as it is the common name for the plant.

Harvest the stems with the hollow joints, or use dried material that has been identified as Equisetum arvense. Combine 1-2 tablespoons of the horsetail material with 1 cup of boiling water, then let it steep for 5-7 minutes. Strain the liquid and discard the solids, then drink the tea as needed.

Equisetum arvense contains silica and other minerals, which can be absorbed by the body when consumed as a tea.

Possible side effects Equisetum arvense tea

If used improperly, Equisetum arvense tea consumption can lead to interactions with certain medications, such as blood thinners and diabetes medications, due to its ability to affect blood sugar levels and increase bleeding risk.

Excessive consumption may cause stomach upset, nausea, and diarrhea, particularly when ingested in large quantities. The tea also contains thujaplicin, a compound that can be toxic if ingested in high amounts, potentially leading to liver damage and other adverse effects. Individuals with certain medical conditions, like kidney disease or liver impairment, should consult a healthcare professional before using Equisetum arvense tea, as it may exacerbate existing health issues.

Pregnant and breastfeeding women should also exercise caution when consuming the tea, as there is limited information on its effects on fetal development and infant health.
