What is the morphology of Achillea millefolium?

The Achillea millefolium (or yarrow), is mainly composed of roots, stems, leaves, flowers, fruits, and seeds, on a morphological level.

It has a deep taproot system reaching 60 cm deep and spreading meters horizontally, along with shallow fibrous roots. These roots are slender (0.1-0.5 mm) and have dense root hairs (200-300 micrometers long) for nutrient and water absorption.

Its stems varies in color and thickness, starting thin (2-4mm) and thickening (5-8mm+) as it matures, while the leaves are feathery, bright green, well-veined, and serrated, optimized for ecological functions.

This plant has compound corymb inflorescences, attracting pollinators with 2-4 cm diameter capitulums. Also, they have central disc florets that are vital for pollination.

The A. millefolium produces fruits called achenes that are dry and one-seeded achenes (2-3mm long, 1-2mm wide), in brown, black, or grayish-brown colors, while the seeds from these fruits vary in size (1.5 to 2.5mm) and have a dark brown to black color.

What is the morphology of A. millefolium roots?

Achillea millefolium's root system comprises taproots and fibrous roots. Taproots can reach over 60 cm deep and spread meters horizontally, while fibrous roots form a shallow mat, averaging 150-200 roots per square meter.

These roots' slender diameter (0.1-0.5 mm) aids in nutrient absorption and their densely distributed root hairs (200-300 micrometers long) further enhance nutrient and water uptake.

Root growth follows a seasonal pattern, with increased growth in spring and slower growth in winter. Also, the plant exhibits root system plasticity, allocating resources to root growth during drought stress to access deeper water sources.

The full description of the Achillea millefolium root morphology is given in the following list.

  • Root Types: The root system of Achillea millefolium consists primarily of two types of roots, the taproots and the fibrous roots. The primary taproot originates from the seedling and serves as the central axis of the root system. Fibrous roots, on the other hand, develop from the base of the stem and branch extensively, forming a dense network in the soil.
  • Root Depth and Spread: The taproot of Achillea millefolium can penetrate the soil to depths of up to 60 centimeters (approximately 24 inches) or more, depending on soil conditions. The lateral spread of the taproot can extend several meters horizontally. In contrast, the fibrous roots form a mat-like structure close to the soil surface, typically within the top 20 centimeters (approximately 8 inches).
  • Root Fibrous Density: Achillea millefolium is characterized by its profuse production of fibrous roots. Studies have reported an average density of approximately 150 to 200 fibrous roots per square meter of soil. This high density of fibrous roots aids in nutrient uptake and anchorage, making the plant well-suited to compete with neighboring vegetation.
  • Root Diameter: The fibrous roots of Achillea millefolium typically have a diameter ranging from 0.1 to 0.5 millimeters. This slender root structure allows for efficient nutrient and water absorption, as well as the exploration of a large soil volume.
  • Root Hairs: Root hairs, which are tiny outgrowths from the root surface, significantly increase the root's absorptive surface area. In Achillea millefolium, the root hairs are densely distributed along the fibrous roots, with an estimated length of 200 to 300 micrometers. This microstructure facilitates the uptake of water and essential nutrients, such as nitrogen and phosphorus.
  • Root Mycorrhizal Associations: Many Achillea millefolium plants form mycorrhizal associations with beneficial fungi, such as arbuscular mycorrhizae. These symbiotic relationships enhance nutrient absorption, particularly phosphorus, and contribute to the plant's overall health and vigor.
  • Root Growth Patterns: The growth of Achillea millefolium roots follows a seasonal pattern. During the spring and early summer, when the plant is actively growing, there is a notable increase in root length and branching. Root growth tends to slow down during the winter months, reflecting the plant's adaptation to seasonal changes.
  • Root System Plasticity: Achillea millefolium exhibits root system plasticity, allowing it to adapt to various soil conditions and moisture levels. In response to drought stress, the plant can allocate more resources to root growth, enhancing its ability to access water in deeper soil layers.

What is the morphology of A. millefolium stems?

The Achillea millefolium stem exhibits variable colors, ranging from green to reddish-brown due to environmental factors and genetics.

It starts thin (2-4mm) in youth and thickens (5-8mm+) as it matures. Internodes, the stem segments between nodes, range from 1 to 10 centimeters in length depending on the growth stage and conditions. Nodes are oppositely paired, with fern-like leaves attached, often aromatic.

The stem's branching pattern varies, contributing to its bushy form. Also, a secondary growth may occur in older plants, thickening the stem. These adaptations support Achillea millefolium's thriving in diverse habitats and facilitate pollination of its composite flowers.

The full description of the Achillea millefolium stems morphology is given in the following list.

  • Stem Color and Texture: The stem of Achillea millefolium is generally erect, with a variable coloration that can range from green to reddish-brown, depending on environmental conditions and genetic variation within the species. The texture of the stem is typically herbaceous, though it may become woody at the base in older individuals.
  • Stem Diameter: The stem's diameter can vary considerably, with younger stems being thinner (around 2-4 millimeters in diameter) and older stems thickening to 5-8 millimeters or more.
  • Stem Internodes: The stem is characterized by a series of distinct internodes, which are the segments of the stem between nodes (points where leaves, branches, or flowers emerge). These internodes can range from 1 to 10 centimeters in length, depending on the specific growth stage and environmental conditions.
  • Stem Node Arrangement: Nodes on the Achillea millefolium stem are arranged in an opposite fashion, meaning that they occur in pairs along the stem, with each pair oriented at right angles to the pair above or below it. This node arrangement is common among many dicotyledonous plants.
  • Stem Attachment to Leaf: At each node, a pair of finely dissected, alternate leaves is attached to the stem. The leaves are highly characteristic of the species, being fern-like in appearance and often aromatic due to the presence of essential oils.
  • Stem Architecture: The stem of Achillea millefolium exhibits a branching pattern that contributes to its bushy and spreading growth form. The number and angle of branches can vary widely among individual plants and populations.
  • Stem Secondary Growth: In some instances, especially in older individuals, the stem may undergo secondary growth, resulting in an increase in stem thickness and the development of a woody base. This woody portion can extend several centimeters above the ground.
  • Stem Adaptations: The stem morphology of Achillea millefolium is adaptive, allowing the plant to thrive in diverse habitats, from meadows to disturbed areas. The erect stem provides structural support for the inflorescences, ensuring that the small, composite flowers are well-exposed to pollinators.

What is the morphology of A. millefolium leaves?

Achillea millefolium boasts pinnately compound leaves with intricate, fern-like arrangements. These leaves, typically 5 to 20 centimeters long, consist of linear or lanceolate leaflets, meticulously divided into smaller segments, creating a feathery texture.

They are predominantly bright green, showcasing serrated margins and a smooth, slightly leathery texture, courtesy of a protective waxy cuticle.

These leaves are well-veined, with a central midrib and lateral veins aiding in nutrient and water transport, optimizing their ecological and physiological functions.

The full description of the Achillea millefolium leaves morphology is given in the following list.

  • Leaves Shape and Arrangement: Achillea millefolium typically produces alternate, pinnately compound leaves that are finely dissected into numerous segments. The leaves are arranged along the stem in an alternating fashion, forming an overall fern-like appearance. Each leaf is composed of multiple leaflets or segments, which give the plant its species name "millefolium", meaning "thousand-leaved".
  • Leaves Size: The size of yarrow leaves can vary, but they are generally small, measuring between 5 to 20 centimeters in length. Each leaflet is typically 2 to 10 centimeters long and 1 to 5 millimeters wide. The small size of the leaves contributes to the plant's ability to conserve water and thrive in a variety of ecological niches.
  • Leaves Dissection: One of the most distinctive features of Achillea millefolium leaves is their degree of dissection. Each leaf is deeply divided into numerous segments, creating a feathery appearance. The leaflets are linear or lanceolate in shape and are further divided into smaller segments, giving the appearance of a "thousand" tiny leaves on a single leaf. This intricate dissection aids in maximizing the surface area for various ecological and physiological functions.
  • Leaves Color: Yarrow leaves typically exhibit a bright green coloration, although there can be variations in shade depending on environmental conditions. The upper surface of the leaves is usually a darker green, while the lower surface is slightly lighter. The green pigmentation is due to the presence of chlorophyll, the pigment responsible for photosynthesis.
  • Leaves Margin and Texture: The leaf margins of Achillea millefolium are serrated, featuring fine teeth or lobes along the edges. This serration can vary in intensity, with some leaves exhibiting more pronounced teeth than others. The texture of yarrow leaves is typically smooth, with a slightly leathery feel due to the presence of a waxy cuticle that helps reduce water loss.
  • Leaves Venation: The leaves of Achillea millefolium are prominently veined. The midrib runs down the center of each leaflet, with lateral veins branching out toward the leaf margins. This vascular system plays a crucial role in transporting water, nutrients, and photosynthates throughout the leaf.

What is the morphology of A. millefolium flowers?

Achillea millefolium features compound corymb inflorescences, each with multiple small florets radiating outward, making it attractive to pollinators.

Individual capitulums typically measure 2-4 cm in diameter, with variations due to environmental factors. The outer ray florets are often white or pink, have 3-8 per capitulum, and serve as pollinator attractors. On the other hand, the central yellow disc florets have 20-40 capitulum, which are vital for pollination and seed production.

The pedicel connecting the capitula to the stem ranges from 1-10 cm in length. and the involucre (composed of 5-8 phyllaries) shields florets and enhances aesthetics. That said, this plant provides pollinators with a steady nectar supply as it blooms from late spring to early autumn.

The full description of the Achillea millefolium flowers morphology is given in the following list.

  • Inflorescence Type: Achillea millefolium bears compound inflorescences known as corymbs. These corymbs consist of multiple individual flowerheads or capitula, each housing numerous tiny florets. The inflorescence structure is typically flat-topped, with florets radiating outward, making it an attractive choice for pollinators seeking nectar and pollen.
  • Capitulum Diameter: The diameter of the individual capitulum in Achillea millefolium typically ranges from 2 to 4 centimeters, although variations can occur based on environmental factors and cultivar differences. This compact arrangement allows for a high concentration of florets in a relatively small space.
  • Ray Florets: These are the outermost florets surrounding the central disc florets. In Achillea millefolium, they are typically white or occasionally pink, and they are sterile, serving primarily as attractive structures for pollinators. A single Achillea capitulum can have anywhere from 3 to 8 ray florets, with an average of 5.
  • Disc Florets: The central disc florets are small and tubular, with a characteristic yellow coloration. These fertile florets are densely packed within the capitulum and are the primary sites of pollination and seed production. A single Achillea capitulum can contain as many as 20 to 40 disc florets, depending on the cultivar and growing conditions.
  • Pedicel Length: The pedicel length, which is the stalk connecting individual capitula to the main stem, ranges from 1 to 10 centimeters, with longer pedicels often observed in plants growing in more open or exposed habitats.
  • Phyllaries: The involucre, consisting of phyllaries, encases the base of the capitulum. Achillea millefolium typically possesses 5 to 8 phyllaries, which can vary in size and shape. These protective structures serve to shield the developing florets and add to the overall aesthetic appeal of the inflorescence.
  • Flowering Period: Achillea millefolium is a prolific bloomer, with its flowering period extending from late spring to early autumn, depending on geographic location and climate. This extended blooming duration ensures a steady supply of nectar for pollinators throughout the growing season.

What is the morphology of A. millefolium fruits?

Achillea millefolium produces achenes as its fruit. Achenes are dry, one-seeded fruits that don't split open. They're small, oblong (2-3mm long, 1-2mm wide), and come in brown, black, or grayish-brown colors.

These achenes have a smooth, shiny surface, aiding in wind dispersal and animal transport. They attach to a pappus, a modified receptacle with awns, helping them travel long distances through the wind.

The full description of the Achillea millefolium fruits morphology is given in the following list.

  • Fruit Type: Achillea millefolium produces achenes as its primary fruit type. Achenes are dry, indehiscent, one-seeded fruits that do not split open to release their seeds. They are often referred to as "seeds" in a botanical context.
  • Fruit Size and Shape: The achenes of Achillea millefolium are typically small and oblong in shape, measuring approximately 2-3 mm in length and 1-2 mm in width. These dimensions can vary slightly among individual achenes.
  • Fruit Color: When mature, the achenes of Achillea millefolium exhibit a range of colors, including brown, black, or grayish-brown. The coloration is primarily due to the presence of pigments in the seed coat.
  • Fruit Surface Texture: The surface of these achenes is smooth, with a shiny appearance. This smooth texture aids in wind dispersal and facilitates the achenes' adherence to passing animals.
  • Fruit Attachment Mechanism: Achillea millefolium achenes are attached to a modified receptacle called a pappus. The pappus consists of numerous bristle-like structures known as awns. These awns are often arranged in a ring around the base of the achene. The pappus functions as a dispersal mechanism, allowing the achenes to be carried by the wind over long distances.

What is the morphology of A. millefolium seeds?

Achillea millefolium seeds vary in size (1.5 to 2.5mm) and are dark brown to black. Their textured surface aids water absorption and attachment to soil. Each seed weighs 0.2 to 0.4 milligrams, allowing wind dispersal.

Wind is their primary dispersal method due to bristle-like structures. These seeds remain viable for years and thrive in various conditions. Germination depends on temperature (15°C to 25°C) and soil placement. Light can enhance germination, but darkness is also suitable. Its adaptable seed characteristics enable it to flourish in diverse environments.

The full description of the Achillea millefolium seeds morphology is given in the following list.

  • Seed Size: The seeds of Achillea millefolium exhibit a considerable range in size, with an average length ranging from 1.5 to 2.5 millimeters. These seeds are typically oblong or elliptical in shape, but variations exist within the species, with some seeds appearing slightly more flattened or curved.
  • Seed Color: Yarrow seeds typically display a dark brown to black coloration, contributing to their resilience in various ecological niches. This dark coloration aids in heat absorption, which can promote germination, especially in sunny environments.
  • Seed Surface Texture: The surface of Achillea millefolium seeds is often textured with fine ridges and indentations. These surface features play a crucial role in water absorption and retention, as well as facilitating adhesion to soil particles during germination.
  • Seed Weight: On average, the weight of a single Achillea millefolium seed falls within the range of 0.2 to 0.4 milligrams. This lightweight characteristic enables the seeds to be readily dispersed by wind, a common mechanism for the plant's reproduction.
  • Seed Dispersal Mechanisms: Yarrow seeds employ various dispersal mechanisms, with wind being the primary method. The lightweight seeds are equipped with a pappus, a modified calyx consisting of bristle-like structures, enabling them to be carried by even the slightest breeze. Yarrow's efficient wind dispersal strategy allows it to colonize a wide range of habitats and expand its distribution.
  • Seed Viability and Longevity: Achillea millefolium seeds have demonstrated remarkable longevity. Studies have reported seed viability to persist for several years under favorable storage conditions. This longevity contributes to the species' resilience, allowing it to maintain a presence in ecosystems even in the face of environmental fluctuations.
  • Seed Germination Requirements: Germination in Achillea millefolium seeds is influenced by various factors, including temperature, light, and soil moisture content. Optimal germination typically occurs when soil temperatures range from 15°C to 25°C (59°F to 77°F) and when seeds are placed on or slightly below the soil surface. Additionally, some evidence suggests that yarrow seeds may exhibit improved germination rates when exposed to light, but they can also germinate in darkness.
