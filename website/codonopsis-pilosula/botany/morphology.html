Codonopsis pilosula morphology

Codonopsis pilosula, commonly known as dang shen, possesses a fibrous root system, with thin roots that provide stability and facilitate efficient nutrient and water absorption. The primary root is taproot in structure, branching into lateral roots, creating a robust network. The branching pattern is dichotomous, promoting exploration of a wide soil area. The root tips are sharp for soil penetration.

The root density is around 5-10 roots per square centimeter, and they extend 30-60 cm deep with a diameter of 0.3-0.5 cm. They are pale brown to beige and have fine root hairs to aid nutrient absorption, although they lack root nodules.

The herbaceous stems of this plant are cylindrical, green, and soft. Leaves are simple, elliptical to lanceolate, with green color and a slightly waxy texture. They are arranged alternately, with entire margins, and have petioles connecting them to the stem. Stipules are absent.

The racemose flowers are solitary, tubular, and come in pale blue, purple, or white. They exhibit radial symmetry and consist of a corolla with five petals, five fused sepals, five stamens, and a pistil with a superior ovary. Small bracts are present at the base of the flower.

The capsules are globose, pendulous, and change from green to brown as they mature. They split open loculicidally to release seeds.

The seeds are orthospermous and ovoid, with a hard, impermeable brown coat, housing a straight embryo with two cotyledons and a radicle.

Roots

Codonopsis pilosula has a fibrous root system, which means that it primarily consists of thin roots that branch off in various directions. These roots help the plant anchor itself in the soil and efficiently absorb nutrients and water.

The root structure of Codonopsis pilosula is predominantly taproot, with a primary root that grows deep into the soil. This primary root gives rise to lateral roots, forming a sturdy network that supports the plant's overall stability and resource acquisition.

The root branching in Codonopsis pilosula is moderate and follows a dichotomous pattern, where the main root repeatedly splits into two branches, each of which further subdivides as it grows. This branching structure contributes to the root's ability to explore a wide area in the soil.

The root tips of Codonopsis pilosula are sharp and pointed, facilitating their penetration into the soil. This sharpness helps the plant navigate through the earth and access deeper soil layers.

The root density of Codonopsis pilosula is approximately 5-10 roots per square centimeter. This indicates that the plant forms a reasonably dense network of roots, which is beneficial for resource uptake and stability.

The roots of Codonopsis pilosula typically extend to a depth of 30-60 cm (12-24 inches) in the soil. This root depth allows the plant to tap into water and nutrients from deeper soil layers.

In terms of diameter, the roots of Codonopsis pilosula measure around 0.3-0.5 cm (0.12-0.2 inches), and their length can reach up to 60 cm (24 inches). These dimensions are well-suited to the plant's needs, as they balance efficient resource uptake and structural support.

The color of the roots is pale brown to beige, and they have a smooth surface texture with fine root hairs. These characteristics aid in nutrient absorption and the plant's interaction with the surrounding soil.

Notably, Codonopsis pilosula does not form root nodules, and it does have fine and numerous root hairs, which enhance its ability to absorb nutrients from the soil.

Stems

The stems of Codonopsis pilosula are herbaceous, which means they lack woody tissue and remain relatively soft. They have an alternate arrangement, with leaves attached one per node along the stem. The stem shape is cylindrical, giving the plant a slender appearance.

In terms of size, Codonopsis pilosula stems typically reach a height of 30 to 60 cm (12 to 24 inches) and have a diameter ranging from 0.2 to 0.5 cm (0.08 to 0.2 inches). Their color is predominantly green, reflecting their active photosynthetic activity. The stems boast a smooth texture, and their surface is characterized by a lack of hair, making them glabrous.

The stems of Codonopsis pilosula exhibit nodes where leaves attach, marking the points of leaf growth. These nodes are distinct features along the stem. The internodes, which are the spaces between these nodes, measure approximately 1 to 2 cm (0.4 to 0.8 inches) in length.

Leaves

The leaves of Codonopsis pilosula are simple, not compound, and have a unique elliptical to lanceolate shape, typically measuring 4 to 12 centimeters (1.6 to 4.7 inches) in length and 1.5 to 4 centimeters (0.6 to 1.6 inches) in width. They are thin, and their color is predominantly green, often with a slightly waxy texture, giving them a characteristic shine.

In terms of arrangement, these leaves are alternate, meaning they grow singly along the stem. They are oriented in an erect position, standing upright from the stem. The leaf margins are entire, providing a smooth and unbroken edge. The apex of the leaf is acute or pointed, while the base is cuneate, resembling a wedge shape.

Leaf venation in Codonopsis pilosula is pinnate, with prominent veins and a reticulate venation pattern. The lamina is simple and entire, retaining the elliptic to lanceolate shape observed in the entire leaf. A prominent midrib runs along the center of the leaf, contributing to its structural integrity.

Regarding leaf attachment, these leaves are petiolate, meaning they have petioles that connect them to the stem. The petioles are slender and measure 1 to 4 centimeters (0.4 to 1.6 inches) in length. Notably, Codonopsis pilosula lacks stipules, which are small leaf-like structures often found at the base of leaf petioles in some plant species.

Flowers

The inflorescence type of Codonopsis pilosula is racemose, with 1 to 3 flowers per node, typically measuring 0.39 to 1.18 inches in length. These flowers exhibit a simple, unbranched structure and are solitary, arising from the leaf axils.

The flowers of Codonopsis pilosula are actinomorphic, meaning they display radial symmetry, and are tubular in shape. The corolla, composed of five petals, forms a funnel-like structure with a diameter of up to 0.79 inches and a length of 0.79 to 1.57 inches. They come in varying shades of pale blue, purple, or white, and have a smooth, glossy texture.

These flowers have a pentamerous arrangement, with five sepals fused at the base and five petals united to create the corolla. The reproductive components consist of five fertile stamens attached to the corolla tube and a single pistil with a superior ovary containing three locules. The style is solitary, with a three-lobed stigma at its tip. Codonopsis pilosula lacks stipules but does have small bracts at the base of the flower.

Supporting these flowers are slender peduncles, which are the stems that bear the inflorescence, and short pedicels, the stalks that attach individual flowers. This combination of features defines the unique morphology of Codonopsis pilosula, making it easily distinguishable within the plant kingdom.

Fruits

The fruits of Codonopsis pilosula are capsules, typically found either individually or in clusters throughout the plant. These capsules are pendulous, meaning they hang down from the plant. In terms of their shape, they are generally globose in form, with a length of about 1.5 cm (0.59 inches), a width of approximately 1.2 cm (0.47 inches), and a thickness of 1.0 cm (0.39 inches).

As they mature, the color of these capsules changes from green to brown. The texture of the fruit is smooth and somewhat waxy, and the surface is described as glabrous, meaning it is hairless and lacks any fine hairs or fuzz. There are no distinct markings on the fruit's surface.

One notable characteristic of these capsules is their method of dehiscence, which is loculicidal. This means that the capsules split open along the septa, releasing their seeds from within.

Seeds

The seeds of Codonopsis pilosula belong to the orthospermous type, characterized by a solitary arrangement within its fruit. They have an ovoid shape, measuring 2.5 mm in length, 1.5 mm in width, and 1 mm in thickness. These seeds are notably hard, rendering them resilient.

The seed coat of Codonopsis pilosula seeds is brown, with a smooth and shiny surface. Its thickness measures 0.2 mm, and it is impermeable, preventing water or gases from easily passing through. The hilum, the scar where the seed was attached to the plant, is small and inconspicuous.

Inside the seed, a straight embryo is present, and it has two cotyledons, indicating a dicotyledonous nature. Additionally, a radicle is found at the micropylar end of the seed, serving as the embryonic root.
