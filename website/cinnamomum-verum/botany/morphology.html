Cinnamomum verum morphology

Cinnamomum verum, commonly known as true cinnamon, has a taproot system with fibrous structures, moderately branched, pointed tips, and notable root density of around 400 roots per cubic centimeter (or 2,548 roots per cubic inch). Root depth ranges from 30 to 60 centimeters (11.8 to 23.6 inches), with a diameter of 0.2 to 0.5 centimeters (0.08 to 0.2 inches) and a length of 20 to 30 centimeters (7.9 to 11.8 inches). The roots are creamy white, smooth, and lack nodules but have fine and numerous root hairs.

The stems of Cinnamomum verum start as herbaceous and transition to a woody structure. They are typically opposite but occasionally appear in an alternate, decussate pattern. The stems are cylindrical, about 2.5 cm (1 inch) in diameter, with a height of up to 10 meters (32.8 feet). They are brown to reddish-brown, smooth with fine pubescence, and feature clearly defined nodes spaced about 10 to 15 cm (4-6 inches) apart.

Cinnamomum verum leaves are simple, elliptical, alternate, and vertical. They are approximately 7.5 cm (3 inches) in length, 2.5 cm (1 inch) in width, and 0.2 cm (0.08 inches) thick, with a smooth and shiny green surface. The leaf apex is acute, the base is cuneate, and the margin is entire. Leaves have pinnate venation and are petiolate with a length of approximately 2.0 cm (0.8 inches), lacking stipules.

The flowers of Cinnamomum verum are found in panicles, actinomorphic, and composed of six floral parts: three sepals and three petals, forming a perianth. There are nine stamens and a single, short style with a capitate stigma. The flowers are about 1 cm (0.39 inches) in length, 0.5 cm (0.20 inches) in width, cream to pale yellow, smooth, and glabrous. They are attached in leaf axils and often accompanied by small bracts, with a short peduncle and pedicel.

Cinnamomum verum fruits are drupes, typically clustered throughout the tree canopy, with ellipsoid shape and dimensions of about 1.5 cm (0.59 inches) in length, 0.7 cm (0.28 inches) in width, and 0.5 cm (0.20 inches) in thickness. When ripe, they are dark purple, smooth, and indehiscent, lacking distinct markings.

The seeds are simple, often solitary or in clusters, with elliptical shape, measuring about 10-12 mm (0.39-0.47 inches) in length, 5-6 mm (0.20-0.24 inches) in width, and 2-3 mm (0.08-0.12 inches) in thickness. They have a brown, smooth, and shiny seed coat, which is impermeable. The hilum is located at the base of the seed, and inside, there is a straight embryo with two cotyledons and a radicle.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Cinnamomum verum by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

Roots

The root structure of Cinnamomum verum is fibrous, comprising many fine roots that spread out from the central taproot. These fine roots contribute to the plant's ability to gather nutrients and water effectively.

In terms of branching, C. verum exhibits moderate branching. The roots have a balanced distribution, ensuring that the plant can access resources in its surroundings efficiently.

The root tips of C. verum are pointed, which aids in their penetration of the soil. This is especially useful in the quest for water and minerals.

Root density is noteworthy, with approximately 400 roots per cubic centimeter (or 2,548 roots per cubic inch). This high density is a key factor in the plant's ability to absorb nutrients from the soil.

Root depth typically ranges from 30 to 60 centimeters (11.8 to 23.6 inches). This depth allows the plant to access water and minerals even in relatively dry or shallow soil conditions.

Root diameter falls within the range of 0.2 to 0.5 centimeters (0.08 to 0.2 inches), and root length averages between 20 and 30 centimeters (7.9 to 11.8 inches). These dimensions provide a balance between efficient resource uptake and structural stability.

The root color of C. verum is creamy white, and the root surface texture is smooth, enabling smooth penetration of the soil.

Notably, Cinnamomum verum does not form root nodules. However, it possesses fine and numerous root hairs, further enhancing its ability to absorb water and nutrients.

Stems

The stem type of Cinnamomum verum are be classified as herbaceous when young and gradually transitions into a woody structure as it matures. This change is integral to the plant's growth and development. The stem arrangement is generally opposite, though it may occasionally appear in an alternate, decussate pattern, where leaves or branches attach in pairs at right angles to one another.

In terms of shape, the stems are typically cylindrical, presenting a round appearance. They may also display slight ribbing, which can be helpful in distinguishing them from other species. The stem height of Cinnamomum verum usually reaches a maximum of 10 meters (approximately 32.8 feet), making it a relatively tall plant. The stem diameter averages around 2.5 cm (1 inch), offering a medium-sized profile.

The stem color of this species is distinctive, ranging from brown to reddish-brown. This hue is a key characteristic that separates Cinnamomum verum from other cinnamon varieties. The stem texture is smooth with fine pubescence, while the surface appears glabrous (smooth), often featuring lenticels, small raised structures that facilitate gas exchange.

Nodes on the stems are clearly defined, serving as attachment points for leaves and branches. These nodes play a crucial role in the overall growth and structure of the plant. The internodes, the spaces between these nodes, are typically spaced about 10 to 15 cm (4-6 inches) apart, contributing to the overall arrangement and appearance of the stems.

Leaves

The leaves of Cinnamomum verum are simple, which means they consist of a single, undivided leaf blade. They have an elliptical shape and are arranged alternately on the branches. These leaves stand vertically, with an average length of 7.5 cm (3 inches) and a width of 2.5 cm (1 inch). Their thickness measures about 0.2 cm (0.08 inches).

In terms of color, Cinnamomum verum leaves are typically green, presenting a smooth texture and a shiny surface. The leaf apex is acute, forming a pointed tip, while the base is cuneate, creating a wedge-like attachment to the stem. The leaf margin is entire, lacking any serrations or lobes.

The venation of these leaves is pinnate, with a prominent midrib running along the center of the leaf blade. The leaf attachment is petiolate, meaning the leaves are attached to the stem by a slender stalk, which measures approximately 2.0 cm (0.8 inches) in length. Notably, Cinnamomum verum lacks stipules, small leaf-like structures at the base of the leaf stalk that are often found in other plant species.

Flowers

The flowers of Cinnamomum verum have a simple and complete structure, typically found in the form of panicles. Their symmetry is actinomorphic, meaning they are radially symmetric.

Each Cinnamomum verum flower comprises six floral parts. The flower possesses three sepals and three petals, collectively forming a perianth with indistinguishable sepals and petals. Additionally, there are nine stamens and a single, short style in each flower, which features a small, capitate stigma.

The flowers of Cinnamomum verum are relatively petite, with dimensions of approximately 1 centimeter (0.39 inches) in length and 0.5 centimeters (0.20 inches) in width. They exhibit a cream to pale yellow coloration and have a smooth, glabrous texture.

These flowers are attached in the leaf axils, making their inflorescence type axillary. They lack stipules and are often accompanied by small bracts. The peduncle, which is the flower stalk, is short, measuring less than 1 centimeter (0.39 inches) in length, and the pedicel, the stalk that attaches the individual flower to the peduncle, is even shorter, typically less than 0.5 centimeters (0.20 inches).

Fruits

Cinnamomum verum fruits are typically arranged in clusters throughout the tree canopy, attached to the branches by slender, pendulous stalks.

The shape of Cinnamomum verum fruits is ellipsoid, with precise measurements indicating a length of approximately 1.5 cm (0.59 inches), a width of about 0.7 cm (0.28 inches), and a thickness of 0.5 cm (0.20 inches). When fully ripe, these fruits display a dark purple color, adding to their visual appeal.

The texture of the fruit surface is smooth, providing a glossy appearance. Unlike some other fruits, Cinnamomum verum fruits do not exhibit distinct markings on their surfaces. Furthermore, these fruits are indehiscent, which means they do not split open when they ripen.

Seeds

The seeds of Cinnamomum verum are simple in structure, often found as solitary units or in small clusters. These seeds have an elliptical shape, measuring approximately 10-12 millimeters in length, 5-6 millimeters in width, and 2-3 millimeters in thickness. They are characterized by their hardness, making them resilient.

The seed coat of Cinnamomum verum seeds is typically brown, with a smooth and shiny surface. The coat is relatively thin, measuring about 0.5-1 millimeter in thickness. This thin seed coat is impermeable, which means it does not readily allow the passage of water or gases.

At the base of the seed, you can find the hilum, a distinct mark or scar that serves as a point of attachment. Inside the seed, there is a straight embryo with two cotyledons (dicotyledon). These cotyledons are essential for the initial growth of the seedling. Additionally, there is a radicle present, which is the embryonic root that emerges from one end of the embryo.
