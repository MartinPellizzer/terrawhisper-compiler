Atropa belladonna morphology

Atropa belladonna, commonly known as deadly nightshade, has a root system that consists of a taproot with secondary roots branching off, featuring pointed tips and a fibrous structure that extends 30 to 60 centimeters below the soil surface. The stems are herbaceous, cylindrical, and can grow to heights of 60 to 150 centimeters, with a smooth, green surface marked by fine longitudinal lines.

The leaves are simple, elliptical, and dark green, arranged alternately with an ascending orientation. They have smooth margins and a pinnate venation pattern. Attached to the stem via short petioles, they lack stipules. Atropa belladonna's flowers are either solitary or in clusters, displaying a tubular structure with five fused petals and sepals, with colors ranging from purple to brownish-purple. These axillary flowers have pentamerous characteristics, featuring five sepals, petals, and stamens. The pistil is singular with an inferior ovary.

The fruits are small, globose berries found in the leaf axils and attached via pedicels. When ripe, they turn shiny black, remaining indehiscent, meaning they do not split open. Atropa belladonna seeds are solitary, oval, and known for their hardness. They have a dark brown to black, impermeable seed coat and contain two cotyledons, serving as the initial food source for the developing seedling.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Atropa belladonna by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

Roots

Atropa belladonna has a taproot system, which means it has one main root that goes deep into the ground with smaller secondary roots branching off from it. These secondary roots display dichotomous branching patterns.

The root tips of Atropa belladonna are pointed, and the root structure is fibrous, composed of multiple fine roots. The density of these roots varies but is typically around 0.25 g/cm3. In terms of depth, the roots of this plant typically extend to a depth of 30 to 60 centimeters (approximately 12 to 24 inches) below the soil surface. Their diameter is approximately 0.5 centimeters (0.2 inches), and they can grow up to 60 centimeters (24 inches) in length.

Roots of Atropa belladonna are dark brown to black in color and possess a rough, slightly wrinkled surface texture. Unlike some other plants, this species does not form root nodules. Root hairs on Atropa belladonna are sparse, fine, and non-glandular.

Stems

The stem type of Atropa belladonna is herbaceous, meaning it lacks woody tissues, making it flexible and non-permanent. Stem arrangement is opposite, indicating that leaves grow in pairs at each node along the stem.

The stem shape is cylindrical, with a uniform, rounded form. In terms of size, the stem can reach a height of 60 to 150 centimeters (23.6 to 59.1 inches) and has a diameter of 1 to 2 centimeters (0.4 to 0.8 inches). This size range provides an idea of the plant's overall stature.

The stem's color is primarily green, typical of most plants. It has a smooth and hairless texture, referred to as glabrous, which is consistent throughout. The stem surface is marked with fine longitudinal lines, described as striated.

Stem nodes are prominent points along the stem where leaves are attached, and the internodes, which are the spaces between nodes, measure approximately 10 to 15 centimeters (3.9 to 5.9 inches).

Leaves

Atropa belladonna leaves are simple, meaning they consist of a single blade with no divisions or leaflets. They have an elliptical shape, with typical measurements of 2.5 to 6 centimeters in length (1 to 2.5 inches) and 1.5 to 4 centimeters in width (0.5 to 1.5 inches). The leaves are thin and display a dark green coloration, which is a notable feature.

In terms of arrangement, these leaves are positioned alternately along the stem. They have an ascending and erect orientation, contributing to their distinct appearance.

The leaf margins are smooth and entire, lacking any serrations or lobes. A closer look at the venation reveals a pinnate pattern, where secondary veins branch off from the central midrib, which is prominent and runs through the center of the leaf.

The attachment of Atropa belladonna leaves to the stem is petiolate, meaning they have a short petiole measuring 2 to 5 centimeters (0.8 to 2 inches) in length. Notably, stipules are absent in this species.

The leaves' apex is acute, forming a pointed tip, and their bases are rounded. They possess a smooth, glabrous texture and a simple, unlobed lamina.

Flowers

Atropa belladonna's inflorescence type can be solitary, where a single flower blooms, or cymose, forming clusters with 2 to 4 flowers. The flower's complexity can range from simple to complex, with variations in its structure.

In terms of flower symmetry, Atropa belladonna can be either actinomorphic, displaying radial symmetry, or zygomorphic, exhibiting bilateral symmetry. The flower structure is tubular, featuring five fused petals and sepals.

Flower attachment in Atropa belladonna is axillary, meaning the flowers grow in the leaf axils. These bell-shaped flowers have specific dimensions, with a length ranging from 4 to 5 centimeters (1.6 to 2 inches) and a width of 2 to 3 centimeters (0.8 to 1.2 inches). Their coloration typically falls within the spectrum of purple to brownish-purple, with a smooth and glossy texture.

Each Atropa belladonna flower possesses pentamerous characteristics, meaning it consists of five sepals, five petals, and five stamens. The sepals, each measuring 0.8 to 1 centimeter (0.3 to 0.4 inches), are fused at the base, forming a protective structure around the bud.

The petals are also fused, creating a tubular shape that mirrors the length of the sepals. Five stamens are positioned alternately with the petals and measure 0.8 to 1 centimeter (0.3 to 0.4 inches) each. The entire structure forms the perianth, which includes both sepals and petals.

The pistil in an Atropa belladonna flower is singular, composed of an inferior ovary positioned below the attachment point. It is accompanied by a single, slender style that extends from the ovary, measuring 0.8 to 1 centimeter (0.3 to 0.4 inches) and capped by a rounded stigma.

Notably, Atropa belladonna lacks stipules, and its bracts are small and leaf-like. Additionally, the flowers are held on short peduncles and pedicels, contributing to the distinctive appearance of this fascinating plant.

Fruits

The fruit type of Atropa belladonna is classified as a berry, a small, fleshy fruit. These berries are solitary, typically found in the leaf axils, and attached to the plant via pedicels, or stalks.

In terms of physical dimensions, the fruits are approximately 1 centimeter (0.39 inches) in length, width, and thickness, giving them a globose shape. When fully ripe, they take on a shiny black color with a smooth and glossy surface, devoid of distinctive markings.

One notable trait of Atropa belladonna's fruit is that it is indehiscent, meaning it does not split open upon ripening. This characteristic sets it apart from dehiscent fruits, which naturally split open to release their seeds.

Seeds

The seeds of Atropa belladonna are typically solitary, with an oval shape measuring about 4 mm in length, 2 mm in width, and 1 mm in thickness. They are known for their hardness, as they have a hard and woody texture. The seed coat is dark brown to black, appearing smooth and slightly wrinkled, with a thickness of approximately 0.1 mm. Notably, this seed coat is impermeable to both water and gases.

The seed hilum, the point of attachment for the radicle (the embryonic root), is small and inconspicuous. Beneath the seed coat, one can find the tiny embryo, which is often hidden from view. Interestingly, Atropa belladonna seeds contain two cotyledons. These cotyledons are a part of the embryonic plant and serve as the initial food source for the developing seedling.
