Astragalus membranaceus morphology

Astragalus membranaceus has a robust taproot system that extends at least 1 meter into the soil, with numerous thin, branching roots that have a dichotomous pattern. These roots facilitate nutrient and water absorption and host nitrogen-fixing nodules, improving soil fertility.

The plant's stems are primarily herbaceous but can be woody, varying in height and diameter. The leaves are compound with three ovate leaflets, smooth, uniformly green, and attached to short petioles with stipules at the base.

The raceme inflorescence produces zygomorphic, banner-shaped flowers in shades of purple or pink. These flowers have a smooth texture, with five sepals, five petals forming the perianth, ten stamens, and a single pistil. They lack stipules and are supported by peduncles and pedicels.

The legume-type fruits are stipitate, oblong to ellipsoid, changing from green to brown when mature, and they split into two valves, aiding seed dispersal.

The ellipsoidal seeds are hard, impermeable, and lack appendages. Each pod typically contains a single seed with a small hilum and a centrally located, straight embryo with two cotyledons. The article provides detailed characteristics of these plant parts, emphasizing the unique attributes of Astragalus membranaceus.

Roots

The root system of Astragalus membranaceus is primarily taproot in nature, with a fibrous structure. It exhibits minimal lateral branching, which sets it apart from many other plant species. The root tips are pointed, aiding in soil penetration.

Root density is a key metric for cultivation. Astragalus membranaceus typically features around 1,000 roots per square meter. This dense network of roots is an advantage for nutrient uptake and anchorage.

Root depth is essential for assessing its adaptability to different soil conditions. These roots typically extend to a depth of 50-80 cm (20-31 inches) in the soil, allowing the plant to access nutrients and water resources effectively. The root diameter ranges from 0.3 to 0.5 cm (0.12-0.20 inches), indicating a moderate thickness.

In terms of length, Astragalus membranaceus roots can grow up to 100 cm (39 inches), making them suitable for harvesting. The color of these roots is pale yellow-brown, and their surface texture is rough and wrinkled, possibly aiding in nutrient absorption and providing some protection.

One distinctive feature of Astragalus membranaceus roots is the presence of nitrogen-fixing nodules. These nodules contribute to soil health by facilitating nitrogen fixation, benefiting both the plant and the surrounding ecosystem.

Additionally, these roots feature abundant fine root hairs. These root hairs play a crucial role in absorbing water and essential nutrients from the soil.

Stems

Astragalus membranaceus stems are primarily herbaceous, although woody stems can occur in some cases. Stem arrangement is typically alternate, but opposite or whorled arrangements can also be observed. The stem shape is generally cylindrical, but it may display angular or ribbed features, giving it a slightly squared appearance.

In terms of size, the stems of Astragalus membranaceus can reach heights ranging from 20 to 80 centimeters (7.9 to 31.5 inches). Their diameter typically falls within the range of 0.2 to 0.5 centimeters (0.08 to 0.2 inches). The color of the stems is predominantly green, although variations exist, with some stems appearing brown or reddish-brown.

Stem texture is another distinguishing characteristic, and these stems often have a smooth surface. However, some stems can be rough or covered in fine hairs, giving them a slightly hairy or pubescent texture. The stem surface can be described as glabrous, meaning it lacks hair or scales.

Stem nodes are sometimes present on the stems of Astragalus membranaceus, which are points where leaves, branches, or flowers may arise. Depending on the plant, nodes may be absent altogether. The distance between nodes, known as stem internodes, typically measures between 3 to 6 centimeters (1.2 to 2.4 inches).

Leaves

Astragalus membranaceus leaves are compound, specifically pinnately compound, consisting of three leaflets. Each leaflet is ovate in shape, with dimensions typically ranging from 1.5 to 3 centimeters in length and 1.4 to 3.1 inches in width. These leaflets are thin and membranous, imparting a delicate appearance to the overall leaf structure.

The coloration of these leaves is uniformly green, and their texture is smooth and glabrous. This smoothness is consistent across the entire leaf surface, contributing to a sleek and polished look. The leaf apex is acute, creating a pointed tip, while the base is rounded, leading to a gentle curve in the leaf's outline.

Leaf margins are described as entire, meaning they are smooth and undivided. The venation pattern on the leaflets is pinnate, with a prominent midrib running along the center of each leaflet. The leaflets themselves are simple, not divided further into smaller units.

The attachment of the leaflets is at a common point, radiating out from a shared junction. Each leaflet is attached to the stem through a short petiole. Notably, stipules are present at the base of these petioles, enhancing the leaf's distinct appearance.

Flowers

Astragalus membranaceus inflorescence type is a raceme, featuring a simple, complete flower structure. These flowers exhibit zygomorphic symmetry, meaning they possess a single bilateral symmetry plane, resulting in an uneven arrangement of their floral parts.

The flowers are attached axillary, meaning they grow in the axil of a leaf. Their shape is notably banner-shaped, forming standard, keel, and wing petals. With a modest size, the flowers typically measure approximately 1.5 cm (0.59 inches) in length and 1 cm (0.39 inches) in width.

The most common colors of Astragalus membranaceus flowers are shades of purple or pink. Their texture is smooth, and the surface is glabrous, meaning they lack hairs or trichomes.

Each flower comprises five sepals that are fused at the base, and five separate petals that together form the perianth. Inside the perianth, there are ten stamens, with nine of them fused and one remaining free. Additionally, there is a single pistil with a superior ovary and a long, slender style. The stigma, the receptive surface for pollen, is capitate, or round in shape.

Notably, Astragalus membranaceus flowers lack stipules, and they have bracts, which are small leaf-like structures associated with the flowers. The flowers are supported by peduncles of varying lengths, typically between 2 to 5 cm (0.79-1.97 inches), and pedicels, which are usually 0.5 to 1 cm (0.20-0.39 inches) long.

Fruits

The fruits of Astragalus membranaceus are of the legume type, typical of many plants in the legume family. They can be found arranged either singly or in clusters, with distribution occurring at axillary or terminal positions on the plant. These fruits are stipitate, meaning they are attached to the stem by a stalk.

In terms of shape, Astragalus membranaceus fruits are oblong to ellipsoid, with dimensions typically measuring 2.5 to 4 centimeters (0.98 to 1.57 inches) in length, 0.4 to 0.6 centimeters (0.16 to 0.24 inches) in width, and 0.2 to 0.3 centimeters (0.08 to 0.12 inches) in thickness. They exhibit a color progression from pale green to brown as they mature.

The fruit surface is smooth and membranous, and the fruits themselves are glabrous, devoid of hairs. Notably, there are usually no conspicuous markings on the fruit.

One distinctive feature of Astragalus membranaceus fruits is their dehiscence. These legumes are bivalve, meaning they split open into two valves when they reach maturity. This characteristic facilitates the dispersal of seeds contained within.

Seeds

Astragalus membranaceus seeds are orthotropic, which means they lack endosperm. They are typically solitary in arrangement, with a distinctive ellipsoidal shape. These seeds are approximately 4.5 millimeters long, 2 millimeters wide, and 1.5 millimeters thick (0.177 inches in length, 0.079 inches in width, and 0.059 inches in thickness).

Unlike some seeds, Astragalus membranaceus seeds lack appendages like wings or awns. Typically, only one seed is found within each seed pod. The seeds are hard and firm, featuring a smooth surface with a fine, slightly wrinkled texture. The seed coat is generally light brown, shining with a thickness of about 0.2 millimeters (0.008 inches).

Seed coat permeability is limited, with some seeds being nearly impermeable. The hilum, a scar marking the seed's attachment point in the pod, is small and inconspicuous. The embryo inside the seed is straight, small, and centrally located. Each seed has two cotyledons, which is a characteristic of dicotyledonous plants.
