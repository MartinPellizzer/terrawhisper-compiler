Angelica sinensis morphology

Angelica sinensis, commonly known as Dong Quai, is a herbaceous plant with distinctive characteristics. Its fibrous root system consists of a taproot and adventitious roots, with a moderate lateral branching pattern. These roots are brown to reddish-brown, extending 15-30 cm below the soil and reaching a maximum length of 60 cm. They lack root nodules and have sparse root hairs.

The herbaceous stem of Angelica sinensis is round in cross-section, can grow up to 150 cm in height, and has a small diameter of approximately 0.5 cm. The stem is green with a purplish tinge, smooth (glabrous), and marked with fine lines or ridges. Nodes are distinct, and internodes span 20-30 cm.

The pinnately compound leaves are large, with 3-7 triangular-ovate leaflets, arranged alternately along the stem. They are dark green, thin, and serrated at the margin. The leaves lack stipules and have petioles 5-20 cm long.

The compound umbel inflorescence of Angelica sinensis features small, actinomorphic flowers with white to pale pink petals. Each flower has five floral parts in each whorl, and the sepals and petals are indistinguishable. The flowers are borne on peduncles up to 10 cm long and have two styles and two capitate stigmas.

Its schizocarp fruits are elliptical, dark brown, and split into two mericarps upon maturity. The seeds are elliptical, hard, and impermeable, with a brown, smooth, and shiny seed coat. They lack a visible hilum and contain a straight embryo with two fleshy cotyledons and a small radicle.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Angelica sinensis by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

Roots

Angelica sinensis has a fibrous root system, which consists of a taproot (main root) and adventitious roots. The branching pattern of these roots is moderately lateral, giving rise to a well-structured network.

Angelica sinensis roots feature pointed tips, making them suitable for penetrating the soil efficiently. The root density is approximately 24 roots per square centimeter, or about 150 roots per square inch. These roots typically extend to a depth of 15 to 30 centimeters (6 to 12 inches) below the soil surface and have a diameter ranging from 0.2 to 0.5 centimeters (0.08 to 0.2 inches). The maximum root length can reach up to 60 centimeters (24 inches).

In terms of color, these roots exhibit a brown to reddish-brown hue, contributing to their distinctive appearance. Their texture is rough, characterized by longitudinal wrinkles, while the root surface remains smooth.

Notably, Angelica sinensis roots do not form root nodules, which distinguishes them from nitrogen-fixing plants. Additionally, root hairs on these roots are sparsely distributed.

Stems

The Angelica sinensis has a herbaceous stem, which means it is non-woody, and the plant primarily consists of soft, green tissue. Its stems are arranged alternately, with leaves and branches growing in an alternating pattern along the main stem.

In terms of shape, the stem of Angelica sinensis is primarily round when viewed in cross-section. The stem can reach a height of up to 150 cm (59 inches), making it a relatively tall plant. However, the stem's diameter is quite small, measuring approximately 0.5 cm (0.2 inches).

The stem's color is green, but it often has a purplish tinge, particularly in the younger growth. This distinctive coloration aids in the identification of the plant. The stem's texture is described as glabrous, meaning it is smooth and lacks any hair or fuzz.

The surface of the stem is characterized by striations, marked with fine lines or ridges. These striations contribute to the plant's overall appearance. Angelica sinensis stems also exhibit distinct nodes at intervals along the stem, contributing to the segmented appearance.

The internodes, or the sections of the stem between the nodes, measure approximately 20-30 cm (7.9-11.8 inches). This spacing between nodes is an important aspect of the plant's growth and branching pattern.

Leaves

Angelica sinensis have leaves that are pinnately compound, typically consisting of 3 to 7 leaflets. These leaflets are triangular-ovate in shape, creating a distinct appearance. They are arranged alternately along the stem, with an upright orientation.

In terms of size, Angelica sinensis leaves can be substantial, with lengths ranging from 20 to 60 cm (7.87-23.62 inches) and widths spanning 15 to 40 cm (5.91-15.75 inches). Despite their size, the leaves are relatively thin, measuring only 0.3 to 0.8 mm (0.0118-0.0315 inches) in thickness.

These leaves are characterized by their dark green color, giving the plant its lush appearance. The surface of the leaves is smooth and glabrous, lacking hair-like structures. At the apex, the leaves taper to an acuminate point, while the base is cordate in shape. The margin is serrated, adding to the overall visual texture.

Leaf venation is pinnate, with a prominent midrib running along the center. The leaf attachment is petiolate, with petioles measuring 5 to 20 cm (1.97-7.87 inches) in length. Notably, Angelica sinensis leaves lack stipules.

Flowers

Angelica sinensis produces its flowers in a compound umbel inflorescence. This means that multiple smaller umbels form a larger, umbrella-like structure, creating a visually striking display of flowers. The individual flowers of this species are relatively simple, with a characteristic actinomorphic or regular symmetry. They consist of five distinct floral parts in each whorl, a trait known as pentamerous, which is common among many plant species.

The flowers of Angelica sinensis are small, with an ovate shape. They measure approximately 2.5 cm (0.98 inches) in length and 1.5 cm (0.59 inches) in width. These flowers are predominantly white to pale pink in color and have a smooth, glabrous texture.

In terms of floral structure, each Angelica sinensis flower is composed of five floral parts, which include the sepals, petals, stamens, and carpels. The sepals are greenish-white and are typically 1-2 mm long (0.04-0.08 inches), while the petals are elliptic to oblong, measuring about 1.5-2 mm long (0.059-0.079 inches).

Notably, the sepals and petals of Angelica sinensis are indistinguishable, forming a single perianth structure. The stamens, numbering five, are equipped with filaments measuring 1.5-2 mm long (0.059-0.079 inches), and the pistil is composed of two fused carpels. The ovary is situated below the floral parts, making it hypogynous with a superior ovary.

The flowers have two styles measuring 2-3 mm long (0.079-0.12 inches) and two capitate stigmas. Importantly, Angelica sinensis flowers lack stipules and have inconspicuous bracts. They are borne on peduncles up to 10 cm (3.94 inches) long, with pedicels up to 1 cm (0.39 inches) long.

Fruits

Angelica sinensis has fruits that are classified as schizocarps, which means they consist of two mericarps that split upon maturity. They are arranged in umbels, with a divergent distribution pattern and basal attachment to the plant.

In terms of shape, Angelica sinensis fruits are elliptical, measuring approximately 4 cm (1.57 inches) in length, 2 cm (0.79 inches) in width, and 1 cm (0.39 inches) in thickness. They have a dark brown coloration and a dry, slightly rough texture. The fruit's surface appears wrinkled, and it is distinctly ribbed, giving it a textured appearance.

One of the notable features of these fruits is their dehiscence behavior. As they mature, they split into two mericarps, releasing their seeds for dispersal. This splitting characteristic is essential for the natural propagation and spread of Angelica sinensis in its habitat.

Seeds

Angelica sinensis has seeds that belong to the angiosperm group, specifically dicots. They are found in solitary arrangement within the plant's fruits.

In terms of size and shape, these seeds are elliptical, measuring 5 mm in length, 3 mm in width, and 2 mm in thickness. Their hardness is noteworthy, as they possess a hard texture. The color of the seed coat is brown, and its surface is smooth and shiny, with a thickness of approximately 0.2 mm.

One striking feature of Angelica sinensis seeds is their impermeable seed coat, which prevents the entry of water and external substances. They lack a visible hilum, the scar indicating the point of attachment to the fruit, which is a distinctive characteristic.

Inside the seeds, the embryo is straight and small, accompanied by two fleshy cotyledons. Additionally, there is a small radicle present within the seed.
