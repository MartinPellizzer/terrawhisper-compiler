Roots Morphology

The full description of the Acorus calamus root morphology is given in the following list.

  • Shape: The roots of Acorus calamus are characterized by a fibrous root system, which means they consist of many fine, thread-like roots. These roots grow in clusters, originating from the plant's rhizome.
  • Color: These roots are typically light to medium brown in color, with a smooth and slightly glossy surface.
  • Length: The average length of the individual roots can vary, but they typically range from 5 to 20 cm (2 to 8 inches) long.
  • Diameter: The diameter of the roots can range from 1 to 3 mm, depending on the age of the root and its location within the rhizome system.
  • Texture: The root surface is often described as smooth and slightly waxy to the touch, providing a characteristic sheen.
  • Odor: When crushed or broken, the roots emit a distinctive aroma often described as sweet, earthy, and mildly spicy. This aroma is due to the presence of essential oils, including β-asarone and other aromatic compounds.
  • Taste: The roots have a sweet, somewhat spicy, and slightly bitter taste, which can vary in intensity depending on the chemical composition of the specific root.
  • Anatomy: Microscopically, the roots display a typical structure with epidermis, cortex, endodermis, pericycle, and vascular tissue. The cortex contains starch grains and other storage structures.
  • Growth Pattern: The roots grow horizontally and radially from a central rhizome, creating a dense network just beneath the soil surface. This pattern aids in the plant's ability to form colonies in wetland habitats.
  • Buds and Offshoots: The roots may have dormant buds along their length. These buds can give rise to new shoots, facilitating vegetative reproduction and clonal expansion of the plant.
  • Branching: The roots exhibit a high degree of branching, especially near the rhizome, allowing for efficient nutrient absorption and anchoring of the plant in its wetland habitat.

Rhizomes Morphology

The full description of the Acorus calamus rhizomes morphology is given in the following list.

  • Structure: Acorus calamus rhizomes are thick, fleshy, and typically cylindrical in shape. They can grow up to 1-2 cm in diameter. The rhizomes extend horizontally beneath the soil's surface.
  • Color: The color of the rhizomes is typically pale to dark brown. The outer surface may have a slightly rough texture.
  • Growth Habit: They exhibit sympodial growth, where new shoots and roots form from the nodes along the rhizome. These rhizomes can grow horizontally or slightly diagonally, allowing the plant to spread vegetatively.
  • Nodes and Internodes: They have distinct nodes and internodes. Nodes are the points along the rhizome where shoots, roots, and leaves emerge. Internodes are the spaces between nodes.
  • Root Development: Adventitious roots develop at the nodes of the rhizome. The number of roots and their branching can vary, depending on environmental conditions and age of the plant. These roots help anchor the plant and absorb water and nutrients from the soil.
  • Buds and Shoots: Axillary buds at the nodes of the rhizome give rise to new shoots. The shoots may develop into aerial leaves, while some remain subterranean. The number of shoots can increase with the age and size of the rhizome.
  • Storage of Nutrients: Rhizomes are storage organs, storing carbohydrates, starches, and other nutrients. This storage allows the plant to survive adverse conditions and generate new growth when environmental conditions improve.
  • Adaptations for Waterlogged Conditions: This plant is often found in wetland habitats. Rhizomes possess aerenchyma, which are specialized air channels that facilitate oxygen transport in waterlogged soils. This adaptation is essential for the survival of the plant in anaerobic conditions.
  • Length: The length of the rhizomes can vary significantly. They can range from a few centimeters to several meters in length. Longer rhizomes enable the plant to colonize larger areas.
  • Propagation: This plant primarily reproduces vegetatively through its rhizomes. Small sections of rhizome, each containing a node and some internode, can be cut and replanted to establish new plants.

Leaves Morphology

The full description of the Acorus calamus leaves morphology is given in the following list.

  • Arrangement: Acorus calamus leaves are arranged in a basal rosette. The leaves emerge directly from the base of the plant.
  • Shape: The leaves are typically lance-shaped. They can be linear, sword-like, or ensiform. The length of the leaves can range from 30 to 120 cm.
  • Margin: The leaves have entire margins. The edges of the leaves are smooth and lack serrations or teeth.
  • Venation: The leaves exhibit parallel venation. Numerous parallel veins run the length of each leaf. The venation pattern is characteristic of monocotyledonous plants.
  • Base: The leaf base is sheathing, which means the leaf surrounds the stem. These sheaths are tightly attached to the stem and can be green or light brown.
  • Color: The upper surface of the leaves is typically a glossy dark green. The lower surface may appear slightly paler in color.
  • Texture: The leaves have a smooth and glabrous (hairless) texture. The surface is shiny and often waxy to the touch.
  • Size: The leaves can vary in length, but they are generally 1.5-2.5 cm wide. Leaf width remains relatively constant along the length of the leaf.
  • Aroma: An important feature of the leaves is their distinct aromatic quality. They emit a sweet, spicy, and slightly earthy fragrance. This aroma is due to the presence of essential oils, such as β-asarone.
  • Persistence: This is a deciduous plant, and its leaves die back in the winter. The plant remains in a dormant state until new leaves emerge in the spring.
  • Placement: The leaves are arranged alternately along the stem. This means that a single leaf emerges from the stem at a time, alternating sides.
  • Tip: The leaf tips are acute, tapering to a point. The acute tips help in distinguishing them from other similar plants.
  • Petiole: The leaves have petioles (leaf stems) that are absent or very short. The petioles do not extend significantly beyond the leaf base.
  • Surface Features: The upper surface of the leaves may have a slightly raised midrib. This midrib is often a shade of yellow-green or light green.
  • Senescence: As the leaves mature, they may turn slightly yellow before falling off during the autumn season. Senescence is a natural process before the leaves shed.

Flowers Morphology

The full description of the Acorus calamus flowers morphology is given in the following list.

  • Inflorescence Type: Acorus calamus produces small, inconspicuous flowers that are arranged in a dense, cylindrical, spadix inflorescence. The spadix is a characteristic feature of the Acoraceae family.
  • Size: The individual flowers are quite small, typically measuring around 4-6 mm in length. These diminutive flowers are often overlooked due to their size.
  • Color: The flowers are typically a pale yellow to light greenish-yellow. The tepals (undifferentiated sepals and petals) have a subtle, almost translucent appearance.
  • Tepal Count: The flowers are composed of six tepals, arranged in two whorls of three each. These tepals are similar in appearance and are often referred to as "tepals" because they are difficult to distinguish as sepals or petals.
  • Tepal Morphology: The tepals are lance-shaped and possess a distinct midrib running down the length. They are often around 2-3 mm long and have a waxy texture.
  • Stamens: The flowers have six stamens, which are typically shorter than the tepals. Stamens are the male reproductive organs of the flower they bear anthers at the tip, which release pollen for pollination.
  • Pistil: The female reproductive structure, known as the pistil, consists of a single, simple ovary. The ovary is positioned superiorly and contains numerous ovules.
  • Carpels: The pistil is composed of a single carpel, which is a characteristic feature of monocotyledonous plants like this. The carpel is fused, and the ovules are attached along the ventral suture.
