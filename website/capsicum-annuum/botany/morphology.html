Capsicum annuum morphology

Capsicum annuum, known as bell pepper, has a fibrous root system that with a taproot structure, creating a moderate branching network of non-woody roots, allowing efficient water and nutrient uptake. The roots are white to light brown and non-nodulated, with sparse, fine, unbranched root hairs.

The herbaceous stem is round, with green color that matures to brown or purple, while its smooth surface lacks hair. Leaves are simple, elliptical, arranged alternately with smooth margins, and have a prominent midrib. Flowers form an umbel inflorescence with actinomorphic symmetry and a complete structure, typically found in the leaf axils, while the petals are usually white, green, or yellow.

Bell peppers, as fruits, are fleshy, indehiscent, and ellipsoid, varying in size and transitioning from green to red, yellow, or orange. The seeds are dicotyledonous, elliptical, hard, and axially arranged within the fruit. Their thin seed coat can be cream, brown, or black, with a shiny or matte surface, and they have an impermeable seed coat. Inside the seed, an embryo consists of two cotyledons and a radicle for potential plant growth.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Capsicum annuum by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

Roots

The root system type of Capsicum annuum is fibrous, which means it consists of numerous fine roots. Unlike some plants with deep and extensive roots, Capsicum annuum has a taproot structure, where the primary root is more dominant than its lateral roots.

The branching pattern of its roots is moderate, creating a well-balanced network to support the plant's growth and nutrient absorption. The root tips are non-woody, presenting a herbaceous nature.

In terms of root density, you can find approximately 125 roots per cubic decimeter of soil. This abundant root system helps the plant efficiently access water and nutrients from the soil.

The root depth of Capsicum annuum typically ranges between 15 to 30 centimeters (approximately 6 to 12 inches). This depth allows the plant to anchor itself securely in the soil and reach moisture and nutrients.

The root diameter is relatively small, measuring between 0.2 to 0.5 centimeters (about 0.08 to 0.2 inches), which is characteristic of many herbaceous plants. Root length can vary, but it typically falls within the range of 20 to 60 centimeters (approximately 8 to 24 inches).

The color of the roots is typically whitish to light brown, reflecting their young and herbaceous nature. Their surface texture is smooth, allowing for efficient water and nutrient uptake from the surrounding soil.

Notably, Capsicum annuum does not form root nodules, which are commonly found in leguminous plants. Additionally, the plant's root hairs are sparse, fine, and unbranched.

Stems

The stem type of Capsicum annuum is herbaceous, meaning it is non-woody, and it provides support for the plant without the development of wood-like tissue. Stem arrangement is opposite, with leaves occurring in pairs at each node along the stem. This arrangement creates a pattern of leaves alternating on either side of the stem.

The stem shape of Capsicum annuum is typically round, resembling a cylindrical structure. In terms of size, the stem can reach heights ranging from 30 to 120 cm (11.8 to 47.2 inches). The stem's diameter falls within the range of 0.5 to 1.5 cm (0.2 to 0.6 inches), with variations depending on the plant's age and health.

The stem color is predominantly green, especially in young plants, but it matures to brown or even purple as the plant develops. The stem texture is smooth, and the surface is glabrous, meaning it lacks hair or trichomes. This contributes to the stem's characteristic appearance.

Stem nodes are frequent, occurring at the attachment points of leaves, leading to the distinct opposite leaf arrangement mentioned earlier. The internodes, which are the segments of stem between nodes, typically measure between 5 to 15 cm (2 to 6 inches) in length.

Leaves

The leaves of Capsicum annuum are generally simple, with an elliptical shape that ranges from 2 to 8 centimeters in length and 0.5 to 3 centimeters in width (or approximately 0.79 to 3.15 inches long and 0.2 to 1.18 inches wide in the imperial system). They are arranged alternately on the stem and stand erect with a vertical orientation.

The leaves have a characteristic green color and a smooth or slightly textured surface, appearing glabrous, meaning they lack hair. At the apex, the leaves come to a point with an acute tip, while their base is wedge-shaped or cuneate. The margins of Capsicum annuum leaves are typically smooth and unbroken, exhibiting an entire configuration.

Vein patterns on the leaves follow a pinnate arrangement, resembling a feather with numerous smaller veins stemming from the midrib. The leaves themselves are simple, indicating they are undivided and not segmented.

The midrib, a prominent feature, runs down the center of the leaf. The attachment of the leaf to the stem occurs via a petiole, which measures 2 to 8 centimeters in length (or approximately 0.79 to 3.15 inches in the imperial system). Notably, stipules are usually absent, or if present, they are small and inconspicuous.

Flowers

The inflorescence type of Capsicum annuum is an "umbel," where multiple flowers radiate from a common point, similar to the spokes of an umbrella. Each node typically bears a single flower, indicating a simple inflorescence arrangement.

The flowers of Capsicum annuum display an actinomorphic symmetry, meaning they are radially symmetrical and can be divided into equal halves in any direction. This symmetry is an important aspect of its floral structure.

In terms of flower structure, Capsicum annuum features complete flowers, where all floral parts are present, including sepals, petals, stamens, pistils, and other essential components.

Flowers are axillary, attaching themselves in the leaf axils, giving rise to a well-distributed floral pattern on the plant.

The shape of the Capsicum annuum flower is generally flat and circular, referred to as "rotate." Each flower measures approximately 2.5 cm (1 inch) in both length and width.

The color of these flowers varies, with common shades being white, green, or yellow. They possess a smooth texture and a glabrous (hairless) surface.

The flowers exhibit a pentamerous arrangement, meaning there are five of each floral part: sepals, petals, stamens, and a single pistil. The combination of sepals and petals is referred to as the perianth.

Stamens, the male reproductive parts, number five, while there is one pistil, the female reproductive organ, per flower. The ovary is superior in position.

The style is singular, and the stigma, the receptive part of the pistil, can be lobed or capitate, having a head-shaped appearance.

Notably, Capsicum annuum flowers lack stipules, small leaf-like structures often found at the base of the petioles.

At the base of the flower, you can find bracts, modified leaves that protect the developing flower bud. These flowers are attached to the plant via a variable-length peduncle, typically measuring between 1 to 3 cm (0.4-1.2 inches), and the individual flowers are connected to the peduncle by short pedicels, often less than 1 cm (0.4 inch) in length.

Fruits

Bell peppers belong to the category of berries, featuring fleshy, indehiscent (non-splitting) fruit types. They are typically solitary, hanging from the plant in a pendulous fashion, with axial distribution, which means they are attached at the stem end. The attachment is pendent, as the fruits hang downward.

In terms of shape, bell peppers are generally described as ellipsoid, showcasing a well-defined elongated form. Their size can vary, with lengths ranging from 4 to 10 centimeters (1.6 to 3.9 inches), widths of 2 to 5 centimeters (0.8 to 2 inches), and thicknesses ranging from 0.5 to 1.5 centimeters (0.2 to 0.6 inches).

One of the most striking features of bell peppers is their color diversity. These fruits can be found in various hues, transitioning from green to red, yellow, or orange as they ripen. Their texture is notably smooth, and the surface appears glossy.

In terms of surface characteristics, bell peppers may exhibit wrinkles or indentations, adding to their visual appeal. Importantly, these fruits do not undergo dehiscence, meaning they do not split open to release seeds.

Seeds

The seeds of Capsicum annuum are dicotyledonous, which means they have two cotyledons or seed leaves. They are typically arranged axially within the fruit. In terms of shape, these seeds are elliptical, with dimensions ranging from 0.3 to 0.5 centimeters in length and 0.15 to 0.25 centimeters in width. Their thickness falls between 1 to 1.5 millimeters.

One noteworthy aspect is the hardness of these seeds. They are characterized as hard, indicating resistance to damage. The color of the seed coat can vary but is commonly found in shades of cream, brown, or black. This outer coat may be either smooth or slightly textured and have a shiny or matte surface.

The thickness of the seed coat is relatively thin, measuring between 0.1 to 0.2 millimeters. It's worth noting that Capsicum annuum seeds have impermeable seed coats, meaning they do not readily allow the passage of water or gases. This trait can impact germination and early growth.

The seed hilum, often referred to as the scar, is where the seed was attached to the fruit. This tiny feature carries significance for researchers studying seed development and attachment.

Inside the seed, an embryo is present, indicating the potential for future plant growth. This embryo typically consists of two cotyledons, the initial leaves that provide nourishment to the developing plant, and a radicle, which is the embryonic root.
