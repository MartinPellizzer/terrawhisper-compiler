Bupleurum chinense morphology

Bupleurum chinense is characterized by its fibrous root system, primarily consisting of a taproot that reaches 5-10 cm in length and displays moderate branching. These roots have a slender diameter and grow 10-20 cm deep in the soil, with a light brown color and a generally smooth surface. They lack nodules and have sparse, short, non-glandular root hairs.

The herbaceous stems of Bupleurum chinense are typically arranged alternately, with heights ranging from 30 to 100 cm and diameters of 0.2 to 0.5 cm. Stem color varies from green to brown or reddish-brown, and texture can be smooth, rough, or pubescent. Distinct nodes serve as leaf attachment points, while internodes vary in length, typically measuring 5 to 15 cm.

The simple leaves are linear-oblong, measuring 2-5 cm in length, 0.2-0.5 cm in width, and 0.1-0.2 cm in thickness. They are green, smooth, and hairless, with an acute apex and cuneate base. The leaves lack teeth or lobes, have parallel venation, are sessile (directly attached to the stem), and do not have petioles or stipules.

The flowers are arranged in umbels, typically 5-10 cm across, with actinomorphic symmetry. The herbaceous, glabrous flowers are yellow, consisting of five sepals and five petals. Five stamens surround the pistil, which has an ovary, short style, and two-lobed stigma. The flowers are supported by pedicels and have bracts.

Bupleurum chinense produces schizocarp fruits arranged in umbels with 1-3 fruits per cluster, attached to pedicels. The elliptical fruits measure 4.5-6.5 mm in length and change from green to dark brown as they mature. They have a smooth, shiny, glabrous surface and two mericarps that separate into two one-seeded units.

The seeds are dicotyledonous, oval in shape, and lack appendages. They are small, firm, brown, and have a smooth, shiny seed coat with low permeability. The seed hilum is distinct, and the seeds contain two cotyledons and a straight embryo with a present radicle.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Bupleurum chinense by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

Roots

Bupleurum chinense has a fibrous root system, which means they consist of numerous fine roots rather than one dominant central root. The root structure primarily consists of a taproot that extends to a length of approximately 5-10 cm (2-4 inches).

These roots display moderate branching, with branches forming in an alternate pattern. The tips of these roots are fine and pointed. In terms of density, you can expect to find around 10-20 roots per 100 cm² of soil.

The depth at which Bupleurum chinense roots typically grow ranges from 10-20 cm (4-8 inches) below the soil surface. They have a slender diameter, measuring about 0.1-0.2 cm (0.04-0.08 inches).

Regarding length, these roots can extend to a sizeable 20-30 cm (8-12 inches). The roots have a light brown color and a generally smooth surface with occasional lateral scars.

Notably, Bupleurum chinense roots do not form nodules, which are commonly seen in leguminous plants that engage in nitrogen fixation. Root hairs on these plants are sparse, short, and non-glandular.

Stems

Bupleurum chinense typically has herbaceous stems, although they can occasionally take on a woody appearance. These stems are arranged alternately, meaning that leaves emerge from different points along the stem, rather than directly opposite each other.

In terms of shape, the stems are generally round but can also be cylindrical, tapering, or even ribbed. They reach a height of 30 to 100 cm (11.8 to 39.4 inches), making them relatively tall, and have a diameter ranging from 0.2 to 0.5 cm (0.08 to 0.2 inches). This diameter measurement gives an idea of their thickness.

The stems of Bupleurum chinense primarily display a green color, although in some instances, they may appear brown or reddish-brown, indicating possible variations or environmental influences. Their texture is smooth, but they can also be rough or pubescent, depending on the individual plant and its habitat.

Regarding the stem's surface, it can either be glabrous (smooth and hairless) or exhibit hairiness and small scales. The nodes on the stem are distinct, with leaves attached to them, serving as points of leaf attachment. The internodes, the segments between the nodes, vary in length but usually measure between 5 to 15 cm (2 to 5.9 inches). These segments contribute to the overall structure of the plant and the spacing between leaves.

Leaves

Bupleurum chinense features simple leaves that are arranged alternately along its stems. These leaves are typically linear-oblong, with lengths ranging from 2 to 5 cm (0.79 to 1.97 inches) and widths between 0.2 to 0.5 cm (0.08 to 0.2 inches). Their thickness measures about 0.1 to 0.2 cm (0.04 to 0.08 inches).

The color of Bupleurum chinense leaves is generally green, and the leaf surfaces are smooth and hairless, known as glabrous. The leaf apex is acute, forming a pointed tip, while the base is cuneate, creating a wedge-shaped appearance.

The margins of these leaves are entire, meaning they lack teeth or lobes. Furthermore, Bupleurum chinense leaves have parallel venation, with prominent midribs running down their centers.

The leaf attachment is sessile, meaning the leaves are directly attached to the stem without a stalk. Additionally, these leaves lack petioles, which are the stalks that often connect leaves to the stem. Stipules, which are small leaf-like structures at the base of the leaf stalk, are also absent in this species.

The overall shape of the leaf is oblong-lanceolate, which means it is elongated with the widest portion in the middle.

Flowers

The inflorescence type of Bupleurum chinense is an umbel, typically measuring 5-10 cm across. Its flowers exhibit a simple structure, composed of basic floral parts. In terms of symmetry, they are actinomorphic, which means they display radial symmetry.

These herbaceous flowers are attached to the stem via pedicels. They are ovate in shape, resembling an egg, with a modest size of 3-4 mm in length and 2-3 mm in width. The dominant color of Bupleurum chinense flowers is yellow, contributing to their distinct appearance.

When touched, the flowers have a glabrous texture, giving them a smooth, hairless feel, and their surfaces appear shiny. The flowers consist of five floral parts, encompassing sepals, petals, stamens, and the pistil.

The sepals, five in number, form the outermost layer and are greenish-yellow. In contrast, the inner layer comprises five yellow petals. This collective term for sepals and petals is known as the perianth.

The stamen, numbering five, surrounds the pistil. The pistil, made up of an ovary, style, and two-lobed stigma, is superior, positioned above the other floral parts. The style is short, connecting the ovary to the stigma, the pollen-receptive tip.

Bupleurum chinense flowers lack stipules, and they have bracts, small leaf-like structures, located below the flowers. These flowers are supported by a peduncle, the main stalk of the inflorescence, and individual pedicels that attach them to the peduncle.

Fruits

Bupleurum chinense produces schizocarp fruits that are arranged in umbels, typically clustered with 1 to 3 fruits per cluster. These fruits are attached to the plant through pedicels measuring 2 to 4 millimeters in length.

In terms of their physical appearance, the fruits are elliptical in shape, with a length ranging from 4.5 to 6.5 millimeters (approximately 0.18 to 0.26 inches), a width of 2.5 to 3.5 millimeters (around 0.10 to 0.14 inches), and a thickness of 1.5 to 2.5 millimeters (roughly 0.06 to 0.10 inches).

The color of these fruits changes as they mature. While immature, they are green, but as they ripen, they turn dark brown. Their texture is described as smooth and shiny, and the fruit surface is glabrous, meaning it lacks any hairs or trichomes.

Notably, these fruits do not have distinct markings, and they exhibit a unique dehiscence mechanism. Each fruit consists of two mericarps, which eventually separate into two one-seeded units.

Seeds

The seeds of Bupleurum chinense are of dicotyledonous type, arranged singly within the fruit, and possess an oval shape. One notable feature is the absence of seed appendages.

In terms of physical dimensions, these seeds are relatively small. They have a length of approximately 2.5 millimeters, a width of 1.2 millimeters, and a thickness of 0.6 millimeters. These seeds are firm in texture, and their coloration is typically brown. The seed coat is smooth, shiny, and has a thickness of about 0.2 millimeters.

An essential characteristic of these seeds is their impermeable seed coat, which restricts water absorption. In metric measurements, the seed coat permeability is quite low, approximately 0.008 inches in the imperial system.

The seed hilum, a scar indicating the point of seed attachment within the fruit, is distinct. The embryo inside the seed is straight, and the seeds contain two cotyledons, which are the primary leaves of the young plant.

Furthermore, these seeds have a present radicle, which is the embryonic root that emerges upon germination.
