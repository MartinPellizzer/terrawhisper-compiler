# Centella asiatica morphology

![Centella asiatica morphology](/images/centella-asiatica-botany-morphology.jpg "Centella asiatica morphology")

Centella asiatica's root system is fibrous, characterized by numerous fine roots with taproot structure, moderately branching in a dichotomous pattern. Root tips are sharp and tapering, allowing for effective soil penetration. The root density is approximately 2,000 roots per square meter, extending to a depth of 15-20 cm (6-8 inches), with a diameter of 1-2 mm (0.04-0.08 inches). Root color is pale beige-brown, and the surface is smooth with fine lateral lines. This plant lacks root nodules but has sparse, fine, unbranched root hairs.

Stems are herbaceous, opposite in arrangement, with a circular cross-section, reaching a height of 10-30 cm (3.9-11.8 inches) and a diameter of 2-6 mm (0.08-0.24 inches). Stems are green, smooth, and glabrous. Nodes are present, and internodes are short.

Leaves are simple, reniform (kidney-shaped), arranged oppositely, and horizontally oriented. They measure 2-6 cm (0.8-2.4 inches) in length, 1-3 cm (0.4-1.2 inches) in width, and 0.1-0.2 cm (0.04-0.08 inches) in thickness. They are green, smooth, and glabrous, with a blunt apex, cordate base, smooth margins, and palmate venation. The leaf lamina is orbicular, attached with a petiole of 2-10 cm (0.8-4 inches), lacking stipules.

Flowers are umbellate, simple, actinomorphic, and solitary, measuring 0.5-1 cm (0.2-0.4 inches) in length and 1-2 cm (0.4-0.8 inches) in width, typically pink or light purple. They have five sepals and five petals, with two stamens and one pistil, featuring a short style and capitate stigma. Flowers are glabrous, lack stipules, and have inconspicuous bracts, with a short peduncle and pedicels.

Fruits are follicles, arranged solitarily in axillary positions, attached with a pedicel, elliptical in shape (0.4 cm x 0.2 cm x 0.1 cm) and light brown when mature. They have a smooth, shiny surface, and they split open when ripe (dehiscent) to release seeds.

Seeds are dicotyledonous, ovate, solitary, measuring 0.3 cm x 0.2 cm, with a brown to dark brown seed coat that is smooth and somewhat wrinkled, around 0.1 mm thick, semi-permeable. The hilum is small and inconspicuous, and the embryo is straight with two cotyledons and a short radicle.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Centella asiatica by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

## Roots



The root system of Centella asiatica is classified as fibrous, consisting of numerous fine roots. These roots have a taproot structure, with moderate branching, typically following a dichotomous pattern. At the tips, these roots are sharp and tapering, allowing for effective penetration of the soil.

The root density of Centella asiatica is noteworthy, with approximately 2,000 roots per square meter. This high density contributes to the plant's stability and ability to efficiently absorb nutrients and water from the soil. In terms of depth, the roots typically extend to a depth of 15-20 cm (6-8 inches), which is well-suited for the plant's growth and development.

Root diameter varies from 1-2 mm (0.04-0.08 inches), indicating the fine and delicate nature of the roots. The maximum root length observed in Centella asiatica can reach up to 20 cm (8 inches). Root color is pale beige-brown, and the root surface texture is smooth with fine lateral lines, providing the plant with a unique visual and textural identity.

Notably, Centella asiatica does not form root nodules, which are specialized structures for nitrogen fixation commonly found in some other plant species. Root hairs, on the other hand, are present but sparse. They are fine and unbranched, serving to increase the root's surface area for improved nutrient and water absorption.

## Stems



Centella asiatica stems are herbaceous, meaning they are non-woody and flexible, making them distinctly different from woody shrubs and trees. Stem arrangement in this plant is opposite, with leaves attaching in pairs at nodes along the stem. This arrangement creates a symmetrical appearance along the stem, which aids in plant recognition.

In terms of stem shape, Centella asiatica exhibits a circular cross-section. This cylindrical form is a notable characteristic and sets it apart from plants with more angular stem shapes. The height of the stems typically ranges from 10 to 30 centimeters (3.9 to 11.8 inches), and the stem diameter falls between 2 to 6 millimeters (0.08 to 0.24 inches). These dimensions provide an idea of the plant's overall size and structure.

The stem color of Centella asiatica is a vibrant green, consistent with its healthy and thriving appearance. The stem texture is smooth to the touch, contributing to its characteristic appearance. Additionally, the stem surface is glabrous, meaning it lacks fine hairs or trichomes.

One can observe nodes along the stem, where leaves are attached. These nodes are significant points in the plant's anatomy, influencing leaf distribution and growth patterns. The internodes, the spaces between nodes, are relatively short, contributing to the compact and low-growing nature of Centella asiatica.

## Leaves



The leaves of Centella asiatica are simple and have a distinct kidney-shaped, or reniform, shape. They are arranged oppositely on the stem, with a horizontal orientation.

The leaves of Centella asiatica are relatively small, typically measuring 2 to 6 centimeters (0.8 to 2.4 inches) in length and 1 to 3 centimeters (0.4 to 1.2 inches) in width. They are quite thin, with a thickness ranging from 0.1 to 0.2 centimeters (0.04 to 0.08 inches). The color of the leaves is generally green, and their texture is smooth. The leaf surface is described as glabrous, meaning it is hairless.

When examining the leaves closely, you'll notice that their apex is blunt, and the base is heart-shaped, or cordate. The leaf margins are smooth and entire, meaning they lack teeth or serrations along the edges. The venation of the leaves is palmate, which means the veins radiate from a common point.

The leaf lamina is orbicular, giving the leaves a circular appearance. The midrib of the leaf is prominent, running through the center. The leaves are petiolate, meaning they are attached to the stem by a petiole, which is typically 2 to 10 centimeters (0.8 to 4 inches) in length. However, Centella asiatica lacks stipules.

## Flowers



The flowers of Centella asiatica are typically small, with a circular shape, measuring between 0.5 to 1 cm (0.2 to 0.4 inches) in length and 1 to 2 cm (0.4 to 0.8 inches) in width. Their colors are predominantly pink or light purple, rendering them visually appealing.

The flowers exhibit actinomorphic symmetry, meaning they are radially symmetrical, and they follow a simple structure, featuring all the essential floral parts. Each flower is solitary, with a solitary attachment to its stem, giving it a sense of individuality.

The floral anatomy of Centella asiatica includes five floral parts, comprising five sepals and five petals. The petals and sepals together form the perianth, with both looking similar. The flowers have two stamens and one pistil, and their ovary is superior, indicating it is attached above the other floral parts.

In terms of texture and surface, the flowers are smooth and glabrous, which means they are hairless. They lack stipules and have inconspicuous bracts. The peduncle that supports the inflorescence is short, as are the pedicels that support individual flowers.

Moreover, the style of these flowers is short, and their stigma is capitate, resembling a rounded knob.

## Fruits



The fruit type of Centella asiatica is a follicle, a dry fruit that splits open when mature to release seeds. These fruits are typically arranged in a solitary manner and are distributed axillary, meaning they are found at the leaf axils of the plant.

The attachment of these fruits is via a pedicel, which is a slender stalk connecting the fruit to the plant. In terms of shape, the fruits are elliptical in form, with precise measurements of 0.4 cm (0.16 inches) in length, 0.2 cm (0.08 inches) in width, and 0.1 cm (0.04 inches) in thickness. When mature, they exhibit a light brown color, and their texture is smooth and firm. The surface of these fruits appears shiny and lacks distinctive markings.

One noteworthy characteristic is their dehiscence, meaning they split open when ripe to release their seeds. This natural mechanism aids in seed dispersal, allowing the plant to propagate effectively.

## Seeds



Centella asiatica seeds are dicotyledonous, typically occurring singly. They have an ovate shape, measuring approximately 0.3 cm in length and 0.2 cm in width. The seeds lack appendages and are firm in hardness.

The seed coat of Centella asiatica seeds is brown to dark brown in color, with a smooth surface that appears somewhat wrinkled. The thickness of the seed coat is relatively thin, around 0.1 mm. This thin coat is semi-permeable, allowing for the exchange of gases and moisture during germination.

The hilum, a scar on the seed indicating the point of attachment to the ovary, is small and inconspicuous. The embryo within the seed is straight, and there are two cotyledons, the primary leaves that emerge upon germination. The radicle (the embryonic root) is short.
