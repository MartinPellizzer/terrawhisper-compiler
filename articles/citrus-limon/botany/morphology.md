# Citrus limon morphology

![Citrus limon morphology](/images/citrus-limon-botany-morphology.jpg "Citrus limon morphology")

Citrus limon, or the lemon tree, possesses a distinct fibrous root system, forming an intricate network of adventitious roots that aid in rapid establishment. The dichotomous branching pattern enhances its ability to access water and nutrients efficiently. The root tips are acute, enabling easy soil penetration. Lemon roots are white or pale yellow, reflecting light and avoiding excessive heating, with smooth surfaces and abundant fine root hairs for absorption.

The stems of the lemon tree are woody, with an alternate arrangement of leaves. They are cylindrical, with variable color, reflecting the tree's age. Stems are smooth and glossy, featuring nodes where leaves and branches attach.

Lemon tree leaves are simple, elliptical, with glossy dark green color and leathery texture. They are alternately arranged, with an acute apex, cuneate base, and smooth margins. A prominent midrib and pinnate venation characterize these leaves, which attach to branches via petioles and lack stipules.

Lemon flowers are apetalous, actinomorphic, and hermaphroditic, arising in clusters at branch ends. They have a cup-shaped appearance with white color, 20 stamens, and a single pistil. Lemon fruits are ovoid, bright yellow, and indehiscent, often found near leaves and attached by short pedicels. Lemon seeds are dicotyledonous, polyembryonic, elliptical, with brown, impermeable seed coats, a visible hilum, and straight embryos with two cotyledons and a prominent radicle.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Citrus limon by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

## Roots



Citrus limon's root system is categorized as fibrous, comprising numerous slender roots that emanate from the base of the tree. These roots form an intricate network within the topsoil layers. The root structure is classified as adventitious, meaning they do not originate from the embryonic root (radicle) but instead emerge from the stem's nodes, aiding in rapid establishment.

Root branching in Citrus limon follows a dichotomous pattern, where the main roots repeatedly divide into two similar-sized branches. This branching structure enhances the tree's ability to explore a larger volume of soil, accessing water and nutrients efficiently. The root tips are typically acute, enabling them to penetrate the soil with ease.

Regarding root density, Citrus limon exhibits approximately 0.7 roots per cubic centimeter of soil within its root zone. The root depth ranges from 15 to 30 centimeters (6 to 12 inches), while the root diameter averages between 0.2 to 0.5 millimeters (0.008 to 0.02 inches). These dimensions are adapted to suit the tree's requirements for stability and resource uptake.

The color of Citrus limon's roots is predominantly white or pale yellow, contributing to their capacity for light reflection and avoidance of excessive heating. The root surface texture is smooth, facilitating the passage through the soil matrix.

Citrus limon's roots do not form nodules. However, they are equipped with abundant fine root hairs, enhancing their capacity for water and nutrient absorption. These fine root hairs amplify the surface area for absorption, further aiding the tree's growth and development.

## Stems



Citrus limon stems are of the woody type and tend to be branched. They have an alternate arrangement, meaning that leaves are attached singly at nodes along the stem. The stem's shape is typically cylindrical, with a diameter ranging from 0.5 to 2 centimeters (0.2 to 0.8 inches).

In terms of height, lemon trees can grow to a maximum of about 3 meters (9.8 feet). The stems themselves have a diameter of 0.5 to 2 centimeters (0.2 to 0.8 inches). The color of the stems can vary from green to brown, providing a hint of the tree's age and health.

The texture of the stem is smooth, and the surface is glossy, giving it a somewhat shiny appearance. Stems have nodes where leaves and branches attach, and the internodes, the spaces between these nodes, can vary in length.

## Leaves



The leaves of Citrus limon are simple, elliptical in shape, and typically measure between 5 to 7 centimeters (2 to 2.75 inches) in length and 2.5 to 4 centimeters (1 to 1.5 inches) in width. They have a thickness of 0.1 to 0.2 centimeters (0.04 to 0.08 inches).

In terms of arrangement, lemon tree leaves are alternately positioned and spirally arranged along the branches. Their orientation is predominantly horizontal, creating a dense canopy.

The color of lemon tree leaves is glossy dark green, giving them a distinctive appearance. The upper surface is shiny and smooth, while the underside is dull. These leaves are known for their leathery texture.

The leaf apex is acute or pointed, and the base is cuneate, forming a wedge shape. Their margins are entire, meaning they are smooth and lack any serrations or lobes.

A prominent midrib runs down the center of each leaf, which is supported by a pinnate venation system, with smaller veins branching out from the main vein.

The leaf lamina is simple and undivided, and the leaves attach to the branches through petioles, which are 1 to 2 centimeters (0.4 to 0.8 inches) long. Stipules, small leaf-like structures at the base of the petioles, are notably absent in lemon tree leaves.

## Flowers



The inflorescence type of Citrus limon is a panicle, where multiple flowers are arranged in clusters at the ends of branches. These flowers are relatively simple in structure, lacking petals, which makes them apetalous. In terms of symmetry, they are actinomorphic, meaning they display radial symmetry.

Each lemon flower is hermaphroditic, containing both male and female reproductive organs in the same flower. These flowers are axillary, meaning they arise from the leaf axils, typically found at the junction between the leaves and stems.

The shape of the lemon flower is cup-shaped, with a white color and a smooth, glabrous texture. They have a diameter of about 2.5 centimeters (0.98 inches) and a width of 2.0 centimeters (0.79 inches). The flowers consist of five floral parts, with five sepals but no distinct petals. Instead, the tepals, which are fused at the base, give the appearance of petals.

The lemon flowers are highly abundant in stamens, with approximately 20 stamens, and they possess a single pistil. The ovary of the flower is superior, indicating its position above the attachment point of other flower parts. The style of the pistil is slender and terminates in a capitate stigma.

Unlike some other plant species, Citrus limon flowers lack stipules, and their bracts are small and inconspicuous. The peduncle, which is the stalk that supports the flower cluster, is short and slender, as is the pedicel, the stalk connecting individual flowers to the peduncle.

## Fruits



The fruits of Citrus limon fruits are classified as simple, indehiscent berries. They are typically found arranged either solitarily or in clusters, often near the leaves of the lemon tree.

Lemons are attached to the tree by a short pedicel, and their shape is generally ovoid or ellipsoidal. In terms of size, they typically measure 5 to 7 centimeters (approximately 2 to 2.75 inches) in length, 4 to 6 centimeters (approximately 1.5 to 2.4 inches) in width, and have a thickness of about 4 centimeters (approximately 1.5 inches).

The color of ripe lemons is a bright yellow, making them easily distinguishable. Their texture is smooth, with thin, leathery skin that gives them a glossy appearance. The surface of a lemon may be slightly bumpy, and some may have small oil glands, which contribute to their unique flavor and aroma.

One notable characteristic of lemons is their lack of dehiscence, meaning they do not split open naturally. This is in contrast to some other fruits that disperse their seeds through splitting.

## Seeds



The seeds of Citrus limon are dicotyledonous, meaning they have two embryonic leaves. Lemon trees are polyembryonic, and as a result, they produce multiple seeds per fruit, typically ranging from 3 to 10 seeds in a single lemon. Each lemon seed is elliptical in shape, measuring approximately 1.5 centimeters in length, 1.0 centimeters in width, and 0.3 centimeters in thickness. These seeds are hard and woody, which makes them durable.

The seed coat of Citrus limon seeds is brown, and it has a smooth and glossy surface. The thickness of the seed coat is relatively thin, measuring about 0.1 millimeters. This thin seed coat is impermeable, preventing water and other substances from easily penetrating the seed.

A distinctive feature of lemon seeds is the presence of a small, visible scar known as the hilum. This hilum serves as a point of attachment to the fruit and is a notable characteristic of lemon seeds. 

Inside the lemon seed, the embryo is straight and has two cotyledons. These cotyledons are fleshy and provide nourishment to the developing seedling. The presence of a prominent radicle within the lemon seed is another noteworthy feature. The radicle is the embryonic root that will develop into the primary root of the new lemon plant.
