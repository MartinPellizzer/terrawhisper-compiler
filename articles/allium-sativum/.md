# What to know before using garlic (Allium sativum)

![Garlic Overview](/images/allium-sativum-Overview.jpg "Garlic Overview")



This article gives an overview on the many uses of garlic and what you need to know before using it.

## What are the medicinal uses of garlic?

Garlic (Allium sativum) has been widely recognized for its modern medicinal uses, contributing to various health benefits. Rich in bioactive compounds, garlic is known for its potential in reducing blood pressure and cholesterol levels. Studies have demonstrated that a daily intake of garlic supplements can lead to a significant decrease in systolic and diastolic blood pressure, with an average reduction of approximately 8.4 mmHg and 7.3 mmHg, respectively. Additionally, garlic's anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties contribute to its role in supporting the immune system and reducing the risk of chronic diseases.



The following illustration lists the most important uses of [garlic in medicine](/allium-sativum/medicine.html).



![Allium sativum Medicine](/images/allium-sativum-medicine-general.jpg "Allium sativum Medicine")



The active compounds responsible for garlic's medicinal properties include allicin, ajoene, and diallyl disulfide. Allicin, formed when garlic is crushed or chopped, exhibits potent antibacterial and antifungal effects. Ajoene has been studied for its antiplatelet and antithrombotic properties, potentially aiding in cardiovascular health. Diallyl disulfide contributes to garlic's antioxidant capabilities, neutralizing harmful free radicals in the body. These compounds collectively provide garlic with its medicinal potency, making it a valuable natural remedy.







Various preparations of garlic exist for medicinal purposes, such as garlic oil, powder, and aged garlic extract (AGE). Garlic oil is often used topically for its antimicrobial properties, while garlic powder is a convenient supplement form. Aged garlic extract, produced by fermenting garlic, contains unique compounds and is associated with cardiovascular benefits, including improved endothelial function and reduced arterial stiffness. These diverse preparations allow individuals to choose the most suitable form based on their specific health needs and preferences.







While garlic is generally considered safe for consumption, precautions should be taken, especially for individuals with bleeding disorders or those scheduled for surgery, as garlic may enhance the risk of bleeding. High doses of garlic supplements can cause gastrointestinal discomfort, and allergic reactions are possible in some individuals. It is advisable to consult with a healthcare professional before incorporating garlic into a medicinal regimen, particularly for those taking anticoagulant medications or with known allergies to Allium species. Overall, when used judiciously, garlic can be a valuable addition to a holistic approach to health and wellness.





## What are the culinary uses of garlic?

Garlic (Allium sativum) is a versatile culinary ingredient cherished worldwide for its pungent flavor and numerous health benefits. In terms of culinary uses, garlic is a staple in various cuisines, adding depth and complexity to dishes. It is utilized in both raw and cooked forms, with minced, crushed, or chopped garlic releasing its distinct aroma and flavor. On average, a single bulb of garlic typically contains around 10 to 20 cloves, with each clove weighing approximately 3 to 5 grams. With its antibacterial and antifungal properties, garlic not only enhances the taste of food but also contributes to its medicinal qualities.





The following illustration lists the most common uses of garlic for culinary purposes.



![Allium sativum Cuisine](/images/allium-sativum-cuisine-general.jpg "Allium sativum Cuisine")



The flavor profile of garlic is characterized by a combination of spiciness and a rich, savory depth. The primary compound responsible for its pungency is allicin, formed when garlic is crushed or chopped. Allicin is a sulfur-containing compound, giving garlic its signature aroma and taste. Additionally, garlic contains various organosulfur compounds that contribute to its unique flavor profile. The intensity of the flavor can vary based on the preparation method, with raw garlic offering a sharper taste compared to milder, sweeter notes when roasted. Understanding these nuances allows chefs to tailor the use of garlic to complement specific dishes.







When incorporating garlic into culinary creations, a few tips can elevate the overall dining experience. To maximize the health benefits, it is recommended to let crushed or chopped garlic sit for about 10 minutes before cooking, allowing the enzymatic process that forms allicin to occur. To avoid bitterness, it is essential not to overcook garlic, as prolonged heat exposure can diminish its flavor and medicinal properties. Additionally, balancing the amount of garlic used is crucial, as its potency can easily dominate other flavors. Experimenting with different varieties, such as softneck or hardneck garlic, can also introduce subtle variations in taste and aroma.





## How to cultivate garlic in your garden?

Garlic (Allium sativum) is a versatile and easy-to-grow plant that can thrive in gardens or landscapes. To cultivate garlic, plant individual cloves in well-drained soil during the fall, around 5 cm deep and 10-15 cm apart, with pointed ends facing up. Ensure the location receives ample sunlight and maintain a consistent watering schedule during the growing season.





The following illustration lists the most important tips to cutlitvate garlic.



![Allium sativum Horticulture](/images/allium-sativum-horticulture-general.jpg "Allium sativum Horticulture")

Ideal growing conditions for garlic include a sunny location with at least six hours of sunlight daily. The plant prefers loose, well-draining soil with a pH range of 6.0 to 7.5. Incorporating organic matter, such as compost, into the soil enhances fertility. Adequate moisture is crucial, especially during the early growth stages, but garlic is susceptible to waterlogging, so avoid overwatering. Fertilize with a balanced fertilizer in early spring, and provide additional nutrients if the soil lacks essential elements.





Pruning and maintenance are essential to ensure a healthy garlic crop. Remove any scapes that emerge, as they divert energy away from bulb development. Weed regularly to minimize competition for nutrients and space. Mulching around the plants helps retain moisture, suppress weeds, and regulate soil temperature. Harvest when the lower leaves turn yellow and dry, typically in late spring or early summer. Allow the bulbs to cure in a dry, well-ventilated area for a few weeks before storing them in a cool, dark place. Proper care throughout the growing season ensures a bountiful harvest of flavorful garlic bulbs.





## What is the botanical profile of garlic?

Garlic, scientifically known as Allium sativum, belongs to the domain Eukarya, the kingdom Plantae, the phylum Angiosperms, the class Monocots, the order Asparagales, the family Amaryllidaceae, the genus Allium, and the species sativum.





The following illustration show the traditional taxonomy of garlic.



![Allium sativum Botany](/images/allium-sativum-botany-general.jpg "Allium sativum Botany")

Garlic is commonly known by its straightforward and widely recognized name, "garlic." Variants may include regional or cultural names, but the plant is generally referred to as garlic globally. This versatile plant may have local names in various languages, but its recognition is consistent due to its widespread culinary and cultural importance.





Garlic exhibits a herbaceous, non-woody stem with a basal rosette arrangement of linear, lanceolate leaves. The cylindrical stems, reaching heights of 30-90 cm, have a smooth, fleshy texture and are typically green. The leaves, with parallel veins and entire margins, are erect, sessile, and grow 15-40 cm in length, 1-3 cm in width, and 0.2-0.4 mm in thickness. The inflorescence is an umbel, bearing simple, actinomorphic flowers with six tepals. The fruits are capsules with ovate shapes, approximately 1.5 cm in length, and turn brown or gray upon maturity.




Garlic is distributed globally, thriving in a variety of climates. It is commonly found in temperate regions with well-drained soils. The plant often occupies open fields, gardens, and agricultural settings. Its shallow root system, extending up to 20 cm deep, supports its adaptation to diverse soil conditions. Garlic's ability to tolerate cold temperatures is reflected in its fibrous, tuberous roots, which typically die back after the growing season. The plant's widespread distribution and adaptability contribute to its prominence in various culinary traditions and agricultural practices worldwide.





## What is the history and folklore of garlic?

Garlic (Allium sativum) has held a significant place in traditional folk medicine across various cultures. Renowned for its purported health benefits, it has been employed to treat a myriad of ailments, from respiratory conditions to cardiovascular issues. In traditional Chinese medicine, garlic was utilized for its warming properties to expel cold and dampness from the body. Ancient Egyptians revered it for its potential to enhance physical strength, while in medieval Europe, it was believed to ward off evil spirits and infectious diseases.





The following illustration lists the most well known historical uses of garlic.



![Allium sativum History](/images/allium-sativum-history-general.jpg "Allium sativum History")

In the realm of divination, garlic found its place among ancient practices. The Greeks, Romans, and Egyptians utilized garlic bulbs as a form of protection against malevolent spirits and as a means to discern the future. The practice of scrying, or divination through reflective surfaces, often involved placing garlic cloves around the scrying area to ward off negative energies. The pungent aroma of garlic was believed to repel unwanted entities, allowing practitioners to focus on their divinatory pursuits without interference.





Legends and myths surround garlic, contributing to its mystique. In Slavic folklore, garlic was considered a powerful ward against vampires, with bulbs hung on windows and worn as protective charms. Ancient Greek mythology tells of garlic sprouting from the blood of the slain giant Tityos, imbuing it with both medicinal and magical properties. These tales echo the enduring cultural significance of garlic as a symbol of protection and resilience across diverse societies and time periods.
