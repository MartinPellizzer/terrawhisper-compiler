# Apium graveolens morphology

![Apium graveolens morphology](/assets/images/apium-graveolens-botany-morphology.jpg "Apium graveolens morphology")

Celery (Apium graveolens) has a fibrous root system with a single, central taproot and acute tips that efficiently penetrate the soil. The roots are approximately 0.2 cm in diameter and 20-30 cm long, light tan to brown, and have a smooth texture. Unlike some plants, celery does not form root nodules for nitrogen fixation. Sparse, fine root hairs aid in nutrient and water absorption.

The herbaceous stems are hollow, round, up to 60 cm tall, and green with smooth surfaces. They are segmented by nodes where leaves attach and have internodes of 2-5 cm.

Apium graveolens leaves are compound, pinnate, and alternately arranged, with a bright green color, serrated margins, and a smooth, slightly glossy texture. They lack stipules and are attached to the stem by petioles, which range from 10 to 30 cm.

Celery flowers are in compound umbels, each with five actinomorphic petals and an inferior ovary. They measure 2-3 mm, are white or greenish-white, and lack sepals. Each flower has five stamens and a bifid stigma, attached to a peduncle and pedicel.

The fruits are schizocarps that split into two mericarps upon maturity and are ovoid, measuring 1.5-2 cm. Initially green, they turn brown or gray, and the surface is smooth with slight ribbing. They split open upon maturation for seed dispersal.

Apium graveolens seeds are solitary, dicotyledonous, ovoid, with an impermeable, light to dark brown seed coat. The cotyledons are fleshy and straight, and the radicle is short.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Apium graveolens by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

## Roots



Apium graveolens has a root system that is classified as fibrous, characterized by a network of fine roots that spread out in various directions from the base of the plant. Unlike some other plants with taproot systems, celery primarily develops a single, central taproot. This taproot has limited lateral branching.

The tips of celery roots are acute, which means they come to a sharp point. This feature is particularly useful for penetrating the soil efficiently. The roots of celery are relatively dense, with an approximate density of 3.5 roots per square centimeter (or 18 roots per square inch).

In terms of size, celery roots typically have a diameter of about 0.2 centimeters (0.08 inches). The length of these roots generally falls within the range of 20 to 30 centimeters (8 to 12 inches). Their color can be described as light tan to brown, contributing to their overall appearance.

The surface texture of celery roots is smooth, which aids in their ability to move through the soil with minimal resistance. Unlike some other plant species, celery does not form root nodules, which are specialized structures for nitrogen fixation in some leguminous plants.

Root hairs on celery are relatively sparse and fine. These fine hairs help in nutrient and water absorption from the surrounding soil. They are essential for the overall health and growth of the plant.

## Stems



Apium graveolens stems are herbaceous, meaning they are non-woody and hollow. They have an alternate arrangement along the main stem, with each stem growing from a different point. In terms of shape, celery stems are typically round, with a diameter of about 0.5 cm (approximately 1/5 inch). 

The height of celery stems can reach up to 60 cm (approximately 24 inches), making them fairly tall. However, their diameter is relatively small, measuring between 0.2-0.5 cm (about 1/16 to 1/5 inch). 

In color, celery stems are uniformly green. Their texture is smooth to the touch, and the surface lacks any fine hairs or trichomes, making them glabrous. 

Stems of Apium graveolens are segmented by nodes, where leaves are attached. These nodes are an essential part of the plant's structure and play a role in the growth and development of celery. The internodes, which are the spaces between the nodes, typically measure 2-5 cm (approximately 0.8-2 inches) in length.

## Leaves



Apium graveolens leaves are classified as compound, with a pinnate structure, which means they are composed of multiple smaller leaflets. These leaflets have an oblong, lanceolate shape and are arranged alternately along the celery stem. The leaves have a horizontal orientation, spreading outwards from the central stem.

In terms of size, celery leaves typically measure between 5 to 15 cm in length (2 to 6 inches) and 2 to 5 cm in width (0.8 to 2 inches). They have a thickness of approximately 0.1 to 0.3 cm (0.04 to 0.12 inches). The color of celery leaves is a bright green, and they have a smooth, slightly glossy texture with a glabrous (hairless) surface.

The apex of celery leaves is described as acute, and the base is truncate. The leaf margins are serrated, giving them a somewhat toothed appearance. The venation in celery leaves is pinnate, resembling a feather-like pattern, and also exhibits a reticulate venation structure. The lamina of the leaf is bipinnate, which means it is double pinnate and somewhat palmate in shape.

One prominent feature of celery leaves is the central midrib that runs along the leaf's length. The leaves are petiolate, meaning they are attached to the stem via a stalk-like structure called the petiole, which can vary in length from 10 to 30 cm (4 to 12 inches).

Notably, celery leaves lack stipules, which are small leaf-like structures at the base of the petiole found in some plant species.

## Flowers



In terms of inflorescence type, Apium graveolens displays a compound umbel, where multiple flower stalks radiate from a common point, resembling an umbrella. The flowers themselves are simple in their overall complexity. Each flower is actinomorphic, or radially symmetrical, with five distinct lobes in the corolla and a short corolla tube.

Flowers of Apium graveolens have a hypogynous attachment, which means the floral parts are attached beneath the ovary. Their shape is defined as umbellate, resembling an umbrella-like cluster of flowers. Each individual flower measures approximately 2-3 millimeters in both length and width.

The color of Apium graveolens flowers typically ranges from white to greenish-white, and they have a smooth, glabrous texture. Notably, they lack sepals but possess five distinct, alternating petals, forming a perianth. Inside, there are five stamens and a pistil composed of two fused carpels.

The ovary of the flower is inferior, positioned below the attachment point of other floral parts. The style is short, and the stigma is bifid, split into two parts. Stipules are absent, and bracts are present at the base of the umbel. Additionally, each flower is attached to a peduncle, which is the main stalk of the umbel, and a pedicel, which is the stalk of an individual flower in the umbel.

## Fruits



The fruits of Apium graveolens, commonly known as celery, possess distinctive morphological characteristics. These traits provide essential information for botanists and researchers studying this plant species.

Apium graveolens has fruits that are schizocarp, meaning it splits into two mericarps upon maturity. This characteristic is crucial for the dispersal of its seeds. The arrangement of celery fruits is in umbels, where multiple fruits are clustered and attached to the central point of the umbel. The fruits are held by pedicels, often occurring in pairs, measuring approximately 1-3 centimeters (0.4-1.2 inches) in length.

In terms of shape, celery fruits are ovoid, typically measuring 1.5-2 centimeters (0.6-0.8 inches) in length, 1-1.5 centimeters (0.4-0.6 inches) in width, and about 1 centimeter (0.4 inches) in thickness. The color of these fruits varies, with an initial green hue when unripe, transitioning to brown or gray upon maturation. Celery fruits have a smooth texture with slight ribbing, and their surface is glabrous, which means they are hairless and lack any external fuzz or trichomes.

One notable characteristic of celery fruits is the absence of distinct markings. Additionally, these fruits exhibit dehiscence, which means they split open upon reaching maturity, aiding in seed dispersal.

## Seeds



The seeds of Apium graveolens are dicotyledonous, meaning they contain two cotyledons, which are fleshy and thick. The seeds are solitary, not found in clusters, and they are ovoid in shape, measuring approximately 2 millimeters in length, 1 millimeter in width, and 1 millimeter in thickness. Their hardness is notable, as they are not easily crushed.

In terms of appearance, celery seeds typically have a light to dark brown seed coat, which is smooth and lacks prominent ridges or furrows. The seed coat's texture is shiny and slightly wrinkled, with a thickness of about 0.1 millimeters. This thickness, combined with its impermeability, makes the seed coat water-resistant.

When examining the seed itself, a small scar known as the hilum can be observed at the base of the seed. The embryo within the seed is straight and minimally curved, while the two cotyledons are distinct and fleshy. The radicle, or the embryonic root, is short and emerges from the embryo.

These precise morphological characteristics of celery seeds play a vital role in its reproduction and growth. The seed's impermeable coat helps protect it from environmental factors, and the two cotyledons stored inside provide essential nutrients for the germinating plant. The straight embryo and short radicle are key elements in the early stages of seed germination.
