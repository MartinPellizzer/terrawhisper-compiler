# What to know before using celery (Apium graveolens)

![Celery Overview](/images/apium-graveolens-overview.jpg "Celery Overview")



This article gives an overview on the many uses of celery and what you need to know before using it.

## What are the medicinal uses of celery?

Celery has garnered attention in modern medicine due to its potential health benefits. Its extract has been utilized in traditional and alternative medicine for treating ailments like high blood pressure, inflammation, and digestive issues. Moreover, its antioxidant properties have been associated with reducing oxidative stress, thus potentially lowering the risk of chronic diseases like heart conditions.

The following illustration lists the most important uses of [celery in medicine](/apium-graveolens/medicine.html).

![Apium graveolens Medicine](/images/apium-graveolens-medicine.jpg "Apium graveolens Medicine")

The medicinal efficacy of celery primarily stems from its active compounds, such as apigenin, luteolin, and polyacetylenes. Apigenin and luteolin exhibit anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, aiding in the prevention of various diseases. Polyacetylenes have shown potential anti-cancer effects in laboratory studies, highlighting the plant's diverse biochemical composition.



Celery is prepared for medicinal use in various forms, including fresh juice, extracts, supplements, and essential oils. Celery seed extract, rich in compounds like apiol and myristicin, is commonly used for its diuretic properties, promoting urine production and potentially aiding in conditions like edema or high blood pressure. Additionally, the consumption of fresh celery juice is favored for its potential digestive benefits.



Improper usage of celery, especially in high doses or in sensitive individuals, may lead to adverse effects. Its high levels of certain compounds like psoralens can cause skin sensitivity, increasing the risk of sunburn or rashes when exposed to sunlight. Additionally, some people may experience allergic reactions or gastrointestinal disturbances upon excessive consumption.



Precautions should be taken when using celery for medicinal purposes. Individuals taking blood-thinning medications should consult a healthcare professional due to celery's potential anticoagulant effects. Moreover, pregnant women should avoid excessive consumption as it may stimulate uterine contractions. Those prone to allergies should be cautious and monitor any adverse reactions while using celery-based products for medicinal purposes.

## What are the culinary uses of celery?

Celery (Apium graveolens) is a versatile and widely used vegetable in culinary practices worldwide. Its culinary uses encompass various applications, including raw consumption in salads, as a flavorful addition to soups, stews, and stocks, and as a crucial component in many traditional dishes. The plant's stalks are commonly utilized, while the leaves, seeds, and roots are also occasionally incorporated into dishes. Approximately 95% of celery is water, making it low in calories, yet rich in essential vitamins such as vitamin K (29.3 µg per 100g), vitamin A (449 IU per 100g), and vitamin C (3.1 mg per 100g), contributing to its nutritional value.

The following illustration lists the most common uses of celery for culinary purposes.

![Apium graveolens Cuisine](/images/apium-graveolens-cuisine.jpg "Apium graveolens Cuisine")

Celery possesses a distinct flavor profile characterized by its crisp and crunchy texture, offering a refreshing and slightly bitter taste with earthy undertones. The stalks provide a mild and herbal flavor, while the leaves exhibit a more robust taste, often used as an herb for seasoning due to their concentrated flavor. The seeds of the celery plant are commonly used as a spice, imparting a warm, slightly bitter, and aromatic flavor to various dishes, especially in Mediterranean and Indian cuisines. This plant's unique taste adds depth and complexity to dishes, enhancing the overall flavor profile.



When utilizing celery in culinary preparations, it's essential to select firm, compact stalks with fresh, vibrant green leaves. To maintain its crispness and flavor, store celery in the refrigerator, ideally wrapped in aluminum foil, which helps preserve its freshness for up to two weeks. Before consumption, thoroughly wash the stalks to remove any dirt or pesticide residue, especially if consuming raw. To maximize its culinary use, incorporate celery into dishes like stir-fries, casseroles, or use it as a base for stocks and soups, enriching them with its distinct taste and nutritional benefits.

## How to cultivate celery in your garden?

Celery (Apium graveolens) is a versatile plant that thrives in gardens or landscapes when provided with the right conditions. To grow celery, start by sowing seeds indoors about 10-12 weeks before the last frost date. Once the seedlings have grown to about 8 centimeters tall, transplant them outdoors in well-prepared soil. Space the plants about 30 centimeters apart in rows that are approximately 45 centimeters apart. Adequate spacing allows for proper growth and development.

The following illustration lists the most important tips to cutlitvate celery.

![Apium graveolens Horticulture](/images/apium-graveolens-horticulture.jpg "Apium graveolens Horticulture")

For optimal growth, celery requires moist, fertile soil with good drainage. The ideal pH level for the soil should be between 6.0 and 7.0. Ensure the plants receive full sun but provide some shade during hot afternoons to prevent wilting. Consistent watering is crucial, aiming for about 2.5 centimeters of water per week. Mulching around the plants helps retain moisture and suppress weeds. Additionally, incorporating compost or organic matter into the soil before planting provides necessary nutrients for healthy growth.



Pruning and maintenance play vital roles in ensuring healthy celery plants. Regularly remove yellowing or damaged leaves to encourage airflow and prevent disease. Keep the soil consistently moist, as celery is sensitive to drought. Fertilize the plants every three weeks with a balanced fertilizer to support steady growth. Blanching the stalks by gently tying them with paper or cardboard helps to achieve a milder flavor. Regular monitoring for pests like aphids or celery leaf miners is essential, controlling them with organic pesticides if necessary to maintain plant health.

## What is the botanical profile of celery?

Celery, scientifically known as Apium graveolens, belongs to the Plantae domain, Magnoliophyta kingdom, Magnoliopsida class, Apiales order, Apiaceae family, Apium genus, and graveolens species.

The following illustration show the traditional taxonomy of celery.

![Apium graveolens Botany](/images/apium-graveolens-botany.jpg "Apium graveolens Botany")

Its common name, celery, encompasses various cultivars like Pascal celery, celeriac (Apium graveolens var. rapaceum), and leaf or Chinese celery (Apium graveolens var. secalinum). These variants differ in their growth habits and uses but share the same botanical classification.



Celery is a biennial plant characterized by a rosette of compound leaves with stalks forming a prominent edible petiole. Its roots are fibrous and fleshy, while the leaves are pinnate, displaying small, ovate leaflets. During its second year, celery produces flowers arranged in compound umbels, each containing small white or yellow blossoms. The plant's overall height can range from 30 to 100 centimeters, depending on the variety.



This plant is native to the Mediterranean region and prefers moist, temperate climates. It thrives in rich, well-drained soils and can be found growing in marshy areas, along stream banks, and in cultivated fields across Europe, Asia, and North America. Celery is often cultivated as a crop in various regions worldwide due to its adaptability to diverse growing conditions.

## What is the history and folklore of celery?

Celery (Apium graveolens) has a rich history in traditional folk medicine across diverse cultures. Ancient Egyptians used it for its purported medicinal properties, believing it to have aphrodisiac effects and to treat various ailments, such as arthritis and colds. In Chinese traditional medicine, celery was valued for its diuretic properties, using it to promote urination and cleanse the body of toxins. Greeks and Romans utilized celery to treat various conditions, including insomnia and anxiety, and considered it an offering to the dead due to its association with funeral rites.

The following illustration lists the most well known historical uses of celery.

![Apium graveolens History](/images/apium-graveolens-history.jpg "Apium graveolens History")

In ancient times, celery held significance in divination practices. Greeks associated it with victory and used it in competitions, crowning the winners with celery leaves. Romans believed it had prophetic powers and used it in rituals to seek guidance from the gods. Celery was utilized in divination rituals where individuals would observe the behavior of the plant, such as the direction of leaf growth or the patterns formed by its leaves, to interpret omens and make predictions about the future.



Legends and myths surround celery, adding to its mystique. According to Greek mythology, celery was linked to Hercules, who was said to have used it to regain strength after his twelve labors. Another legend tells the story of selinon, the Greek word for celery, originating from the tears shed by Ajax, a heroic figure from the Trojan War. The plant was believed to have sprung from his blood, symbolizing grief and remembrance. These tales contributed to celery's symbolism in ancient cultures, intertwining it with stories of strength, heroism, and sorrow.
