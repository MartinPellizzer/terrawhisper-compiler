## Roots

The root system of Acorus calamus is fibrous, characterized by numerous thin roots that spread extensively in the soil. These roots are adventitious, originating from the plant's base rather than from a primary taproot. They predominantly exhibit dichotomous branching, where each root repeatedly divides into two equal branches, facilitating efficient nutrient and water uptake.

The root tips are acute, helping the plant penetrate the soil effectively. In terms of density, Acorus calamus typically has approximately 1,500 roots per square meter, indicating a substantial root presence in its habitat.

Root depth for Acorus calamus ranges from 30 to 60 centimeters (12 to 24 inches) below the soil surface. The roots themselves have a diameter of 2 to 4 millimeters (0.08 to 0.16 inches) and can reach lengths of up to 60 centimeters (24 inches). The color of these roots is typically creamy-white to pale brown, and they have a smooth texture.

Notably, Acorus calamus lacks root hairs and lenticels on its roots' surface. There are no root nodules present, and root modifications are non-specialized. In terms of adaptations, Acorus calamus exhibits hydrophytic traits, well-suited for its habitat in wetlands.

Roots of Acorus calamus undergo seasonal senescence, especially during colder months, to conserve energy and resources. These roots serve essential functions, primarily anchoring the plant in the soil, and efficiently absorbing nutrients and water.

Furthermore, the roots release allelopathic compounds, such as β-asarone, which can impact neighboring plants and microorganisms in their vicinity. In terms of symbiotic relationships, Acorus calamus has limited mycorrhizal associations, indicating a relatively independent nutrient acquisition strategy.

While these roots do not form nitrogen-fixing nodules, they may engage in mycorrhizal associations, enhancing their capacity to absorb nutrients from the soil.

## Stems

The stems of Acorus calamus are herbaceous in nature, which means they are non-woody and relatively soft. They are arranged in a whorled pattern, with multiple stems emerging from the same point in a circular fashion. These stems are cylindrical, giving them a round, uniform shape. 

In terms of height, Acorus calamus stems can reach up to 1.5 meters (4.9 feet), making them quite tall in comparison to the plant's overall size. Their diameter is approximately 1.5 cm (0.6 inches), and they have nodes present at regular intervals along their length. The internodes, the sections of stem between nodes, measure about 15-20 cm (5.9-7.9 inches) apart. This results in a smooth-textured, green-colored stem, which gives Acorus calamus its characteristic appearance.

While the stems of Acorus calamus are fascinating, the rhizomes, which grow underground, have their own set of unique characteristics. These rhizomes are horizontally arranged, running parallel to the ground. 

Their shape varies, ranging from tuberous to cylindrical, and they typically have a height of 10-30 cm (3.9-11.8 inches). Rhizomes have a diameter of about 1-2 cm (0.4-0.8 inches), and their nodes are sporadically present, meaning they aren't as consistently spaced as the stem nodes. The internodes of the rhizomes can vary in length, typically measuring 1-5 cm (0.4-2 inches). They have a fleshy texture with a somewhat waxy surface and are often light to dark brown in color.

## Leaves

The leaves of Acorus calamus are simple in structure, linear, and lanceolate in shape, with a basal arrangement, meaning they emerge from the base of the plant. When fully grown, they stand erect and upright, reaching a length of 30 to 80 cm (11.8 to 31.5 inches) and a width of 1.5 to 2.5 cm (0.6 to 1 inch). Their thickness measures approximately 2 to 4 mm (0.08 to 0.16 inches).

Acorus calamus leaves are typically green and possess a waxy sheen, contributing to their smooth and glossy texture. The leaf surface is glabrous, meaning it is devoid of any hair-like structures. These leaves have an acute to acuminate apex and are sheathing at the stem base, which is a distinctive characteristic.

The margin of Acorus calamus leaves is typically entire and may have a slightly wavy appearance. Their venation is parallel, and the lamina is simple and undivided. A prominent midrib runs through the length of each leaf. These leaves attach directly to the stem without petioles and lack stipules.

One notable feature of Acorus calamus leaves is their persistence, as they remain evergreen throughout the plant's life cycle.

## Flowers

The flowers of Acorus calamus are hermaphrodite, meaning they possess both male and female reproductive structures. They exhibit simplicity in their structure, composed of three sepals and three petals. These six floral parts collectively form the perianth, the protective layer surrounding the reproductive organs.

The flowers are radially symmetrical, meaning they can be divided into equal halves in any direction, resembling a tube with a length ranging from 3 to 8 centimeters and a width of approximately 0.5 centimeters. The thickness of the flower measures about 0.2 centimeters.

In terms of color, Acorus calamus flowers are pale yellow-green, giving them a distinct appearance. These flowers are unisexual, with separate male and female individuals on the same plant. Male flowers have six stamens with yellow anthers, while female flowers feature a single pistil.

The ovary is positioned above the sepals and petals, which is referred to as a superior ovary. It consists of three carpels. The style, a slender structure about 1 centimeter long, leads to a capitate stigma in female flowers.

Notably, Acorus calamus flowers are enclosed by green bracts, a feature that distinguishes them. However, they lack stipules. Interestingly, these flowers do not have a peduncle and are borne directly on the stem.

## Fruits

Acorus calamus produces berries as its fruit type. These berries are small, oblong in shape, and reach a length of approximately 1.5 cm (0.59 inches), with a width of about 0.3 cm (0.12 inches) and a thickness of roughly 0.2 cm (0.08 inches). The fruit is fleshy, and it undergoes a color transformation from green to brownish-green as it matures.

The fruit's surface is smooth and shiny, giving it an attractive appearance. In terms of texture, the fruit is soft and fleshy to the touch. One distinctive characteristic of these fruits is the absence of any discernible markings or patterns.

The arrangement of these fruits is clustered, meaning they grow in groups close to one another on the plant. They are distributed throughout the flowering stem, indicating that they are not limited to a specific region of the plant.

The fruits of Acorus calamus are attached to a central axis, which is a common feature in many clustered fruit arrangements. Their pedicels, or the small stalks that attach the fruits to the stem, are short, measuring less than 1 cm (0.39 inches) in length.

## Seeds

The seeds of Acorus calamus fall into the category of endospermic, indicating the presence of endosperm within.

In terms of arrangement, Acorus calamus seeds are typically solitary, occurring individually in the fruit structure. Their shape is elliptical or oblong, with a length of approximately 2.5 millimeters (0.098 inches), a width of about 1.0 millimeter (0.039 inches), and a thickness of 1.0 millimeter (0.039 inches). These seeds exhibit a hard texture, making them durable and resistant to mechanical damage.

The seeds' outer coat is brown in color, characterized by a smooth texture and a relatively thin thickness. This outer coat is impermeable to water, which helps protect the inner seed contents. The seed's hilum, or the scar where it was attached to the fruit, is linear and positioned along the length of the seed.

Within the seed, there is a straight embryo that is centrally located within the endosperm. The presence of endosperm, the protective seed coat, and the central embryo are essential characteristics for the successful development and germination of Acorus calamus seeds.
