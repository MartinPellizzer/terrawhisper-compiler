# Azadirachta indica morphology

![Azadirachta indica morphology](/images/azadirachta-indica-botany-morphology.jpg "Azadirachta indica morphology")

The Azadirachta indica, or neem tree, has a root system that is of type taproot, with a deep main root aiding in stability and water absorption. Its fibrous lateral roots form a web-like structure, anchoring the tree and maximizing nutrient uptake. The stems are woody, oppositely arranged, and can grow up to 15 meters, with rough, fissured bark. Neem leaves are lanceolate, dark green, and thin, featuring serrated margins and pinnate venation.

Neem flowers form panicles with small, pale yellow, unisexual blossoms that exhibit radial symmetry. They have five sepals and five petals, with ten stamens and a single ovary. The flowers lack stipules and have short peduncles and pedicels. The fruits are drupes, ranging from green to yellow, with a smooth, fleshy exterior marked by longitudinal ridges. These dehiscent fruits split open to disperse the seeds.

Neem seeds are dicotyledonous, hard, oval, and woody, with a thin, impermeable, brown seed coat. They contain a well-developed embryo with two fleshy cotyledons and a single rudimentary radicle. The neem tree's distinct characteristics in its various parts contribute to its unique appearance and reproductive strategies.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Azadirachta indica by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

## Roots



The Azadirachta indica root system type is taproot, which means it has one main root extending deep into the ground and is pivotal for plant stability. This deep-reaching root system contributes to the plant's ability to access water and nutrients in the soil.

The root structure of Azadirachta indica is fibrous, consisting of many fine lateral roots branching off the primary taproot. These roots divide dichotomously, forming a web-like structure that spreads through the soil. This fibrous root system helps anchor the tree and maximizes its capacity to absorb water and nutrients from a wide soil area.

The root tips of Azadirachta indica are sharp and pointed, which aids in their penetration of the soil. The root density is relatively high, with approximately 450 roots per cubic centimeter (or 2,900 roots per cubic inch), contributing to the plant's efficient nutrient uptake.

In terms of root depth, the primary taproot can extend up to 2 meters (approximately 6.56 feet) into the ground, providing stability and access to deeper soil resources. The average root diameter is about 0.5 centimeters (0.2 inches), while root length can reach up to 3 meters (about 9.84 feet).

The color of Azadirachta indica roots varies from light brown to dark brown. The root surface texture is generally smooth, with fine lateral roots extending from the main branches. Root nodules are typically absent in this species, and root hairs are sparse, fine, and unbranched.

## Stems



The stems of Azadirachta indica are woody, covered in bark, and are arranged oppositely, meaning that two stems emerge from each node along the branches. They have a typical cylindrical shape and can reach a considerable height of up to 15 meters (approximately 49.2 feet). In terms of diameter, these stems typically measure around 30 cm (about 11.8 inches).

The color of the neem tree's stem is primarily grayish-brown to dark brown, giving it a characteristic appearance. The texture of the stem is notably rough, owing to its fissured bark, which provides protection and support. The surface of the stem is furrowed and scaly, adding to the tree's distinctiveness.

Nodes are a feature of the neem tree's stems, with typically two nodes per stem segment. These nodes play a role in leaf and branch development. Meanwhile, the internodes, the segments between the nodes, exhibit variable lengths, contributing to the overall height and growth pattern of the tree.

## Leaves



The leaves of Azadirachta indica are simple, which means they consist of a single leaf blade without subdivisions. They have a lanceolate shape, typically measuring 15-40 cm (5.9-15.7 inches) in length and 3-8 cm (1.2-3.1 inches) in width. The leaves are relatively thin, contributing to their characteristic drooping or horizontal orientation.

The color of Neem leaves is typically dark green, and their texture is smooth and waxy to the touch. They have a glabrous (hairless) surface, making them stand out from many other plant species. The leaf apex tapers to a point, a feature known as acuminate, and the base is uneven and rounded. The leaf margin is serrate, with tooth-like indentations along the edges.

One distinctive aspect of Neem leaves is their pinnate venation, where a single prominent midrib runs along the center of the leaf, giving rise to smaller veins branching off from it. The leaf lamina is pinnately compound, composed of 6-9 leaflets arranged along the midrib. These leaflets are attached to the main stem by petioles, which are relatively short, measuring 1-3 cm (0.4-1.2 inches) in length.

## Flowers



In terms of inflorescence type, Azadirachta indica produces panicles, which are branched clusters of small flowers. These flowers are relatively simple, characterized by their unisexual nature. They exhibit actinomorphic symmetry, meaning they have a radial, symmetrical shape.

The structure of the neem tree's flowers is considered complete, consisting of all essential parts for reproduction. The flowers are attached axillary, which means they grow in the leaf axils of the tree. They have a regular shape with a pentamerous arrangement, typically featuring five sepals and five petals.

These flowers are relatively small, with a length ranging from 0.6 to 0.8 cm (0.24 to 0.31 inches) and a width of 0.5 to 0.7 cm (0.20 to 0.28 inches). They are pale yellow in color, and their texture is smooth, with a glabrous (hairless) surface.

The neem tree's flowers consist of five floral parts, including five polysepalous sepals and five polypetalous petals. The perianth, which encompasses both sepals and petals, is united at the base.

Reproductive structures include ten diplostemonous stamens and a single monocarpellary ovary. The style is short, measuring 1-2 mm (0.04-0.08 inches) in length, and the stigma is two-lobed. Notably, neem flowers lack stipules, which are small leaf-like structures often found at the base of the petiole in many other plants.

The bracts of Azadirachta indica's flowers are small and inconspicuous, and the peduncle (the main stalk of the inflorescence) and pedicel (the stalk of an individual flower) are relatively short, further defining the flower's appearance.

## Fruits



These fruits of Azadirachta indica are classified as drupes, a type of fruit with a hard, stony seed enclosed by a fleshy outer layer. They can be found either singly or in clusters throughout the tree, usually attached to the tree via a pedicel measuring 0.5 to 2 centimeters (0.2 to 0.8 inches) in length.

The neem fruit exhibits an ellipsoid shape, with measurements of approximately 1.5 to 3 centimeters (0.6 to 1.2 inches) in length, 1 to 1.5 centimeters (0.4 to 0.6 inches) in width, and 0.6 to 1 centimeter (0.2 to 0.4 inches) in thickness. The color of the fruit varies during its maturation process. When young, neem fruits are green, but they turn yellow when they ripen.

In terms of texture, these fruits are characterized by a smooth and fleshy exterior, giving them a glossy appearance. One distinctive feature of neem fruits is the presence of longitudinal ridges or markings on their surface, which adds to their unique visual identity.

Furthermore, neem fruits are dehiscent, meaning they split open when they reach full ripeness. This characteristic facilitates the dispersal of seeds and is a significant feature for the tree's reproductive cycle.

## Seeds



These seeds of Azadirachta indica are dicotyledonous, typically found either singly or in clusters. They have an oval shape, measuring approximately 1.5 cm in length and 1 cm in width, with a thickness of about 0.5 cm. 

One notable feature of these seeds is their hardness; they are woody in texture. The seed coat is uniformly brown, exhibiting a smooth and glossy surface. This coat is thin and impermeable, making it resistant to moisture penetration.

The hilum, which is the attachment point of the seed to the fruit, is small and often inconspicuous. Within the seed, a large, well-developed embryo is present, characterized by two fleshy cotyledons. Additionally, the seed contains a single, rudimentary radicle.
