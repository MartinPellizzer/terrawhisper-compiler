# Cinchona officinalis morphology

![Cinchona officinalis morphology](/images/cinchona-officinalis-botany-morphology.jpg "Cinchona officinalis morphology")

Cinchona officinalis, a plant with fibrous root system, features dichotomous branching in its fine roots for efficient water and nutrient absorption. The root structure is adventitious with a taproot anchoring the plant, typically 30-40 cm (11.8-15.7 inches) deep. Root tips are acute, and the roots have a diameter of 2-4 mm (0.08-0.16 inches) and can grow up to 50 cm (19.7 inches) in length, brown in color, with a smooth surface.

There are no root nodules, but abundant fine root hairs increase water and nutrient absorption. The root density is about 1.2 roots per cubic centimeter (0.74 roots per cubic inch).

Cinchona officinalis stems are herbaceous, opposite, cylindrical, with a height of 1.5 meters (4.92 feet) and a diameter of 5 cm (1.97 inches). They are primarily green with brown spots, have a smooth texture, and are hairless. Stem nodes and internodes are well-defined, approximately 10 cm (3.94 inches) apart.

The leaves are simple, elliptical, horizontally oriented, measuring 10 cm (3.94 inches) in length, 4 cm (1.57 inches) in width, and 0.2 cm (0.08 inches) in thickness. They are dark green, smooth, and glossy, with an acute apex and cuneate base, smooth margin, and pinnate venation. The lamina is broad and flat, with a prominent midrib, stipules, and a 2 cm (0.79 inches) petiole.

Cinchona officinalis flowers are racemose, zygomorphic, tubular, 2.5 cm (0.98 inches) long, and 1.5 cm (0.59 inches) wide, with pink or white colors. They have a smooth, hairless surface, five tepals, five stamens adnate to the corolla, a solitary pistil with an inferior ovary, long and slender style, and a capitate stigma. Stipules are absent, and pedicels are short, about 0.5 cm (0.2 inches).

The ovate fruits are 2.5 cm long, 1 cm wide, and 0.5 cm thick, turning brown when mature, with a smooth, hairless surface and dehiscence. The seeds are elliptical, 1.5 cm long, 0.5 cm wide, and 0.3 cm thick, with a hard, brown seed coat, a thin impermeable layer (0.1 mm), a hilum scar, straight embryo, two cotyledons, and a short radicle (0.2 cm or 0.08 inches). Seeds lack wings or appendages.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Cinchona officinalis by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

## Roots



The root system of Cinchona officinalis is fibrous in nature, comprised of numerous fine roots. These roots exhibit dichotomous branching, where a single root divides into two equal branches, enhancing the plant's ability to efficiently absorb water and nutrients from the soil.

The root structure is adventitious, with the presence of a taproot. This taproot serves as the primary anchor for the plant and aids in deep soil penetration. The taproot typically reaches a depth of 30-40 cm (11.8-15.7 inches), which is essential for the plant's stability and access to water resources.

Root tips of Cinchona officinalis are acute, allowing them to penetrate the soil effectively. The roots have a diameter ranging from 2-4 mm (0.08-0.16 inches) and can grow up to 50 cm (19.7 inches) in length. Their brown color and smooth surface texture contribute to their overall appearance.

One notable characteristic is the absence of root nodules, which are commonly found in some other plant species. Instead, Cinchona officinalis features fine and abundant root hairs. These root hairs play a pivotal role in increasing the surface area for water and nutrient absorption, enabling the plant to thrive in its natural habitat.

Additionally, the root density of Cinchona officinalis is approximately 1.2 roots per cubic centimeter (0.74 roots per cubic inch), indicating a high degree of root presence in the soil.

## Stems



Cinchona officinalis has herbaceous stems that are opposite in arrangement. The stems are generally cylindrical in shape, with a height of approximately 1.5 meters (4.92 feet). The stem diameter measures about 5 centimeters (1.97 inches).

In terms of color, the stems are primarily green with brown spots, giving them a distinctive appearance. They have a smooth texture with fine ridges and a glabrous (hairless) surface, adding to their unique physical traits.

Stem nodes on Cinchona officinalis are clearly defined at the points where leaves attach to the stem. This characteristic is essential for botanists and researchers when identifying the plant. The internodes, which are the segments between nodes, are approximately 10 centimeters (3.94 inches) apart.

## Leaves



The leaves of Cinchona officinalis are relatively simple, with an elliptical shape. They are arranged oppositely on the stem, and their orientation is typically horizontal. The average leaf length measures approximately 10 cm (3.94 inches), while the width is about 4 cm (1.57 inches), making them relatively broad. The thickness of the leaves is relatively thin, with a measurement of 0.2 cm (0.08 inches).

In terms of color, the leaves of Cinchona officinalis are dark green, giving the plant a characteristic appearance. The leaf surface is smooth and glossy, and the leaves themselves are glabrous, meaning they lack hair or trichomes.

Leaf morphology includes an acute apex, which means the tip of the leaf is pointed. The base of the leaf is cuneate, giving it a wedge-shaped appearance. The leaf margin is entirely smooth and continuous. These leaves exhibit pinnate venation, characterized by a single prominent central vein with smaller veins branching outwards.

The lamina of the leaves is broad and flat, providing ample surface area for various plant processes. The midrib, or central vein, is notably prominent within the leaf structure. Additionally, the leaves are stipulate, meaning they are attached with small, membranous stipules. The petiole, or leaf stalk, has a length of approximately 2 cm (0.79 inches).

## Flowers



The inflorescence type of Cinchona officinalis is racemose, which means its flowers are arranged along a central stem. The flowers possess a simple structure, with a complete flower that includes all essential floral parts. They are typically attached in the axillary position, emerging from the leaf axils.

The flowers of Cinchona officinalis are characterized by their zygomorphic symmetry, meaning they can be divided into two equal halves along one plane. They have a tubular shape, with a length of approximately 2.5 cm (0.98 inches) and a width of around 1.5 cm (0.59 inches).

The color of these flowers can be pink or white, giving them an elegant and pleasing appearance. Their texture is smooth, and the surface is glabrous, meaning they are hairless.

Each Cinchona officinalis flower consists of five floral parts, including sepals and petals. In this species, it's challenging to distinguish between sepals and petals, so they are collectively referred to as tepals. There are also five stamens adnate to the corolla. The pistil is solitary, and it has an inferior ovary, which means the ovary is situated below the attachment of the other floral parts.

The style of the pistil is long and slender, with a capitate stigma. Notably, Cinchona officinalis lacks stipules and has inconspicuous green bracts. The length of the peduncle varies, and the pedicel is short, measuring about 0.5 cm (0.2 inches).

## Fruits



The fruits of Cinchona officinalis are arranged either singly or in clusters, and they are distributed throughout the plant, mainly attached at the base.

In terms of shape, these fruits exhibit an ovate form, with precise measurements of 2.5 cm in length, 1 cm in width, and 0.5 cm in thickness. Initially green, the fruits gradually turn brown as they mature. Their surface is smooth and hairless, described as glabrous, with no distinct markings.

One notable feature of these fruits is their dehiscence, which means they split open when mature to release their seeds. This allows for the dispersal of seeds, contributing to the plant's reproductive cycle.

## Seeds



The seeds of Cinchona officinalis are dehiscent, contained within capsules. They are arranged polyvalently, with multiple seeds found within a single fruit.

The seeds are elliptical in shape, measuring approximately 1.5 centimeters in length, 0.5 centimeters in width, and 0.3 centimeters in thickness. They have a hard texture and a brown seed coat. The seed coat is smooth and glossy, with a thin thickness of about 0.1 millimeters, making it impermeable to water.

A scar, known as the hilum, is present on the seed, indicating the point of attachment to the fruit. The embryo within the seed is straight, and each seed contains two cotyledons.

Notably, the radicle, the embryonic root, is relatively short, measuring around 0.2 centimeters in length. The absence of wings or other appendages distinguishes these seeds.
