# What to know before using dong quai (Angelica sinensis)

![Dong quai Overview](/images/angelica-sinensis-overview.jpg "Dong quai Overview")



This article gives an overview on the many uses of dong quai and what you need to know before using it.

## What are the medicinal uses of dong quai?

Dong Quai is widely used in modern medicine for various health purposes. Primarily recognized for its potential in women's health, it's utilized to regulate menstrual cycles, alleviate menopausal symptoms, and address conditions like dysmenorrhea. Research suggests its compounds exhibit estrogenic effects, aiding in hormonal balance. Studies indicate its effectiveness in treating gynecological disorders, with doses ranging from 1 to 6 grams of Dong Quai powder daily, demonstrating positive outcomes in managing menopausal symptoms and menstrual irregularities.

The following illustration lists the most important uses of [dong quai in medicine](/angelica-sinensis/medicine.html).

![Angelica sinensis Medicine](/images/angelica-sinensis-medicine.jpg "Angelica sinensis Medicine")

This plant contains various active compounds, including ferulic acid, ligustilide, and polysaccharides, contributing to its medicinal properties. Ligustilide, the primary bioactive component, exhibits anti-inflammatory and vasodilatory effects, while ferulic acid displays antioxidant properties, potentially reducing oxidative stress. Polysaccharides found in Dong Quai possess immune-enhancing characteristics, aiding in overall health and potentially supporting the immune system.



Dong Quai is available in diverse medicinal preparations, such as teas, capsules, tinctures, and creams. Dong Quai tea, made from dried roots, is used for menstrual regulation, while tinctures, often in alcohol solutions, offer concentrated doses for hormonal balance. Capsules containing Dong Quai powder provide a convenient dosage form for reproductive health. Creams and oils, applied topically, aim to alleviate pain and reduce inflammation, benefiting skin and muscular issues. Additionally, Dong Quai extracts, pills, syrups, and poultices are tailored for various health concerns, offering versatility in consumption and application methods.



While Dong Quai is generally considered safe, precautions are essential. Excessive doses may lead to side effects such as nausea, diarrhea, or skin sensitivity. Pregnant women and individuals with bleeding disorders should avoid its use. Interactions with blood-thinning medications are also noted. It's crucial to consult a healthcare professional before using Dong Quai, especially in combination with other medications or supplements, to prevent adverse reactions and ensure safe usage.

## What are the culinary uses of dong quai?

Dong Quai is primarily valued for its medicinal properties rather than its culinary uses. While not a commonly consumed ingredient, in Chinese cuisine, Dong Quai leaves are occasionally used in salads, soups, and stir-fries. However, the root is the most utilized part of the plant in traditional medicine and herbal remedies due to its therapeutic benefits.

The following illustration lists the most common uses of dong quai for culinary purposes.

![Angelica sinensis Cuisine](/images/angelica-sinensis-cuisine.jpg "Angelica sinensis Cuisine")

In terms of flavor, Dong Quai root possesses a distinctive and pungent aroma with slightly bitter and earthy undertones. It is often described as having a strong, musky, and slightly sweet flavor profile. This flavor profile makes it an acquired taste, and it is often combined with other herbs or ingredients to balance its strong taste when used in culinary applications.



When incorporating Dong Quai in cooking, it's essential to note that the root's potency and strong flavor may overpower other ingredients. Hence, it's advisable to use it sparingly and pair it with complementary flavors to balance its intensity. Additionally, blanching or soaking the root before use can help mitigate its bitter notes. While Dong Quai is not widely regarded for its culinary appeal, its strong flavor and potential medicinal benefits make it an occasional inclusion in certain dishes in Chinese cuisine.

## How to cultivate dong quai in your garden?

Dong Quai (Angelica sinensis) can be cultivated in gardens or landscapes with specific considerations. It is a biennial or perennial plant that thrives in well-drained, moist soil with partial shade. When planting, ensure the seeds are sown at a depth of about 0.5 to 1 centimeter in the soil. The spacing between seeds should be approximately 30 to 45 centimeters apart to allow ample room for growth. Regular watering is essential, especially during dry periods, to maintain soil moisture levels.

The following illustration lists the most important tips to cutlitvate dong quai.

![Angelica sinensis Horticulture](/images/angelica-sinensis-horticulture.jpg "Angelica sinensis Horticulture")

Ideal growing conditions for Dong Quai include a cool climate with temperatures ranging between 15 to 20 degrees Celsius. It flourishes in partial shade or dappled sunlight, requiring a location with good air circulation to prevent fungal issues. The soil should be rich in organic matter and have a pH level of around 6.5 to 7.5. Incorporating compost or well-rotted manure into the soil before planting aids in fertility and drainage. Mulching the soil around the plants helps retain moisture and suppresses weed growth, contributing to healthier growth of Dong Quai.



Pruning and maintenance are crucial for sustaining the health of Dong Quai plants. Deadheading spent flowers can encourage continuous growth and prevent self-seeding, focusing the plant's energy on root and foliage development. Regularly remove any diseased or damaged leaves to prevent the spread of infections. In autumn, when the plant starts to die back, cutting the stems close to the ground helps prepare for the next growing season. Additionally, applying a layer of organic mulch in late autumn safeguards the plant's roots from extreme temperatures during winter, ensuring a stronger resurgence in the following spring.

## What is the botanical profile of dong quai?

The traditional taxonomy of Dong Quai (Angelica sinensis) places it in the following classifications: domain Eukaryota, kingdom Plantae, phylum Angiosperms, class Eudicots, order Apiales, family Apiaceae, genus Angelica, species Angelica sinensis. This categorization helps to understand its evolutionary relationships and botanical characteristics within the plant kingdom.

The following illustration show the traditional taxonomy of dong quai.

![Angelica sinensis Botany](/images/angelica-sinensis-botany.jpg "Angelica sinensis Botany")

Dong Quai is commonly known by various names, including Chinese Angelica, Female Ginseng, and Dang Gui. Variants of Angelica sinensis might differ slightly in terms of geographic origin or subspecies but generally share similar characteristics and medicinal properties.



Angelica sinensis is a biennial or perennial plant characterized by its stout, hollow stems reaching heights of up to 1-2 meters. Its deeply lobed, serrated leaves form a rosette at the base of the plant, while the flowers, arranged in compound umbels, bloom in clusters, displaying small, numerous white or greenish-yellow flowers. The root, which is the most utilized part of the plant for medicinal purposes, is long, slender, and brown in color, emitting a distinctive aromatic odor.



This plant is indigenous to China, Japan, and Korea, predominantly found in cool, high-altitude mountainous regions and damp, forested areas. It thrives in rich, well-draining soil and partial shade, often growing along riverbanks or in areas with moist conditions. Angelica sinensis is adapted to a temperate climate and is cultivated in specific regions due to its environmental requirements for optimal growth.

## What is the history and folklore of dong quai?

In traditional folk medicine, Dong Quai (Angelica sinensis) holds a revered status across various cultures. Known as "female ginseng," it's a cornerstone in Chinese traditional medicine, primarily used for women's health. It's believed to regulate menstruation, alleviate menopausal symptoms, and promote blood circulation. Additionally, in Korean and Japanese traditional medicine, Dong Quai is utilized similarly for gynecological issues and as a general health tonic. Its usage extends to herbal remedies addressing anemia, arthritis, and improving overall vitality, with the dried root commonly employed in teas, tinctures, and powders.

The following illustration lists the most well known historical uses of dong quai.

![Angelica sinensis History](/images/angelica-sinensis-history.jpg "Angelica sinensis History")

Ancient cultures utilized Dong Quai not just for medicinal purposes but also in divination practices. In Chinese history, the root was used in rituals, burned for its aromatic properties, and its unique shape was believed to hold mystical powers. This plant's association with spirituality and the metaphysical led to its use in predicting future events, especially in Taoist rituals and spiritual practices.



Legends and myths surround Dong Quai in various cultures. Chinese folklore depicts Dong Quai as a symbol of female energy, often associated with the female reproductive system and fertility. Legends speak of its origins tied to angelic intervention, attributing its healing properties to celestial beings. Some tales also describe Dong Quai's discovery as a result of a dream guiding an individual to the plant, emphasizing its mystical origins and healing powers. Across cultures, Dong Quai is shrouded in myths that highlight its significance in promoting well-being and vitality.
