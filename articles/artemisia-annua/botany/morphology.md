# Artemisia annua morphology

![Artemisia annua morphology](/images/artemisia-annua-botany-morphology.jpg "Artemisia annua morphology")

Artemisia annua, commonly known as sweet wormwood, has fibrous root system with a dominant taproot, facilitating stability and nutrient absorption. Its branching pattern is dichotomous, with pointed roots and a density of about 2,000 roots per square meter. The roots extend 10-30 cm deep and have a diameter of 0.2-0.5 mm. They are pale beige to white and have sparse, unbranched root hairs.

The herbaceous stems are opposite in arrangement, reaching heights of 30-100 cm, with a green color and pubescent texture. Frequent nodes contribute to the plant's structure, and internodes measure 3-10 cm.

The lanceolate leaves are alternately arranged, erect, and grayish-green, with pubescent texture and entire margins. They have pinnate venation and are attached to the stem by petioles, lacking stipules.

Artemisia annua's yellow flowers are small and exhibit radial symmetry with five floral parts. The inflorescence is corymb, and the flowers lack sepals but have fused petals forming a tube. Each flower contains five stamens and one superior ovary, along with a two-lobed style and stigma.

The plant's fruits are small, dry achenes attached to the stem by pedicels. They are oblong-ellipsoid and light brown, with a smooth, glabrous surface and non-dehiscent nature, meaning they do not split open at maturity.

The seeds are elliptical and typically one per fruit, with a hard, impermeable seed coat. The seed coat is brown or yellowish-brown and has a thin, papery texture. The seed has a distinct scar called the hilum and contains a straight embryo with two cotyledons. The seeds lack appendages and have a smooth surface.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Artemisia annua by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

## Roots



The root system of Artemisia annua is classified as fibrous, featuring numerous thin roots that spread extensively. However, the main root structure is a taproot, characterized by a dominant central root. This taproot gives the plant stability and facilitates nutrient absorption.

The branching pattern of Artemisia annua's roots is dichotomous, where the roots repeatedly divide into two equal branches. These roots typically have pointed tips, allowing them to penetrate the soil effectively. The plant exhibits a root density of approximately 2,000 roots per square meter, ensuring efficient resource uptake from the soil.

Root depth for Artemisia annua typically ranges from 10 to 30 centimeters (4 to 12 inches), while root diameter measures between 0.2 to 0.5 millimeters (0.008 to 0.02 inches). Root length extends from 30 to 60 centimeters (12 to 24 inches), giving the plant the ability to access water and nutrients across various soil depths.

The color of Artemisia annua's roots is pale beige to white, and the root surface texture is smooth. Notably, this plant does not form root nodules, which are often associated with nitrogen-fixing plants. Instead, it relies on other mechanisms for nutrient acquisition.

Root hairs in Artemisia annua are sparse, fine, and unbranched. These root hairs serve to increase the root's surface area, aiding in the absorption of water and minerals. The unique combination of these root characteristics equips Artemisia annua to thrive in diverse environmental conditions, contributing to its resilience and adaptability.

## Stems



The stems of Artemisia annua are herbaceous (not woody), and they exhibit an opposite arrangement, meaning that leaves and stems occur in pairs along the main stem.

These stems have a round, cylindrical shape with a height ranging from 30 to 100 centimeters (approximately 11.8 to 39.4 inches). Their diameter falls within the range of 2 to 6 millimeters (about 0.08 to 0.24 inches), making them relatively thin.

In terms of color, the stems of Artemisia annua can vary, but they generally appear green. This green hue is found in different shades, which may depend on various factors such as environmental conditions and plant age.

One distinctive characteristic of the stem texture is that it is pubescent, meaning it is covered with fine, soft hairs, giving it a somewhat fuzzy or downy appearance. The stem surface is ridged, with longitudinal ridges running along its length, adding to its unique appearance.

Stems also feature frequent nodes, with nodes being regularly spaced along the stem's length. These nodes are important for leaf and branch attachment, contributing to the overall structure of the plant. Between these nodes, the internodes measure approximately 3 to 10 centimeters (about 1.2 to 3.9 inches) in length.

## Leaves



Artemisia annua has simple leaves, meaning they are not divided into smaller leaflets. The shape of these leaves is lanceolate, typically measuring 2-5 centimeters (0.8-2 inches) in length and 0.5-1 centimeter (0.2-0.4 inches) in width. These leaves are thin, with a thickness ranging from 0.1 to 0.3 millimeters (0.004-0.012 inches).

The leaves are arranged alternately along the stem, and their orientation is erect, meaning they stand upright. The color of Artemisia annua leaves is generally grayish-green, and the texture is described as pubescent, indicating the presence of fine, soft hairs on the leaf surface.

These leaves are dorsiventral, which means they have distinct upper and lower surfaces. At the apex (the tip), the leaves taper to a point, a characteristic known as acuminate. The base of the leaf is cuneate, resembling a wedge shape. The margin of the leaves is described as entire, meaning it is smooth and lacks any teeth or serrations.

The venation of the leaves is pinnate, with a single prominent midrib running along the center of the leaf and lateral veins branching out from it. The lamina of the leaf is simple, not dissected or divided into smaller units. The midrib is notably prominent, and the entire leaf is attached to the stem through a petiole, which typically measures 1-3 centimeters (0.4-1.2 inches) in length. Artemisia annua leaves lack stipules, which are leaf-like structures often found at the base of leaves in some plant species.

## Flowers



The flowers of Artemisia annua are organized in a compound corymb inflorescence, featuring simple and unbranched structures. In terms of symmetry, they exhibit radial symmetry, known as actinomorphic.

Each flower is relatively small, with a tubular or campanulate shape and measures approximately 1.5 mm in length and 1 mm in width. The dominant color of these flowers is yellow, and their texture is smooth with a glabrous surface.

Artemisia annua flowers have a pentamerous floral arrangement, meaning they consist of five floral parts. Notably, they lack true sepals, and their five petals are fused to form a tube, giving them the appearance of having ten petal-like structures, known as tepals. In addition, each flower contains five stamens and one superior ovary, along with a two-lobed style and a corresponding two-lobed stigma.

The flowers do not feature stipules, and their bracts are small and inconspicuous. Moreover, the flowers are sessile, meaning they lack stalks or pedicels. Instead, individual flowers are directly attached to the plant.

## Fruits

In terms of shape, they are oblong-ellipsoid, with dimensions measuring approximately 1.5-2.5 mm in length, 0.5-1 mm in width, and 0.3-0.5 mm in thickness. Their color is typically light brown, and their surface is smooth, appearing glabrous, devoid of any hair or trichomes.

These achenes do not exhibit distinct markings, presenting a uniform appearance. A notable characteristic of Artemisia annua's fruits is their non-dehiscent nature, meaning they do not split open at maturity. ## Seeds



The seeds of Artemisia annua are simple, with each fruit typically containing a single seed, although occasionally two may be found. They are small and elliptical, measuring about 2.5 mm in length, 1.5 mm in width, and 0.6 mm in thickness. 

The hardness of these seeds is notable, and they have a smooth texture. Their seed coat is typically brown or yellowish-brown, with a thin, papery texture and a thickness of approximately 0.1 mm. The seed coat is impermeable to water, making these seeds effectively waterproof.

At the attachment point of the seed, there is a distinct scar known as the hilum. Inside the seed, there is a straight embryo with two cotyledons. Cotyledons are the initial leaf-like structures that provide nutrients to the developing plant. 

In terms of appendages, Artemisia annua seeds do not possess any, and their surface is smooth, lacking ridges or projections. These characteristics contribute to the unique identity of these seeds.
