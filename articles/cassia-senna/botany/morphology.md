# Cassia senna morphology

![Cassia senna morphology](/images/cassia-senna-botany-morphology.jpg "Cassia senna morphology")

Cassia senna has a root system that is characterized by a dominant taproot, which enhances stability and deep soil penetration. Fibrous roots branch out from this taproot, facilitating efficient water and nutrient absorption. The roots have acute tips for easy soil penetration and a high density of approximately 350 roots per cubic centimeter, optimizing resource extraction. They extend to a depth of about 30 centimeters and have a diameter of 0.5 centimeters, striking a balance between stability and nutrient uptake. The light brown, smooth roots reduce soil adhesion.

The plant's stems are typically herbaceous, although woody variants exist, with cylindrical or angular shapes, reaching heights from 30 to 90 centimeters. The stems are green but can turn brown with smooth or slightly rough textures. Stem nodes and internodes play a crucial role in plant structure and spacing.

Cassia senna leaves are compound and pinnate, elliptical, with horizontal orientation, and petiolate. They measure 15-30 cm in length and 2-5 cm in width, with smooth, glabrous surfaces and entire margins. Leaf venation is pinnate, with prominent midribs.

The flowers of Cassia senna form racemes, exhibit bilateral symmetry, and have banner-shaped petals, primarily yellow with faint reddish veins. The stamens and pistil are distinct, and the ovary is superior. The fruits are legumes, stalked, and dehiscent, with dimensions of 7-20 cm. They are light to dark brown, smooth, and slightly curved.

Seeds are found within elongated pods, with elliptical or oblong shapes. The brown seed coat is shiny and impermeable, and each pod contains 5-10 seeds. Seeds are hard, with two cotyledons and a radicle for germination. These characteristics make Cassia senna a distinctive plant with various applications.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Cassia senna by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

## Roots



Cassia senna possesses a taproot system, with a single, dominant primary root. This primary root structure provides stability to the plant and allows it to penetrate deeper into the soil.

The roots of Cassia senna are predominantly fibrous, indicating a network of fine roots that branch out from the primary taproot. This fibrous root structure enhances the plant's ability to absorb water and nutrients from a wider soil area.

Root branching in Cassia senna is moderate, ensuring a balance between stability and resource acquisition. The acute tips of the roots facilitate easy penetration into the soil, further aiding in nutrient uptake.

Root density in Cassia senna is noteworthy, with approximately 350 roots per cubic centimeter (2,240 roots per cubic inch). This high root density is a significant advantage, as it allows for efficient soil exploration and resource extraction.

The root system of Cassia senna reaches a depth of about 30 centimeters (11.8 inches), enabling the plant to access moisture and nutrients from deeper soil layers.

Roots of this plant have a diameter of approximately 0.5 centimeters (0.2 inches), which provides an appropriate balance between stability and nutrient uptake capacity. The root length extends to around 60 centimeters (23.6 inches), ensuring effective soil exploration.

The color of Cassia senna roots is light brown, and their surface texture is smooth, which aids in reducing soil adhesion and facilitating easier penetration.

Notably, Cassia senna lacks root nodules, which are typically associated with nitrogen-fixing plants. However, sparse fine root hairs are present, enhancing the plant's ability to absorb water and nutrients from the soil.

## Stems



Cassia senna typically features herbaceous stems, although woody variants can also be found. These stems are arranged alternately along the plant, creating a consistent pattern of leaves. The shape of the stem is usually cylindrical, but angular stems are not uncommon. In terms of size, Cassia senna can reach a height ranging from 30 to 90 centimeters, with a diameter that falls between 0.5 and 1.5 centimeters.

The stem color is predominantly green, but it may turn brown as the plant matures. When you touch the stem, you'll notice that it has a smooth texture, although variations can lead to a rougher feel. The stem's surface can either be glabrous (smooth and hairless) or pubescent (having fine hairs).

Stem nodes, which are the points on the stem from which leaves and branches grow, are present in Cassia senna. These nodes play a crucial role in the plant's growth and development. The distance between these nodes, known as internodes, varies from 3 to 10 centimeters. These internodes contribute to the overall structure and spacing of the plant's foliage.

## Leaves



The leaves of Cassia senna have a compound, pinnate structure, meaning that they consist of multiple leaflets arranged in a feather-like pattern along a central stalk.

In terms of leaf shape, Cassia senna leaves are elliptical, exhibiting a horizontal orientation. They are typically 15-30 cm (6-12 inches) in length and 2-5 cm (0.8-2 inches) in width. The leaves are relatively thin, measuring about 0.2-0.5 mm (0.008-0.02 inches) in thickness. Their color is predominantly green, with a smooth and glabrous texture on the leaf surface.

The leaves of Cassia senna have an acute apex and a cuneate base, which means the tips are pointed, and the bases are wedge-shaped. The leaf margins are entire, without serrations or lobes. Venation in these leaves is pinnate, with prominent and raised midribs on the lower surface of the leaflets.

One notable feature is the presence of stipules, which are lanceolate and about 1 cm (0.4 inches) in length. Stipules are small, leaf-like structures found at the base of the petiole.

Speaking of petioles, the leaves of Cassia senna are petiolate, meaning they have stalks that attach them to the stem. The petioles are typically 2-6 cm (0.8-2.4 inches) long and are glabrous.

## Flowers



The inflorescence type of Cassia senna is a raceme, typically measuring between 10 to 25 centimeters in length. The flowers exhibit a simple complexity, consisting of five distinct floral parts. These blooms are notably zygomorphic, displaying bilateral symmetry.

The flower structure of Cassia senna is quite regular, and it features a total of five floral parts. These flowers are axillary, meaning they arise from the leaf axils. The shape of Cassia senna flowers is banner-shaped, with five unequal petals, where the banner petal is the largest. The individual flowers have a length of approximately 3 centimeters (or 1.18 inches) and a width of about 2 centimeters (or 0.79 inches).

In terms of color, Cassia senna flowers are primarily yellow, often with faint reddish veins. They exhibit a smooth texture and a glabrous (hairless) surface. The total number of floral parts is five, which includes five green, lanceolate sepals measuring around 1 centimeter (or 0.39 inches) each and five yellow petals.

The flower perianth, which consists of both sepals and petals, is present in Cassia senna. The stamens, numbering ten in total, have distinct filaments, with a length ranging from 3 to 4 centimeters (approximately 1.18 to 1.57 inches). The plant has a single elongated style and stigma, along with a superior ovary. The style itself measures around 3 centimeters (or 1.18 inches) and possesses a capitate, or rounded, stigma. Notably, Cassia senna lacks stipules, and its bracts are typically small and inconspicuous.

Both the peduncle (the main stalk) and pedicel (the individual flower stalk) are present, further characterizing the flower's attachment and structure. These morphological features collectively contribute to the distinctive appearance and classification of Cassia senna, which is not only botanically intriguing but also valuable for various purposes in traditional medicine and horticulture.

## Fruits



The fruit type of Cassia senna is a legume, often referred to as a pod. These fruits are typically arranged either singly or in clusters. They are distributed along terminal racemes, giving the plant a unique appearance.

The attachment of the fruits is stalked, with a length of 1-2 cm (0.39-0.79 inches). The fruits themselves exhibit specific dimensions. They are linear, oblong, flattened, and slightly curved, with lengths ranging from 7 to 20 cm (2.76-7.87 inches), widths of 0.5-1 cm (0.2-0.39 inches), and thicknesses of 0.3-0.4 cm (0.12-0.16 inches).

In terms of color, Cassia senna fruits are typically light to dark brown, making them easily distinguishable in the plant. They have a smooth and leathery texture, with a glabrous (hairless) surface. Some may exhibit transverse lines or mottling, adding to their distinct appearance.

One significant characteristic of these fruits is their mode of dehiscence. They are dehiscent, which means they split longitudinally into two valves to release their seeds. This dehiscence mechanism is an essential feature for seed dispersal and reproduction.

## Seeds



These seeds of Cassia senna are typically found within elongated pods, with each pod containing approximately 5 to 10 seeds. The seeds themselves have an elliptical or oblong shape, measuring about 4 to 6 millimeters in length, 2 to 3 millimeters in width, and 1 to 2 millimeters in thickness.

The outermost layer of these seeds, known as the seed coat, is brown in color, smooth to the touch, and possesses a shiny texture. The seed coat is relatively thin, measuring around 0.1 millimeters in thickness, and is impermeable. The hilum, a small scar on the seed's surface, marks the point of attachment to the pod.

The seeds of Cassia senna are quite hard and firm, with a significant level of hardness. Inside the seed, an embryo is present, complete with cotyledons. Typically, there are two cotyledons, which are fleshy structures within the seed that serve as the initial food source for the developing plant.

Moreover, the seed includes a radicle, which is part of the embryo and will develop into the plant's primary root upon germination.
