# Calendula officinalis morphology

![Calendula officinalis morphology](/images/calendula-officinalis-botany-morphology.jpg "Calendula officinalis morphology")

Calendula officinalis, commonly known as marigold, boasts a fibrous root system with numerous fine, adventitious roots. These roots exhibit a dichotomous branching pattern and reach a depth of 5-10 centimeters. Their diameter is thin, ranging from 0.2 to 0.5 centimeters, and they are creamy white with smooth tips. While they lack root nodules, they have fine root hairs.

The plant's herbaceous stems are non-woody, arranged oppositely, and grow erect. They measure 30-60 centimeters in height with a slender diameter, featuring a slightly pubescent, green surface that's mostly smooth. Clearly defined nodes and internodes provide structure.

Calendula officinalis leaves are lanceolate, arranged oppositely in a decussate pattern, and range from 5-12 cm in length. They lack stipules and petioles, attaching directly to the stem. Leaf color is predominantly green with a slightly hairy underside.

The flowers are arranged in a dense cluster called a capitulum, comprising ray and disk florets. Ray florets are strap-like, while disk florets are tubular. Flowers lack sepals, and the number of petals varies. They are typically orange or yellow, with smooth, glabrous surfaces. Each flower has one superior ovary, five fused stamens, and bifurcated styles.

Calendula officinalis produces achenes, which are small, dry, indehiscent fruits. These elongated achenes are light brown, smooth, and glabrous. Seeds within are dicotyledonous, featuring a straight embryo with two cotyledons.

Overall, Calendula officinalis is characterized by its fibrous roots, herbaceous stems, lanceolate leaves, complex flower structure, and distinctive achenes with hard, brown seeds.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Calendula officinalis by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

## Roots



The root system of Calendula officinalis is fibrous, consisting of numerous fine roots. These roots are adventitious and fasciculated, with a dichotomous branching pattern, which means they repeatedly divide into two nearly equal branches.

Calendula officinalis roots have smooth tips and a high root density, with approximately 700 roots per square centimeter. Their root depth is shallow, typically reaching a depth of 5-10 centimeters (2-4 inches), and they have thin root diameters, measuring between 0.2 to 0.5 centimeters (0.08-0.2 inches).

In terms of length, Calendula officinalis roots are of moderate size, typically ranging from 15 to 30 centimeters (6-12 inches). The color of these roots is creamy white, and their surface texture is smooth.

Notably, Calendula officinalis roots do not develop root nodules, which are often associated with nitrogen-fixing plants. However, they do have root hairs that are quite fine, measuring approximately 0.1 millimeter (0.004 inches) in diameter.

## Stems



The stems of Calendula officinalis are herbaceous, meaning they are non-woody in nature, making them flexible and generally not capable of supporting substantial weight. These stems are arranged oppositely, with leaves and branches emerging in pairs along the stem. In terms of shape, Calendula officinalis stems are erect and cylindrical, giving the plant an upright appearance.

In terms of size, the stem height typically ranges from 30 to 60 centimeters (12 to 24 inches), while the stem diameter falls between 0.3 and 0.6 centimeters (0.12 to 0.24 inches). This slender diameter is consistent with the herbaceous nature of the plant.

The stem color is typically green, with a slight pubescence, meaning there may be fine hairs on the surface. These hairs contribute to the stem's texture, which is smooth but not entirely hairless, giving it a slightly soft or velvety feel. The stem surface is primarily glabrous, meaning it's mostly smooth with a minimal presence of trichomes or hairs.

Calendula officinalis stems possess clearly defined nodes, which are the points on the stem where leaves, branches, or flowers are attached. These nodes are spaced along the stem at regular intervals. In contrast, the internodes, which are the segments of the stem between nodes, are approximately 5 to 15 centimeters (2 to 6 inches) apart, providing a sense of spacing and structure to the plant's overall form.

## Leaves



The leaves of Calendula officinalis are simple, meaning they are undivided. They have a lanceolate shape, typically measuring 5-12 cm (2-4.7 inches) in length and 1-4 cm (0.4-1.6 inches) in width. These leaves are rather thin, with a thickness ranging from 0.2-0.5 mm (0.008-0.02 inches).

In terms of arrangement, the leaves grow opposite each other on the stem. They are oriented alternately, forming a decussate pattern. The leaf margins are entire, exhibiting a smooth and unbroken edge.

Calendula officinalis leaves are usually green, occasionally featuring a slight grayish hue. Their texture is characterized by smoothness, with a slightly hairy or pubescent underside. This pubescence contributes to the leaves' texture. The upper surface of the leaves is typically glabrous or smooth.

The leaf apex is acute, meaning it has a pointed tip, while the base is cuneate, forming a wedge-like shape. Prominently running down the center of the leaf is the midrib. The leaves have pinnate venation, with a single main vein and smaller veins branching from it.

Notably, Calendula officinalis leaves lack stipules, and they are attached directly to the stem, making them sessile. These leaves also lack petioles, which are the stalks that typically attach leaves to the stem.

## Flowers



The inflorescence type of Calendula officinalis is a "capitulum," which is a dense cluster of individual florets. These florets are organized into two categories: ray florets and disk florets. Ray florets are ligulate (strap-like) and have a length of 1.5 to 4 cm (0.59 - 1.57 inches) and a width of 0.3 to 1 cm (0.12 - 0.39 inches). In contrast, disk florets are tubular with a length of 0.2 to 0.4 cm (0.08 - 0.16 inches) and a width of 0.1 to 0.2 cm (0.04 - 0.08 inches).

The flowers of Calendula officinalis exhibit radial or actinomorphic symmetry, meaning they can be divided into equal halves along multiple planes. The flower structure is composed of ray and disk florets, giving it a complex appearance.

These flowers lack sepals, and the number of petals varies between ray and disk florets. Ray florets have a single petal, while disk florets have five. The perianth of ray florets is ligulate, and that of disk florets is tubular.

In terms of reproductive structures, each flower possesses five stamens that are fused to the corolla in disk florets. The pistil consists of one superior ovary, a bifurcated style, and two linear stigmas. This arrangement is common in the Asteraceae family, to which Calendula officinalis belongs.

The flower attachment can be solitary or clustered, and the plant features leaf-like bracts. The absence of stipules is another notable characteristic.

The color of Calendula officinalis flowers is typically orange or yellow, though variations can occur. The texture is smooth, and the surface of the flowers is glabrous, meaning they are hairless.

## Fruits



The fruit type of Calendula officinalis is an achene, a small, dry, one-seeded structure. These achenes are arranged singly on the receptacle (disc) of the flower and are attached in an indehiscent manner, meaning they do not split open at maturity.

In terms of shape, Calendula officinalis achenes are elongated, measuring about 1.5 cm (0.59 inches) in length, with a width of approximately 0.2 cm (0.08 inches) and a thickness of around 0.1 cm (0.04 inches). Their color is typically light brown, and the surface of these achenes is smooth and glabrous, meaning they lack hairs.

Notably, there are no distinct markings on the fruit surface. Unlike some plants, Calendula officinalis achenes do not exhibit dehiscence; they remain indehiscent, which is an important botanical characteristic.

## Seeds



The seeds of Calendula officinalis belong to the angiosperms and specifically fall into the dicotyledonous category. They are found singly within the fruit, and their shape can be described as elongated ovals, measuring approximately 4.5 mm in length, 2 mm in width, and about 1 mm in thickness. The seeds are notably hard, making them resilient to various environmental conditions.

The seed coat of Calendula officinalis is brownish, with a smooth and shiny surface. The thickness of the seed coat is quite thin, approximately 0.1 mm. This thinness, combined with its impermeable nature, may contribute to the seed's ability to resist environmental stressors.

A distinct hilum, or scar, is present on the seed, marking the point of attachment to the plant during development. The embryo within the seed is straight, and the seed is dicotyledonous, meaning it contains two cotyledons. Additionally, the seed features a radicle, which is a crucial embryonic structure responsible for developing into the plant's primary root upon germination.
