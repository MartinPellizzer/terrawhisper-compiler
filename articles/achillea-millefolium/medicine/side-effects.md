# 10 Possible Health Side Effects of Yarrow (Achillea millefolium): Reasons, Dosages, and Precautions

![Yarrow's Health Side Effects](/images/achillea-millefolium-medicine-side-effects-overview.jpg "Yarrow's Health Side Effects")


Yarrow possesses various medicinal properties, but its use may trigger adverse effects if improperly administered. The plant can induce skin irritation due to compounds like sesquiterpene lactones, leading to redness or rashes upon contact. This reaction can worsen conditions like eczema and affect individuals with Asteraceae family plant allergies. To minimize this risk, cautious topical application and proper dilution in oils or teas, typically around 1-5%, are recommended.

Additionally, Yarrow might induce digestive discomfort, manifesting as nausea or diarrhea, mainly attributed to its volatile oils and bitter compounds. Excessive consumption could aggravate gastrointestinal issues like gastritis or peptic ulcers. To prevent such effects, maintaining moderate dosages, such as 1-4 grams of dried herb for teas, and gradual increases while monitoring symptoms are advised.

Furthermore, Yarrow could lead to allergic reactions due to sesquiterpene lactones, potentially causing severe skin issues or respiratory difficulties. This risk heightens for individuals allergic to Asteraceae plants or those with asthma or hay fever. Consulting healthcare professionals, starting with smaller doses, and careful monitoring can help minimize allergic responses.

These adverse effects underscore the importance of using Yarrow cautiously, adhering to recommended dosages, and seeking guidance to mitigate potential health complications. Properly preparing Yarrow, being mindful of individual sensitivities, and consulting healthcare experts remain crucial for safe usage.

This article lists the possible health side effects of yarrow and how to avoid them.

## 1. Can cause skin irritation

Yarrow's potential to cause skin irritation refers to its ability to induce redness, itching, or rash when applied topically or in some cases, when ingested. This adverse reaction could be attributed to constituents such as sesquiterpene lactones, specifically achillin and achillicin, found in the plant. These compounds possess known anti-inflammatory properties but can also trigger allergic reactions in some individuals, leading to skin irritation upon contact or application.

Yarrow's ability to cause skin irritation can aggravate many health conditions, like the ones shown in the illustration below.

![Yarrow Can Cause Skin Irritation](/images/achillea-millefolium-medicine-side-effects-can-cause-skin-irritation.jpg "Yarrow Can Cause Skin Irritation")


Skin irritation caused by Yarrow can worsen conditions such as eczema, dermatitis, or sensitive skin disorders. Individuals with existing allergies to plants in the Asteraceae family, including ragweed, daisies, or marigolds, may be more susceptible to yarrow-induced skin irritation. Moreover, people with hypersensitive skin or compromised skin barriers due to certain medical conditions might also experience exacerbated symptoms upon contact with Yarrow.

To minimize the risk of skin irritation when using Yarrow, it's crucial to employ proper preparations and dosages. Topical applications, such as ointments or creams containing Yarrow extracts, should be patch-tested on a small area of skin before broader use to check for any adverse reactions. Proper dilution ratios are essential; for instance, a typical recommendation might include diluting essential oil with carrier oils at concentrations of 1-5%. Furthermore, following recommended dosages in herbal teas or supplements can help avoid adverse skin reactions. Dosages of Yarrow preparations can vary, but a general guideline might suggest 2-4 grams of dried herb steeped in hot water for tea, up to three times daily. Always consult with a healthcare professional or herbalist for personalized guidance and to ensure safe use.

## 2. Might induce digestive discomfort

Yarrow might induce digestive discomfort when used improperly. This discomfort typically involves symptoms like nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, or gastrointestinal irritation. The constituents responsible for these effects include the plant's volatile oils, particularly thujone, which can irritate the digestive system when taken in excessive amounts. Additionally, the presence of bitter compounds such as sesquiterpene lactones and tannins might contribute to gastrointestinal upset if consumed in high doses.

Yarrow's ability to induce digestive discomfort can aggravate many health conditions, like the ones shown in the illustration below.

![Yarrow Might Induce Digestive Discomfort](/images/achillea-millefolium-medicine-side-effects-might-induce-digestive-discomfort.jpg "Yarrow Might Induce Digestive Discomfort")


The side effect of digestive discomfort induced by yarrow can aggravate existing gastrointestinal conditions such as gastritis, peptic ulcers, or inflammatory bowel diseases. Individuals who are particularly sensitive to yarrow's constituents or those with known allergies to plants in the Asteraceae family might experience heightened reactions. Moreover, pregnant women, nursing mothers, and children should be cautious with yarrow due to its potential effects on the digestive system. The potency of these effects can vary among individuals, but excessive consumption or prolonged use can heighten the risk of adverse reactions.

To avoid digestive discomfort when using yarrow, it's essential to follow proper preparation methods and dosage guidelines. Infusions made from dried yarrow flowers or leaves are commonly used. A recommended dosage for yarrow tea is about 1 to 4 grams of the dried herb steeped in hot water for 5 to 10 minutes, taken up to three times a day. Tinctures may also be consumed, with a typical dosage ranging from 2 to 4 milliliters, diluted in water, up to three times daily. It's crucial to start with the lowest effective dose and gradually increase if needed, monitoring for any signs of digestive irritation. Additionally, consulting a healthcare professional before using yarrow, especially in higher doses or for extended periods, is advisable to minimize the risk of digestive side effects.

Yarrow, when used improperly, could lead to allergic reactions, resulting in various symptoms ranging from mild to severe. This adverse effect primarily arises due to the presence of sesquiterpene lactones, notably achillin and lactones such as matricin and chamazulene, which are key constituents in Yarrow. These compounds are known to trigger allergic responses in sensitive individuals when consumed or applied topically, causing symptoms such as skin irritation, rashes, itching, swelling, and in severe cases, respiratory difficulties or anaphylaxis.

Yarrow's ability to lead to allergic reactions can aggravate many health conditions, like the ones shown in the illustration below.

To minimize the risk of allergic reactions when using Yarrow, proper preparation methods and cautious dosages are essential. ## 4. May interfere with blood clotting

Yarrow may interfere with blood clotting, meaning it possesses properties that can affect the coagulation process within the body. This interference occurs due to constituents present in Yarrow, particularly sesquiterpene lactones and coumarins. Sesquiterpene lactones can inhibit platelet aggregation and interfere with certain clotting factors, while coumarins possess anticoagulant properties, both contributing to the potential disruption of blood clotting mechanisms.

Yarrow's ability to interfere with blood clotting can aggravate many health conditions, like the ones shown in the illustration below.

![Yarrow May Interfere With Blood Clotting](/images/achillea-millefolium-medicine-side-effects-may-interfere-with-blood-clotting.jpg "Yarrow May Interfere With Blood Clotting")


The side effect of Yarrow interfering with blood clotting can potentially exacerbate health conditions such as hemophilia, where individuals already have impaired blood clotting mechanisms. Furthermore, those undergoing surgery or individuals on anticoagulant medication like warfarin should exercise caution when using Yarrow as it might heighten the risk of excessive bleeding. People sensitive to this effect include those with bleeding disorders, individuals on blood-thinning medications, or those preparing for surgery. For instance, the use of Yarrow in these scenarios might lead to prolonged bleeding, increased bruising, or complications during surgical procedures.

To avoid adverse effects related to blood clotting interference, it's crucial to use Yarrow cautiously and follow recommended dosages. The appropriate preparation involves standardized extracts or teas made from dried Yarrow flowers and leaves. A typical dosage for an adult ranges between 3 to 5 grams of dried herb infused in 150 ml of hot water, consumed up to three times daily. However, it is advisable to consult with a healthcare professional before using Yarrow, especially for individuals with pre-existing clotting disorders or those taking anticoagulant medications, as they might require specific adjustments or complete avoidance of Yarrow usage to prevent potential complications.

The constituents responsible for this effect in Yarrow are primarily the phototoxic compounds called sesquiterpene lactones, such as achilleine and achillicin. These components, when exposed to sunlight, can cause a reaction on the skin, resulting in photosensitivity.

Yarrow's ability to induce photosensitivity can aggravate many health conditions, like the ones shown in the illustration below.

Moreover, prolonged exposure to Yarrow preparations, particularly on the skin, can increase the risk of photosensitivity reactions.

To avoid the side effect of photosensitivity when using Yarrow, proper preparations and dosage are essential. A safe dosage typically involves using Yarrow extracts with concentrations of no more than 10% in topical preparations.
