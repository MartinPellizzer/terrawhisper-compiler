# yarrow (Achillea millefolium) Culinary Guide: Uses, Flavor Profile, and Tips

![Achillea millefolium Culinary Guide](/images/achillea-millefolium-cuisine.jpg "Achillea millefolium Culinary Guide")



## What are the culinary uses of yarrow?

Yarrow is a versatile culinary herb. Its leaves and flowers can be used in diverse ways, such as brewing herbal teas for digestion and relaxation, adding flavor to soups and stews, garnishing dishes with its edible flowers, seasoning meats such as lamb and pork, infusing vinegars and oils, creating herb-infused butter, incorporating it into baking recipes, and using it for pickling. This herb should be used in moderation, as its slightly bitter and aromatic flavor can be overpowering if not balanced properly. Whether in teas, salads, or as a unique pickle ingredient, yarrow adds a distinct and delightful herbal touch to your culinary creations.

The following illustration shows the most common culinary uses of yarrow.



![Achillea millefolium Culinary Uses](/images/achillea-millefolium-cuisine-uses.jpg "Achillea millefolium Culinary Uses")


One of the most common uses of yarrow is as the base for a soothing herbal tea. Dried yarrow leaves and flowers, measuring about 2-3 grams per cup of boiling water, produce a brew known for its mild bitterness and relaxation-inducing properties.

In the realm of savory dishes, yarrow steps in as a versatile flavor enhancer. Its leaves and young shoots, finely chopped and used in moderation (around a tablespoon per liter of soup or stew), can elevate the taste of these hearty classics.

Yarrow isn't limited to savory fare. In fact, it can add depth to your salads. Incorporating approximately 15-20 grams of fresh yarrow leaves in a salad introduces a subtle, yet distinct, bitter flavor, enriching your greens.

Moreover, yarrow isn't just about flavor. It's also about aesthetics. The herb's flowers make for an exquisite edible garnish, adding visual appeal and a mild herbal touch to your dishes. Just a handful (around 5-10 grams) of these delicate blooms can transform your plate into a work of art.

When it comes to meats, yarrow can be used as a seasoning. A mere teaspoon of dried yarrow rubbed onto a 500-gram piece of meat before roasting or grilling infuses it with a unique flavor.

Yarrow is also adaptable in the realm of condiments. By finely chopping yarrow leaves and flowers (approximately 20 grams) and blending them with 100 grams of softened butter, you can create a distinctive herb butter to elevate your dishes.

Diving deeper, yarrow can be used to infuse vinegar and oil. A combination of around 10-15 grams of dried yarrow per 250 ml of vinegar results in a flavorful infusion, while about 15-20 grams of dried yarrow mixed with 250 ml of cooking oil creates an herb-infused oil for various culinary applications.

For those who dabble in baking, adding approximately 5-10 grams of dried yarrow leaves and flowers to bread or pastry recipes imparts a subtle herbal essence.

Even the realm of pickling isn't off-limits to yarrow. Around 5-10 grams of dried yarrow per liter of pickling brine can lend a unique and memorable flavor to your preserved vegetables, such as cucumbers or beets.



The following list summarize the 10 most common culinary uses of Achillea millefolium.



- **Herbal Tea**:   Yarrow leaves and flowers can be dried and used to make a pleasant and mildly bitter herbal tea. It is often used to aid digestion and promote relaxation.
- **Flavoring Soups and Stews**:   Yarrow can be used as a flavoring herb in soups, stews, and broths, adding a subtle earthy and slightly peppery flavor.
- **Salad Ingredient**:   Yarrow leaves and young shoots can be added to salads to provide a unique, slightly bitter taste and a decorative touch.
- **Garnish**:   Yarrow flowers make an attractive and edible garnish for a variety of dishes, adding color and a mild herbal flavor.
- **Seasoning for Meats**:   Yarrow can be used as a seasoning for roasted or grilled meats. It pairs well with lamb, chicken, and pork.
- **Herbal Butter**:   Blend finely chopped yarrow leaves and flowers into softened butter to create a flavorful herb butter. This can be used to season vegetables, meats, or as a spread for bread.
- **Vinegar Infusion**:   Infuse vinegar with yarrow to create a unique herbal vinegar that can be used in salad dressings or as a condiment.
- **Herb-Infused Oils**:   Yarrow can be used to infuse olive oil or other cooking oils, adding its unique flavor to your cooking.
- **Baking**:   Some bakers use dried yarrow leaves and flowers in bread or pastry recipes to impart a subtle herbal taste to their creations.
- **Pickling**:   Yarrow can be added to pickling brines for vegetables like cucumbers or beets to create unique and flavorful pickles.
