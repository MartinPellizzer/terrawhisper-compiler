## What is the taxonomy and classification of Achillea millefolium?

It falls within the phylum Angiosperms (or Magnoliophyta), indicating that it's a flowering plant. Among flowering plants, it is categorized as a Eudicot, which is a class of dicotyledonous plants.

In a more specific taxonomic context, yarrow is placed in the order Asterales, a diverse group that includes daisies and sunflowers. Yarrow's family is Asteraceae, one of the largest and most widespread plant families in the world, encompassing a wide array of daisy-like flowers.

The genus of yarrow is Achillea, a name rooted in Greek mythology, associated with the hero Achilles who, according to legend, used yarrow to treat wounds. Finally, at the species level, it is known as Achillea millefolium, emphasizing its unique and distinct characteristics within the Achillea genus.

### Stems

The stems of Achillea millefolium are herbaceous, non-woody in nature, and arranged oppositely in pairs of leaves at each node. 

They have a cylindrical shape, with a height ranging from 30 to 60 centimeters (11.8 to 23.6 inches) and a diameter of 2 to 4 millimeters (0.08 to 0.16 inches). 

These green stems are pubescent, covered in fine hairs, and have a smooth surface. The nodes are frequent and closely spaced along the stem, while the internodes are relatively short.

### Leaves

The leaves of Achillea millefolium are simple and not compound. 

They are lanceolate in shape, typically measuring 3 to 12 centimeters (1.18 to 4.72 inches) in length and 1 to 4 centimeters (0.39 to 1.57 inches) in width. 

These thin leaves are green, with a smooth, glabrous surface, and they have an acute apex and cuneate (wedge-shaped) base. Their margins are entire and exhibit pinnate venation, giving them a feather-like appearance. The leaves are bipinnate in lamina structure and have a prominent midrib. They are directly attached to the stem, sessile in nature, with no petioles or stipules.

### Flowers

Achillea millefolium produces compound corymb inflorescences, with numerous small flower heads clustered together. The flowers are radially symmetrical (actinomorphic) and consist of both disk flowers in the central region and ray flowers on the outer periphery. 

These flowers are stalked (pedicellate) and come in various shapes and sizes. Disk flowers measure 2 to 4 millimeters (0.08 to 0.16 inches) in length, while ray flowers are slightly larger at 3 to 8 millimeters (0.12 to 0.31 inches) in length. 

They are typically white or pinkish with yellow centers and have a smooth and glabrous surface. The flowers lack sepals but possess five petals, which are tubular in disk flowers and strap-shaped in ray flowers. The flowers are attached to a central receptacle and have an inferior ovary, a short style, and a bifid stigma. Linear green bracts are present, and the flowers are pedunculate.

### Fruits

The fruits of Achillea millefolium are achenes, with a diameter ranging from 1/16 to 1/8 inch. They are arranged in capitula, forming a cluster of numerous achenes. 

These achenes are evenly dispersed across the flower head and are attached to the receptacle. They exhibit an oblong or cylindrical shape, with a length of 1.5 to 3.2 millimeters (1/16 to 1/8 inch), a width of 0.6 to 1 millimeter (1/32 to 1/16 inch), and a thickness of approximately 0.3 millimeters (1/64 inch). 

The color of these achenes varies from brown to gray, and they have a smooth, glossy surface with no distinctive markings. They do not dehisce and remain intact.

### Seeds

The seeds of Achillea millefolium are achene-type seeds and are typically present as a single seed per flower head. 

They are ovoid in shape, with an average length of 2.5 millimeters (0.098 inches), a width of 0.8 millimeters (0.0315 inches), and a thickness of 0.3 millimeters (0.0118 inches). These seeds are hard, with a brown to dark brown seed coat that has a smooth and shiny surface. The seed coat is relatively thin, measuring 0.1 millimeter (0.0039 inches) in thickness and is impermeable. 

The seeds often have a visible hilum, appearing as a small scar, and a straight embryo with two cotyledons. The radicle is tiny and underdeveloped. Pappus is absent from these seeds.

### How to manage and control invasive Achillea millefolium?

Invasive yarrow is managed and controlled by using several strategies.

Prevention is the foremost approach. Avoid introducing yarrow to non-native environments to curb its spread. 

Mechanical control methods include hand-pulling, best suited for small infestations, and regular mowing to prevent seed production. Chemical herbicides can be used with caution, guided by local experts, and taking care to minimize their impact on non-target species.

Biological control can also be considered, with the introduction of yarrow-feeding insects like the yarrow leaf beetle, though this must be done under expert guidance to prevent unintended consequences. Soil amendments, such as reducing soil fertility through organic matter, can deter yarrow growth. Competitive planting with native or non-invasive species is effective in outcompeting yarrow.

Cultural control methods like tilling and regular disturbance of the area help prevent yarrow from establishing itself. Employing an Integrated Pest Management (IPM) approach, customized to the specific site and infestation scale, can be highly effective. Lastly, ongoing monitoring is crucial to detect yarrow resurgence and act promptly.
