## Common Names


Achillea millefolium goes by various names due to its distinctive characteristics and historical uses. This herbaceous plant features clusters of small, white or pink flowers and finely divided leaves. It's often recognized as "Milfoil" or "Thousand-leaf" because of its abundant and finely segmented foliage.

One historical alias, "Soldier's woundwort," hints at its role in battlefield medicine. Yarrow was traditionally employed to treat wounds, hence the name that emphasizes its relevance to soldiers. Another practical use gave rise to the name "Nosebleed plant." Yarrow can constrict blood vessels, aiding in the cessation of nosebleeds.

The moniker "Old man's pepper" could be attributed to its spicy aroma, somewhat reminiscent of pepper. The term "Staunchweed" underlines its historical function as a hemostatic agent, effectively stopping bleeding. Its Latin name, "millefolium," means "thousand leaves," which is the origin of several of its common names, like "Thousand-leaf."

"Sanguinary" also highlights its historical association with blood due to its Latin root, "sanguis," meaning blood. "Western yarrow" distinguishes the plant's regional occurrence, indicating its presence in western parts of its native range. Lastly, "Knight's milfoil" might allude to the historical use by knights for wound treatment, aligning with its association with soldiers and battlefield care.
