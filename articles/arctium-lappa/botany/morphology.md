# Arctium lappa morphology

![Arctium lappa morphology](/images/arctium-lappa-botany-morphology.jpg "Arctium lappa morphology")

Arctium lappa, commonly known as burdock, exhibits various distinctive morphological characteristics. Its roots form a taproot system with a dichotomous branching pattern, reaching up to 90 cm in length. The stems are herbaceous, not woody, and alternate in arrangement, with a rough, pubescent surface. They can vary in height from 50 to 200 cm. The leaves are broadly ovate, pubescent, and have coarsely serrate margins. They are attached to the stem via petioles and have pinnate venation.

Burdock's flowers are arranged in clusters at the tip of the stem, with tubular, bell-shaped, bisexual, and actinomorphic petals. The flowers are typically purple, pink, or white, and the plant has an inferior ovary. The fruits are small, dry achenes that are oval to oblong, attached within involucral bracts, and do not split open at maturity. These achenes are adapted for attachment to various surfaces for dispersal.

The seeds of Arctium lappa are hard, dicotyledonous, and ellipsoid in shape, measuring about 4-5 mm in length. They have a brown, impermeable seed coat, requiring scarification for germination. Each seed contains two cotyledons, a small embryo, and a distinct hilum marking the attachment point.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Arctium lappa by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

## Roots



Arctium lappa has a taproot system, characterized by a single, dominant primary root from which numerous secondary roots branch out. The branching pattern is dichotomous, creating a fibrous structure beneath the soil. These roots typically extend up to 90 cm (35 inches) in length and have a diameter ranging from 0.2 to 0.5 cm (0.08 to 0.2 inches). The presence of root hairs, fine and dense, enhances the root's ability to absorb water and nutrients from the soil.

Root tips are sharp and pointed, allowing for efficient penetration into the ground. The root system exhibits a variable density, with an average of 750 to 1,500 roots per square meter (m2). Root coloration tends to be whitish to beige, and the surface texture is notably rough and wrinkled.

In terms of depth, Arctium lappa's roots typically extend between 30 and 60 cm (12 to 24 inches) into the soil, aiding the plant's stability and access to resources. Unlike some other plants, Arctium lappa does not form root nodules.

## Stems



Arctium lappa has a herbaceous, non-woody stems. These stems are not comprised of hard, lignified tissue and remain flexible throughout their lifecycle. This characteristic is in contrast to woody plants like trees and shrubs.

Stem arrangement in Arctium lappa is alternating, meaning that the stems' leaves or branches emerge at different points along the stem. This alternating arrangement helps optimize light exposure for each leaf, allowing for efficient photosynthesis.

The stem shape of Arctium lappa is generally erect and cylindrical. This upright form supports the plant's growth and ensures that it can reach its full height potential. Stem height can vary, typically ranging from 50 to 200 centimeters (20 to 79 inches). The stem's height is an essential factor in determining the overall size of the burdock plant.

The stem's diameter typically measures between 1 to 2 centimeters (0.4 to 0.8 inches). This diameter provides structural support and enables the plant to stand tall.

The color of Arctium lappa stems is predominantly green, which is a typical characteristic of healthy, photosynthesizing plants. Occasionally, the stem base may take on a purplish hue.

Stem texture is described as pubescent, indicating that the stem surface is covered in fine hairs. This feature may serve various purposes, including reducing water loss and deterring herbivores.

Arctium lappa stems have a rough surface, which aids in protecting the plant from external elements and potential threats.

Stems also feature distinct nodes where leaves and branches grow, and these nodes are separated by varying lengths of internodes, providing attachment points for leaves, flowers, and fruit.

## Leaves



Arctium lappa leaves are relatively simple, typically displaying a broadly ovate shape. Their arrangement is alternate, meaning that the leaves are not directly opposite each other along the stem but rather positioned in a staggered pattern. These leaves have an erect orientation, angling at approximately 45 degrees from the vertical axis.

In terms of size, Arctium lappa leaves typically measure 20-40 cm (7.9-15.7 inches) in length and 15-25 cm (5.9-9.8 inches) in width. They possess a thickness of about 0.2-0.3 cm (0.08-0.12 inches). Their coloration is a dark shade of green, and their texture is characterized by fine hairs, rendering them pubescent. The leaf surface is smooth or glabrous.

The leaf apex tapers to a slender point, described as acuminate, while the base is cordate or heart-shaped. The leaf margin features large, sharp teeth, referred to as coarsely serrate. The venation is pinnate, with a single prominent main vein and smaller branching veins.

These leaves have an undivided lamina, and a noticeable midrib runs along the center. They attach to the stem via a petiole, which measures 5-15 cm (2-5.9 inches) in length. Additionally, stipules, in a lanceolate shape, are present near the leaf base.

## Flowers



The inflorescence type of Arctium lappa is categorized as a "capitulum," which refers to a clustered arrangement of 2-5 flowers at the tip of the stem. These flowers are relatively simple, composed of a single layer. They display actinomorphic or radial symmetry, with five floral parts.

The flowers of Arctium lappa are bisexual and complete, containing both male and female reproductive structures. They attach at the terminal end of the stem, and their shape is tubular, with a tubular-campanulate or bell-shaped appearance. The flowers are relatively small, with a length ranging from 1.5 to 3 cm (0.6 to 1.2 inches) and a width between 1 and 2 cm (0.4 to 0.8 inches).

In terms of color, these flowers typically appear in shades of purple to pink or white, contributing to their visual appeal. The surface of the flowers is smooth and hairless, with 10 petal-like segments, formed by the fusion of sepals and petals, collectively referred to as the perianth.

Each flower possesses five green, lanceolate sepals and five fused petals with small lobes at the tip, making up the corolla. Within the flowers, there are five stamens, alternately positioned with the petals, bearing filaments and anthers for pollen production. The single pistil consists of an elongated ovary, style, and a bifurcated stigma.

These flowers exhibit an inferior ovary, positioned below the attachment point, and a long, slender style. The absence of stipules is a notable feature, while the involucre surrounds the capitulum with hooked bracts. The tall peduncle supports the capitulum, with a height ranging from 50 to 150 cm (20 to 60 inches), while the pedicels are short, measuring 1 to 2 cm (0.4 to 0.8 inches).

## Fruits



Arctium lappa has small dry fruits, or achenes, that are typically oval to oblong in shape, measuring between 4 to 7 millimeters (approximately 0.16 to 0.28 inches) in length, 2 to 4 millimeters (roughly 0.08 to 0.16 inches) in width, and about 1 millimeter (approximately 0.04 inches) in thickness. When fully mature, they adopt a dark brown to black coloration.

One noteworthy feature of Arctium lappa is the arrangement of its fruits. They are found in spherical clusters, each containing multiple achenes. These achenes are attached within the receptacle of the plant, which is primarily made up of involucral bracts. The outer surface of these fruits is smooth and shiny, with no prominent hairs or spines. There are no distinct markings on the fruits, and they do not exhibit dehiscence, meaning they do not split open when they reach maturity.

The specific morphology of Arctium lappa's fruits plays a vital role in their dispersal and propagation. The achenes are well-suited for attachment to animal fur, clothing, or passing objects due to their small size and smooth surface. This facilitates their distribution and the establishment of new plants in different locations.

## Seeds



Arctium lappa seeds are dicotyledonous, meaning they have two embryonic leaves. They are found in clusters within the plant's flower heads, with typically 1 to 2 seeds per fruit.

In terms of size and shape, burdock seeds are ellipsoid, measuring about 4-5 mm in length, 2-3 mm in width, and approximately 2 mm in thickness. These seeds are known for their hardness, having a woody texture.

The seeds are further distinguished by their brown to dark brown color and a smooth, shiny seed coat. The surface of the seed coat appears glossy with fine wrinkles, and its thickness is about 0.2 mm.

Notably, the seed coat of burdock seeds is impermeable to water, which necessitates a process called scarification to aid germination. Scarification involves mechanically or chemically breaking the seed coat to allow water absorption.

The seeds also feature a distinct hilum, which is the scar marking the point of attachment to the fruit. Within the seed, a small embryo is embedded, along with two cotyledons (seed leaves) and a short, embryonic root called the radicle.
