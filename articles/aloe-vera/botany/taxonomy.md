# Aloe vera taxonomy

![alt](/images/aloe-vera-botany-taxonomy.jpg "title")

Aloe vera, scientifically classified under the domain Eukarya and the Kingdom Plantae, is a remarkable botanical specimen that transcends the boundaries of taxonomy. 

Belonging to the Phylum Angiosperms (Magnoliophyta) as a flowering plant, it further narrows its taxonomic position to the Order Asparagales and the Family Xanthorrhoeaceae. This classification provides an intricate insight into its botanical lineage. 

Beyond its formal taxonomy, Aloe vera boasts an array of common names, ranging from "True Aloe" and "Burn Plant" to "Medicinal Aloe" and "Miracle Plant," each name revealing a facet of its rich history and extensive utility. 

Moreover, Aloe vera showcases a diverse array of varieties, from the well-known Aloe barbadensis miller, with its soothing properties, to the visually captivating spiral aloe, Aloe polyphylla, native to South Africa. These varieties offer a wide range of options for horticultural enthusiasts and enthusiasts of natural remedies alike, making Aloe vera a plant of both scientific and practical fascination.

This article gives a detailed explanation of the taxonomy, common names, and varieties of Aloe vera.

## Taxonomy



Aloe vera belongs to the domain Eukarya and falls under the Kingdom Plantae. In the world of taxonomy, it is classified in the Phylum Angiosperms (Magnoliophyta), which means it is a flowering plant. Its Class is Liliopsida, which is a category that includes monocotyledonous plants, commonly known as monocots.

Aloe vera further narrows down its taxonomic position in the Order Asparagales. Asparagales is a diverse order that includes various plant families. Aloe vera, however, belongs to the Family Xanthorrhoeaceae.

The Genus of Aloe vera is simply "Aloe," which is a broad group of succulent plants. Within this genus, the specific species is Aloe vera.

## Common Names

Aloe vera is known by various common names across the world. One of the most familiar names is "Aloe Barbadensis Miller," its scientific designation. This formal name acknowledges the botanist who initially described its properties, Miller.

Aloe vera is often called "True Aloe" to emphasize its authentic and well-studied properties. It's also referred to as the "Burn Plant" due to its renowned ability to soothe minor burns and sunburns. In fact, it's a staple in many first-aid kits and is sometimes dubbed the "First Aid Plant."

With a long history of medicinal use, it's not surprising that "Medicinal Aloe" is another common name. This plant has been used for various health purposes, especially for skin ailments and digestive issues. In some cultures, it's known as "Elephant's Gall" because of its bitter-tasting yellowish sap, which holds medicinal properties.

The name "Lily of the Desert" draws attention to its slender, elongated leaves and the fact that it thrives in arid regions. For the Chinese, Aloe vera is the "Wand of Heaven" because it's believed to have rejuvenating properties, particularly for the skin. In India, it's called "Ghee Kanwar" for its traditional use in eye drops and benefits for eye health.

"Aloe vera" is often referred to as the "Miracle Plant" due to its myriad of uses, both medicinal and cosmetic. These diverse common names reflect the rich history, cultural significance, and the wide array of applications that this plant offers.


## Varieties

Aloe vera comes in several varieties, each with unique characteristics. The most common type is Aloe barbadensis miller, recognized by its thick green leaves with serrated edges. This variety is widely used in skincare products and has soothing and healing properties.

The Aloe arborescens, or tree aloe, stands out as a large, multi-branched shrub or small tree with long, narrow, blue-green leaves. Aloe saponaria, also known as the soap aloe, has soft, toothless leaves filled with gel, making it suitable for cleansing and skincare.

Aloe plicatilis, the fan aloe, features a unique fan-shaped leaf arrangement. Aloe maculata, or the zebra aloe, has green leaves with white spots or stripes, adding a decorative touch to gardens and landscapes.

Aloe brevifolia is a miniature Aloe vera with stubby, compact leaves. Aloe ciliaris, or the climbing aloe, boasts slender, arching leaves and coral-colored tubular flowers.

Aloe juvenna, the tiger tooth aloe, has upright leaves with spiky white edges, making it suitable for indoor succulent collections. Aloe variegata, or the partridge breast aloe, is slow-growing and features variegated leaves, making it a good fit for rock gardens.

Lastly, the spiral aloe, Aloe polyphylla, showcases a captivating spiral leaf arrangement. Native to South Africa, it's a rare and unique succulent that demands specific care.

## Morphology

Knowing the [morphology of Aloe vera](/aloe-vera/botany/morphology.html) is useful if you want to be able to classify this plant. 

Aloe vera features a rosette arrangement of lanceolate, succulent leaves with smooth, hairless surfaces, upright orientation, and parallel venation. Its succulent stem is cylindrical, green, and covered in a waxy layer. 

The plant produces tubular, bilaterally symmetric flowers in raceme inflorescences, typically orange, yellow, or red, with six sepals and petals. Aloe vera's fruit is a capsule that hangs vertically in clusters, while its seeds are hard, dark brown, ovate, and found in multiple seed clusters within capsules.
