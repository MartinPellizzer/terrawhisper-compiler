# Cimicifuga racemosa morphology

![Cimicifuga racemosa morphology](/images/cimicifuga-racemosa-botany-morphology.jpg "Cimicifuga racemosa morphology")

Cimicifuga racemosa's roots are fibrous, with a primary taproot, moderately branching. Root tips are pointed, density about 0.8 roots per cubic centimeter (4.9 per cubic inch), found around 15 cm (5.9 inches) deep, with a diameter of 0.3 cm (0.12 inches) and a maximum length of 30 cm (11.8 inches). They're whitish-brown, smooth, without root nodules, but with sparse fine hairs for absorption.

Its herbaceous stems are arranged alternately, round with a circular cross-section. They grow 1-2 meters (3.28-6.56 feet) tall and have a diameter of 0.5-1 cm (0.2-0.4 inches). They're green, smooth, and hairless, segmented by nodes every 10-20 cm (4-8 inches).

Compound leaves are palmate with 5-7 leaflets. They're alternate, horizontal, about 10-20 cm (4-8 inches) in length and width, thin, dark green, smooth, and hairless. Leaf margins are serrated, and venation is palmate. Leaves are petiolate, with no stipules.

The flowers form racemes, are simple and actinomorphic, with complete symmetry. They're small, 0.5-1.5 cm (0.2-0.6 inches) long and 0.2-0.5 cm (0.08-0.2 inches) wide, creamy white, and glabrous. Typically, they have 4-7 floral parts and 15-25 stamens. The style is long and slender, with branched or feathery stigmas. Stipules are absent.

Cimicifuga racemosa produces follicle-type fruits, solitary or in racemes, axial attachment, elliptical to oblong, 1.5-2.5 cm (0.6-1 inch) long, dark brown to black, smooth, and glabrous. They split open when mature.

The seeds are achenes, elliptical or oblong, 5-6 mm long, 2-3 mm wide, and 1-2 mm thick. They have a hard, dark brown to black, smooth, shiny seed coat, a small hilum scar, straight embryo with two cotyledons, and a short radicle.

In this article you will learn about the morphology of Cimicifuga racemosa by analyzing the main parts of this plant and their characteristics.

Regarding branching, these roots show moderate branching, which means that they develop secondary roots to a moderate extent. The root tips are pointed, allowing for easier penetration into the soil.

Root density is approximately 0.8 roots per cubic centimeter (4.9 roots per cubic inch), indicating the number of roots within a given volume of soil. They can be found at a depth of about 15 centimeters (5.9 inches) below the soil surface. The root diameter measures around 0.3 centimeters (0.12 inches), while the maximum root length can reach up to 30 centimeters (11.8 inches).

The roots typically have a whitish-brown color, and their surface texture is smooth, lacking any prominent ridges or textures. Importantly, Cimicifuga racemosa roots do not develop root nodules.

Furthermore, these roots have sparse, fine hairs, which aid in nutrient and water absorption. These hairs increase the root's surface area, enabling the plant to take up essential resources from the soil.

## Leaves



Cimicifuga racemosa displays compound leaves, which means that a single leaf consists of multiple smaller leaflets. In this case, there are usually 5 to 7 leaflets per compound leaf. These leaflets contribute to the plant's palmate shape, which is characterized by lobes extending from a central point, resembling the shape of an open hand.

The arrangement of the leaves is alternate, with each leaf positioned individually along the stem. These leaves are oriented horizontally, providing an even, flat distribution across the plant. The typical dimensions of the leaves are approximately 10 to 20 centimeters (4 to 8 inches) in both length and width, and they are relatively thin.

The color of Cimicifuga racemosa leaves is typically a dark green, giving the plant a vibrant appearance. The leaves have a smooth texture and are glabrous, meaning they are hairless.

Leaf features also include an acute apex, giving them a pointed appearance, and a cordate base, which is heart-shaped. The leaf margins are serrated, finely toothed along the edges. The venation pattern is palmate, radiating out from the central midrib.

In terms of attachment, the leaves are petiolate, meaning they are attached to the stem by petioles, which are 10 to 20 centimeters (4 to 8 inches) long. Notably, Cimicifuga racemosa lacks stipules, the small leaf-like structures often found at the base of leaf petioles.

In terms of reproductive structures, these flowers have a substantial number of stamens, usually ranging from 15 to 25, with an average of 20 to 22. They possess a single superior ovary, meaning it is located above the attachment point. The style of the flower is long and slender, often ending with branched or feathery stigmas. Notably, stipules are absent in these flowers.
