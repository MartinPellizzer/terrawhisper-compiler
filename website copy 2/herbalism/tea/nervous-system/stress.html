10 best herbal teas for stress

Herbal teas, renowned for their soothing properties, offer an excellent solution to combat stress.

Passionflower tea, with its calming effects, brings tranquility by reducing anxiety. Known for its stress-relieving benefits, chamomile tea soothes nerves, encouraging relaxation. Lemon balm tea, with its mild sedative effect, eases anxiety and promotes sleep, while lavender tea creates a peaceful atmosphere, aiding stress reduction.

Drinking these herbal teas daily helps maintain balance and composure during stressful times.

1. Chamomile

Chamomile tea contains the antioxidant apigenin, which binds to certain receptors in the brain that decrease anxiety and initiate sleep.

This natural herb, with its soothing scent and calming properties, promotes relaxation by lowering the levels of stress hormones such as cortisol. Regular consumption of chamomile tea aids in stress relief, leading to improved sleep quality, emotional stability, and overall well-being. This caffeine-free beverage has been used for centuries as a stress remedy, offering a drug-free method for reducing anxiety and promoting mental clarity.

Its therapeutic benefits extend beyond stress relief, alleviating symptoms of depression and inducing a sense of inner peace and tranquility.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in chamomile tea that help with stress.

  • Flowers: Contain high levels of antioxidants and flavonoids that help reduce stress and promote relaxation.
  • Leaves: Rich in soothing properties, known to alleviate anxiety and help induce sleep.
  • Seeds: Packed with essential oils and calming compounds, they aid in reducing stress and promoting overall well-being.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in chamomile tea that aid with stress.

  • Apigenin: A flavonoid that binds to benzodiazepine receptors in the brain, helping to reduce anxiety and promote sleep.
  • Chamazulene: A terpene with anti-inflammatory properties, helping to soothe nerves and alleviate stress-related tension.
  • Alpha-Bisabolol: A sesquiterpene alcohol with calming and anti-inflammatory effects, reducing stress-induced inflammation and irritability.
  • Matricin: A precursor to chamazulene, exhibiting anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties, helping to alleviate pain and discomfort associated with stress.
  • Spiroether: A sesquiterpene lactone with potential anxiolytic effects, contributing to chamomile's stress-relieving and calming properties.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic chamomile tea for stress.

  1. Boil 8 ounces of water to 212 degrees Fahrenheit, the optimal temperature for chamomile tea.
  2. Place one teabag or 1-2 teaspoons of loose chamomile flowers in a cup.
  3. Pour the boiling water over the chamomile in the cup.
  4. Steep the chamomile in the water for 5-10 minutes to allow the flavors and medicinal properties to infuse.
  5. Remove the teabag or strain out the loose flowers before drinking. Enjoy your soothing, stress-relieving chamomile tea.

2. Lavender

Lavender tea's soothing properties stem from its active compounds, particularly linalool and linalyl acetate, which bind to GABA receptors in the brain, promoting relaxation.

This interaction reduces anxiety and lowers stress levels, making lavender tea an effective natural remedy. Moreover, lavender's comforting aroma generates a calming ambiance that further alleviates tension and instills tranquility. By consuming lavender tea, individuals experience these stress-relieving benefits, enhancing their overall well-being.

Traditional medicine and recent scientific studies support these claims, solidifying lavender tea's reputation as a valuable aid in stress reduction.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in lavender tea that help with stress.

  • Flowers: Contain essential oils and antioxidants known to promote relaxation.
  • Leaves: Rich in calming compounds, including linalool and apigenin, that help reduce anxiety.
  • Buds: High in lavender's active ingredients, providing potent stress-relieving properties.
  • Stems: Contribute to tea's soothing effect by releasing beneficial constituents during infusion.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in lavender tea that aid with stress.

  • Linalool: A terpene alcohol responsible for lavender's calming scent, it decreases anxiety and promotes sleep.
  • Eucalyptol: A natural compound with anti-inflammatory properties that helps reduce physical symptoms of stress.
  • Camphor: This volatile oil improves mood and reduces stress by promoting relaxation.
  • Geraniol: A plant-based compound with known anxiety-reducing effects, contributing to lavender's soothing reputation.
  • Caryophyllene: A sesquiterpene that interacts with the endocannabinoid system, producing calming effects and reducing stress.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic lavender tea for stress.

  1. Boil 1 cup of water in a teapot or kettle for 3-5 minutes.
  2. Add 1-2 teaspoons of dried lavender flowers or 1 lavender tea bag to a teapot or mug.
  3. Pour the boiling water over the lavender in the teapot or mug.
  4. Steep the lavender in the hot water for 5-7 minutes.
  5. Strain out the lavender (if using loose leaves) and enjoy the soothing lavender tea.

3. Lemon Balm

Lemon balm tea possesses natural compounds that actively reduce stress, such as rosmarinic acid and eugenol.

These substances work by inhibiting GABA transaminase, an enzyme that breaks down the calming neurotransmitter GABA. Consequently, lemon balm tea consumption leads to elevated GABA levels, promoting relaxation and alleviating anxiety. Furthermore, lemon balm has antioxidant properties that protect cells from damage caused by stress-induced free radicals.

Lastly, lemon balm tea's soothing aroma, resulting from its primary component citral, triggers a psychological response that encourages stress relief.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in lemon balm tea that help with stress.

  • Leaves: Contain rosmarinic acid and eugenol, compounds known to reduce anxiety and provide sedative effects.
  • Flowers: Rich in antioxidants, helping to alleviate stress-related damage and promoting relaxation.
  • Stems: Packed with essential oils, such as citronellal and geraniol, contributing to the calming effects of lemon balm tea.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in lemon balm tea that aid with stress.

  • Rosmarinic acid: Possesses anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties that help reduce stress-induced oxidative damage and inflammation.
  • Citral: A type of terpene known for its calming and analgesic effects, which may alleviate anxiety and stress-related symptoms.
  • Nerol: A volatile oil that exhibits relaxing and sedative properties, contributing to the stress-reducing effect of lemon balm tea.
  • Geraniol: A naturally occurring compound with antidepressant qualities, which may help improve mood and reduce stress levels.
  • Caryophyllene: A sesquiterpene with known anxiolytic (anti-anxiety) effects, assisting in relaxation and stress management.
  • Eugenol: A phenolic compound exhibiting analgesic, anti-inflammatory, and sedative properties, further supporting stress reduction.
  • Tannins: Polyphenolic biomolecules with astringent and antianxiety effects, contributing to the overall calming impact of lemon balm tea.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic lemon balm tea for stress.

  1. Gather 2 teaspoons of dried lemon balm leaves and 1 cup of water.
  2. Boil the water and then pour it over the lemon balm leaves.
  3. Steep the tea for 5-7 minutes to allow the herb to release its beneficial compounds.
  4. Strain the tea to remove the leaves, creating a clear liquid.
  5. Sip the warm lemon balm tea slowly to help alleviate stress.

4. Peppermint

Peppermint tea combats stress due to its natural soothing properties.

This calming beverage contains menthol, a compound that induces relaxation. Peppermint tea's warmth also promotes stress relief by increasing the feeling of comfort. Furthermore, it encourages mindfulness, as sipping and enjoying the tea demands focus on the present moment.

Lastly, peppermint tea has antispasmodic qualities that alleviate physical tension, another common stress manifestation.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in peppermint tea that help with stress.

  • Leaves: Contain menthol and menthone, which have a calming and soothing effect on the body.
  • Flowers: Rich in volatile oils and antioxidants, known to reduce anxiety and promote relaxation.
  • Stems: Contain rosmarinic acid, a compound that helps reduce stress and promote sleep.
  • Seeds: High in essential oils and fatty acids, known to have a calming effect on the nervous system.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in peppermint tea that aid with stress.

  • Menthol: Acts as a natural analgesic and relaxant, helping to alleviate tension and ease anxiety.
  • Mentone: Exhibits calming properties that help reduce restlessness and promote relaxation.
  • Limonene: Known for its stress-relieving and mood-enhancing effects, promoting a sense of well-being.
  • Pulegone: Contributes to the soothing effect of peppermint tea, helping to ease stress-related symptoms.
  • Rosmarinic acid: Possesses anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties that support the body's response to stress.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic peppermint tea for stress.

  1. Boil 8 ounces of water, taking 5-10 minutes to reach a rolling boil.
  2. Add 1 heaped teaspoon of dried peppermint leaves to a tea infuser or teapot.
  3. Pour the boiling water over the peppermint leaves and let it steep for 5-7 minutes.
  4. Remove the tea infuser or strain the tea to eliminate leaves.
  5. Serve the peppermint tea hot, enjoying its soothing effects during stressful moments.

5. Valerian

Valerian tea contains valerenic acid and other antioxidants that influence GABA, a neurotransmitter in the brain that promotes relaxation.

This interaction reduces excitability in brain cells, which alleviates anxiety and stress. Valerian tea also contains isovaleric acid, which has a sedative effect that soothes the nervous system, further combating stress symptoms. Moreover, this herbal tea's calming properties encourage better sleep quality, helping the body maintain balance and resist stress.

Drinking valerian tea ultimately lowers cortisol levels, a stress hormone, and fosters a general sense of well-being.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in valerian tea that help with stress.

  • Roots: Contain high levels of antioxidants and volatile oils that help promote relaxation.
  • Rhizomes: Rich in valerenic acid, which has been shown to reduce anxiety and improve sleep quality.
  • Leaves: Packed with soothing compounds, such as flavonoids and terpenoids, that help alleviate stress and calm the mind.
  • Flowers: Contain essential oils and other active constituents that have a calming effect on the nervous system.
  • Seeds: Source of valerenic acid and other beneficial compounds, which can help reduce anxiety and promote better sleep.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in valerian tea that aid with stress.

  • Valepotriates: These are a group of compounds in valerian that possess sedative and muscle relaxant properties, making them effective in reducing stress-related symptoms such as anxiety and muscle tension.
  • Isovaleric acid: This compound is responsible for the characteristic smell of valerian and is known to have a calming effect on the central nervous system, which can help alleviate stress and promote relaxation.
  • GABA: Valerian contains GABA, a neurotransmitter that helps regulate nerve cell activity and can reduce anxiety and stress by promoting relaxation.
  • Flavonoids: These plant compounds have antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties that can help manage stress by reducing oxidative stress and inflammation in the body.
  • Essential oils: Valerian contains essential oils with calming effects, such as hesperidin, which can reduce anxiety and promote relaxation, thus helping manage stress.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic valerian tea for stress.

  1. Boil 1 cup of water to 212°F (100°C) for 1-2 minutes.
  2. Add 1 teaspoon (2-3 grams) of dried valerian root to a tea infuser.
  3. Pour the hot water over the valerian root in a teapot or mug.
  4. Steep the tea for 5-10 minutes to extract the medicinal compounds.
  5. Remove the tea infuser and enjoy the warm valerian tea, avoiding caffeine and sugar.

6. Passionflower

Passionflower tea contains harman alkaloids, flavonoids, and passiflortin, which have calming effects on the nervous system.

These compounds increase the levels of gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) in the brain, promoting relaxation and reducing stress. Passionflower tea also has antioxidant properties that protect the body from oxidative stress caused by free radicals. Additionally, it helps lower heart rate and blood pressure, further contributing to stress reduction.

Lastly, passionflower tea has a soothing effect on the digestive system, alleviating stress-related symptoms such as nausea, cramps, and indigestion.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in passionflower tea that help with stress.

  • Leaves: Contain Passiflora alkaloids and flavonoids that help promote relaxation and reduce anxiety.
  • Flowers: Rich in antioxidants that support stress reduction and provide calming effects.
  • Stems: Contain beneficial compounds that help alleviate symptoms of stress and anxiety.
  • Buds: Packed with stress-relieving compounds, offering a potent solution for relaxation.
  • Bark: Has passive alkaloids that aid in reducing stress and promoting better sleep.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in passionflower tea that aid with stress.

  • Flavonoids: These antioxidants protect cells from damage, reducing oxidative stress.
  • Alkaloids: Harman alkaloids, like harmine and harmaline, inhibit the breakdown of feel-good neurotransmitters like serotonin and dopamine.
  • GABA: A neurotransmitter that inhibits excitability in the brain, reducing anxiety.
  • Chrysin: A flavonoid that helps reduce anxiety by binding to GABA receptors.
  • Benzoflavones: Flavonoids that work as anti-anxiety and antidepressant agents in the brain.
  • Essential oils: Containing sesquiterpenes, passionflower essential oils have calming and antispasmodic properties, improving relaxation.
  • Maltol: A natural substance known to have soothing, anti-anxiety effects.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic passionflower tea for stress.

  1. Measure 1 teaspoon of dried passionflower leaves, placing them in an infuser or teapot.
  2. Boil water, then pour it over the passionflower leaves, allowing them to steep for 5-10 minutes.
  3. Cover the teapot or cup to retain heat and essential oils.
  4. Remove the infuser or strain the tea into a cup to eliminate loose leaves.
  5. Sip the passionflower tea slowly, enjoying its soothing effects throughout the day.

  • Seeds: Contain high levels of triethylene glycol, linked to stress reduction.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in ashwagandha tea that aid with stress.

  • Withanolides: These compounds possess adaptogenic properties, helping the body to cope with stress by improving the resilience to physical and mental stressors.
  • Choline: This nutrient supports the production of acetylcholine, a neurotransmitter that regulates the stress response and promotes relaxation.
  • Sitoindosides: These compounds exhibit anti-stress effects by reducing anxiety and depression symptoms, promoting overall mental well-being.

  • Steroidal lactones: These compounds, including withaferin A, demonstrate anti-stress properties by protecting against oxidative stress and cellular damage.
  • Flavonoids: These antioxidant compounds protect the body from stress-induced oxidative damage and help regulate stress hormones.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic ashwagandha tea for stress.

  1. Boil 1 cup of water for 3-5 minutes.
  2. Add 1 teaspoon of dried ashwagandha root to boiling water.
  3. Reduce heat and simmer for 10-15 minutes.
  4. Strain the tea into a cup, then add honey or lemon to taste.
  5. Drink the tea while warm, once or twice a day, for stress relief.
