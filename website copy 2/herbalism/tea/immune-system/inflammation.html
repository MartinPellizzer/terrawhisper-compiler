10 best herbal teas for inflammation

Several herbal teas offer proven anti-inflammatory properties, providing a natural and delicious alternative to over-the-counter medications.

Ginger tea, made from the rhizome of the Zingiber officinale plant, contains potent compounds called gingerols that inhibit the production of inflammatory molecules in the body. Another excellent choice is turmeric tea, derived from Curcuma longa, which boasts curcumin, a powerful anti-inflammatory agent. Green tea, obtained from the leaves of Camellia sinensis, is rich in epigallocatechin-3-gallate (EGCG), an antioxidant that demonstrates remarkable anti-inflammatory effects.

Lastly, chamomile tea, prepared from Matricaria recutita or Chamaemelum nobile, possesses anti-inflammatory flavonoids and essential oils that help alleviate inflammation and promote relaxation.

1. Chamomile

Chamomile tea contains anti-inflammatory compounds, specifically the terpene class of bisabolol oxides and the flavonoid class of apigenin and luteolin.

These compounds inhibit the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines, molecules that signal for immune cells to move towards sites of injury or infection. Moreover, chamomile tea directly suppresses the activation of NF-κB, a protein complex crucial for mediating immune responses, reducing overall inflammation in the body. Additionally, it increases the production of anti-inflammatory cytokines, further promoting a balanced immune response.

Consequently, regular consumption of chamomile tea aids in managing inflammation in individuals, providing a natural and soothing remedy.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in chamomile tea that help with inflammation.

  • Flowers: Contain the highest concentration of anti-inflammatory compounds, making them the most potent part for reducing inflammation.
  • Leaves: Rich in soothing agents that help alleviate redness and swelling associated with inflammation.
  • Seeds: Contain essential oils with potent anti-inflammatory properties, making them a valuable addition to medicinal tea.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in chamomile tea that aid with inflammation.

  • Apigenin: A flavonoid that possesses antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties, which help reduce inflammation by neutralizing free radicals and modulating the immune response.
  • Chamazulene: A chemical compound that has potent anti-inflammatory effects, resulting from its ability to inhibit the production of inflammatory chemicals like leukotrienes and prostaglandins.
  • Alpha-Bisabolol: A sesquiterpene alcohol with notable anti-inflammatory properties, attributed to its capacity to suppress the activation of inflammatory cells and enzymes.
  • Matricin: A precursor to chamazulene, which demonstrates anti-inflammatory effects by inhibiting the synthesis of pro-inflammatory mediators and promoting the resolution of inflammation.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic chamomile tea for inflammation.

  1. Measure - Pour 1 cup of boiling water over 1 heaping teaspoon of dried chamomile flowers.
  2. Steep - Let the mixture steep for 5-10 minutes, depending on desired strength.
  3. Strain - Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the chamomile flowers.
  4. Optional Add-ins - Incorporate honey or lemon to taste, if desired.
  5. Serve - Enjoy the soothing tea while it's still warm to help alleviate inflammation.

2. Ginger

Ginger tea provides relief from inflammation due to the presence of gingerol, a bioactive compound in ginger.

This compound inhibits the production of inflammatory molecules in the body, reducing inflammation. Additionally, gingerol has analgesic properties that alleviate pain caused by inflammation. Furthermore, ginger tea enhances blood circulation, promoting the removal of inflammatory substances from the affected areas.

Lastly, ginger tea's antioxidant properties protect the body from cellular damage, aiding in the healing process and reducing overall inflammation.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in ginger tea that help with inflammation.

  • Rhizomes: Ginger's underground stems, or rhizomes, contain anti-inflammatory compounds like gingerols and shogaols that help alleviate inflammation.
  • Leaves: Rich in antioxidants and anti-inflammatory agents, ginger leaves have been used in traditional medicine to treat inflammatory conditions.
  • Flowers: Ginger flowers contain bioactive compounds that exhibit potent anti-inflammatory properties, making them useful for inflammation-related ailments.
  • Seeds: Ginger seeds, although not commonly used in teas, possess anti-inflammatory properties and can be ground into a powder for medicinal teas.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in ginger tea that aid with inflammation.

  • Gingerols: Gingerols possess potent anti-inflammatory properties, helping to inhibit the production of inflammatory cytokines and enzymes.
  • Shogaols: Shogaols exhibit strong inhibitory effects on inflammatory pathways, specifically targeting the production of prostaglandins and leukotrienes.
  • Paradols: Paradols demonstrate significant anti-inflammatory effects by suppressing the activation of inflammatory cells and reducing the expression of adhesion molecules.
  • Zingiberene: Zingiberene, a volatile oil, shows anti-inflammatory potential by impeding the activation of nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-kB), thereby reducing the expression of pro-inflammatory genes.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic ginger tea for inflammation.

  1. Peel and slice 1-2 inches of fresh ginger root into small pieces.
  2. Bring 4 cups of water to a boil, then add ginger pieces and reduce heat to a simmer.
  3. Simmer ginger pieces in water, covered, for 15-20 minutes to extract flavors and gingerols.
  4. Strain the ginger-infused water to remove solid pieces, then add honey and lemon juice to taste.
  5. Serve the ginger tea hot to soothe inflammation.

3. Turmeric

Turmeric tea reduces inflammation due to its active ingredient, curcumin, which has been proven to possess potent anti-inflammatory properties.

This compound inhibits the activity of several enzymes and proteins that play a key role in inflammation, thereby reducing swelling, pain, and redness. Furthermore, turmeric's antioxidant effects help neutralize free radicals, preventing them from causing damage to cells and tissues, and exacerbating the inflammatory response. Regularly consuming turmeric tea promotes a strong immune system, enabling the body to combat inflammation effectively.

With its well-documented health benefits, turmeric tea stands out as a powerful, natural remedy for alleviating inflammation.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in turmeric tea that help with inflammation.

  • Rhizomes: Turmeric tea commonly uses the underground stem or rhizome, which contains curcumin known to reduce inflammation.
  • Roots: The roots of the turmeric plant also contain curcumin and are used interchangeably with rhizomes for medicinal tea.
  • Leaves: Although lesser-known, turmeric leaves are rich in anti-inflammatory compounds, providing an alternative source for tea preparation.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in turmeric tea that aid with inflammation.

  • Curcumin: This polyphenol has strong anti-inflammatory properties due to its ability to inhibit the activity of several enzymes and proteins that play a role in inflammation.
  • Turmerone: A sesquiterpene found in turmeric, turmerone has been shown to reduce inflammation by modulating the immune response and suppressing the production of pro-inflammatory molecules.
  • Curdione: This sesquiterpene is known for its potent anti-inflammatory effects, which result from its inhibition of nitric oxide production and its ability to regulate intracellular signaling pathways involved in inflammation.
  • Bisacurone: A curcuminoid, bisacurone potently suppresses the activation of various inflammatory factors and has been shown to ameliorate inflammation-related conditions.
  • Cyclocurcumin: A derivative of curcumin, cyclocurcumin demonstrates significant anti-inflammatory activity by inhibiting the activation of nuclear factor kappa B (NF-κB), a key regulator of inflammation.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic turmeric tea for inflammation.

  1. Boil 4 cups of water in a saucepan for 5 minutes.
  2. Stir in 1 teaspoon of ground turmeric and let it simmer for 10 minutes.
  3. Strain the tea through a mesh sieve into a teapot or mug.
  4. Add 1 tablespoon of honey and 1/2 teaspoon of lemon juice, stirring until dissolved.
  5. Serve immediately, sipping slowly to enjoy the full benefits.

4. Willow

Willow tea reduces inflammation due to the presence of salicin, a chemical similar to aspirin.

This compound blocks the production of prostaglandins, chemicals that cause inflammation and pain. Moreover, willow bark has tannins, which possess strong anti-inflammatory properties.

By inhibiting the inflammatory response, willow tea alleviates symptoms of various conditions like arthritis, bursitis, and tendonitis. Furthermore, willow tea's anti-inflammatory effects stem from flavonoids, antioxidant compounds that enhance the effects of salicin and tannins.

Therefore, regular consumption of willow tea offers an effective, natural remedy for inflammation.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in willow tea that help with inflammation.

  • Bark: The bark of the willow tea plant contains salicin, a compound that has strong anti-inflammatory properties.
  • Leaves: The leaves of the willow tea plant are rich in flavonoids, which have been shown to have anti-inflammatory effects.
  • Rhyzomes: The rhyzomes of the willow tea plant contain high levels of phenolic acids, which have been found to have potent anti-inflammatory effects.
  • Flowers: The flowers of the willow tea plant are a source of polyphenols, which have been found to have anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.
  • Seeds: The seeds of the willow tea plant contain essential oils that have been found to have anti-inflammatory effects.
  • Stems: The stems of the willow tea plant contain salicin and other compounds that have been found to have anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in willow tea that aid with inflammation.

  • Salicin: A natural compound that possesses potent anti-inflammatory properties, reducing inflammation by inhibiting the production of prostaglandins.
  • Flavonoids: These antioxidant compounds reduce inflammation by neutralizing free radicals, stabilizing cell membranes, and inhibiting the production of pro-inflammatory enzymes.
  • Phenolic acids: These compounds have been shown to possess anti-inflammatory effects by inhibiting the activity of enzymes and proteins that promote inflammation.
  • Tannins: Astringent compounds that alleviate inflammation by reducing swelling, soothing irritated tissues, and inhibiting the release of inflammatory mediators.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic willow tea for inflammation.

  1. Gather 2-3 willow bark strips from a willow tree, ensuring they are at least 1 inch wide and 4 inches long.
  2. Fill a pot with 4 cups of water, add willow bark, and bring it to a boil on high heat.
  3. Reduce heat to low, cover the pot, and let it simmer for 15-20 minutes.
  4. Remove the pot from heat, mix in 1 teaspoon of lemon juice and honey to taste, then let the tea cool down.
  5. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the willow bark, and drink it in 3-4 portions throughout the day.

5. Green Tea

Green tea contains polyphenolic compounds, particularly catechins, which possess potent anti-inflammatory properties.

Epigallocatechin-3-gallate (EGCG), a key catechin in green tea, inhibits the production of inflammatory molecules like tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α) and interleukin-6 (IL-6). By reducing the activation of nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-κB), a transcription factor that regulates inflammation, green tea helps maintain a balanced immune response.

Furthermore, green tea's antioxidants counteract oxidative stress, a contributing factor to chronic inflammation, thus promoting overall health and well-being.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in green tea tea that help with inflammation.

  • Leaves: Contain potent anti-inflammatory compounds, such as catechins and EGCG, which inhibit the production of inflammatory chemicals in the body.
  • Buds: Rich in polyphenols and antioxidants, buds can help reduce inflammation and protect cells from damage caused by oxidative stress.
  • Stems: High in flavonoids, stems help alleviate inflammation by modulating immune system responses and suppressing the activity of pro-inflammatory enzymes.
  • Seeds: Packed with gamma-linolenic acid (GLA), seeds provide a natural anti-inflammatory effect, potentially aiding in the management of chronic inflammatory conditions.
  • Flowers: Contain essential oils and antioxidants that support the reduction of inflammation and promote overall health by scavenging free radicals and preventing cellular damage.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in green tea tea that aid with inflammation.

  • EGCG: This catechin is a powerful antioxidant that inhibits the production of inflammatory chemicals.
  • ECG: This flavan-3-ol has antioxidant properties that reduce inflammation by neutralizing free radicals.
  • EGC: This catechin has anti-inflammatory properties due to its ability to inhibit the activation of inflammatory genes.
  • Gallic acid: This phenolic acid has potent anti-inflammatory effects by suppressing the activation of inflammatory enzymes.
  • Thearubigins: These polyphenols have anti-inflammatory effects by inhibiting the production of inflammatory mediators.
  • Theaflavins: These polyphenols have antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties by suppressing the activation of inflammatory genes.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic green tea tea for inflammation.

  1. Boil 8 ounces of water to 160-170°F, taking 3-5 minutes.
  2. Place one teaspoon of high-quality green tea leaves in a strainer or infuser.
  3. Pour the hot water over the tea leaves, allowing them to steep for 2-3 minutes.
  4. Remove the tea leaves and enjoy the soothing, inflammation-fighting green tea.
  5. For best results, drink 2-3 cups of green tea daily between meals.

Right below you will find a list of the most important parts in peppermint tea that help with inflammation.

  • Menthol: A natural analgesic and anti-inflammatory agent that works by inhibiting the release of inflammatory mediators.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic peppermint tea for inflammation.

  1. Boil 8 ounces of water to a rolling boil for 1-2 minutes.
  2. Crush 1 tablespoon of dried peppermint leaves, releasing oils for 30 seconds.
  3. Combine boiled water and crushed peppermint leaves in a teapot, steeping for 5-10 minutes.
  4. Strain the tea to remove leaves, pouring into a cup.
  5. Sip the hot peppermint tea, providing instant relief for inflammation.

  • Flowers: Rich in antioxidants and volatile oils, contributing to the plant's anti-inflammatory properties and providing relief from inflammation.
  • Seeds: High in essential oils, fatty acids, and antioxidants, making them valuable for reducing inflammation and supporting overall health.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in basil tea that aid with inflammation.

  • Rosmarinic acid: This antioxidant in basil tea inhibits the production of pro-inflammatory molecules, reducing inflammation.

  • Linalool: This compound in basil tea has been shown to have anti-inflammatory properties, possibly by reducing the production of nitric oxide, a pro-inflammatory molecule.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic basil tea for inflammation.

  1. Gather 1 tablespoon of fresh basil leaves and rinse them thoroughly under cold water.
  2. Boil 1 cup of water in a teapot or saucepan, then add the basil leaves and let it simmer for 5 minutes.
  3. Strain the tea into a cup, discarding the basil leaves.
  4. Add honey or lemon to taste, if desired.
  5. Serve the tea hot and enjoy its anti-inflammatory benefits.

Moreover, cinnamon contains powerful antioxidants such as polyphenols, which help neutralize free radicals and minimize tissue damage caused by inflammation. In vitro and animal studies reveal that cinnamon extract lowers the expression of inflammatory cytokines, further supporting its anti-inflammatory properties.

Additionally, cinnamon tea's warm temperature increases blood circulation, helping to alleviate inflammation by promoting healing in affected areas.

The list below shows the primary active constituents in cinnamon tea that aid with inflammation.

  • Cinnamaldehyde: The primary active compound in cinnamon, cinnamaldehyde inhibits the release of inflammatory arachidonic acid, reducing overall inflammation.
  • Eugenol: This phenolic compound found in cinnamon acts as a potent anti-inflammatory agent by suppressing the activation of pro-inflammatory enzymes and mediators.
  • Linalool: A terpene alcohol present in cinnamon, linalool inhibits pro-inflammatory cytokines, responsible for initiating and maintaining the inflammatory response.
  • Coumarin: Coumarin exerts anti-inflammatory effects by decreasing nitric oxide production and inhibiting cyclooxygenase-2 enzyme, responsible for inflammation and pain.
  • O-methoxycinnamaldehyde: Acting as an antioxidant and anti-inflammatory agent, this compound inhibits the nuclear factor kappa B (NF-κB) pathway, suppressing the expression of pro-inflammatory genes.

The following recipe gives a procedure to make a basic cinnamon tea for inflammation.

  3. Simmer the mixture on low heat for 10-15 minutes.
  4. Remove cinnamon stick and add honey to taste, if desired.
  5. Serve the tea hot and enjoy its anti-inflammatory benefits.

  4. Add 1-2 teaspoons of raw honey to the hot tea, stirring well to dissolve.
