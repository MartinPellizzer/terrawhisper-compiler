What to know before using Danthonia decumbens (Heath Grass)?

Danthonia decumbens, also known as poverty oatgrass, is a perennial grass native to North America that has been used in herbal medicine for its diuretic and anti-inflammatory properties.

This grass typically grows in dry, sandy soils and can tolerate a range of temperatures and sunlight exposure, making it adaptable to various horticultural conditions. Danthonia decumbens has a tufted growth habit, growing in tight clumps, and produces narrow, flat leaves that are rolled inward along the edges.

The flowering stems of Danthonia decumbens are slender and can reach up to 30 inches in height, bearing tiny, compact spikelets that contain the seeds.

What is the morphology of Danthonia decumbens?

Morphologically, Danthonia decumbens is a perennial grass with short, slender rhizomes and densely tufted, ascending to erect culms, growing up to 90 cm tall (USDA & NRCS, 2022).

The leaves are typically 2-5 mm wide with a conspicuous, membranous ligule that helps distinguish it from other Danthonia species (Ross, 1986). Its inflorescence is typically a panicle, containing short, compact spikelets, which are distributed uniformly along the rachis (Hitchcock, 1951).
