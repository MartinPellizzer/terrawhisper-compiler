What to know before using Bellis perennis (Daisy)?

Bellis perennis, also known as the common daisy, is a low-growing perennial native to Europe and western Asia that has been naturalized in many parts of the world.

It has a fibrous root system and forms dense mats of narrow, spoon-shaped leaves that are hairy on both sides. The flower heads are composed of white ray florets surrounding a cluster of yellow disc florets, and they typically bloom from early spring to late fall.

In addition to its ornamental value, Bellis perennis has a long history of use in traditional medicine, particularly for treating wounds, skin irritations, and various digestive ailments. It contains several bioactive compounds, including flavonoids, phenolic acids, and essential oils, that are believed to have anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties. Bellis perennis prefers well-drained soil and partial shade, but it is adaptable to a wide range of growing conditions.

It is often used as a groundcover or in rock gardens, and it is also a popular choice for meadows and wildflower gardens.
