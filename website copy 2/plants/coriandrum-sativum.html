What to know before using Coriandrum sativum (Coriander)?

Coriandrum sativum, also known as cilantro or coriander, is an herb with a variety of uses and benefits.

Its medicinal properties are extensive, including aiding in digestion, reducing anxiety, and even possessing anti-cancer effects due to its rich antioxidant content. In terms of horticultural conditions, Coriandrum sativum thrives in well-drained soil and full sun, and it is a cool-season annual that can be grown in most regions.

As for its botanical characteristics, Coriandrum sativum has delicate, bright green leaves and delicate white or pale pink flowers, and it produces small, round seeds that are used as a spice in many cuisines around the world.

When it comes to planting Coriandrum sativum, it is essential to sow the seeds directly into the garden rather than transplanting seedlings.

This is because the plant has a long taproot that can be damaged during transplanting. Sow the seeds about 1/4 to 1/2 inch deep and about 1 to 2 inches apart. Thin the seedlings to about 6 to 8 inches apart once they have their first true leaves.

It is best to plant Coriandrum sativum in the early spring or late summer, as it bolts and goes to seed quickly in hot weather.
