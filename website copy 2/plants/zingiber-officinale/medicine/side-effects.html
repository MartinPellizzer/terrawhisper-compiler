10 possible side effects of Zingiber officinale (Ginger) if used improperly

Zingiber officinale, commonly known as ginger, is generally considered safe when used appropriately.

However, if used improperly, it can lead to several health issues. Overconsumption may cause digestive discomfort, including bloating, diarrhea, and stomach pain.

In extreme cases, it might lead to heartburn and gastric ulcers due to its irritant effects on the gastrointestinal lining. Moreover, improper preparation or storage can result in mold growth, which may cause allergic reactions, respiratory issues, or infections. Pregnant women should avoid large doses of ginger as it may increase the risk of miscarriage or stillbirth.

Lastly, individuals on blood-thinning medications should use ginger cautiously since it might interfere with blood clotting, leading to excessive bleeding.

1. Diarrhea

Diarrhea caused by Zingiber Officinale refers to an upset in the normal pattern of bowel movements resulting in loose, watery stools that occur more frequently than usual.

This side effect is primarily caused by the gingerols and shogaols present in ginger, which are known to stimulate digestion and increase intestinal motility. The main medicinal preparations of ginger that can cause diarrhea include ginger extracts, ginger powder, ginger oil, and ginger tea, especially when consumed in large quantities or over a long period of time.

Individuals who are more likely to experience diarrhea from ginger include those who are sensitive or allergic to ginger, those who consume ginger on an empty stomach, and those who take ginger in combination with other medications or supplements that stimulate digestion. To avoid this side effect, it is important to start with a low dose of ginger and gradually increase the dosage, consume ginger with food, and avoid taking ginger in combination with other laxatives or digestive stimulants.

It is also recommended to consult with a healthcare provider before taking ginger as a dietary supplement.

2. Heartburn

Heartburn caused by Zingiber Officinale refers to a burning sensation in the chest or throat that can occur after consuming this natural remedy.

The main active constituents in ginger that can cause heartburn are gingerols and shogaols, which can relax the lower esophageal sphincter and allow stomach acid to flow back up into the esophagus. The main medicinal preparations of ginger that can cause this side effect if misused include ginger supplements, ginger tea, and ginger extracts. People who are more likely to experience heartburn after consuming ginger are those with a history of gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), pregnant women, and individuals who consume large amounts of ginger in a short period of time.

To avoid experiencing heartburn after consuming ginger, it is recommended to start with small doses and gradually increase the amount, avoid consuming ginger on an empty stomach, and avoid consuming large amounts of ginger in a short period of time.

3. Stomach Upset

Stomach upset caused by Zingiber Officinale refers to a range of symptoms such as nausea, bloating, and stomach cramps that may occur after consuming this plant.

The main active constituents of ginger that can cause this side effect are gingerols and shogaols, which can stimulate the production of stomach acid and irritate the lining of the stomach. Medicinal preparations of ginger, such as ginger supplements and ginger tea, can cause stomach upset if taken in excessive amounts or not taken with food. Individuals who are more likely to experience stomach upset from ginger include those with sensitive stomachs, acid reflux, or inflammatory bowel disease.

To avoid stomach upset from ginger, it is recommended to start with small doses and gradually increase, consume ginger with meals, and avoid consuming large amounts of ginger on an empty stomach.

4. Mouth Irritation

Mouth irritation caused by Zingiber Officinale refers to a sensitive, inflamed, or irritated condition of the mouth or oral mucosa that may result from the use of this herb.

The main active constituents in ginger that can cause this side effect are gingerols and shogaols, which give ginger its characteristic flavor and aroma. The primary medicinal preparations of ginger that can cause mouth irritation if misused include ginger powder, ginger oil, ginger tea, and ginger supplements.

Individuals who are more likely to experience mouth irritation from using ginger are those who are sensitive or allergic to ginger, have a history of mouth irritation or inflammation, or consume large amounts of ginger in a short period. To avoid mouth irritation when using ginger, it is essential to use it in moderation, avoid consuming it on an empty stomach, and rinse the mouth with water after use.

It is also recommended to consult with a healthcare provider before using ginger as a medicinal preparation, especially for those with pre-existing medical conditions or taking other medications.

5. Lightheadedness

Lightheadedness caused by Zingiber Officinale refers to a feeling of faintness or floating that can occur as a side effect of using this plant.

This sensation is mainly due to the active constituents of ginger, such as gingerols and shogaols, which can cause a drop in blood pressure when taken in large amounts or when used improperly. Medicinal preparations such as ginger essential oil, ginger powder, and ginger extract can all lead to lightheadedness if not used according to recommended dosages. Individuals who are pregnant, elderly, or have low blood pressure are more prone to experiencing this side effect.

To avoid this side effect, it is important to follow the recommended dosages and consult a healthcare professional before using ginger as a medicinal preparation, particularly if taking other medications or have pre-existing medical conditions.

6. Rash

Rash caused by Zingiber Officinale refers to a skin irritation that can occur as a side effect of using this plant.

The main active constituents in ginger that can cause this side effect are gingerols and shogaols, which can cause an allergic reaction in some individuals. The main medicinal preparations of ginger that can cause a rash if misused include ginger essential oil, ginger powder, and fresh ginger root.

People who are more likely to experience a rash from ginger are those with a history of allergies to plants in the Zingiberaceae family, those with sensitive skin, and those who consume or use large amounts of ginger. To avoid a rash from ginger, it is recommended to start with small amounts, and gradually increase the dosage over time.

It is also important to perform a patch test before using ginger topically, and to seek medical advice if a rash or other adverse reaction occurs.

7. Bruising

Bruising caused by Zingiber Officinale refers to the discoloration and swelling of the skin resulting from broken blood vessels underneath, which can occur due to the use of this plant.

The main active constituents in ginger that can cause bruising include gingerols and shogaols, which have anti-inflammatory and blood-thinning properties. Medicinal preparations such as ginger supplements, ginger essential oil, and ginger tea, if misused or taken in excessive amounts, can increase the risk of bruising. Those who are more likely to experience bruising from ginger use are individuals who have a bleeding disorder or take blood-thinning medication, are pregnant or breastfeeding, or have sensitive skin.

To avoid bruising, it is essential to use ginger in moderation, follow recommended dosages, and consult with a healthcare provider before use, especially if there are any pre-existing medical conditions or medication use.

8. Nausea

Nausea caused by Zingiber Officinale refers to a queasy or uncomfortable feeling in the stomach that can lead to vomiting.

This side effect can be attributed to the presence of active constituents such as gingerols and shogaols. Ginger's medicinal preparations, including fresh, dried, or powdered root, as well as ginger tea and essential oil, can cause nausea if misused or consumed in excessive amounts. Those who are more likely to experience this side effect include pregnant women, individuals with low iron levels, and people taking certain medications such as blood thinners.

To avoid this side effect, it is recommended to consume ginger in moderate amounts, consult with a healthcare provider before use, and pay attention to any potential interactions with existing medical conditions or medications.

9. Vomiting

Vomiting caused by Zingiber Officinale refers to the involuntary forceful expulsion of stomach contents through the mouth.

This side effect is primarily linked to the presence of gingerols and shogaols, the active constituents in ginger that can stimulate the digestive tract and, in some cases, induce vomiting. Ginger teas, tinctures, and capsules are the main medicinal preparations that can cause this side effect if misused or consumed in excessive amounts. Individuals with a sensitive or irritable digestive system, pregnant women, and those with preexisting gastrointestinal disorders are more likely to experience vomiting as a side effect of ginger.

To avoid this side effect, it is crucial to consume ginger in moderation, stay hydrated, and consult a healthcare professional for proper guidance on appropriate dosages and usage, especially when combining ginger with other medications.

10. Drowsiness

Drowsiness caused by Zingiber Officinale refers to the feeling of sleepiness or decreased level of consciousness that can occur in some individuals after using this plant.

The main active constituents of ginger that can cause this side effect are gingerols and shogaols, which have been found to have sedative properties. The main medicinal preparations of ginger that can cause drowsiness if misused include ginger tea, ginger extract, and ginger capsules. People who are more likely to experience drowsiness after using ginger are those who are sensitive to its sedative effects, those who consume large amounts of ginger, and those who take ginger in conjunction with other sedative medications.

To avoid experiencing drowsiness after using ginger, it is important to follow the recommended dosages, to not exceed the maximum daily dosage, and to avoid using ginger in the evening or before bedtime.
