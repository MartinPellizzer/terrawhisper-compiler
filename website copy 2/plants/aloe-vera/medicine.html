Aloe vera (Aloe Vera) Medicinal Guide

Aloe vera, a succulent plant native to North Africa, has been used for centuries for its medicinal properties.

The benefits of Aloe vera include its ability to soothe burns, heal wounds, and reduce inflammation, making it a popular natural remedy for skin conditions. The constituents of Aloe vera include water, vitamins, minerals, enzymes, and antioxidants, which contribute to its healing properties.

Preparations of Aloe vera include gels, creams, juices, and supplements, which can be applied topically or ingested orally. However, Aloe vera can cause side effects such as diarrhea, stomach cramps, and electrolyte imbalances when taken orally in large amounts. Precautions should be taken when using Aloe vera, especially for pregnant women and people with certain medical conditions, as it can interact with certain medications and cause adverse reactions.

Therefore, it is important to consult with a healthcare provider before using Aloe vera for medicinal purposes.

It also enhances digestion by reducing inflammation in the gut, supporting healthy blood sugar levels, and relieving constipation.

Furthermore, aloe vera improves skin condition by hydrating and nourishing the skin, reducing redness and inflammation, and alleviating arthritis pain.

What are the active constituents of Aloe vera (Aloe Vera)?

Aloe vera, also known as Aloe barbadensis, has several active constituents that contribute to its beneficial properties.

These include Vitamin C and E, which are antioxidants that help protect the skin from damage caused by free radicals. Resveratrol, another antioxidant, has anti-aging and anti-inflammatory effects.

Salicylic acid and methyl salicylate are compounds that have exfoliating and analgesic properties, respectively. Allantoin is known for its soothing and skin-protective effects, while phenolic compounds have antimicrobial and antioxidant properties. Glycoproteins and polysaccharides, including acemannan, have been shown to have immunomodulatory and wound-healing effects.

Tocopherols, a class of compounds similar to Vitamin E, also contribute to the antioxidant properties of aloe vera.

The following list shows the active constituents of Aloe vera.

What are the medicinal preparations of Aloe vera (Aloe Vera)?

Aloe vera has many medicinal preparations, such as gel, extract, juice, capsules, ointment, powder, emulsion, suppository, tonic, and syrup.

The gel is obtained from the inner leaf and is commonly used topically for skin conditions like burns, sunburn, and eczema. Aloe extract is a concentrated form of the gel and can be found in various pharmaceutical and cosmetic products. Aloe juice is made from the entire leaf and is consumed orally for its laxative and digestive properties. Aloe capsules provide a convenient way to take aloe orally for various health benefits.

Aloe ointment is a mixture of aloe gel and other ingredients, often used for skin conditions and wound healing. Aloe powder is a dried form of the gel, which can be mixed with water or other liquids for consumption or topical use. An aloe emulsion is a stable mixture of aloe gel and oil, often used in cosmetics and skincare products. Aloe suppositories are used for the treatment of hemorrhoids and other rectal conditions. Aloe tonic is a diluted form of aloe juice, taken orally for its health benefits.

Aloe syrup is a sweetened form of aloe juice, often used as a flavoring agent or sweetener in beverages and foods.

The following list shows the medicinal preparations of Aloe vera.

  • Gel: Aloe vera Gel is an efficacious, natural, transparent exudate obtained from the succulent leaves of the aloe vera plant, known for its potent healing, hydrating, and anti-inflammatory properties, which can soothe burns, moisturize skin, improve wound healing, reduce redness and irritation, and boost collagen production.
  • Extract: Aloe vera Extract is a clear liquid obtained from the inner gel of the aloe vera leaf, known for its soothing, hydrating, and anti-inflammatory properties, which help to heal wounds, reduce redness and irritation, and improve the appearance of skin.
  • Juice: Aloe vera Juice is an all-natural, nutrient-rich, healing elixir derived from the succulent leaves of the aloe barbadensis plant, known for its soothing properties on digestive systems, boosting immune function, and providing antioxidant support to combat cellular damage.
  • Capsule: Aloe vera Capsule is a dietary supplement, containing the inner gel of the aloe vera leaf, which provides therapeutic properties such as anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and digestive support, making it beneficial for individuals seeking relief from gastrointestinal issues, joint pain, or immune system enhancement.
  • Ointment: Aloe vera Ointment is a soothing topical preparation made from the gel-like substance extracted from the succulent leaves of the aloe vera plant, which contains various nutrients, vitamins, minerals, and enzymes that help in healing wounds, reducing inflammation, soothing skin irritations, and moisturizing dry skin.
  • Powder: Aloe vera Powder is a fine, dehydrated form of aloe vera gel that retains its healing and soothing properties, providing internal and external health benefits such as aiding in digestion, boosting immunity, promoting skin renewal, and reducing inflammation.

What are the possible side effects of using Aloe vera (Aloe Vera) for medicinal purposes?

Aloe vera can have side effects if used improperly, such as allergic reaction, which includes symptoms like difficulty breathing, hives, or swelling of the face, lips, or tongue.

Additionally, it can cause skin irritation, dermatitis, and throat irritation, resulting in discomfort and redness. Internal consumption of aloe vera may lead to diarrhea, stomach pain, constipation, heartburn, low blood sugar, and may worsen existing cases of latex allergy.

It is crucial to use aloe vera cautiously and in moderation, as excessive use can lead to these adverse effects.

The following list shows the possible side effects of Aloe vera.

  • Allergic Reaction: Aloe vera may cause allergic reaction if applied or ingested incorrectly due to its potential cross-reactivity with certain food substances and the presence of allergens like latex proteins in some varieties.
  • Diarrhea: Aloe vera may cause diarrhea when ingested in large quantities or taken without adequate water due to its natural laxative properties and anthraquinone content, which stimulate intestinal contractions and increase bowel movements.
  • Skin Irritation: Aloe vera may cause skin irritation when applied incorrectly due to its potential allergenic components, including latex or sulfates present in some commercial products, as well as over-application or application on broken or sensitive skin.
  • Low Blood Sugar: Aloe vera may cause low blood sugar when it is applied topically and absorbed into the body in large amounts, interfering with glucose absorption in the gut and increasing insulin release.
  • Latex Allergy: Aloe vera may cause latex allergy when its inner sap, which contains the allergens ALM and MAL, is mistakenly applied to broken or irritated skin, triggering an immune response against these proteins.
  • Stomach Pain: Aloe vera may cause stomach pain when ingested incorrectly due to its strong laxative effects and harsh bitter compounds, which can irritate the gastrointestinal tract and lead to discomfort or cramps.
  • Throat Irritation: Aloe vera may cause throat irritation if ingested incorrectly or in excessive amounts due to its natural latex compound containing anthraquinone, which can act as a laxative and potentially damage the mucous membranes in the throat.
  • Constipation: Aloe vera may cause constipation if ingested incorrectly due to its natural laxative properties when excessive amounts are taken or the inner leaf gel is consumed without proper processing, which contains anthraquinone compounds that can irritate the intestinal lining and disrupt normal bowel function.
  • Dermatitis: Aloe vera may cause dermatitis when it is applied to broken or irritated skin, as its active components can penetrate deeper into damaged skin, leading to an allergic reaction and inflammation.
  • Heartburn: Aloe vera may cause heartburn when ingested in large quantities or taken without food due to its strong laxative effects that can stimulate the lower esophageal sphincter and lead to acid reflux, resulting in discomfort and burning sensation in the chest.

What are the precautions to take when using Aloe vera (Aloe Vera) medicinally?

It's important to take some precautions when using Aloe vera for medicinal purposes, such as consulting a doctor first, as they can provide guidance on appropriate usage and potential interactions with other medications.

Additionally, it's crucial to avoid using aloe vera in combination with diabetes medication, as it may lower blood sugar levels and lead to hypoglycemia. Using only pure aloe vera gel is another essential precaution, as some products may contain additives that can cause adverse reactions. Furthermore, it's vital to avoid overdosing, applying aloe vera externally only, and checking for allergic reactions before using it.

Lastly, certain groups, such as pregnant women and children, should avoid using aloe vera, and it's important to store it properly to maintain freshness, dilute it in water for internal use, and keep it out of reach of children.

The following list shows the precautions to take when using Aloe vera.

  • Consult Doctor First: Consult doctor first when using Aloe vera internally as its latex component may cause allergic reactions and excessive use can lead to kidney damage, interacting with certain medications and having contraindications for pregnant women and people with diabetes.
  • Avoid Diabetes Medication Interaction: Avoid diabetes medication interaction when using Aloe Vera internally, as its natural components may lower blood sugar levels and potentially exacerbate hypoglycemic episodes in individuals taking diabetes medications.
  • Use Only Pure Aloe Gel: Use only pure aloe gel because impurities or additives in other forms of aloe vera may negate its therapeutic benefits, potentially causing skin irritation and allergic reactions.
  • Do Not Overdose: Do not overdose on Aloe vera as excessive intake may lead to potentially harmful side effects such as kidney damage, low blood sugar, or interact negatively with certain medications due to its natural laxative properties and powerful active compounds.
  • Apply Externally Only: Apply externally only because Aloe vera contains aloin and anthraquinone, potent substances that stimulate bowel movements when ingested orally, which can cause laxative effects, cramps, and electrolyte imbalance if used internally in excess, potentially leading to dehydration and other health issues.
  • Check For Allergic Reactions: Check for allergic reactions before applying Aloe vera topically, as it contains substances such as aloin and anthraquinone, which may cause skin irritation or contact dermatitis in some individuals.
  • Do Not Use During Pregnancy: Do not use during pregnancy as Aloe vera contains hormone-like substances called anthraquinones, which may stimulate uterine contractions and cause potential harm to the developing fetus.
  • Keep Out Of Reach Of Children: Keep out of reach of children when using Aloe vera because its sap contains anthraquinone, a natural laxative that can lead to serious digestive upset and dehydration if ingested in large quantities by young children.
  • Use Diluted In Water For Internal Use: Use diluted in water for internal use as Aloe vera contains potent active compounds like anthraquinones, which can have strong laxative effects and may cause stomach discomfort or diarrhea when consumed in concentrated form. Therefore, it's crucial to mix Aloe vera gel with water before ingesting it to reduce the risk of adverse reactions and ensure proper absorption of its beneficial nutrients.
  • Store Properly To Maintain Freshness: Store properly to maintain freshness is crucial for Aloe vera because the nutrients, specifically the active compounds like gels and polysaccharides that provide health benefits, begin to deteriorate over time once the plant or gel is harvested, therefore refrigerating or keeping it in a cool, dark place can help slow down this process and ensure optimal freshness and efficacy.
