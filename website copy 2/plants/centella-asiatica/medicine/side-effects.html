10 possible side effects of Centella asiatica (Gotu Kola) if used improperly

Centella asiatica, also known as gotu kola, is a medicinal plant that has been used in traditional medicine for centuries.

When used improperly, it can cause a number of adverse health effects. First, high doses of Centella asiatica may lead to gastrointestinal issues such as nausea, stomach upset, and diarrhea.

Additionally, it may cause headaches, dizziness, and mouth ulcers. Prolonged use or high doses may result in liver toxicity, which can manifest as jaundice, dark urine, and fatigue. Furthermore, allergic reactions, including skin rashes and itching, have been reported in some cases.

Pregnant or breastfeeding women should avoid using Centella asiatica, as its safety during these periods is not well-established.

1. Headache

Headache caused by Centella Asiatica refers to a painful sensation in the head that may occur as a side effect of using this plant.

The main active constituents of Centella Asiatica that can cause this side effect are triterpene saponins, particularly asiaticoside, madecassoside, and madasiatic acid. Medicinal preparations of Centella Asiatica that can cause headaches if misused include teas, capsules, and topical creams. People who are more likely to experience headaches from using Centella Asiatica are those who consume high doses, use it frequently, or have a sensitivity to the plant.

To avoid this side effect, it is recommended to start with a low dose and gradually increase, follow the recommended dosage on the label, and discontinue use if a headache occurs.

2. Upset Stomach

Upset stomach caused by Centella Asiatica refers to a gastrointestinal disturbance that can occur as a side effect of consuming this herbal supplement.

The main active constituents of Centella Asiatica that can cause this side effect are triterpenoid saponins, such as asiaticoside, madecassoside, and madasiatic acid. These compounds can irritate the stomach lining and cause digestive discomfort, particularly if consumed in large quantities or with insufficient water.

Medicinal preparations of Centella Asiatica that can cause this side effect if misused include concentrated extracts, capsules, and teas. Individuals who are more likely to experience upset stomach as a side effect of Centella Asiatica are those with a sensitive digestive system, a history of gastrointestinal disorders, or those who consume large amounts of the herb. To avoid this side effect, it is recommended to start with a low dose and gradually increase, drink plenty of water, and avoid consuming Centella Asiatica on an empty stomach.

Additionally, it is always advisable to consult with a healthcare professional before starting any new supplement regimen.

3. Nausea

Nausea caused by Centella Asiatica refers to a feeling of discomfort in the stomach that may be accompanied by an urge to vomit.

This side effect can be attributed to the main active constituents of Centella Asiatica, which include triterpenoid saponins like asiaticoside, madecassoside, and madasiatic acid. These compounds can irritate the stomach lining, leading to nausea.

Medicinal preparations such as Centella Asiatica teas, capsules, and tinctures, when taken in excessive quantities, can cause nausea. People who are more likely to experience this side effect include those with a sensitive stomach or a history of stomach problems. To avoid this side effect, it is recommended to start with a low dose of Centella Asiatica and gradually increase the dosage over time.

Additionally, taking Centella Asiatica with food or after a meal may help reduce the likelihood of experiencing nausea.

4. Dizziness

Dizziness caused by Centella Asiatica refers to a feeling of lightheadedness or unsteadiness that can occur in some individuals.

This side effect is primarily attributed to the triterpenoid compounds found in Centella Asiatica, particularly asiaticoside, madecassoside, and brahmoside. These active constituents can have a sedative effect on the central nervous system, leading to dizziness if taken in large quantities or misused.

Medicinal preparations such as capsules, tablets, and teas can cause this side effect if not used as directed. Elderly people, those with a history of low blood pressure, and individuals taking sedatives or other central nervous system depressants are more likely to experience dizziness after using Centella Asiatica.

To avoid this side effect, it's important to follow the recommended dosage instructions carefully, avoid taking Centella Asiatica with other sedatives, and consult with a healthcare provider before using this herb if you have any pre-existing medical conditions or are taking other medications.

5. Skin Irritation

Skin irritation caused by Centella Asiatica refers to discomforts such as redness, itching, and swelling of the skin that can occur as a side effect of using this plant.

The main active constituents of Centella Asiatica that can cause skin irritation include triterpenoid saponins, alkaloids, and tannins. Medicinal preparations such as creams, ointments, and capsules that contain high concentrations of these compounds may cause skin irritation if misused or used on sensitive skin. Individuals with allergies to members of the Apiaceae/Carrot family or those with sensitive skin are more likely to experience skin irritation from Centella Asiatica.

To avoid skin irritation, it is recommended to start with a small dose, perform a patch test before using any new preparations, and discontinue use if any signs of irritation occur.

Mouth ulcers caused by Centella Asiatica refer to small, painful lesions that can form inside the mouth as a potential side effect of using this herbal supplement.

The main active constituents in Centella Asiatica that can cause mouth ulcers include triterpene saponins such as asiaticoside, madecassoside, and madasiatic acid. These constituents can cause irritation and inflammation in the mouth, leading to the development of ulcers.

Medicinal preparations of Centella Asiatica that can cause this side effect if misused include concentrated extracts and tinctures, which can increase the risk of mouth ulcers due to their higher potency. Individuals who are more likely to experience mouth ulcers as a side effect of using Centella Asiatica include those who are sensitive or allergic to this herb, as well as those who use high doses or misuse the supplement.

To avoid this side effect, it is important to use Centella Asiatica as directed, follow recommended dosages, and consult with a healthcare provider before using this supplement, especially if you have a history of mouth ulcers or other oral health issues.

10. Dry Mouth

Dry mouth caused by Centella Asiatica refers to the decreased production of saliva resulting in a dry, parched mouth and throat.

The main active constituents of Centella Asiatica that can cause this side effect are triterpene saponins, particularly asiaticoside, madecassoside, and madasiatic acid. This side effect is most commonly associated with oral preparations of Centella Asiatica, such as tinctures and teas, when they are misused or taken in large quantities over an extended period of time. People who are more likely to experience dry mouth as a side effect of Centella Asiatica are those with a history of dry mouth or reduced saliva production, smokers, and the elderly.

To avoid this side effect, it is crucial to use Centella Asiatica as directed, avoid taking it in large quantities or for extended periods, and stay adequately hydrated by drinking plenty of fluids throughout the day.
