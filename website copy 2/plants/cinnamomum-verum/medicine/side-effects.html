10 possible side effects of Cinnamomum verum (Cinnamon) if used improperly

Cinnamomum verum, also known as true cinnamon, has been used for its medicinal properties for centuries.

However, when used improperly, it can cause several health issues. First, ingesting large amounts of cinnamon can lead to mouth, skin, and liver irritation due to its potent essential oils.

Second, it can lower blood sugar levels, which can be dangerous for diabetics if not properly managed. Third, cinnamon has natural blood-thinning properties, which, when taken in excessive amounts, can lead to increased bleeding and may interfere with blood clotting. Furthermore, improper usage can cause allergic reactions, ranging from mild skin rashes to anaphylactic shock.

Lastly, long-term exposure to large quantities of cinnamon may negatively affect the respiratory system and cause scarring of the lung tissue.

1. Mouth Sores

Mouth sores caused by Cinnamomum Verum refers to the development of lesions or inflammation in the mouth, including the gums, tongue, and inner cheeks, after coming into contact with products containing this spice.

The main active constituents of Cinnamomum verum that can cause this side effect are cinnamaldehyde and eugenol, which have irritant properties that can trigger an inflammatory response in the mucous membranes of the oral cavity. Medicinal preparations of Cinnamomum verum that can cause mouth sores if misused include essential oils, powdered spice, and tinctures, which can come into direct contact with the oral tissues and cause irritation. People who are more likely to experience mouth sores from Cinnamomum verum include those with sensitive mucous membranes, pre-existing oral conditions such as ulcers or inflammation, and those who consume large amounts of cinnamon-containing products over an extended period.

To prevent mouth sores caused by Cinnamomum verum, it is recommended to use high-quality cinnamon products, avoid consuming large amounts of cinnamon-containing foods and supplements, and practice good oral hygiene, such as brushing and rinsing the mouth thoroughly after using cinnamon-based oral products.

2. Diarrhea

Diarrhea caused by Cinnamomum Verum refers to an upset in the normal bowel movement pattern, leading to its loosening and increased frequency.

This side effect can be attributed mainly to the volatile oils and phenolic compounds found in cinnamon, which include cinnamaldehyde, eugenol, and linalool. These constituents can stimulate the gastrointestinal tract, promoting motility and leading to diarrhea when consumed in large quantities.

Medicinal preparations such as cinnamon oil and powder, when misused or consumed in excess, may result in this side effect. Those with pre-existing gastrointestinal disorders, such as irritable bowel syndrome, are more likely to experience diarrhea after consuming cinnamon. To avoid this side effect, it is advisable to use cinnamon in moderation, start with small doses, and avoid consuming it on an empty stomach.

In case of any adverse effects, it is recommended to discontinue use and consult a healthcare professional.

3. Thirst

Thirst caused by Cinnamomum Verum refers to the sensation of dryness in the mouth and increased desire for liquids that may occur after consuming this spice.

This side effect is primarily caused by the essential oils and other active constituents found in cinnamon, such as cinnamaldehyde, eugenol, and linalool. These compounds can irritate the mucous membranes in the mouth and throat, leading to dryness and increased thirst.

Medicinal preparations of cinnamon, such as powders, capsules, and essential oils, are more likely to cause thirst if misused or consumed in excessive amounts. People who are more sensitive to the effects of cinnamon, such as those with dry mouth or a history of oral dryness, are at a higher risk of experiencing thirst as a side effect. To avoid this side effect, it is recommended to consume cinnamon in moderate amounts, stay hydrated, and avoid consuming cinnamon on an empty stomach.

Additionally, individuals with a history of sensitivity to cinnamon or its constituents should consult with a healthcare professional before using cinnamon for medicinal purposes.

4. Dizziness

Dizziness caused by Cinnamomum Verum refers to a sensation of lightheadedness, unsteadiness, or vertigo that may occur following the use of this plant.

The main active constituents in Cinnamomum verum that can cause this side effect include cinnamaldehyde, eugenol, and linalool. These compounds can cause dizziness when they react with the central nervous system.

Medicinal preparations such as cinnamon oil and large amounts of cinnamon bark or powder are most likely to cause this side effect if misused. People who are more prone to experiencing dizziness from Cinnamomum verum include those with a history of motion sickness, inner ear problems, or those using high doses of this herb.

To avoid this side effect, it is important to follow recommended dosages and usage guidelines, and to consult with a healthcare provider before using Cinnamomum verum, especially if you have any pre-existing medical conditions or are taking any medications.

5. Headache

Headache caused by Cinnamomum Verum refers to pain or discomfort in the head or neck region that may occur as a side effect of using this spice.

The main active constituents in cinnamon that can cause headaches are primarily essential oils such as cinnamaldehyde, eugenol, and linalool. These compounds can cause irritation and inflammation in the mucous membranes, leading to headaches in some individuals.

Medicinal preparations of cinnamon, such as essential oils, supplements, and concentrated extracts, are more likely to cause headaches if misused or used in excessive amounts. People with a history of migraines, allergies, or sensitivities to cinnamon or related substances are more likely to experience headaches as a side effect. To avoid headaches, it is essential to use cinnamon in moderation, follow recommended dosages, and avoid inhaling cinnamon-containing substances.

If headaches persist or worsen, it is advisable to discontinue use and consult a healthcare professional.

6. Skin Irritation

Skin irritation caused by Cinnamomum Verum refers to the discomfort or inflammation of the skin that can occur after coming into contact with this plant.

The main active constituents of Cinnamomum Verum that can cause this side effect are cinnamaldehyde, eugenol, and linalool, which are natural oils found in the bark and leaves of the plant. Medicinal preparations such as cinnamon essential oil, cinnamon powder, and cinnamon tinctures can cause skin irritation if not used properly or if they come into contact with broken or damaged skin. Individuals with sensitive skin or allergies, as well as those with existing skin conditions like eczema or dermatitis, are more likely to experience skin irritation from Cinnamomum Verum.

To avoid this side effect, it's important to dilute cinnamon essential oil with a carrier oil before applying it to the skin, to avoid skin contact when using cinnamon powder or tinctures, and to do a patch test on a small area of skin before using cinnamon topically.

7. Low Blood Sugar

Low blood sugar caused by Cinnamomum Verum refers to a condition where the level of glucose in the blood is lower than normal.

This is because Cinnamomum Verum contains certain active constituents, such as methylhydroxychalcone polymer and sodium benzoate, which can mimic the action of insulin and increase the rate at which glucose is removed from the bloodstream. The main medicinal preparations of Cinnamomum Verum that can cause this side effect if misused include cinnamon supplements, cinnamon essential oil, and cinnamon bark powder. People who are more likely to experience this side effect are those with diabetes, particularly those who take medications to lower blood sugar, as well as individuals with poor glucose tolerance.

To avoid this side effect, it is important to use Cinnamomum Verum in moderation and only under the guidance of a healthcare professional, monitor blood sugar levels regularly, and adjust the dosage or discontinue use if necessary.

8. Liver Damage

Liver damage caused by Cinnamomum Verum refers to the potential harm that can occur to the liver when consuming this commonly used spice.

The main active constituents in cinnamon that can cause liver damage are coumarin and certain essential oils. These compounds can be found in both the bark and essential oil of the cinnamon plant.

Medicinal preparations such as cinnamon supplements, essential oils, and large amounts of ground cinnamon can cause liver damage if misused or consumed in excessive quantities. People who are at a higher risk of experiencing liver damage from cinnamon include those with pre-existing liver conditions, those taking certain medications, and those who consume large amounts of cinnamon on a regular basis.

To avoid liver damage, it is recommended to use cinnamon in moderation, follow recommended dosages, and consult with a healthcare provider before using cinnamon supplements or large amounts of ground cinnamon.

9. Allergic Reactions

Allergic reactions caused by Cinnamomum Verum refers to the immune system's abnormal response to one or more of its constituents.

The main active constituents that can cause this side effect are essential oils, such as cinnamaldehyde, eugenol, and linalool. These compounds are found in higher concentrations in the bark, leaf, and fruit of the cinnamon tree, and are commonly used in various medicinal preparations like essential oils, teas, and spices.

People who are most likely to experience this side effect include those with existing allergies to plants in the Lauraceae family, those with respiratory conditions like asthma, and those with skin conditions. To avoid this side effect, it is important to start with small doses of cinnamon and gradually increase the dosage over time, under the guidance of a healthcare professional.

It is also recommended to perform a patch test before using cinnamon topically, and to avoid inhaling cinnamon powder or essential oils.

10. Mouth Irritation

Mouth irritation caused by Cinnamomum Verum refers to a sensitive or painful sensation in the mouth that occurs after coming into contact with products containing this spice.

This effect is primarily due to the presence of certain active constituents, particularly cinnamaldehyde and eugenol, which can cause irritation when they come into contact with mucous membranes. Medicinal preparations such as cinnamon oil, cinnamon supplements, and cinnamon-based lozenges or candies have a higher concentration of these compounds and, thus, pose a greater risk of causing mouth irritation if misused or overused.

Individuals with sensitive mouths or existing mouth sores are more likely to experience this side effect. To avoid mouth irritation when using Cinnamomum Verum, it's essential to follow the recommended dosage, avoid rubbing or holding the cinnamon-based product in the mouth for extended periods, and immediately discontinue use if any discomfort arises.

Additionally, thoroughly diluting cinnamon oil before use and seeking professional advice for proper usage can help mitigate this risk.
