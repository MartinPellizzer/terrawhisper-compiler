10 possible side effects of Lycium chinense (Goji Berry) if used improperly

Lycium chinense, also known as Chinese boxthorn or wolfberry, is a plant that has been used in traditional Chinese medicine for centuries.

However, if used improperly, it can have several potential negative health effects. First, overconsumption can lead to stomach discomfort, nausea, and diarrhea. Second, it may interact with certain medications, such as blood thinners, and cause adverse reactions.

Third, individuals with autoimmune diseases should avoid it as it can stimulate the immune system and worsen symptoms. Fourth, pregnant women should avoid consuming it as it may cause miscarriage or preterm labor. Lastly, long-term use of large amounts may lead to toxicity, with symptoms such as dizziness, headache, and muscle weakness.

Therefore, it is crucial to consult with a healthcare provider before using Lycium chinense for medicinal purposes.

1. Diarrhea

Diarrhea caused by Lycium Chinense refers to a condition of loose, watery stools occurring frequently.

This side effect is primarily linked to the alkaloids, polysaccharides, and flavonoids present in the plant. Preparations such as teas, capsules, and powders, if misused or consumed in excess, can lead to this unpleasant condition.

People who are more susceptible to this side effect are those with a sensitive digestive system, those who consume large amounts of the berry, or those who are unaccustomed to consuming it regularly. To avoid this side effect, it is advisable to start with small doses and gradually increase, monitor individual tolerance, and avoid consuming it on an empty stomach.

Additionally, staying hydrated and maintaining a balanced diet can help mitigate the risk.

2. Nausea

Nausea caused by Lycium Chinense refers to the feeling of discomfort and restlessness in the stomach, which may be accompanied by an urge to vomit.

This side effect is typically caused by the active constituents of Lycium chinense, including betaine, scopoletin, and zeaxanthin dipalmitate. These constituents can irritate the stomach and digestive system, particularly when consumed in large quantities or when not used properly.

Medicinal preparations such as Lycium chinense teas, supplements, and extracts are most likely to cause nausea if misused or overused. People who are more susceptible to experiencing nausea as a side effect of Lycium chinense use include those with sensitive stomachs, pregnant women, and individuals with a history of digestive issues. To avoid nausea, it is recommended to start with a low dose of Lycium chinense and gradually increase the dosage over time, to drink plenty of water when consuming the berries or their extracts, and to avoid consuming them on an empty stomach.

It is also advised to consult with a healthcare provider before starting any new supplement regimen, especially for those who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or have pre-existing medical conditions.

3. Vomiting

Vomiting caused by Lycium Chinense refers to the forceful expulsion of stomach contents through the mouth as a result of using this herb.

This side effect can be primarily attributed to the alkaloids, polysaccharides, and betaine present in the fruit. The medicinal preparations that can cause vomiting if misused include Lycium chinense teas, capsules, and extracts.

Individuals with a history of digestive sensitivity or allergies are more likely to experience vomiting after consuming this herb. To avoid this side effect, it is crucial to start with a low dose, gradually increasing it over time, and to avoid consuming the herb on an empty stomach.

Additionally, it is important to consult with a healthcare provider before using Lycium chinense, especially for those with pre-existing medical conditions or those taking other medications.

4. Dry Mouth

Dry mouth caused by Lycium Chinense refers to the decreased production of saliva or the feeling of constant dryness in the mouth.

This can be attributed mainly to the alkaloids, flavonoids, and sesquiterpenoids present in the plant. These active constituents can interact with the salivary glands, leading to reduced saliva production and, consequently, a dry mouth.

Medicinal preparations such as decoctions, capsules, and teas made from the whole plant or its fruit can cause this side effect if misused or taken in large quantities. Individuals who are sensitive to these constituents or have a history of dry mouth are more likely to experience this side effect. To avoid this side effect, it is essential to consume Lycium chinense in moderation and stay hydrated by drinking plenty of fluids.

It is also advised to consult with a healthcare professional before taking any medicinal preparations containing this plant.

5. Increased Heart Rate

Increased heart rate caused by Lycium Chinense refers to an elevated pulse rate that may occur following the consumption or use of this plant.

The main active constituents in Lycium chinense that have been linked to this side effect include betaine, beta-sitosterol, and taurine. Preparations such as teas, capsules, and extracts, particularly when taken in large doses or combined with other stimulants, may increase the risk of this side effect. Individuals who are more likely to experience increased heart rate from Lycium chinense include those with pre-existing heart conditions, those who are sensitive to stimulants, and those who consume large amounts of caffeine or other stimulant substances.

To avoid this side effect, it is important to follow dosage recommendations carefully, avoid combining with other stimulants, and consult with a healthcare provider before using Lycium chinense, especially for those with heart-related health issues or those who are taking medication.

6. High Blood Pressure

High blood pressure caused by Lycium Chinense refers to an abnormally high force of blood against the artery walls during heartbeat, which can lead to serious health problems if not managed.

The main active constituents in Lycium chinense that can cause this side effect are betaine, cerebroside, and scopoletin. The main medicinal preparations of this herb that can cause high blood pressure if misused include dried goji berries, goji berry juice, and goji berry extract. People who are more likely to experience high blood pressure from using Lycium chinense include those with a history of hypertension, kidney disease, and diabetes.

To avoid this side effect, it is crucial to use Lycium chinense in moderation, follow recommended dosages, and consult with a healthcare provider before using this herb, especially if you have a pre-existing medical condition or are taking medication.

7. Hypersensitivity Reactions

Hypersensitivity reactions caused by Lycium Chinense refers to abnormal immune responses that occur after consuming this plant or its extracts.

The main active constituents responsible for this side effect are alkaloids, polysaccharides, and betaine, which can trigger allergic reactions in some individuals. Medicinal preparations such as teas, capsules, and topical ointments made from Lycium chinense contain these active compounds and can cause hypersensitivity reactions if misused or overused.

People with known allergies to other plants in the Solanaceae family or individuals with weakened immune systems may be more prone to experiencing these reactions. To avoid hypersensitivity reactions, it's crucial to start with a low dose of Lycium chinense and gradually increase it over time, while being mindful of any signs of an allergic reaction such as skin rashes, hives, or difficulty breathing.

Discontinuing use immediately and seeking medical attention is advised if an allergic response occurs.

Rash caused by Lycium Chinense refers to an unpleasant skin reaction that can occur as a side effect of consuming this plant.

This reaction is typically caused by the main active constituents of Lycium chinense, which include betaine, physalin, and zeaxanthin. These compounds can trigger skin irritations in susceptible individuals, particularly when the plant is consumed in large quantities or over a long period of time.

The medicinal preparations of Lycium chinense that are most likely to cause this side effect include teas, capsules, and powders. Individuals who are more prone to experiencing rashes, such as those with sensitive skin or allergies, should exercise caution when using Lycium chinense. To avoid this side effect, it is recommended to start with small doses and gradually increase the intake while monitoring for any skin reactions.

9. Dizziness

Dizziness caused by Lycium Chinense refers to a feeling of lightheadedness, unsteadiness, or disorientation that may occur after consuming the herb.

This side effect can be attributed to the main active constituents of Lycium chinense, which include alkaloids, flavonoids, and polysaccharides. These constituents, when misused or consumed in large amounts, can cause a variety of adverse effects, including dizziness.

Individuals who are more likely to experience this side effect include those with a history of motion sickness, low blood pressure, or those taking medication that lowers blood pressure. To avoid this side effect, it is important to use Lycium chinense in moderation, following the recommended dosage and preparation instructions provided by a healthcare professional. It is also advisable to avoid consuming large amounts of the herb, especially on an empty stomach, and to monitor blood pressure levels regularly.

10. Fatigue

Fatigue caused by Lycium Chinense refers to a state of extreme tiredness or lack of energy that can occur with the use of this plant.

The main active constituents of Lycium chinense that can cause this side effect are its polysaccharides, alkaloids, and betaine. This side effect is most commonly associated with medicinal preparations such as teas, capsules, and extracts made from the fruit, leaves, or root of the plant.

Individuals who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or have auto-immune disorders may be more likely to experience fatigue with the use of Lycium chinense. To avoid this side effect, it is important to follow recommended dosages and consult with a healthcare provider before using Lycium chinense, especially if taking any medication or have any pre-existing medical conditions. It is also recommended to gradually increase the dosage of Lycium chinense over a period of time, rather than starting at a high dose.

Additionally, staying hydrated and avoiding alcohol and caffeine can help reduce the risk of fatigue.
