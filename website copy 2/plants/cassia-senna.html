What to know before using Cassia senna (Senna)?

Cassia senna, also known as Senna Alexandrina, is a species of flowering plant native to tropical regions of Africa and Asia.

It is well-known for its medicinal properties, particularly its use as a natural laxative. The plant contains anthraquinone glycosides, which stimulate the colon and promote bowel movements.

Cassia senna prefers well-drained soil and full sun, and it can grow up to 10 feet tall. Its leaves are compound, with 10-20 pairs of leaflets, and its flowers are yellowish-white and borne in terminal racemes.

When harvested and dried, the leaves and seed pods of Cassia senna are used in herbal medicine and as a natural alternative to over-the-counter laxatives.

What are the active constituents of Cassia senna?

Cassia senna has many active constituents, such as anthraquinone glycosides, which are the main medicinally active components of the plant.

These glycosides include sennosides A and B, as well as others like aloe-emodin, rhein, and fragrillin. These compounds work together to provide the herb's well-known purgative effects, stimulating the colon and promoting bowel movements. Additionally, the flavonoids and polyphenols found in senna possess anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, contributing to its potential health benefits.

What are the possible side effects of Cassia senna?

Cassia senna can have some side effects if used improperly, such as digestive discomfort, including cramps, bloating, and gas.

Prolonged use or excessive doses can lead to more serious issues, such as liver damage, seizures, and irregular heartbeat. Additionally, senna may affect the balance of electrolytes in the body, potentially causing symptoms like muscle weakness, confusion, and heart rhythm abnormalities. In some cases, it can also result in unpleasant symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, which can lead to dehydration.

Furthermore, senna can cause pigmentation changes in the colon, a condition known as melanosis coli, which, while typically harmless, might increase the risk of colon cancer in the long term.
