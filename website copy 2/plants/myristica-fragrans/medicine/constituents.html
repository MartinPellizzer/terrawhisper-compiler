10 Best Active Constituents of Myristica fragrans (Nutmeg & Mace)

Myristica fragrans, commonly known as nutmeg, contains several active constituents that have been used for medicinal purposes.

The most well-known active constituent is myristicin, which has been studied for its potential anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antimicrobial properties. Another important compound is elemicin, which has been shown to have neuroprotective effects and may help to improve cognitive function.

The essential oil of nutmeg also contains sabinene, terpineol, and pinene, which have been found to have various medicinal properties such as antioxidant, antimicrobial, and anti-inflammatory activities. Additionally, the compound macelignan found in nutmeg has been shown to have potential chemopreventive effects against cancer.

However, it is important to note that nutmeg should be used in moderation, as high doses can have psychoactive effects and may cause symptoms such as tachycardia, nausea, and seizures.

1. Sabinene

The sabinene contained in Myristica Fragrans, also known as nutmeg and mace, is a terpene with a piney, warm, and woody aroma.

Its main medicinal properties include being an anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antimicrobial agent. Sabinene helps relieve various health conditions such as pain, inflammation, and gastrointestinal issues. This constituent is mainly found in the bark, seeds, and leaves of the Myristica fragrans plant.

Some possible side effects of sabinene, if used improperly, include skin and eye irritation, and potential respiratory issues when inhaled in large amounts.

2. α-Pinene

The α-pinene contained in Myristica Fragrans, also known as nutmeg and mace, is a type of terpene known for its potent medicinal properties.

Its main medicinal properties include anti-inflammatory, bronchodilator, and antibacterial effects, making it useful in treating conditions such as asthma, bronchitis, and other respiratory infections. This compound can help relieve symptoms of these conditions by reducing inflammation, expanding the airways, and killing harmful bacteria.

α-Pinene is found primarily in the essential oil of the nutmeg seed and the aril (the red lacy covering) of the fruit, which is used to produce mace. While α-pinene is generally considered safe when used in appropriate amounts, improper use can lead to some side effects, such as skin irritation, mouth ulcers, and nausea.

Additionally, high doses of nutmeg can cause hallucinations, dizziness, and other central nervous system effects due to the presence of other compounds in the spice.
