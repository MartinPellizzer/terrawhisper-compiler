10 possible side effects of Achillea millefolium (Yarrow) if used improperly

Achillea millefolium, also known as yarrow, is a plant that has been used in herbal medicine for centuries.

However, if used improperly, it can have several potential negative health effects. First, ingesting yarrow in large amounts can cause stomach irritation, dizziness, and kidney irritation. Additionally, touching the plant may cause skin irritation or allergic reactions in some people.

Pregnant women should avoid using yarrow, as it can stimulate uterine contractions. Furthermore, yarrow may interact with certain medications, such as blood thinners and sedatives, and can increase their effects. Lastly, excessive use of yarrow can lead to photosensitivity, making the skin more sensitive to sunlight.

Therefore, it is important to use yarrow with caution and consult with a healthcare professional before use.

1. Allergic Reaction

An allergic reaction to Achillea millefolium refers to an overreaction of the immune system to certain compounds found in this plant.

The main active constituents in Yarrow that can cause an allergic reaction include alkamides, flavonoids, and terpenoids. These compounds can be found in all preparations of Yarrow, but are most concentrated in the essential oil, which is often used in aromatherapy.

People who are more likely to experience an allergic reaction to Yarrow include those with allergies to other members of the Asteraceae family, such as chamomile and ragweed, as well as those with a history of allergic reactions to plants. To avoid an allergic reaction, it is important to use Yarrow in moderation, start with small doses, and discontinue use if any adverse reactions occur.

It is also recommended to consult with a healthcare provider before using Yarrow if you have a history of allergies or sensitivity to plants.

2. Diarrhea

Diarrhea caused by Achillea Millefolium refers to a condition of loose, watery stools occurring frequently and more than usual.

This side effect can be attributed to the main active constituents of yarrow, which include terpene compounds such as camphor, sabinene, and cineole, as well as flavonoids, tannins, and alkaloids. These constituents can stimulate the gastrointestinal tract and increase intestinal motility, leading to diarrhea when taken in large quantities or over a long period of time.

Medicinal preparations such as yarrow tea, tinctures, and essential oils, if misused or taken in excess, can cause this side effect. Those who are more likely to experience diarrhea from yarrow include individuals with sensitive digestive systems, those with a history of gastrointestinal issues, and those who consume large amounts of yarrow for an extended period. To avoid this side effect, it is recommended to follow the recommended dosages, consult with a healthcare professional before using yarrow, and discontinue use if diarrhea occurs.

Proper hydration and monitoring of symptoms is also important for those who are using yarrow.

3. Dizziness

Dizziness caused by Achillea Millefolium refers to a feeling of lightheadedness, unsteadiness, or vertigo that may occur as a side effect of using this herb.

This sensation is primarily triggered by the active constituents of yarrow, which include volatile oils, sesquiterpene lactones, and alkaloids. These compounds can cause dizziness when taken in large quantities or used for extended periods.

Medicinal preparations such as teas, tinctures, and essential oils, especially when misused or consumed excessively, can lead to this side effect. Individuals who are pregnant, have low blood pressure, or are taking blood-thinning medications are more likely to experience dizziness after using yarrow.

To avoid this side effect, it is crucial to use yarrow in moderation, follow recommended dosages, and seek advice from a healthcare provider before using yarrow, especially if you have any pre-existing medical conditions or are taking any medications.

4. Nausea

Nausea caused by Achillea Millefolium refers to a queasy or uncomfortable feeling in the stomach, often accompanied by an urge to vomit.

This side effect can occur due to the presence of active constituents such as flavonoids, tannins, and volatile oils found in the plant. Medicinal preparations such as infusions, decoctions, and tinctures can cause this side effect if misused or taken in large amounts. People who are more likely to experience nausea after using Achillea Millefolium include those with a sensitive stomach, those who consume it on an empty stomach, and those who take excessive amounts.

To avoid this side effect, it is recommended to start with a low dose and gradually increase it, avoid taking it on an empty stomach, and seek advice from a healthcare professional before using it for medicinal purposes.

5. Skin Irritation

Skin irritation caused by Achillea Millefolium refers to the discomfort or inflammation experienced on the skin upon contact with the plant or its preparations.

The main active constituents of yarrow that can cause this side effect are azulene, salicylic acid, and tannins. These constituents can be found in various medicinal preparations such as essential oils, teas, and ointments.

Individuals with sensitive skin or allergies are more likely to experience skin irritation from Achillea millefolium. To avoid this side effect, it is important to use yarrow preparations as directed, and to perform a patch test on a small area of skin before applying it to larger areas.

If skin irritation persists, it is recommended to discontinue use and seek medical attention if necessary.

6. Throat Irritation

Throat irritation caused by Achillea Millefolium refers to a dry, scratchy, or painful sensation in the throat that may occur after using this herb.

This side effect can be traced back to certain active constituents of yarrow, such as the essential oils, sesquiterpene lactones, and alkaloids, which can have irritant properties when ingested or used topically in high concentrations (1). The main medicinal preparations that can cause throat irritation if misused include yarrow teas, tinctures, essential oils, and herbal extracts, especially when used undiluted or in excessive amounts (2). Individuals who are more likely to experience throat irritation from yarrow include those with a history of sensitivity or allergy to the herb, those who consume large amounts of yarrow or use it frequently, and those with pre-existing respiratory conditions, such as asthma or bronchitis (3).

To avoid throat irritation when using yarrow, it is essential to follow the recommended dosages and usage instructions, dilute essential oils and extracts as directed, and discontinue use immediately if any adverse reactions occur (4).

7. Stomach Pain

Stomach pain caused by Achillea Millefolium refers to discomfort or pain in the abdominal area that may occur following the use of this herb.

The main active constituents in Achillea Millefolium that can cause stomach pain include flavonoids, sesquiterpene lactones, and essential oils. These constituents can stimulate the gastrointestinal tract and increase motility, which can lead to stomach pain, particularly in sensitive individuals.

The main medicinal preparations of Achillea Millefolium that can cause stomach pain if misused include teas, tinctures, and capsules. People who are more likely to experience stomach pain after using Achillea Millefolium include those with gastrointestinal sensitivities, digestive disorders, or those who take large doses of the herb. To avoid stomach pain, it is recommended to start with a low dose of Achillea Millefolium and gradually increase the dose over time, as tolerated.

It is also important to follow dosage recommendations carefully and consult with a healthcare provider before using Achillea Millefolium, especially if you have a history of gastrointestinal issues or are taking other medications.

8. Headache

Headache caused by Achillea Millefolium refers to a possible adverse effect characterized by a continuous or throbbing pain in the head.

This side effect can be attributed to the active constituents of yarrow, particularly the sesquiterpene lactones, alkaloids, and flavonoids, which can cause vasodilation or constriction of blood vessels in the head. The medicinal preparations that can cause headache as a side effect include yarrow tea, tincture, and essential oil, especially when used in large amounts or for prolonged periods.

Those who are more likely to experience this side effect include individuals who are sensitive or allergic to yarrow, have a history of migraines or headaches, or consume large amounts of yarrow. To avoid headaches when using yarrow, it is recommended to use it in moderate amounts and for short periods, avoid using it on an empty stomach, and discontinue use if a headache occurs.

It is also advisable to consult a healthcare professional before using yarrow as a medicinal herb.

9. Increased Menstrual Flow

Increased menstrual flow caused by Achillea Millefolium refers to a heavier than normal menstrual period.

This side effect can occur due to the main active constituents of Achillea Millefolium, which include flavonoids, terpenoids, and tannins. These constituents can stimulate the uterus, leading to increased menstrual flow.

Medicinal preparations such as yarrow tea or tincture, when misused or taken in large quantities, can cause this side effect. People who are more likely to experience increased menstrual flow after using Achillea Millefolium are those who are sensitive to its effects or those who have a history of heavy menstrual periods.

To avoid this side effect, it is important to follow the recommended dosage and consult with a healthcare professional before using yarrow, especially for those who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or have a medical condition.

10. Hypotension

Hypotension caused by Achillea Millefolium refers to an abnormally low blood pressure level that can occur as a side effect of using this herb.

The main active constituents in yarrow that can cause this side effect include lactones, flavonoids, and essential oils, which can dilate blood vessels and reduce blood pressure. The medicinal preparations of yarrow that can cause hypotension if misused include teas, tinctures, and essential oils, which can cause a significant drop in blood pressure if taken in large amounts or for extended periods.

People who are more likely to experience hypotension from using yarrow include those who are already taking medication for low blood pressure, those who have a history of fainting or dizziness, and older adults. To avoid hypotension when using yarrow, it is essential to follow the recommended dosage and consult with a healthcare professional before using this herb, especially if you have a history of low blood pressure or are taking other medications.

Additionally, monitoring your blood pressure regularly and avoiding sudden changes in position can help prevent symptoms of hypotension.
